# Vitamin D in Pregnancy: The Complete Guide

Vitamin D is essential during pregnancy for your baby's bone development and your own health. Yet in the UK, where sunlight is limited for much of the year, deficiency is common. This guide explains why vitamin D matters, how much you need, and how to make sure you are getting enough.

## Why Vitamin D Matters {#why-vitamin-d}

Vitamin D helps your body absorb calcium and phosphorus, both of which are needed to build your baby's bones and teeth. It also supports your immune system, healthy cell division, and muscle function. Research suggests that adequate vitamin D in pregnancy may reduce the risk of pre-eclampsia, gestational diabetes, and low birth weight. For your baby, severe deficiency can lead to rickets, soft bones, and poor growth in the first year.

## How Much Do You Need? {#how-much}

The NHS recommends that all pregnant women take 10 micrograms (400 IU) of vitamin D every day, throughout pregnancy and while breastfeeding. This is especially important in the UK between October and March, when the sun is not strong enough for your skin to make vitamin D. Some women with darker skin, those who cover their skin when outdoors, and those who rarely go outside may need a higher dose, which your GP can assess with a blood test.

## Signs of Deficiency {#signs-of-deficiency}

Vitamin D deficiency often has no obvious symptoms, which is why supplementation is recommended for everyone. When symptoms do appear, they can include persistent tiredness, bone or back pain, muscle weakness, low mood, and frequent minor infections. If you experience these, ask your GP for a vitamin D blood test, particularly during the darker winter months.

## Sources of Vitamin D {#sources}

Your main source is sunlight on your skin during the summer months — around 15 minutes a day with some skin exposed is enough for most people. Dietary sources include oily fish such as salmon, mackerel, and sardines; egg yolks; fortified breakfast cereals; and fortified spreads. However, food alone rarely provides enough, which is why a daily supplement is the most reliable way to meet your needs during pregnancy.

## FAQ {#faq}

### Can I take too much vitamin D in pregnancy?

Yes. Very high doses (over 100mcg or 4,000 IU daily) can cause calcium to build up in your body, which may harm your kidneys. Stick to the recommended 10mcg unless your GP advises otherwise.

### Should I take vitamin D if I'm breastfeeding?

Yes. The NHS recommends continuing 10mcg daily while breastfeeding to support your baby's growth and your own bone health. Your baby may also need drops — ask your health visitor.
