# Healthy Diet in Pregnancy: The Complete Guide

Eating well in pregnancy gives your baby the best start in life and helps you feel your best. The good news? A healthy pregnancy diet isn't that different from a normal healthy diet — you just need a few extra nutrients and some sensible precautions.

## The Basics of a Pregnancy Diet {#basics}

A healthy pregnancy diet is based on the same principles as the NHS Eatwell Guide:

- **Eat at least 5 portions of fruit and vegetables per day** — fresh, frozen, tinned, dried, or juiced all count
- **Base meals on starchy carbohydrates** — bread, rice, pasta, potatoes, oats. Choose wholegrain where possible for more fibre
- **Eat protein every day** — lean meat, fish, eggs, beans, pulses, nuts, tofu
- **Include dairy or dairy alternatives** — milk, cheese, yoghurt, or fortified plant alternatives. Choose pasteurised products
- **Choose unsaturated oils and spreads** in small amounts
- **Drink plenty of water** — 6-8 glasses per day
- **Limit foods high in fat, salt, and sugar** — cakes, biscuits, crisps, sugary drinks

You don't need to eat perfectly every single meal. Aim for a balanced pattern over the course of a week, and you'll be getting what you and your baby need.

## Five Key Nutrients You Need {#nutrients}

### 1. Folic Acid
- **Why**: Prevents neural tube defects like spina bifida
- **How much**: 400mcg daily supplement until 12 weeks (higher dose if you have specific risk factors)
- **Food sources**: Leafy greens, fortified cereals, lentils, beans

### 2. Iron
- **Why**: Makes haemoglobin to carry oxygen to your baby. Low iron causes anaemia, which makes you tired
- **How much**: 14.8mg per day (more than non-pregnant women)
- **Food sources**: Lean red meat, dark green vegetables, beans, fortified cereals, dried fruit. Have with vitamin C for better absorption

### 3. Calcium
- **Why**: Builds your baby's bones and teeth. If you don't get enough, the baby takes it from your bones
- **How much**: 700mg per day
- **Food sources**: Milk, cheese, yoghurt, fortified plant milks, leafy greens, tofu, sardines (with bones)

### 4. Vitamin D
- **Why**: Helps your body absorb calcium and supports your immune system. Low levels are common in the UK
- **How much**: 10mcg (400 IU) daily supplement throughout pregnancy and breastfeeding
- **Food sources**: Oily fish, eggs, fortified foods (but sunlight is the main source — hard to get enough in UK winter)

### 5. Omega-3 Fatty Acids
- **Why**: Supports your baby's brain and eye development
- **How much**: Eat 2 portions of fish per week, including 1 oily fish (salmon, mackerel, sardines)
- **Food sources**: Oily fish, walnuts, chia seeds, flaxseeds. If you don't eat fish, consider an algae-based supplement

## The Eatwell Guide in Pregnancy {#eatwell}

The NHS Eatwell Guide shows the proportions of different food groups for a healthy diet:

- **Fruit and vegetables**: 33% of your diet. Aim for at least 5 portions daily.
- **Starchy carbohydrates**: 33%. Choose wholegrain options for more fibre.
- **Protein**: 12%. Meat, fish, eggs, beans, pulses, nuts.
- **Dairy and alternatives**: 15%. Milk, cheese, yoghurt, fortified plant alternatives.
- **Oils and spreads**: 7%. Choose unsaturated options like olive oil.

In pregnancy, you follow the same proportions — you just need slightly more of certain nutrients (iron, calcium, folate) and a daily vitamin D supplement.

## Foods to Avoid {#avoid}

For a full list, see our complete [foods to avoid guide](/nutrition/foods-to-avoid-pregnancy). In summary:

- Soft mould-ripened and blue cheeses (unless cooked)
- Raw or undercooked meat and fish
- Liver and liver products
- Raw eggs (unless British Lion Code stamped)
- Unpasteurised milk
- Shark, swordfish, and marlin
- Limit tuna to 2 steaks or 4 tins per week
- Limit caffeine to 200mg per day
- Avoid alcohol completely

## Supplements You Need {#supplements}

The NHS recommends two supplements for all pregnant women:

- **Folic acid**: 400mcg daily from before conception until 12 weeks. Some women need a higher dose (5mg) — your GP will prescribe this if you have certain risk factors.
- **Vitamin D**: 10mcg daily throughout pregnancy and while breastfeeding.

If you're on a low income, you may be eligible for **Healthy Start** vouchers, which provide free fruit, vegetables, milk, and vitamins. Ask your midwife for details.

You may also benefit from:
- **Iron supplement** if you're anaemic (your midwife will check your levels)
- **Omega-3 supplement** if you don't eat fish (choose a pregnancy-safe one)
- **Pregnancy multivitamin** if your diet is restricted (vegan, vegetarian, food allergies)

Always talk to your midwife before taking supplements — some vitamins can be harmful in high doses (especially vitamin A).

## Managing Weight Gain {#weight}

It's normal to gain weight in pregnancy — you're growing a baby! The amount varies, but most women gain between 10-12.5kg (22-28lb) over the whole pregnancy. In the first trimester, weight gain is usually minimal (1-2kg).

The NHS does NOT recommend "eating for two". You don't need extra calories in the first trimester. In the second trimester, you need about 340 extra calories per day, and in the third, about 450 extra. We cover this in detail in our [extra calories guide](/nutrition/extra-calories-first-trimester).

Focus on the quality of your food, not the quantity. Nutrient-dense foods like fruit, vegetables, wholegrains, and lean protein will support your baby's growth without excessive weight gain.

## Common Diet Challenges in the First Trimester {#challenges}

The first trimester comes with unique dietary challenges:

- **Morning sickness**: Nausea and vomiting can make eating difficult. Try small, frequent meals, bland foods, and ginger. See our [morning sickness foods guide](/nutrition/best-foods-morning-sickness).
- **Food aversions**: You may suddenly hate foods you used to love. This is normal and usually passes by the second trimester. Focus on what you can stomach.
- **Cravings**: Craving certain foods is normal. Most cravings are harmless, but if you crave non-food items (like clay or ice), tell your midwife — this can indicate iron deficiency.
- **Constipation**: Pregnancy hormones slow digestion. Eat plenty of fibre and drink lots of water.
- **Tiredness**: When you're exhausted, cooking is the last thing you want to do. Stock up on healthy convenience foods — pre-cut veg, tinned beans, wholegrain crackers, and fruit.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Do I need to eat organic food in pregnancy?

No. Organic food isn't nutritionally different from non-organic. The most important thing is eating a varied, balanced diet, whether organic or not.

### Can I diet to lose weight in pregnancy?

No. Pregnancy is not the time for weight loss diets. If you're concerned about your weight, talk to your midwife — they can support healthy eating without calorie restriction. Focus on nutritious food, not the scales.

### Should I take a pregnancy multivitamin?

If you eat a varied diet, you may only need folic acid and vitamin D. If your diet is restricted (vegan, allergies, or you're struggling with sickness), a pregnancy-specific multivitamin can help fill gaps. Avoid regular multivitamins — they may contain too much vitamin A.
