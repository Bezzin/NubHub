# Vitamin C in Pregnancy

Vitamin C is important for your immune system and helps your body absorb iron from food — which matters in pregnancy, when your iron needs rise. The good news is that you can almost always get enough from a normal, balanced diet, so there is usually no need for a separate vitamin C supplement on top of your pregnancy multivitamin. This guide covers what vitamin C does, the best food sources, and how much you need.

## Benefits {#s1}

Vitamin C supports several things that are especially useful during pregnancy:

- Immune function, helping your body fight off everyday bugs
- Iron absorption — it helps your body take up the iron from plant foods, which can help protect against iron-deficiency anaemia
- Collagen formation, which your body needs for healthy skin, blood vessels, bones, and gums
- Tissue repair and general wound healing

At the levels found in food and standard prenatal vitamins, vitamin C is safe in pregnancy. A simple tip: pairing iron-rich plant foods (like lentils, beans, or leafy greens) with a source of vitamin C at the same meal can help your body absorb more iron.

## Food Sources {#s2}

Vitamin C is found in lots of everyday fruit and vegetables, so it is easy to include:

- Oranges and other citrus fruits
- Strawberries, kiwi, and blackcurrants
- Bell peppers (capsicum)
- Broccoli, Brussels sprouts, and cabbage
- Tomatoes and potatoes

Vitamin C is sensitive to heat and water, so lightly steaming or eating some vegetables raw helps keep more of it. Most prenatal multivitamins also include vitamin C, so between food and your supplement you are very likely already covered.

## How Much You Need {#s3}

In the UK, adults need around 40mg of vitamin C a day, with a small extra amount recommended during pregnancy. This is an amount you can comfortably get from a balanced diet — for example, one orange or a serving of peppers already provides plenty. Pregnancy multivitamins typically contain a modest top-up as well.

It is best not to take very high-dose vitamin C supplements in pregnancy unless your doctor or midwife has specifically advised it, as more is not better and routine high doses are not recommended. If you have been told you are anaemic, follow the specific advice and any iron supplement your midwife or GP gives you, rather than self-prescribing large amounts of vitamin C.

## FAQ {#faq}

### How much vitamin C do I need in pregnancy?

You need a similar amount to other adults — around 40mg a day with a small extra amount in pregnancy — which a balanced diet easily provides. Most prenatal vitamins also contain some vitamin C, so a separate supplement is usually unnecessary.

### Can I take too much vitamin C in pregnancy?

Getting vitamin C from food is not a concern. However, very high-dose supplements are not recommended in pregnancy and can cause side effects such as tummy upset. Stick to food sources and your usual prenatal vitamin unless a healthcare professional advises otherwise.

### Does vitamin C help with iron absorption?

Yes. Having a source of vitamin C alongside iron-rich foods can help your body absorb iron, which is helpful in pregnancy when iron needs increase. For example, having fruit or vegetables with a lentil or bean-based meal can make a difference.

For personalised advice on vitamins or any supplements in pregnancy, speak to your midwife or GP.
