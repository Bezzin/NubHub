# Magnesium in Pregnancy: Benefits and Safety

Magnesium is a mineral your body relies on every day for healthy muscles and nerves, steady energy, and strong bones, and pregnancy is no exception. The good news is that most people can get the magnesium they need from a balanced, varied diet, so you usually do not need to think about it as a separate supplement. This guide explains what magnesium does, where to find it in food, and how to think about supplements safely during pregnancy.

## Why Magnesium Matters in Pregnancy

Magnesium supports normal muscle and nerve function, helps your body release energy from food, and contributes to building your baby's bones and teeth. Because it is involved in muscle function, some people find it linked to the leg cramps that are common later in pregnancy, and it plays a role in sleep and relaxation too. During pregnancy your body adapts how it handles many nutrients, and a varied diet is the simplest way to keep your intake topped up.

## Good Food Sources of Magnesium

You can find magnesium in lots of everyday foods, which makes it easy to include without much effort. Good sources include wholegrains such as wholemeal bread, brown rice, and oats; nuts and seeds like almonds, cashews, and pumpkin seeds; pulses such as beans, lentils, and chickpeas; leafy green vegetables like spinach and kale; and other foods including bananas and plain dark chocolate. Building meals around these helps cover your needs naturally, alongside the rest of a balanced pregnancy diet.

## Supplements and Staying Safe

If you eat a varied diet, you may not need a magnesium supplement at all. Many standard pregnancy multivitamins contain a modest amount, which is fine. The key NHS-aligned message is that getting magnesium from food is the safe and sensible default, and that very high-dose supplements are not recommended in pregnancy unless a health professional has advised them for a specific reason. Some forms can cause side effects such as loose stools, so do not start a high-dose product on your own.

The two supplements specifically recommended for most pregnancies in the UK are folic acid and vitamin D, not magnesium. If you are considering magnesium for leg cramps, sleep, or any other reason, check first with your midwife, GP, or pharmacist, as the evidence is mixed and the right choice depends on your circumstances. Always tell them about any other supplements or medicines you take.

When to seek advice: if you have persistent or severe leg cramps, ongoing poor sleep, or are tempted to take high doses of any supplement, speak to a health professional rather than self-prescribing. Seek urgent medical help for any severe, one-sided, hot, or swollen calf pain, as this needs to be checked promptly.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Do I need a magnesium supplement in pregnancy?

Usually not. Most people get enough from a varied diet that includes wholegrains, nuts, seeds, pulses, and leafy greens. The supplements routinely recommended in UK pregnancy are folic acid and vitamin D. Check with your midwife or pharmacist before adding magnesium.

### Can magnesium help with pregnancy leg cramps?

Some people find it helpful, but the evidence is mixed and it does not work for everyone. Gentle stretching, staying hydrated, and keeping active can also ease cramps. If cramps are frequent or severe, mention them to your midwife or GP.

### Is it possible to have too much magnesium?

From food, no. From supplements, high doses can cause side effects such as diarrhoea and are not recommended in pregnancy unless advised by a health professional, so avoid large doses unless told otherwise.

For advice suited to your own pregnancy, speak to your midwife, GP, or pharmacist.
