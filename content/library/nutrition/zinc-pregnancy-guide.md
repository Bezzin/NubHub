# Zinc in Pregnancy: Why It Matters

Zinc is an essential mineral that supports your baby's growth, cell division and developing immune system. For most women in the UK, a normal balanced diet provides enough zinc, so you don't usually need a separate supplement. This guide explains what zinc does, how to get it from food, and when it's worth checking in with your midwife or GP.

## Why Zinc Matters {#s1}

Zinc plays a part in many processes your body relies on every day, and these become even more important during pregnancy. It supports cell growth and DNA synthesis, which matters enormously while your baby's organs and tissues are forming. It also helps your immune system work well, supports wound healing, and is involved in taste and appetite.

Because your baby draws on your stores, your need for zinc rises slightly in pregnancy. The good news is that the increase is small, and a varied diet usually covers it without any special effort. Severe zinc deficiency is uncommon in the UK, but it's more likely if you eat very little meat or fish, follow a restricted diet, or have a condition that affects how you absorb nutrients.

## Food Sources {#s2}

You can get zinc from a wide range of everyday foods. Good sources include lean red meat, poultry, dairy products such as cheese and milk, and eggs. Plant sources include beans, lentils, chickpeas, nuts, seeds (such as pumpkin and sesame), and wholegrain bread and cereals.

If you're vegetarian or vegan, you can still meet your needs, but plant-based zinc is absorbed slightly less easily. Including plenty of beans, lentils, nuts, seeds and wholegrains across the day helps. Soaking, sprouting or fermenting pulses and grains can improve how much zinc your body takes up. If you're unsure whether your diet is providing enough, ask your midwife about seeing a dietitian.

## When to Seek Advice {#s3}

Most pregnancy multivitamins designed for the UK contain a sensible amount of zinc, and the NHS specifically recommends folic acid and vitamin D rather than zinc on its own. Avoid taking high-dose zinc supplements unless a healthcare professional has advised it, as too much zinc over time can interfere with how your body absorbs other minerals such as copper.

Speak to your GP or midwife if you follow a very limited diet, have ongoing digestive problems, or feel you may not be getting enough nutrients overall. They can offer tailored advice and arrange any checks that are needed.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Can I take zinc supplements in pregnancy?

A standard pregnancy multivitamin containing a modest amount of zinc is generally considered fine. It's best to avoid separate high-dose zinc tablets unless your GP or midwife has recommended them, because consistently high intakes can affect the balance of other minerals.

### Do I need extra zinc if I'm vegetarian or vegan?

Not necessarily, but you do need to plan a little more carefully, as zinc from plants is absorbed slightly less well. Including beans, lentils, nuts, seeds and wholegrains regularly usually covers your needs. If you're worried, ask about a referral to a dietitian.

### What are the signs of low zinc?

Possible signs include frequent infections, slow wound healing, hair changes or a reduced sense of taste, though these can have many other causes. If you're concerned, see your GP rather than self-diagnosing, as blood tests and a proper assessment give a clearer picture.

For personal advice about your diet or supplements in pregnancy, speak to your midwife or GP.
