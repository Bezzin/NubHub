# Can I Eat Raw Mushrooms Pregnant?

Mushrooms are a common ingredient in salads, dips, and many cold dishes, so it is natural to wonder whether eating them raw is safe during pregnancy. The short answer is that cooked mushrooms are the safer choice, and raw mushrooms are best avoided — not because of any serious toxin risk, but because cooking reduces certain compounds and makes mushrooms easier to digest and more nutritious. Here is what you need to know.

## Are Raw Mushrooms Safe in Pregnancy?

Raw mushrooms are not on the NHS list of foods to avoid in pregnancy, which means they are not considered a major safety risk in the same category as unpasteurised cheese, raw meat, or liver. However, there are practical reasons why cooked mushrooms are generally a better option during pregnancy.

**Digestibility:** Raw mushrooms contain chitin, a tough fibrous compound that makes up their cell walls. Chitin is difficult for humans to digest, meaning your body absorbs fewer nutrients from raw mushrooms than from cooked ones. During pregnancy, when your nutritional needs are higher, getting the most from your food matters.

**Nutrient availability:** Cooking mushrooms — particularly heating them — breaks down the cell walls and makes nutrients such as B vitamins, potassium, and selenium more bioavailable. If you are eating mushrooms for their nutritional value, cooked mushrooms deliver more benefit.

**Bacteria risk:** Like any raw produce, mushrooms can carry surface bacteria if they have not been thoroughly washed. During pregnancy your immune system is working slightly differently, which can make you more susceptible to foodborne illness. Washing mushrooms carefully before eating them raw reduces but does not eliminate this risk.

**Agaritine:** Raw mushrooms, particularly common cultivated varieties, contain a naturally occurring compound called agaritine. Cooking significantly reduces agaritine levels. Current evidence does not suggest that eating occasional raw mushrooms causes harm, but if you are eating mushrooms regularly, cooked is the more cautious choice.

## What Mushrooms Are Safe to Eat Cooked in Pregnancy?

Commonly available cultivated mushrooms — including white button, chestnut, portobello, and cremini — are safe to eat cooked in pregnancy. They are a useful source of B vitamins, including some B12 in UV-exposed varieties, selenium, potassium, and fibre.

Wild mushrooms should be treated with more caution. Unless you are completely certain of the species, wild mushrooms carry a risk of misidentification, and some wild species are toxic. If you are buying wild mushrooms from a reputable source such as a supermarket or farmers' market, they are generally safe to eat once cooked.

Dried mushrooms rehydrated and cooked are also fine during pregnancy.

## Practical Tips

- Wash mushrooms thoroughly before eating or cooking, whether raw or cooked.
- If you want to include mushrooms in a salad, lightly sautéing them first and allowing them to cool is a safer approach than eating them completely raw.
- Avoid foraging for wild mushrooms during pregnancy unless you have genuine expertise in identification.
- Mushrooms cooked in cream sauces, soups, or stir-fries are all fine, provided other ingredients in the dish are also pregnancy-safe.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Can I eat raw mushrooms in a salad when pregnant?

The NHS does not specifically list raw mushrooms as a food to avoid in pregnancy, so an occasional raw mushroom in a salad is unlikely to cause harm. That said, cooked mushrooms are more nutritious and easier to digest. If you are eating mushrooms regularly, lightly cooking them first is the better habit during pregnancy.

### Are mushrooms a good food to eat during pregnancy?

Yes, cooked mushrooms are a nutritious addition to a pregnancy diet. They provide B vitamins, selenium, and potassium. Mushrooms that have been exposed to UV light or sunlight also contain vitamin D, which is important during pregnancy. They are low in calories and work well in a wide range of meals.

### What foods should I actually avoid in pregnancy?

The NHS advises avoiding unpasteurised soft cheeses, raw or undercooked meat and eggs, liver and liver products, raw shellfish, and high-mercury fish such as shark, swordfish, and marlin. It also recommends limiting oily fish to two portions per week and avoiding alcohol entirely. Raw mushrooms are not on this list, though cooked is still preferable.
