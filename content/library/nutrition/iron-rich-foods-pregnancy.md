# Iron-Rich Foods for Pregnancy

Iron is one of the most important nutrients in pregnancy. Your body needs it to make extra blood for you and your baby, and low iron can leave you exhausted. Here's how to get enough iron from your diet.

## Why Iron Matters in Pregnancy {#why}

In pregnancy, your blood volume increases by about **30-50%**. Your body needs iron to make haemoglobin, the protein in red blood cells that carries oxygen around your body — and to your baby. If you don't get enough iron, you develop **iron-deficiency anaemia**, which can cause:

- Severe fatigue and weakness
- Dizziness and breathlessness
- Pale skin
- Increased risk of infections
- Higher risk of premature birth and low birth weight

Anaemia is common in pregnancy — about 1 in 7 pregnant women in the UK develop it. Your midwife will check your iron levels at your booking appointment and again at around 28 weeks.

## How Much Iron Do You Need? {#how-much}

The NHS recommends **14.8mg of iron per day** during pregnancy — significantly more than the 8.7mg recommended for non-pregnant women aged 19-50. This is because your body needs extra iron for the increased blood volume and your baby's development.

Most women can get enough iron from a balanced diet, but if your levels are low, your GP or midwife will prescribe a supplement.

## Top 15 Iron-Rich Foods {#top-foods}

Here are the best dietary sources of iron:

1. **Lean red meat** (beef, lamb): 2.1mg per 100g. The richest and most easily absorbed source.
2. **Liver**: Very high in iron BUT avoid in pregnancy due to vitamin A content.
3. **Lentils** (cooked): 3.3mg per cup. Excellent plant-based source.
4. **Chickpeas** (cooked): 4.7mg per cup. Add to curries, salads, or blend into hummus.
5. **Tofu**: 5.4mg per half cup. Great for vegetarian diets.
6. **Spinach** (cooked): 6.4mg per cup. Though some of this is less well-absorded.
7. **Fortified breakfast cereals**: Many contain 4-8mg per bowl. Check the label.
8. **Pumpkin seeds**: 4.2mg per quarter cup. Sprinkle on salads or yoghurt.
9. **Dark chocolate** (70%+): 3.4mg per 28g. A delicious iron source!
10. **Edamame beans**: 3.5mg per cup. A tasty snack.
11. **Dried apricots**: 1.5mg per quarter cup. Sweet and portable.
12. **Quinoa** (cooked): 2.8mg per cup. Use instead of rice.
13. **Turkey** (dark meat): 2.3mg per 100g. Lean and versatile.
14. **Kale** (cooked): 1.2mg per cup. Nutrient-dense vegetable.
15. **Wholegrain bread**: 0.9mg per slice. Better than white bread for iron.

Other good sources:
- Beans (kidney, black, pinto)
- Sardines
- Eggs
- Broccoli
- Nuts (especially cashews and almonds)
- Fortified plant milks

## Haem vs Non-Haem Iron {#types}

There are two types of iron in food:

- **Haem iron**: Found in meat, poultry, and fish. This is **easily absorbed** by your body. About 15-35% of haem iron is absorbed.
- **Non-haem iron**: Found in plant foods (lentils, beans, spinach, fortified cereals). This is **less easily absorbed** — only about 2-20% is absorbed.

This doesn't mean plant-based iron is useless — you just need to eat more of it and combine it with absorption boosters (see below). Vegetarians and vegans can get enough iron, but it requires more planning.

## How to Boost Iron Absorption {#boost}

You can significantly increase how much iron your body absorbs by combining foods strategically:

### DO combine iron with:
- **Vitamin C**: Dramatically increases non-haem iron absorption. Have a glass of orange juice with your cereal, or tomatoes with your lentils, or peppers with your spinach.
- **Meat, fish, or poultry**: Even a small amount of meat with a plant-based meal boosts iron absorption from the plants (this is called the "meat factor").

### DON'T combine iron with:
- **Tea and coffee**: Contain tannins that block iron absorption. Avoid drinking tea or coffee with meals — wait at least an hour.
- **Calcium**: High calcium foods can inhibit iron absorption. Don't take your iron supplement with milk or a calcium-rich meal.
- **Phytates**: Found in bran and wholegrains, these can reduce absorption. Soaking beans and grains before cooking helps.

### Best combinations:
- Fortified cereal + orange juice
- Lentil curry with tomatoes
- Spinach salad with red pepper
- Lentil soup with a wholegrain roll
- Beans on toast with a side of tomatoes

## Signs of Iron Deficiency {#deficiency}

Watch for these symptoms:
- **Extreme tiredness**: More than normal pregnancy fatigue
- **Weakness and dizziness**: Especially when standing up
- **Shortness of breath**: With minimal exertion
- **Pale skin, lips, and nail beds**
- **Headaches**
- **Cold hands and feet**
- **Brittle nails**
- **Restless legs**

If you notice any of these, tell your midwife. A simple blood test can check your iron levels.

## Iron Supplements {#supplements}

If your iron levels are low, your GP or midwife will prescribe a supplement. Tips for taking iron:

- **Take with vitamin C**: A glass of orange juice or a vitamin C tablet boosts absorption.
- **Take on an empty stomach**: Iron is best absorbed without food, but if it makes you nauseous, take it with a small snack.
- **Don't take with tea, coffee, or milk**: Wait at least an hour.
- **Don't take with your pregnancy multivitamin**: Calcium and other minerals can interfere with absorption.
- **Be aware of side effects**: Iron supplements can cause constipation (drink plenty of water and eat fibre) and dark stools (this is normal).
- **Give it time**: It takes 2-3 months to rebuild iron stores.

Never take iron supplements without having your levels checked first — too much iron can be harmful.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Can I get enough iron from food if I'm vegetarian?

Yes, but it requires planning. Eat plenty of lentils, beans, tofu, fortified cereals, and leafy greens. Always combine non-haem iron with vitamin C for better absorption. Your midwife will check your levels and prescribe a supplement if needed.

### Will my iron levels be checked in pregnancy?

Yes. Your midwife will check at your booking appointment (8-12 weeks) and again at around 28 weeks. If you have risk factors (previous anaemia, vegetarian diet, heavy bleeding), they may check more often.

### Should I take an iron supplement just in case?

No. Only take iron supplements if your levels are low. Too much iron can cause constipation, nausea, and in rare cases, iron overload. A balanced diet is the best approach, with supplements only if a blood test shows you need them.
