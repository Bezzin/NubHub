# Foods to Avoid in Pregnancy: The Complete NHS List

Knowing what to avoid eating during pregnancy can feel overwhelming, but the NHS guidance is clear and straightforward. Here's the complete list of foods to avoid, foods to be careful with, and what you can eat safely.

## Why Some Foods Are Risky {#why}

In pregnancy, your immune system is naturally suppressed to stop your body rejecting your baby. This means you're more susceptible to food poisoning. The main risks the NHS is concerned about are:

- **Listeria**: A bacteria that can cause listeriosis, which can lead to miscarriage, stillbirth, or severe illness in newborns. Found in unpasteurised dairy, soft mould-ripened cheeses, and ready-to-eat foods.
- **Salmonella**: A bacteria causing food poisoning, which can be severe in pregnancy. Found in raw or undercooked eggs, poultry, and meat.
- **Toxoplasmosis**: A parasite found in undercooked meat, unwashed vegetables, and cat litter. Can cause serious harm to your baby.
- **Vitamin A toxicity**: Too much vitamin A (retinol) can harm your baby's development. Found in liver and liver products.
- **Mercury and other contaminants**: Found in certain fish, can affect your baby's nervous system.

## Foods to Avoid Completely {#avoid}

According to the NHS, you should completely avoid the following during pregnancy:

### Cheese
- **Soft mould-ripened cheeses**: Brie, camembert, chèvre (goat's cheese with a white rind)
- **Soft blue-veined cheeses**: Roquefort, gorgonzola, danish blue
- (These can be eaten if **cooked thoroughly** until steaming hot)

### Meat and Pâté
- **Raw or undercooked meat**: No rare steaks, no pink meat. All meat should be cooked until steaming hot throughout
- **Liver and liver products**: Liver pâté, liver sausage, fish liver oil supplements — too much vitamin A
- **All types of pâté**: Including vegetable pâté — risk of listeria

### Fish
- **Shark, swordfish, and marlin**: High mercury levels — avoid completely
- **Raw shellfish**: Oysters, mussels, clams, prawns — risk of food poisoning
- **Limit tuna to 2 tuna steaks (140g each) or 4 medium tins per week**: Mercury risk

### Eggs
- **Raw or partially cooked eggs** (unless they have the British Lion Code stamp): No runny eggs, raw egg in mayonnaise, mousse, or cake batter

### Milk
- **Unpasteurised (raw) milk**: All raw milk products

### Other
- **Unwashed vegetables and salads** (when eating out): Risk of toxoplasmosis
- **Cold cured meats** (unless cooked): Salami, chorizo, pepperoni, prosciutto — risk of toxoplasmosis

## Foods to Be Careful With {#careful}

These foods need special attention:

### Eggs
Eggs with the **British Lion Code** stamp (a red lion mark on the shell) are considered safe to eat runny or raw in the UK. This includes soft-boiled eggs, poached eggs, and eggs in homemade mayonnaise. Eggs without this stamp should be cooked until the yolk and white are solid.

### Fish
- Eat **2 portions of fish per week**, one of which should be oily (salmon, mackerel, sardines)
- **Limit tuna** to 2 steaks or 4 tins per week
- **Avoid shark, swordfish, and marlin** completely
- Cook all fish thoroughly (no raw fish/sushi unless previously frozen)

### Caffeine
- Limit to **200mg per day** (about 2 cups of instant coffee or 3 cups of tea)
- Remember caffeine is also in chocolate, cola, energy drinks, and some medications

### Peanuts
- **Safe to eat** unless you're allergic yourself — previous advice to avoid has been withdrawn
- If you have a family history of allergies, discuss with your midwife

## Foods That Are Safe {#safe}

Despite the list of things to avoid, there's plenty you can eat safely:

- **Hard cheeses**: Cheddar, parmesan, edam, gouda — all safe
- **Pasteurised soft cheeses** (without a mould rind): Mozzarella, feta, halloumi, ricotta, cream cheese, cottage cheese — all safe
- **Cooked cheeses**: Any cheese that's been cooked until steaming hot is safe, including brie and camembert
- **Cooked meat**: All meat cooked until steaming hot throughout (no pink)
- **Cooked fish**: Salmon, cod, haddock, prawns — all safe when cooked
- **British Lion Code eggs**: Safe runny or raw
- **Pasteurised milk**: All standard supermarket milk
- **Washed salads and vegetables**: Wash thoroughly at home
- **Cooked shellfish**: Prawns, mussels, and oysters are safe when cooked until hot
- **Most herbal teas**: (see our pregnancy-safe herbal teas guide)
- **Pasteurised honey**: Safe
- **Nuts**: All nuts are safe unless you're allergic

## Caffeine Limits {#caffeine}

The NHS recommends limiting caffeine to **200mg per day** in pregnancy. High caffeine intake has been linked to miscarriage and low birth weight. To put this in context:

- 1 mug of instant coffee: ~100mg
- 1 mug of filter coffee: ~140mg
- 1 mug of tea: ~75mg
- 1 can of cola: ~40mg
- 1 energy drink: ~80mg
- 1 bar of dark chocolate (50g): ~25mg

So you can have about **2 mugs of instant coffee** or **2-3 mugs of tea** per day and stay within the limit. Remember to count all sources of caffeine, not just coffee.

## Alcohol {#alcohol}

The NHS advises **avoiding alcohol completely during pregnancy**, especially in the first trimester. If you do drink, keep it to no more than 1-2 units once or twice a week. However, the safest approach is to not drink at all, as no level of alcohol has been proven safe in pregnancy.

Alcohol in the first trimester carries the highest risk, as your baby's organs are forming. The first 12 weeks are a critical period for development, so avoiding alcohol is particularly important now.

## What If You Accidentally Eat Something on the List? {#accident}

Don't panic. The risk from a single exposure is low. Here's what to do:

1. **Stop eating it**: No need to finish the rest.
2. **Watch for symptoms**: Nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea, or fever within 24 hours.
3. **Contact your GP or call 111** if you feel unwell or develop a fever.
4. **Don't beat yourself up**: One accidental exposure is very unlikely to cause harm. The risk comes from regular consumption.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Can I eat sushi when pregnant?

Only if the fish has been previously frozen (which kills parasites) and is from a reputable source. Most UK supermarket sushi uses previously frozen fish, but it's best to check. Restaurant sushi may not use frozen fish, so it's riskier.

### Can I eat mayonnaise when pregnant?

Shop-bought mayonnaise in the UK is usually made with pasteurised eggs and is safe. Homemade mayonnaise should use British Lion Code eggs. If you're not sure about the eggs used, ask or avoid.

### Is it safe to eat liver during pregnancy?

No. Liver and liver products contain very high levels of vitamin A (retinol), which can cause birth defects. Avoid liver, liver pâté, liver sausage, and cod liver oil supplements throughout pregnancy.
