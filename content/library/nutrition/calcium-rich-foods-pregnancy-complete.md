# Calcium-Rich Foods for Pregnancy: Complete Guide

Calcium is one of the most important minerals in pregnancy. Your baby needs it to build strong bones and teeth, and if your diet does not supply enough, your body will draw on your own bone stores to make up the difference. The good news is that most women can meet their calcium needs through food alone — you do not need a separate calcium supplement unless advised by your midwife or GP.

## How Much Calcium Do You Need in Pregnancy? {#s1}

The NHS recommends 700mg of calcium per day during pregnancy — the same as the general adult recommendation. This is because your body becomes more efficient at absorbing calcium from food during pregnancy, which compensates for the increased demand. A balanced diet that includes dairy or suitable alternatives will typically cover this.

## Calcium-Rich Foods to Include {#s2}

**Dairy products**
Milk, cheese, and yogurt are among the most concentrated sources of calcium in the UK diet and are safe to eat in pregnancy (with some exceptions — see below). A glass of semi-skimmed milk and a matchbox-sized piece of hard cheese together provide a substantial contribution toward your daily target.

Hard cheeses such as cheddar and parmesan are safe. Soft mould-ripened cheeses like brie and camembert should be avoided unless cooked until piping hot, because of the risk of listeria. Pasteurised soft cheeses such as cream cheese, cottage cheese, and ricotta are fine.

**Fortified plant milks**
If you do not eat dairy, fortified soya, oat, almond, or other plant milks are a good alternative — check the label to confirm they are fortified with calcium. Unfortified versions contain much less calcium.

**Leafy green vegetables**
Kale, pak choi, broccoli, and spring greens contain useful amounts of calcium. Spinach also contains calcium but also contains oxalates, which reduce how much your body can absorb — so it is less efficient as a calcium source than other greens.

**Tofu**
Calcium-set tofu (made with calcium sulphate — check the label) is a good plant-based source. The calcium content varies considerably by brand, so it is worth comparing.

**Tinned fish with bones**
Tinned sardines and tinned salmon eaten with the soft, edible bones are a surprisingly rich source of calcium. They are also a good source of omega-3 fatty acids.

**Fortified foods**
Some breakfast cereals, breads, and orange juices are fortified with calcium. Check the nutrition label for calcium content.

**Dried figs and other foods**
Dried figs, almonds, and white beans contain smaller but meaningful amounts of calcium and can contribute as part of a varied diet.

## If You Are Dairy-Free or Vegan {#s3}

It is entirely possible to meet your calcium needs in pregnancy without dairy, but it does require some thought. Focus on fortified plant milks, calcium-set tofu, generous portions of suitable leafy greens, tinned fish with bones (if you eat fish), and fortified cereals. If you follow a vegan or dairy-free diet, it is worth discussing your intake with your midwife at your booking appointment, who can advise whether a supplement is appropriate for you.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### How much calcium do I need in pregnancy?

The NHS recommends 700mg of calcium per day, which is the same as the general adult recommendation. Your body absorbs calcium more efficiently during pregnancy, so the requirement does not increase significantly. Most women who eat a balanced diet including dairy or fortified alternatives will meet this without difficulty.

### What if I'm dairy-free — how do I get enough calcium in pregnancy?

Choose fortified plant milks (soya, oat, or almond with added calcium), calcium-set tofu, leafy greens such as kale and pak choi, tinned fish with bones, and fortified cereals. Speak to your midwife if you are unsure whether your diet is providing enough, particularly if you follow a vegan diet.

### Do I need a calcium supplement in pregnancy?

Most women in the UK do not need a separate calcium supplement as long as their diet includes adequate sources. A standard pregnancy multivitamin does not usually contain a full daily dose of calcium — it is not designed to. If you are concerned about your intake, discuss it with your midwife or GP rather than self-supplementing, as too much calcium from supplements can cause problems.
