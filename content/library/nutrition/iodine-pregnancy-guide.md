# Iodine in Pregnancy

Iodine is one of those nutrients that rarely gets as much attention as folic acid or iron, but it is just as important. It plays a central role in your baby's brain development and is essential for healthy thyroid function — in both you and your baby — throughout pregnancy.

## Why Iodine Matters {#s1}

Your thyroid gland uses iodine to produce thyroid hormones. During pregnancy, thyroid hormones regulate your baby's metabolism and — critically — their neurological development. In the earliest weeks, before your baby's own thyroid begins to function, they rely entirely on your thyroid hormones. Adequate iodine throughout pregnancy supports normal cognitive development, hearing, and physical growth.

Severe iodine deficiency during pregnancy is associated with impaired brain development in the baby. Even mild-to-moderate deficiency, which is more common in the UK than many people realise, has been studied in relation to children's cognitive outcomes. This is why getting enough iodine matters even if you feel perfectly well yourself.

## How Much Iodine Do You Need? {#s2}

The NHS recommends that pregnant women aim for around 140–200 micrograms (mcg) of iodine per day. Many nutritional guidelines and health bodies, including the World Health Organization, recommend 250 mcg daily during pregnancy and breastfeeding. Your midwife or GP can advise on the target appropriate for your circumstances.

The key point is that your iodine requirement increases during pregnancy, and many women in the UK do not reach even the baseline recommendation through diet alone.

## Best Food Sources of Iodine {#s3}

Iodine is naturally present in the soil, and foods grown or raised in iodine-rich environments contain higher levels. The best dietary sources available in the UK include:

- **Dairy products** — milk, cheese, and yogurt are among the richest sources of iodine in the UK diet. A glass of cow's milk provides a meaningful amount of iodine, and dairy tends to be the primary source for most UK women.
- **White fish and seafood** — white fish such as cod, haddock, and plaice are good sources. Oily fish contain some iodine but are not necessarily higher than white fish.
- **Eggs** — a useful source, particularly if you eat them regularly.
- **Seaweed** — contains iodine, but levels vary enormously depending on the species and source, and some types (particularly brown seaweed such as kelp) can contain dangerously high amounts. The NHS advises against eating seaweed supplements during pregnancy for this reason.
- **Iodised salt** — used in many countries as a public health measure, but iodised salt is not widely used in the UK, so this is not a reliable source for most British women.

Plant foods grown in UK soil generally contain very little iodine, which is why vegans and those who avoid dairy and fish face a particular challenge.

## Iodine Deficiency in the UK {#s4}

The UK does not have a systematic iodine fortification programme the way some other countries do. UK soils are naturally low in iodine, and the main dietary sources — dairy and fish — are avoided by a growing number of people for various reasons.

Research has found that iodine intake in UK pregnant and breastfeeding women is frequently below recommended levels, particularly among those following plant-based diets. If you do not eat dairy or fish regularly, it is worth discussing your iodine intake with your midwife.

## Should You Supplement? {#s5}

Many standard prenatal vitamins sold in the UK do not contain iodine, or contain only small amounts. If you eat dairy and white fish regularly, you may be meeting your needs through diet. If you are vegan, vegetarian, or avoid dairy and fish for any reason, you are likely to benefit from a supplement containing iodine.

Before taking any additional iodine supplement beyond what is in your prenatal vitamin, speak to your GP or midwife. Both too little and too much iodine can affect thyroid function, so supplementing appropriately — rather than in high doses — is important.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Should I take an iodine supplement in pregnancy?

This depends on your diet. If you regularly eat dairy products and white fish, you are more likely to meet your needs through food. If you are vegan or avoid dairy and fish, supplementation is worth discussing with your GP. Check whether your prenatal vitamin contains iodine — many UK brands do not include it, or include only a small amount.

### Which foods are highest in iodine in the UK?

Cow's milk and other dairy products are the main source for most UK women. White fish such as cod and haddock are also good sources. Eggs contribute a useful amount. Iodised salt is common in other countries but not widely available in the UK.

### Is iodine safe to take during pregnancy?

Iodine is safe and necessary during pregnancy at recommended levels. Very high doses — for example from kelp or seaweed supplements — can actually disrupt thyroid function. Stick to the amount in a reputable prenatal vitamin and discuss any additional supplementation with your midwife or GP.
