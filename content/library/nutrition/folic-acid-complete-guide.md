# Folic Acid in Pregnancy: The Complete Guide

Folic acid is one of the most important supplements you can take before and during early pregnancy. It helps your baby's neural tube develop properly and dramatically reduces the risk of serious conditions such as spina bifida. In this guide we explain what folic acid is, why it matters, how much you need, and where to get it.

## What Is Folic Acid? {#what-is-folic-acid}

Folic acid is the synthetic form of folate, a B vitamin (B9) that occurs naturally in foods. Your body uses folate to make new cells, produce DNA, and support rapid cell growth — all of which are essential during pregnancy. Because it is difficult to get enough folate from food alone, the NHS recommends a daily folic acid supplement for anyone who could become pregnant.

Folate and folic acid are often used interchangeably, but there is a key difference: folate is the natural form found in leafy greens and legumes, while folic acid is the stable, synthetic version used in supplements and fortified foods. Once absorbed, your body converts folic acid into the active form it needs.

## Why Folic Acid Matters in Pregnancy {#why-folic-acid-matters}

The neural tube forms very early in pregnancy — usually within the first four weeks, often before you even realise you are pregnant. This tube eventually becomes your baby's brain and spinal cord. Without enough folate, the neural tube may not close properly, leading to neural tube defects (NTDs) such as spina bifida and anencephaly.

Studies show that taking folic acid daily before conception and during the first 12 weeks of pregnancy can reduce the risk of NTDs by up to 70%. Folic acid also supports the growth of the placenta, helps form red blood cells, and may lower the risk of premature birth and low birth weight.

## How Much Do You Need? {#how-much-do-you-need}

The standard NHS recommendation is 400 micrograms (mcg) of folic acid per day, starting before conception and continuing until you are 12 weeks pregnant. Some women need a higher dose of 5mg daily, including those who have had a previous pregnancy affected by an NTD, those taking certain anti-seizure medications, women with diabetes, and women with a higher body mass index. Your midwife or GP can advise on the right dose for your circumstances.

## Folate-Rich Foods {#folate-rich-foods}

While supplements are important, eating folate-rich foods adds extra benefit. Good sources include spinach, kale, Brussels sprouts, broccoli, asparagus, peas, chickpeas, lentils, beans, fortified breakfast cereals, and oranges. Try to include a range of these foods throughout the week, and remember that cooking can reduce folate levels, so lightly steaming vegetables preserves more of it.

## FAQ {#faq}

### Can I take too much folic acid?

Doses up to 1mg per day are safe for most women. The 5mg dose is only for those with specific risk factors and should be prescribed by a clinician. Very high intakes can mask vitamin B12 deficiency, so always follow professional guidance.

### What if I didn't take folic acid before pregnancy?

Start taking it as soon as you find out you are pregnant. The neural tube closes by around week six, so early supplementation still helps, and folate continues to support your baby's growth throughout pregnancy.
