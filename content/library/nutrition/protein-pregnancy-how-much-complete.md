# Protein Pregnancy How Much Complete

How much protein do you need in pregnancy? Complete guide to protein requirements and sources.

Protein is one of the most important nutrients during pregnancy. It is the main building block for your baby's cells, organs, and tissues, and it supports the growth of your placenta, breasts, and increased blood supply. Most women in the UK get enough protein through their normal diet, but it is worth checking that you are consistently including good sources at every meal — particularly if you are vegetarian, vegan, or struggling with nausea that puts you off meat.

## How Much Protein Do You Need in Pregnancy?

The NHS recommends pregnant women consume slightly more protein than they did before pregnancy. A general guide is around 70–100g of protein per day, though the exact amount varies depending on your body weight and trimester. Your needs increase most in the second and third trimesters as your baby grows most rapidly.

To put this in context, a chicken breast provides roughly 30g of protein, a tin of tuna around 25g, two eggs around 12g, and a portion of Greek yoghurt around 10–15g. Building your meals around a protein source at breakfast, lunch, and dinner will usually get you where you need to be.

## Best Protein Sources in Pregnancy

### Animal Sources

- **Lean meat and poultry** — chicken, turkey, and lean beef. Ensure all meat is well cooked with no pink remaining.
- **Fish** — oily fish such as salmon and sardines also provide omega-3 fatty acids. Limit oily fish to two portions per week during pregnancy. Avoid shark, marlin, and swordfish.
- **Eggs** — Lion-marked eggs in the UK are safe to eat runny. Eggs are a versatile, affordable protein source.
- **Dairy** — milk, cheese, and yoghurt provide protein alongside calcium. Choose pasteurised products and avoid mould-ripened soft cheeses.

### Plant-Based Sources

If you eat less meat or no meat at all, you can still meet your protein needs with careful planning:

- **Legumes** — lentils, chickpeas, kidney beans, and black beans are excellent protein sources and also rich in iron and fibre.
- **Tofu and tempeh** — versatile soya-based proteins that work in a wide range of meals.
- **Nuts and nut butter** — almonds, cashews, and peanut butter are useful snacks and additions to meals.
- **Seeds** — hemp, chia, and pumpkin seeds are protein-rich and easy to add to porridge, smoothies, or salads.
- **Wholegrains** — quinoa, oats, and wholegrain bread contribute to protein intake alongside other nutrients.

## Protein and Common Pregnancy Challenges

**Morning sickness** can make it hard to eat protein-rich foods, especially meat, in the first trimester. If meat or fish smells put you off, try eggs, Greek yoghurt, cheese, or nut butter — these tend to be better tolerated when nausea is bad. Protein-rich snacks eaten little and often can also help to stabilise blood sugar and reduce nausea.

**Vegetarian and vegan pregnancies** require a bit more planning to ensure you are combining plant proteins across the day. Speak to your midwife or GP about additional supplementation if you are fully plant-based — in particular, vitamin B12, which is not found in plant foods, needs to come from fortified foods or a supplement.

## Do You Need Protein Supplements?

Most women do not need protein supplements or protein powders during pregnancy. Getting protein through whole food sources is preferable because food also delivers other important nutrients alongside the protein. If you are genuinely struggling to eat enough due to hyperemesis (severe vomiting) or another condition, speak to your midwife or a registered dietitian who can advise you on appropriate supplementation.

Be cautious with protein powders — many contain additives, sweeteners, herbs, or very high doses of nutrients that have not been assessed for safety in pregnancy. Do not take any protein supplement without discussing it with your healthcare team first.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much protein do I need in pregnancy?

A general guide is around 70–100g per day, increasing from your pre-pregnancy intake as your baby grows. Most women in the UK meet this through a balanced diet that includes a protein source at every meal — meat, fish, eggs, dairy, legumes, or tofu. If you are unsure whether you are getting enough, a midwife or dietitian can help you review your diet.

### Do I need protein supplements in pregnancy?

No — most pregnant women do not need protein supplements if they are eating a varied diet. Whole foods are preferred because they deliver a wider range of nutrients. If you have a medical reason for struggling to eat enough, speak to your midwife or a registered dietitian rather than self-prescribing protein powders.

### What are the best plant-based protein sources in pregnancy?

Lentils, chickpeas, kidney beans, tofu, tempeh, edamame, nuts, seeds, and quinoa are all excellent plant-based protein sources. Combining different plant proteins across the day (for example, grains with legumes) helps ensure you get all the essential amino acids your body needs.

Always speak to your midwife or GP for personalised nutritional advice during pregnancy.
