# Gender Blood Test at 7 Weeks: Does It Work?

If you are seven weeks pregnant and wondering whether a blood test can already tell you your baby's sex, the short answer is: it depends which test you are asking about. At seven weeks, the options are limited, and accuracy is not as straightforward as some companies suggest. This guide covers what is realistically available at this stage and what you can expect.

## How Gender Blood Tests Work {#section-1}

From early in pregnancy, small amounts of fetal DNA circulate in the mother's bloodstream. This is called cell-free fetal DNA (cffDNA). Blood tests that predict sex work by detecting whether any Y-chromosome DNA is present in a sample of your blood. If Y-chromosome DNA is found, the prediction is male; if none is detected, the prediction is female.

The challenge at very early stages — such as seven weeks — is that the proportion of fetal DNA in your blood is still relatively low. The less fetal DNA available, the greater the chance of a result that is unclear or incorrect. This is why the gestational age at which you test matters considerably.

## What Is Available at 7 Weeks {#section-2}

The two main types of blood test used for sex prediction in the UK are:

**Early sex prediction tests (such as SneakPeek)**
These are consumer-facing tests marketed for use from around 7–8 weeks. They work by the same cffDNA principle as NIPT and are available privately. They are considerably cheaper than NIPT and can be done at home or through a private clinic. The companies behind these tests publish their own accuracy figures, but independent assessments of early tests suggest that results taken at the very earliest point carry a higher chance of error than those taken slightly later.

**Non-invasive prenatal testing (NIPT)**
NIPT is a more comprehensive blood test that screens for chromosomal conditions as well as reporting fetal sex. It requires a minimum level of fetal DNA in the maternal blood to return a reliable result, which is why most providers in the UK will not offer it before 9–10 weeks. Some require 10 weeks as a minimum. NIPT is considered more reliable for sex prediction than early consumer tests because it is performed at a gestational age when fetal DNA levels are more consistently sufficient.

## Comparing Your Options {#section-3}

| | Early sex test (e.g. SneakPeek) | NIPT |
|---|---|---|
| Earliest available | ~7 weeks | ~9–10 weeks |
| What it tests | Fetal sex only | Sex + chromosomal conditions |
| Availability | Private only | Private only in UK |
| Reliability at earliest point | Lower due to low fetal DNA | Not offered this early |
| Cost | Lower | Higher |

Neither of these tests is available on the NHS for the purpose of finding out fetal sex. The NHS offers NIPT (as the Harmony or similar test) in certain clinical situations — for example, to screen for Down's syndrome — but sex is reported as part of that, not as a standalone purpose.

## Which Should You Choose? {#section-4}

If you are seven weeks pregnant and determined to find out as soon as possible, an early sex prediction test is the only blood test currently marketed for this stage. However, there are good reasons to consider waiting a few weeks:

- **Accuracy improves with gestational age.** The further along you are, the more fetal DNA is present and the more reliable the result.
- **A failed or inconclusive result at 7 weeks** is not uncommon, and you may need to retest anyway — meaning the cost saving of testing early can disappear.
- **NIPT at 10 weeks** provides sex prediction alongside meaningful clinical information about chromosomal health, making it a more comprehensive option for the same blood draw.
- **Nub theory at 12 weeks** is a non-blood-test alternative where a trained sonographer or specialist can estimate sex from scan images, and accuracy is generally good at this stage.

There is no clinical urgency to finding out fetal sex, so if accuracy matters more to you than speed, waiting is usually the more sensible choice.

## FAQ {#faq}

### Can I find out my baby's sex at 7 weeks?

Early sex prediction blood tests are marketed for use from around seven weeks. These work by detecting Y-chromosome DNA in your blood. However, because fetal DNA levels are still low at this stage, the chance of an unclear or incorrect result is higher than at later gestational ages. NIPT — which is generally more reliable — is not offered before around 9–10 weeks. If getting an accurate result is the priority, waiting until 10 weeks for NIPT or 12 weeks for a scan-based assessment is usually advisable.

### Is an early sex prediction blood test accurate at 7 weeks?

Companies producing these tests publish their own accuracy claims, but accuracy at the very earliest testing window is lower than at 8–10 weeks because fetal DNA concentrations in maternal blood are still building. A no-result or a result that needs confirming is a real possibility. If you do test at 7 weeks and receive a result, it may be worth confirming it at your 12-week scan.

### What is the difference between an early sex test and NIPT?

An early sex prediction test looks only for the presence or absence of Y-chromosome DNA to predict fetal sex. NIPT is a more comprehensive test that analyses a broader range of fetal DNA and screens for chromosomal conditions such as Down's syndrome, Edwards' syndrome, and Patau's syndrome, while also reporting fetal sex. NIPT requires 9–10 weeks minimum and costs more, but provides considerably more clinical information.

Speak to your midwife or GP if you have questions about what testing is right for your circumstances.
