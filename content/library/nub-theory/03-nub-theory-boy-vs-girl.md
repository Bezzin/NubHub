# Nub Theory Boy vs Girl: How to Tell the Difference

The most common question we get at Nub Hub is: "Is my baby's nub a boy or a girl?" Understanding the difference between a boy nub and a girl nub is the core of nub theory, and once you know what to look for, it becomes much easier to read your own 12-week scan.

## The Boy Nub {#boy-nub}

A **boy nub** (indicating a male fetus) typically shows the following characteristics on a sagittal (side-profile) ultrasound:

### Angle
- The nub **angles upward at 30 degrees or more** from the horizontal axis of the spine
- It appears to be "pointing to the sky" or reaching toward the baby's head
- The angle is clearly defined — not flat, not slightly raised, but noticeably angled up

### Appearance
- The nub may look **elongated** or stick-like
- The tip may appear **forked** (Y-shaped) — this is sometimes called a "boy fork"
- The nub may appear **stacked** — with white layered lines visible at the tip
- It looks like a small bump or peg pointing upward from between the baby's legs

### Key Features
- **30+ degree upward angle** from the spine
- **Stacked or forked** appearance at the tip
- The nub is typically **longer** than a girl nub at this stage
- May have a visible **caudal notch** (small dip) between the nub and the bottom of the spine

## The Girl Nub {#girl-nub}

A **girl nub** (indicating a female fetus) typically shows:

### Angle
- The nub lies **flat or parallel to the spine**
- It may **point slightly downward**
- The angle is less than 10 degrees from horizontal — essentially flat

### Appearance
- The nub appears **short and flat** — like a small line rather than a bump
- May show **three short parallel lines** (sometimes called the "hamburger" sign, though this is more common at 16+ weeks)
- No upward angle — it sits flush against the body
- Sometimes described as looking like "a little tail" lying flat

### Key Features
- **Parallel to the spine** or pointing down
- **Three short lines** may be visible (not stacked, just flat lines)
- The nub is typically **shorter** than a boy nub
- No forked or stacked appearance
- The nub blends into the body contour

## Side-by-Side Comparison {#comparison}

| Feature | Boy Nub | Girl Nub |
|---------|---------|----------|
| **Angle from spine** | 30+ degrees upward | 0–10 degrees (parallel) |
| **Direction** | Points up toward head | Points flat or down |
| **Length** | Longer, more pronounced | Shorter, flatter |
| **Tip appearance** | Forked or stacked | Three flat lines |
| **Overall look** | "Pointing to the sky" | "Lying flat" |
| **Caudal notch** | Often visible | Not typically present |
| **Confidence at 12w** | High (obvious angle) | Good (flat profile) |

## The "Stacked" and "Forked" Nub {#stacked-forked}

These are two terms you'll see frequently in nub theory discussions:

### Stacked Nub
A **stacked nub** has a layered appearance at the tip — it looks like there are white lines stacked on top of each other. This is commonly associated with a **boy prediction**. The stacking represents the developing penis and scrotum beginning to form.

### Forked Nub
A **forked nub** has a Y-shaped or split appearance at the tip, like the tines of a fork. This is also typically associated with a **boy prediction**. The fork represents the beginning of the foreskin and glans developing.

### Girl "Lines"
A girl nub may show **three short parallel lines** instead of a stacked or forked appearance. These represent the developing labia. However, this sign is more reliable at 16+ weeks and should be treated cautiously at 12 weeks.

## Ambiguous Nubs {#ambiguous}

Not all nubs are clearly boy or girl. Some nubs fall into an **ambiguous zone**:

- **Angle between 10–30 degrees**: This is the hardest to read. The nub is slightly raised but not enough to confidently call boy, and not flat enough to confidently call girl.
- **11-week scans**: At 11 weeks, most nubs are somewhat ambiguous because development isn't far enough along.
- **Poor image quality**: If the image is blurry or the baby is in a bad position, even an experienced reader can't make a confident prediction.

### What to Do with an Ambiguous Nub
- **Wait**: If you're at 11 weeks, wait for a scan at 12+ weeks
- **Get a second opinion**: Post your scan in a nub theory Facebook group or on our community for multiple opinions
- **Consider NIPT**: If you really want to know early and the nub is ambiguous, NIPT is 99%+ accurate from 10 weeks
- **Accept the uncertainty**: Sometimes you just can't tell, and that's OK — the 20-week scan will confirm

## Common Mistakes When Reading Nubs {#mistakes}

1. **Confusing the umbilical cord with the nub**: The umbilical cord can sometimes look like a nub, especially if it's positioned between the legs. The cord is typically thicker and rounder than the nub.

2. **Reading the wrong part of the body**: The nub is specifically the genital tubercle — make sure you're looking at the right area (between the bottom and the legs).

3. **Ignoring the angle**: Some people focus on the "stacked" or "forked" appearance and forget that the **angle is the most important factor**. Always check the angle first.

4. **Reading too early**: Trying to apply nub theory at 10–11 weeks often leads to incorrect predictions because the nub hasn't developed enough.

5. **Using a non-profile image**: Nub theory only works on sagittal (side-profile) views. A face-on view or bottom view won't show the angle correctly.

6. **Confusing leg position with the nub**: Sometimes the baby's leg or foot can look like a nub in certain images. Make sure you can identify the spine, bottom, and legs before assessing the nub.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Can a boy nub be flat?

In very rare cases, a boy's nub may not have fully angled up at 12 weeks, making it look flat like a girl nub. This is more common at 11 weeks. By 12+5 weeks, a boy nub should show a clear upward angle. If your scan is at 12+ weeks and the nub is flat, it's most likely a girl — but there's always a small chance of error.

### Can a girl nub point up?

A girl's nub typically doesn't angle upward significantly. If the nub points up at 30+ degrees, it's very likely a boy. However, at 11 weeks, a girl's nub may still be slightly raised as development isn't complete. By 12+ weeks, a girl nub should be flat or parallel to the spine.

### What does "stacked" mean in nub theory?

"Stacked" refers to a layered, white-line appearance at the tip of the nub. It's commonly associated with boys and represents the developing penis and scrotum. A stacked appearance combined with a 30+ degree upward angle is a strong boy indicator.

### Is the fork always a boy sign?

In most cases, yes — a forked (Y-shaped) nub tip is associated with boys. However, image quality and baby position can sometimes create fork-like appearances that aren't truly the nub. Always verify the angle before making a prediction based on the fork alone.

### What if the nub is at 15 degrees?

A nub at 15 degrees falls in the ambiguous zone (10–30 degrees). At this angle, it could be either sex. If you're at 11 weeks, wait and recheck at 12+ weeks. If you're already at 12+ weeks, this is genuinely uncertain — consider it a 50/50 guess.
