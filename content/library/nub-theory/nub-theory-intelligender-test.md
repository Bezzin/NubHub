# Intelligender Gender Test: Does It Work?

If you have ever gone searching for early gender prediction options, you may have come across Intelligender — a home urine test that claimed to reveal your baby's sex as early as 10 weeks of pregnancy. It promised a colour-change result from a small urine sample. But does the science actually hold up? Here is what you need to know.

## What Was Intelligender?

Intelligender was a home-use gender prediction test sold in a small kit containing a urine cup and a chemical reagent. You were instructed to collect an early morning urine sample and mix it with the reagent. The colour the mixture turned — typically somewhere on a scale from orange to dark green — was supposed to indicate girl or boy respectively.

The test was marketed as working from 10 weeks of pregnancy, before any NHS or private scan would typically offer gender information. It was sold in some UK pharmacies and online retailers, though availability became increasingly patchy and it has largely disappeared from the UK market.

## The Science Behind the Claim

The manufacturers suggested the test worked by detecting different hormonal or chemical markers in urine that differed between pregnancies carrying a boy versus a girl. However, the biological mechanism was never clearly explained or independently verified.

Independent researchers who tested Intelligender found that the colour result was significantly affected by variables that had nothing to do with the baby's sex — including how concentrated the urine was, what the mother had eaten or drunk, certain medications, the ambient temperature, and even how the sample was handled. These confounding factors made any result extremely difficult to interpret consistently.

## What the Evidence Says

The scientific consensus is clear: Intelligender was not a reliable predictor of fetal sex. Independent studies found accuracy rates hovering around 50%, which is exactly what you would expect from random chance — essentially a coin flip. There is no peer-reviewed evidence supporting Intelligender's claims of accuracy beyond chance level.

For this reason, the test should be considered entertainment at best and misleading at worst. Making decisions or forming strong expectations based on an Intelligender result is not advisable.

## What Are the Alternatives?

If you want a more reliable indication of your baby's sex before the 20-week anomaly scan, there are several options with meaningful evidence behind them:

**NIPT (Non-Invasive Prenatal Testing):** A blood test that analyses cell-free fetal DNA circulating in the mother's blood. When sex determination is included, it is highly accurate (generally cited as over 99% for sex chromosome detection) from around 10 weeks. In the UK this is primarily available privately, though it is offered on the NHS in certain clinical circumstances.

**Nub theory at 12+ weeks:** An experienced nub theory reader examining a good quality profile scan image at 12 weeks or beyond can achieve accuracy in the region of 80 to 90%. It is not a medical test and carries no guarantee, but it is based on genuine anatomical differences in how the genital tubercle develops.

**The 20-week anomaly scan:** The most definitive non-invasive method before birth. Accuracy when sex is determined at this stage is generally very high, though it depends on the baby's position and the sonographer's ability to get a clear view.

**Private gender scans:** Many private clinics offer dedicated gender scans from around 16 weeks using ultrasound, which is far more reliable than any home urine test.

## Is Intelligender Still Available?

The test has largely been discontinued or withdrawn from mainstream retail in the UK and many other markets. If you encounter it being sold second-hand or through an unverified online marketplace, there is no quality control on old stock, and any result would be even less reliable than when the test was current.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Did Intelligender work?

No. Intelligender was never scientifically validated as a reliable predictor of fetal sex. Independent testing found accuracy rates of around 50%, which is the same as guessing. The colour result was heavily influenced by factors unrelated to the baby's sex, including urine concentration, diet, and temperature. The test is no longer widely available in the UK.

### What is better than Intelligender for early gender prediction?

Several options are considerably more reliable. NIPT blood testing, available privately from around 10 weeks, analyses fetal DNA directly and is highly accurate for sex determination. Nub theory at 12 weeks or later, read by an experienced reader from a clear profile scan image, offers a meaningful accuracy advantage over guessing. The 20-week anomaly scan remains the most accessible and reliable method for most people in the UK.

### Can I trust any home gender prediction test?

There is no scientifically validated home urine or saliva test for fetal sex currently on the market. Any product making such a claim should be treated with scepticism. If early gender information is important to you, speak to your midwife or GP about your options, which may include NIPT or a private scan.
