# Nosebleeds in Pregnancy

Nosebleeds are more common in pregnancy than most people expect. The increased blood volume and hormonal changes make the tiny blood vessels in the nose more fragile and prone to bleeding. Most are harmless, but knowing how to manage them — and when to seek help — matters.

## Why Nosebleeds Happen in Pregnancy {#why-nosebleeds-happen-in-pregnancy}

Pregnancy raises your blood volume substantially — by the end of the first trimester it is already increasing, and by the third trimester your body is circulating significantly more blood than usual. This higher volume puts extra pressure on delicate nasal blood vessels. At the same time, the hormones oestrogen and progesterone cause the mucous membranes lining the nose to swell and become more sensitive, a condition sometimes called pregnancy rhinitis.

Together, these two factors — higher blood pressure in the nasal vessels and more fragile, swollen lining — mean that even minor irritation such as blowing your nose, dry air, or a slight knock can trigger a bleed. This is entirely normal and does not indicate anything is wrong with your pregnancy.

Nosebleeds tend to be more frequent in:

- The second and third trimesters, as blood volume peaks
- Dry environments or during winter months when central heating dries out the air
- Women who already had a tendency towards nosebleeds before pregnancy
- Women with hayfever or nasal congestion, who may blow their nose more frequently

## How to Stop a Nosebleed During Pregnancy {#how-to-stop}

The technique for stopping a nosebleed is the same in pregnancy as at any other time:

1. **Sit upright and lean slightly forward** — this prevents blood running down the back of your throat, which can cause nausea
2. **Pinch the soft part of your nose** — use your thumb and index finger to press the soft lower part of the nose (below the bony bridge) firmly together
3. **Breathe through your mouth** and hold the pressure for a full ten minutes without checking; releasing too soon can dislodge the clot forming
4. **Do not tilt your head back** — this is a common mistake that can cause you to swallow blood
5. After ten minutes, release gently. If the bleed continues, repeat for another ten minutes.

Once the bleed has stopped, avoid blowing your nose for several hours if possible, and try not to pick at any crusting inside the nose.

## How to Prevent Nosebleeds in Pregnancy {#prevention}

You can reduce how often nosebleeds occur with a few practical steps:

- **Keep the nasal lining moist** — use a saline nasal spray or rinse daily. These are safe in pregnancy and help prevent the dryness that makes vessels more fragile
- **Use a humidifier** in your bedroom, particularly in winter when indoor air is dry
- **Blow gently** — avoid forceful nose-blowing and try to use soft tissues
- **Stay hydrated** — drinking enough water helps keep mucous membranes healthy
- **Avoid picking your nose** — it sounds obvious, but dry or crusty noses often trigger the habit
- **Vaseline inside the nostrils** — a tiny amount applied with a cotton bud can help protect particularly dry nasal skin overnight

## When to Seek Help for a Nosebleed in Pregnancy {#when-to-seek-help}

Most nosebleeds in pregnancy are benign and stop within ten minutes. However, there are times when you should contact a healthcare professional:

- The nosebleed **lasts more than 20–30 minutes** despite sustained pressure
- The bleed is **very heavy** and you are losing a significant amount of blood
- You are **swallowing a lot of blood**, which can cause vomiting
- You have a **known bleeding disorder** or are taking blood-thinning medication
- The nosebleed is accompanied by **severe headache, visual disturbances, or swelling** of the hands and face — these can be signs of high blood pressure in pregnancy (pre-eclampsia) and should be assessed promptly

For a nosebleed alongside a severe headache or visual changes, contact your midwife or maternity unit the same day. If the bleed is extremely heavy and cannot be stopped, attend A&E or call 999.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Are nosebleeds in pregnancy dangerous?

In the vast majority of cases, no. Nosebleeds during pregnancy are a common and benign side effect of increased blood volume and hormonal changes to the nasal lining. They are not a sign that something is wrong with your baby. Frequent or heavy nosebleeds are worth mentioning to your midwife or GP so that any other causes can be ruled out, but isolated nosebleeds are rarely a concern.

### When should I seek medical help for a pregnancy nosebleed?

Contact your GP or midwife if a nosebleed lasts more than 20 minutes despite sustained pressure, if it is very heavy, or if you are experiencing them very frequently. Seek urgent help if the nosebleed is accompanied by a severe headache, vision changes, or facial swelling, as these symptoms together can indicate blood pressure problems that need prompt assessment.

### Can nosebleeds mean something is wrong with the baby?

On their own, nosebleeds are not linked to problems with the baby. They are caused by changes in your body, not your baby's. However, if nosebleeds occur alongside other symptoms such as severe headache, swelling in the face or hands, or changes to your vision, these should be reported to your midwife promptly as they can be signs of elevated blood pressure, which does require monitoring in pregnancy.
