# What Is Nub Theory? The Complete Guide to Predicting Your Baby's Sex at 12 Weeks

Nub theory is a method of predicting your baby's sex by examining the angle of the genital tubercle — known as the "nub" — on your 12-week ultrasound scan. It's one of the earliest ways to guess whether you're having a boy or a girl, and it's become hugely popular among expectant mums across the UK and beyond.

If you've just had your 12-week scan and you're staring at the photo wondering whether that little bump means boy or girl, you're in exactly the right place. This guide explains everything you need to know about nub theory — how it works, how accurate it is, and how to read your own scan.

## What Is Nub Theory? {#what-is-nub-theory}

Nub theory is based on the fact that all babies, regardless of their genetic sex, develop a small protuberance between their legs called the **genital tubercle** (or "nub") during early pregnancy. This nub eventually develops into either the penis (in boys) or the clitoris (in girls).

The key insight of nub theory is that **at around 11–13 weeks of pregnancy, the angle of this nub relative to the baby's spine can indicate whether the baby is male or female**:

- **Boy nub**: The nub angles upward by **30 degrees or more** relative to the spine
- **Girl nub**: The nub is **parallel to the spine** or angles less than 10 degrees, or points downward

This angle difference is visible on a profile (side-view) ultrasound image at the 12-week scan. The theory was first described in medical literature in the early 2000s, and has since become one of the most discussed early gender prediction methods among expectant parents.

## How Does Nub Theory Work? {#how-it-works}

Nub theory works by examining the **sagittal plane** (side profile) of the baby on an ultrasound image. Here's what you're looking for:

### The Nub Angle

The "nub" is the genital tubercle — a small bump that sits between the baby's legs. At 11–13 weeks, all babies have this nub. What matters is the **angle of the nub relative to the horizontal axis of the baby's spine**:

- If the nub **points up at 30 degrees or more** from the spine → likely a **boy**
- If the nub **lies flat (parallel to the spine)** or points downward → likely a **girl**
- If the nub is at a **10–15 degree angle** → it's ambiguous and harder to call

### Why the Angle Matters

In male fetuses, the genital tubercle begins to develop into the penis and starts to angle upward as it elongates. In female fetuses, the tubercle develops into the clitoris and remains relatively flat. This developmental difference is what creates the angle you can see on the scan.

The angle becomes most visible at around **12 weeks of gestation**, which is why the 12-week scan (also known as the dating scan or NT scan) is the ideal time for nub theory analysis.

### What You Need for Nub Theory

To apply nub theory, you need:

1. **A clear profile (sagittal) ultrasound image** — the baby should be lying on its side
2. **The baby measured at 11+0 to 13+6 weeks** — before 11 weeks it's too early, after 14 weeks the nub has already developed further
3. **A good-quality image** — the nub must be visible between the legs
4. **The baby in the right position** — if the legs are crossed or the baby is facing forward, the nub may not be visible

## Nub Theory: Boy vs Girl {#boy-vs-girl}

### Boy Nub

A boy nub typically shows:

- The nub **angling upward at 30 degrees or more** from the spine
- The nub may appear **stacked or forked** at the tip
- The nub looks like it's "pointing to the sky"
- In some cases, you can see a **caudal notch** (a small dip) between the nub and the bottom of the spine

### Girl Nub

A girl nub typically shows:

- The nub lying **flat or parallel to the spine**
- The nub may **point slightly downward**
- The nub appears shorter and less prominent
- Sometimes described as having **three short lines** (the "hamburger" sign) when viewed from below, though this is more relevant at 16+ weeks

### Ambiguous Nub

Sometimes the nub is at an angle between 10 and 30 degrees, making it difficult to determine. This is common at 11 weeks when development is still in progress. In these cases, it's best to wait until 12+ weeks for a clearer reading.

## How Accurate Is Nub Theory? {#accuracy}

Nub theory accuracy depends heavily on **gestational age**:

| Gestational Age | Accuracy Rate |
|-----------------|--------------|
| 11 weeks | ~70% |
| 12 weeks | ~80–85% |
| 12.5 weeks | ~90% |
| 13 weeks | ~92–95% |

The accuracy increases with gestational age because the genital tubercle has had more time to develop into either a penis or clitoris, making the angle more pronounced.

### Studies on Nub Theory

Several studies have examined nub theory accuracy:

- **Manzotti et al. (2012)**: Found nub theory to be approximately 85% accurate at 11–13 weeks
- **Hohmann-Woods et al. (2013)**: Reported accuracy rates of around 70% at 11 weeks and over 90% at 13 weeks
- Various smaller studies have confirmed that accuracy improves significantly as the pregnancy progresses past 12 weeks

It's important to note that **nub theory is not 100% accurate**. It's an educated guess based on developmental patterns. For definitive sex determination, you would need either:
- **NIPT blood test** (can detect fetal DNA from ~10 weeks, 99% accurate for sex)
- **A 20-week anatomy scan** where the genitalia are clearly visible
- **Amniocentesis or CVS** (invasive but 100% definitive)

## When Is the Best Time for Nub Theory? {#best-time}

The **optimal time** for nub theory analysis is between **12 weeks 0 days and 13 weeks 6 days**. This is because:

- At 11 weeks, the nub is still developing and the angle may be ambiguous
- At 12–13 weeks, the angle is most distinct
- After 14 weeks, the nub has already developed into genitalia and is less about "angle" and more about visible anatomy

If you're having your NHS dating scan (typically done between 11+2 and 14+1 weeks), this is the perfect opportunity to try nub theory — as long as the baby cooperates with a good side-profile view!

## How to Read Your Own Scan {#read-your-scan}

If you want to try nub theory on your own scan photo, here's what to do:

1. **Find the profile view**: You need a side-on (sagittal) view of the baby. The baby should be lying on its side with the spine visible along the bottom or top of the image.

2. **Locate the spine**: The spine appears as a bright white line running along the baby's back.

3. **Find the nub**: Look between the baby's bottom and the legs. The nub is a small bump or line protruding from the genital area.

4. **Check the angle**: Draw an imaginary line along the spine. Now look at the angle of the nub:
   - **30+ degrees upward = boy**
   - **Parallel/flat = girl**
   - **Pointing down = girl**

5. **Look for a fork or stack**: A boy nub may show a "forked" or "stacked" appearance at the tip. A girl nub may appear as three short parallel lines.

### Tips for a Good Nub Theory Photo

- Ask the sonographer for a **profile view** (they won't specifically look for the nub on the NHS, but a standard profile image works)
- Make sure the image isn't **too zoomed in or too zoomed out**
- The baby should be **lying on its side**, not face-up or face-down
- **Don't rely on a single photo** — if you have video, you can pause it at the right moment

## Nub Theory Limitations {#limitations}

Nub theory is popular and fun, but it has significant limitations:

1. **It's not 100% accurate** — even at 13 weeks, it's around 92–95%, not 100%
2. **Baby position matters** — if the baby isn't in a good side-profile position, the nub can't be assessed
3. **Image quality matters** — blurry or poorly angled images make it impossible
4. **The gestational age must be right** — too early and the nub hasn't developed enough
5. **Some scans just don't show the nub** — legs may be in the way, or the angle may be ambiguous
6. **Sonographers don't look for it** — NHS sonographers are measuring the baby, not checking the nub angle, so they won't confirm gender
7. **It's a prediction, not a diagnosis** — for confirmation, you need NIPT or the 20-week scan

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Can the NHS sonographer tell me the gender at 12 weeks?

NHS sonographers do not assess the nub or predict gender at the 12-week scan. Their job is to measure the baby, check the heartbeat, and perform the nuchal translucency screening. Some may offer a casual guess if the nub is very clearly visible, but they won't officially confirm sex. Gender is typically confirmed at the 20-week anatomy scan.

### Is nub theory better than Ramzi theory?

Nub theory is generally considered more reliable than Ramzi theory. Ramzi theory (which uses the position of the placenta to predict gender at 6–8 weeks) has not been validated in large studies and has accuracy rates close to chance (~50%). Nub theory, at the right gestational age, has been shown to be 80–95% accurate in published studies.

### Can nub theory be wrong?

Yes. Even at 13 weeks, nub theory can be wrong approximately 5–8% of the time. Common reasons include: the baby being in a poor position, image quality issues, ambiguous nub angles (10–30 degrees), and natural variation in development rates. Nub theory should never be treated as definitive.

### Can I use nub theory at 11 weeks?

You can try, but accuracy at 11 weeks is only around 70%. At 11 weeks, the genital tubercle is still developing and the angle may not be distinct yet. If you can wait until 12+ weeks, your prediction will be significantly more reliable.

### What if I can't see the nub on my scan photo?

If the nub isn't visible on your scan photo, it could be because the baby was in the wrong position, the image wasn't captured at the right angle, or the photo you received was zoomed in on the face rather than the full body. You can try requesting a profile view at your next scan, or book a private scan specifically for gender prediction.

### Does nub theory work for twins?

Nub theory can work for twins if each baby is clearly visible in a profile view. However, it can be more difficult to get clear images of both babies in the right position, and you'll need to identify which nub belongs to which baby. Accuracy per twin is similar to single pregnancies (80–95% at 12+ weeks).

### What's the difference between a "forked" and "stacked" nub?

A "forked" nub has a Y-shaped appearance at the tip, which some nub theory enthusiasts associate with boys. A "stacked" nub has a layered appearance (sometimes described as "white stacked lines"), which is also often associated with boys. Girl nubs tend to show three short parallel lines without the stacked or forked appearance. However, these visual cues are less reliable than the primary angle measurement.
