## What Makes a Boy Nub a Boy Nub? {#what-makes-a-boy}

A boy nub is identified primarily by **angle**. The genital tubercle — the small protuberance between the baby's legs that will become the penis in a male or the clitoris in a female — is visible on a side-profile (sagittal) ultrasound at 11 to 13 weeks. In a male fetus, the tubercle begins to elongate and angle upward as it develops toward the penis. This creates the characteristic **30-degree or greater upward angle** relative to the horizontal axis of the baby's spine.

The secondary visual cues that nub theory readers look for in a boy nub include:

- **Stacked appearance**: The tip of the nub may appear as layered white lines, sometimes described as a "stacked" look.
- **Forked tip**: The nub may show a Y-shaped or forked appearance at the tip, which some associate with male development.
- **Caudal notch**: A small dip or notch visible between the nub and the base of the spine.
- **Length**: Boy nubs often appear slightly longer and more prominent than girl nubs at the same gestational age.

It is important to remember that **angle is the primary indicator**. The stacked, forked, and caudal notch features are secondary and less reliable on their own. A nub that points up at 30 degrees or more is the strongest visual signal of a boy.

Do not rely on the stack alone — confirm the angle is 30 degrees or more before making a boy call. In this example, both the angle and the stack point to boy.

**What to look for**: The fork should be at the tip of the nub, not at the base. It can be subtle and may require zooming in on the image. Some sonographers and experienced readers say the fork is caused by the early development of the glans of the penis. The angle in this example is clearly above 30 degrees, supporting the boy prediction.

**What to look for**: The caudal notch is not always visible and not all boy nubs have one. It is a secondary feature. The primary indicator remains the angle. If the nub is at 30+ degrees and there is a caudal notch, this strengthens the boy prediction.

## Common Mistakes When Reading Boy Nubs {#mistakes}

Even with good examples to compare against, it is easy to misread a nub. Here are the most common mistakes:

1. **Confusing the umbilical cord with the nub**: The umbilical cord can sometimes appear as a bright line between the legs and may be mistaken for the nub. The cord is usually thicker and curves differently. If the line you are looking at connects to the baby's abdomen, it is likely the cord, not the nub.

2. **Misjudging the angle**: Estimating 30 degrees by eye is harder than it sounds. A nub at 20 degrees can look like 30 degrees if the image is slightly rotated. Always try to align the spine horizontally before judging the angle.

3. **Reading a non-profile image**: Nub theory only works on a sagittal (side-profile) view. If the baby is face-on or bottom-on, the nub cannot be assessed using the angle method.

4. **Trusting the stack or fork over the angle**: The stacked and forked appearances are secondary indicators. A girl nub can sometimes show a stacked appearance. Always prioritise the angle.

5. **Ignoring gestational age**: A 30-degree angle at 11 weeks is less reliable than at 12+ weeks. Always factor in the gestational age when making a prediction.

6. **Overlooking image quality**: A blurry or zoomed-out image makes angle assessment unreliable. If you cannot clearly see the nub and the spine, the prediction is not reliable.

## How to Practise on Your Own Scan

If you want to compare your own scan against these examples:

1. Find your clearest profile (sagittal) image from your 12-week scan.
2. Orient the image so the spine runs horizontally.
3. Locate the nub between the baby's bottom and legs.
4. Estimate the angle of the nub relative to the spine.
5. Look for secondary features: stack, fork, caudal notch.
6. Compare your findings to the examples above.

Remember that even experienced readers can be wrong. Nub theory is a prediction, not a diagnosis. For confirmation, you will need NIPT or the 20-week anatomy scan.


## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can a boy nub ever point downward?

It is very unusual for a boy nub to point downward at 12+ weeks. If the nub clearly points downward or is parallel to the spine, it is more likely a girl. However, image quality and baby position can sometimes distort the appearance. If your scan shows a downward nub but you suspect it may be a boy, wait for NIPT or the 20-week scan for confirmation.

### What angle is definitely a boy?

There is no angle that is "definitely" a boy, but an angle of 30 degrees or more upward relative to the spine is the classic boy indicator. At 12+ weeks, this angle has roughly an 85-95% chance of being correct. Angles above 30 degrees at 13 weeks are even more reliable.

### Do all boy nubs have a fork or stack?

No. The forked and stacked appearances are secondary features and are not present in every boy nub. Some boy nubs are simply an upward-angled line with no visible fork or stack. The angle is the primary indicator.

### Can the umbilical cord look like a boy nub?

Yes. The umbilical cord can sometimes appear as a bright line between the legs and may be mistaken for a nub. If the line seems to connect to the baby's abdomen rather than the genital area, it is likely the cord. Experienced readers distinguish the nub from the cord by location and appearance.

### How accurate is nub theory for boys specifically?

Studies suggest nub theory is slightly more accurate for boys than for girls at 12+ weeks, with some studies reporting boy accuracy at 90-95% and girl accuracy at 85-90%. This is because the upward angle of a boy nub tends to be more visually distinct than the flat angle of a girl nub.
