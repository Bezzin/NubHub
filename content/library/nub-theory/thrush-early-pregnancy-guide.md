# Thrush in Early Pregnancy Guide

Thrush is a common yeast infection that often appears in early pregnancy. Hormonal changes make the vaginal environment friendlier to yeast, leading to itching, soreness, and discharge. It is usually straightforward to treat safely in pregnancy with the right guidance — and it does not harm your baby.

## What Causes Thrush in Pregnancy? {#what-causes-thrush-in-pregnancy}

Thrush is caused by an overgrowth of Candida, a yeast that naturally lives in the vagina in small amounts. In pregnancy, rising oestrogen levels increase glycogen (a type of sugar) in vaginal cells, which provides more food for yeast to feed on. This creates conditions where Candida can multiply and cause symptoms.

Several factors can make thrush more likely in pregnancy:

- **Hormonal changes.** Higher oestrogen throughout pregnancy is the primary driver.
- **Antibiotics.** If you need antibiotic treatment during pregnancy, this can disrupt the natural balance of bacteria and allow yeast to overgrow.
- **A weakened immune response.** Pregnancy naturally suppresses certain immune functions to protect the baby, which can make infections including thrush more likely.
- **Tight or synthetic clothing.** Clothing that traps heat and moisture creates an environment where yeast thrives.
- **Scented products.** Perfumed soaps, bubble baths, or wipes near the vulva can disrupt the natural pH balance.

Thrush is not a sexually transmitted infection. It can, however, sometimes be passed between partners, so if your partner develops symptoms, they should seek treatment too.

## Symptoms of Thrush in Pregnancy {#symptoms}

Common signs of thrush include:

- **Itching or soreness** around the vagina and vulva — often the most noticeable symptom.
- **White discharge** that looks thick and cottage-cheese-like, with little or no smell. This is different from the thin, milky leucorrhoea that is normal in pregnancy.
- **Stinging or burning** when urinating or during sex.
- **Redness or swelling** of the vulva.

Not all of these symptoms appear at once. Some women notice only mild itching, while others have more pronounced discomfort.

## Safe Treatment Options in Pregnancy {#symptoms-and-safe-treatment}

Treatment in pregnancy usually involves **topical antifungal creams or pessaries** inserted into the vagina. These are applied locally and are considered safe in pregnancy by most UK guidelines. Common antifungal medicines used include clotrimazole, which is available over the counter.

**Oral antifungal tablets** (such as fluconazole) are generally avoided during pregnancy, particularly in the first trimester, due to uncertainty about safety. Always check with your pharmacist, midwife, or GP before using any treatment — including over-the-counter products — to ensure what you are using is appropriate for your stage of pregnancy.

Your midwife or GP can confirm the diagnosis and recommend a safe option for you. It is worth getting a proper diagnosis, particularly if this is your first episode or you are unsure, as other infections such as bacterial vaginosis can cause similar symptoms but require different treatment.

## Preventing Thrush in Pregnancy {#prevention}

You cannot always prevent thrush in pregnancy, but these steps can reduce the likelihood:

- Wear loose, breathable cotton underwear.
- Avoid tight-fitting trousers or leggings for long periods.
- Wash the external area with plain water rather than soap, and avoid perfumed products.
- Change out of wet swimwear or gym clothes promptly.
- After using the toilet, always wipe from front to back.
- Take probiotics if advised by your healthcare provider — though evidence is mixed, some find them helpful.

If you are prescribed antibiotics during pregnancy, mention your history of thrush to your GP so they can consider whether a simultaneous antifungal would be appropriate.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Can thrush harm my baby during pregnancy?

Thrush does not usually harm your baby during pregnancy. The yeast does not cross the placenta. However, if thrush is present at the time of delivery, the baby may pick it up passing through the birth canal and develop oral thrush (white patches in the mouth) after birth. Oral thrush in newborns is very common and is treatable with antifungal drops.

### Can I use over-the-counter thrush treatments when pregnant?

Some over-the-counter topical treatments are considered safe in pregnancy, but you should always check with a pharmacist or your midwife before using them, as advice can vary depending on your stage of pregnancy and the specific product. Oral fluconazole tablets are generally avoided during pregnancy. Do not rely solely on the packaging — seek a professional recommendation.

### How can I prevent thrush recurring during pregnancy?

Wear breathable cotton underwear, avoid tight clothing, wash with plain water rather than perfumed products, and keep the genital area dry and well-ventilated. If thrush keeps recurring, speak to your GP — they may want to confirm the diagnosis and rule out other causes such as bacterial vaginosis, which requires different treatment.
