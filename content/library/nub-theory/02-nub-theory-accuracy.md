# How Accurate Is Nub Theory? Statistics, Studies & Success Rates

Nub theory is one of the most popular methods for predicting your baby's sex at the 12-week scan, but many parents want to know: just how accurate is it? The short answer is that nub theory is approximately **80–95% accurate** when performed at the right gestational age — but the accuracy depends heavily on how many weeks pregnant you are and the quality of your scan image.

## Nub Theory Accuracy by Week {#accuracy-by-week}

The accuracy of nub theory changes dramatically depending on how many weeks pregnant you are:

| Gestational Age | Accuracy Rate | Confidence Level |
|-----------------|--------------|-----------------|
| 10 weeks | ~50–60% | Very low — too early |
| 11 weeks | ~70% | Moderate |
| 11 weeks 3 days | ~75% | Moderate |
| 12 weeks | ~80–85% | Good |
| 12 weeks 3 days | ~88% | Good |
| 12 weeks 6 days | ~91% | High |
| 13 weeks | ~92–95% | High |
| 13 weeks 6 days | ~95% | Very high |

The key takeaway: **every day matters**. A scan at 11+2 is significantly less reliable than one at 12+5. If your dating scan is scheduled for 11 weeks, you may want to consider a private scan at 12+ weeks for a more reliable nub theory reading.

## The Science Behind the Accuracy {#the-science}

Nub theory works because of how the genital tubercle develops in male and female fetuses:

### Male Development
- The genital tubercle begins to elongate and angle upward as it develops into the penis
- This angle becomes visible at around 11 weeks but is most distinct at 12–13 weeks
- By 14 weeks, the penis is typically fully formed and visible on ultrasound

### Female Development
- The genital tubercle remains relatively flat and develops into the clitoris
- The nub stays parallel to the spine or points slightly downward
- By 14 weeks, the labia and clitoris are formed but less obvious on ultrasound

The accuracy of nub theory depends on **how far along this development has progressed**. At 11 weeks, both boys and girls may have similar-looking nubs. By 12–13 weeks, the developmental divergence is clearer.

## Studies on Nub Theory Accuracy {#studies}

### Manzotti et al. (2012)

A key study published in the journal *Ultrasound in Obstetrics & Gynecology* examined first-trimester sex assignment using the sagittal sign:

- **Sample size**: 5,000+ pregnancies
- **Gestational age**: 11+0 to 13+6 weeks
- **Overall accuracy**: 85%
- **Accuracy at 11 weeks**: ~70%
- **Accuracy at 12+ weeks**: 87–90%
- **Accuracy at 13 weeks**: 93–95%

The study found that accuracy improved significantly with each additional day of gestation, particularly after 12 weeks.

### Hohmann-Woods et al. (2013)

Another important study looked at first-trimester gender determination:

- **Sample size**: ~600 pregnancies
- **Gestational age**: 11–14 weeks
- **Overall accuracy**: 79.5%
- **Accuracy at 11 weeks**: 67%
- **Accuracy at 12 weeks**: 84%
- **Accuracy at 13 weeks**: 92%

This study confirmed that nub theory is unreliable before 12 weeks but becomes increasingly accurate after 12 weeks.

### Other Research

Additional smaller studies have produced similar findings:
- A 2014 study found 91% accuracy at 12–13 weeks
- A 2016 meta-analysis suggested nub theory is most accurate at 12+6 weeks and beyond
- Studies consistently show that **boys are slightly easier to predict than girls** at earlier gestational ages, as the upward angle is more obvious

## Factors That Affect Nub Theory Accuracy {#factors}

Several factors can influence how accurate nub theory is for your specific scan:

### 1. Gestational Age
This is the **most important factor**. Every day between 11 and 13 weeks matters. If your scan is at 11 weeks, accuracy drops to around 70%.

### 2. Baby's Position
The baby needs to be in a **sagittal (side-profile) position** for the nub angle to be visible. If the baby is face-up, face-down, or has legs crossed, the nub may not be assessable.

### 3. Image Quality
A clear, well-focused ultrasound image is essential. Blurry images, overexposed images, or images zoomed in on the wrong area make nub theory impossible.

### 4. Sonographer Skill
While NHS sonographers don't specifically perform nub theory, the quality of the images they capture matters. A skilled sonographer who captures a clear profile view gives you a much better chance.

### 5. Maternal Body Habitus
In some cases, maternal weight can affect ultrasound image quality, making the nub harder to see. This doesn't change the baby's sex, but it can affect whether the nub is visible at all.

### 6. CRL (Crown-Rump Length)
The baby's size matters. If the baby measures smaller or larger than expected, the developmental stage of the nub may not match the expected gestational age.

| Method | Accuracy | Earliest Use | Cost |
|--------|----------|-------------|------|
| Nub theory (12+ weeks) | 80–95% | 11 weeks | Free |
| NIPT blood test | 99%+ | 10 weeks | £150–£500 |
| SneakPeek blood test | 95% (home use) | 7 weeks | £30–£80 |
| 20-week anatomy scan | 95–99% | 16 weeks | Free (NHS) |
| Amniocentesis | 100% | 15+ weeks | NHS (if indicated) |
| CVS | 100% | 11+ weeks | NHS (if indicated) |
| Ramzi theory | ~50% | 6 weeks | Free |
| Skull theory | ~50% | 12 weeks | Free |
| Chinese gender chart | 50% | Any | Free |

Nub theory offers the **best balance of accuracy, accessibility, and cost** for early gender prediction. It's free, uses a scan you're already having, and at 12+ weeks it's reasonably accurate. Only NIPT is more accurate at an earlier stage, but it costs money.

## Real-World Accuracy: What Mums Report {#real-world}

Anecdotal reports from pregnancy forums and Facebook groups suggest real-world accuracy rates that align with the studies:

- **At 12+ weeks**: Most mums who report confirmed results say nub theory was correct approximately 85–90% of the time
- **Boy predictions** tend to be slightly more accurate than girl predictions at 12 weeks
- **Girl predictions at 11 weeks** are the least reliable — the nub may not have flattened yet
- **Ambiguous nubs** (10–30 degree angle) have roughly a 50/50 outcome

### Common Reasons for Wrong Predictions
- Scan done too early (before 12 weeks)
- Baby in a poor position
- Image quality was poor
- The nub was at an ambiguous angle (10–30 degrees)
- Natural variation in fetal development timing

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Is nub theory 100% accurate?

No. Nub theory is never 100% accurate. Even at 13+ weeks, it's approximately 92–95% accurate, meaning roughly 1 in 20 predictions will be wrong. The only 100% accurate methods are amniocentesis, CVS, and NIPT blood tests (99%+).

### Is nub theory more accurate for boys or girls?

Studies suggest nub theory is slightly more accurate for boys at earlier gestational ages. The upward angle of a boy nub tends to be more obvious than the flat angle of a girl nub. However, by 13 weeks, the accuracy is similar for both sexes.

### Can nub theory be wrong at 13 weeks?

Yes. At 13 weeks, nub theory is approximately 92–95% accurate, meaning there's still a 5–8% chance it could be wrong. If accuracy is critical for you, consider NIPT or wait for the 20-week scan.

### What percentage of nub theory predictions are correct?

At 12 weeks, approximately 80–85% of nub theory predictions are correct. At 13 weeks, approximately 92–95% are correct. These figures come from published medical studies.

### Does the quality of my scan photo affect accuracy?

Yes, significantly. A clear profile view with the baby lying on its side is essential. If the photo is blurry, zoomed incorrectly, or the baby is in the wrong position, the nub may not be visible or may appear ambiguous, reducing accuracy dramatically.

### Should I trust nub theory or wait for NIPT?

If you want to know for certain and are willing to pay, NIPT is far more accurate (99%+) and can be done from 10 weeks. If you want a free, fun prediction and your 12-week scan shows a clear nub, nub theory is a reasonable early guess — but don't paint the nursery based on it alone.
