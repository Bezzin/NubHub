# Nub Theory vs Harmony Test

When you're curious about your baby's sex, two very different options are often mentioned in the same breath: nub theory and the Harmony test. One is free and based on scan interpretation; the other is a laboratory blood test with clinical-grade accuracy. Understanding the differences helps you decide what's right for you.

## Comparison {#section-1}

The two methods differ significantly across every dimension that matters:

| | Nub Theory | Harmony Test |
|---|---|---|
| **Earliest timing** | Around 12 weeks | From 10 weeks |
| **Accuracy (gender)** | Variable — best results at 13+ weeks | Very high |
| **Cost** | Free (uses your NHS 12-week scan image) | Paid — prices vary by clinic |
| **What it involves** | Interpreting the angle of the genital tubercle on a scan image | Blood draw analysed in a laboratory |
| **Also screens for** | Nothing — gender only | Chromosomal conditions including Down's syndrome, Edwards' syndrome, Patau's syndrome |
| **NHS availability** | Not an NHS service | Available privately; the NHS offers its own NIPT in some circumstances |

## What the Harmony Test Actually Is {#section-3}

The Harmony test is a brand of non-invasive prenatal testing (NIPT). It analyses fragments of your baby's DNA that circulate in your bloodstream during pregnancy (called cell-free fetal DNA). A laboratory examines the chromosomal makeup of those fragments to screen for certain conditions and, as part of that analysis, can determine the sex chromosomes.

Because it looks directly at DNA, it is highly accurate for sex determination — though no test is absolutely infallible. Importantly, the Harmony test is primarily a screening tool for chromosomal conditions. Many parents choose it because they want reassurance about conditions such as Down's syndrome, and finding out the sex is an additional benefit rather than the main purpose.

In the UK, the Harmony test is offered privately. You will need to book through a private clinic, have a blood draw (usually combined with a scan), and wait several working days for results. NHS NIPT is offered on clinical grounds, not routinely for sex determination.

## What Nub Theory Actually Is {#section-4}

Nub theory is based on the fact that, in early pregnancy, all foetuses have a genital tubercle — sometimes called the "nub" — that looks similar regardless of sex. From around 11 to 12 weeks, the angle at which this nub sits relative to the spine begins to differ between males and females. A nub angled at roughly 30 degrees or more relative to the spine is associated with male development; a nub that is more horizontal or angled downwards is associated with female development.

The catch is that at 12 weeks many babies are not yet clearly differentiated, scan quality varies enormously, and the nub can be hidden by position or placenta. Results improve meaningfully if your scan is taken at 13 weeks or beyond, the image is high quality, and the nub is clearly visible in a true sagittal (side-on) plane. Nub theory is best thought of as an educated read of probabilities rather than a definitive result.

## Which Should You Choose? {#section-2}

The right choice depends on what you're actually looking for:

**Choose the Harmony test if:**
- You want high accuracy on sex determination
- You also want reassurance about chromosomal conditions
- You can access it privately and the cost is manageable for you
- You want an answer before your 12-week scan

**Choose nub theory if:**
- You're curious and want to have a guess from your NHS scan image
- You'd rather not pay for private testing
- You understand it's probabilistic, not definitive
- You're happy to wait until 12–13 weeks for a better read

Many parents do both: they get the Harmony test for clinical reassurance and chromosomal screening, then enjoy nub theory as a fun extra opinion from their scan. The two results often agree, which can be reassuring in its own right.

If you're unsure whether NIPT is right for you, your midwife or GP can explain what NHS screening is available and help you think through the decision without pressure.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Is the Harmony test worth it for gender alone?

If your only goal is finding out the sex, the Harmony test is accurate and early but comes at a cost. Many people find the value lies in the chromosomal screening it also provides — that dual purpose is what makes the price easier to justify. If you only want the sex and cost is a concern, nub theory or waiting for your 20-week anomaly scan (which can often determine sex, though the NHS doesn't do this routinely) may be sufficient.

### Can I use both nub theory and the Harmony test?

Absolutely. You could have the Harmony test from around 10 weeks for clinical accuracy, then revisit nub theory at your 12-week scan as a fun comparison. Many parents find it satisfying when both methods agree — and mildly amusing when they don't.

### How long do Harmony test results take?

Results are typically returned within several working days of the blood draw, though turnaround times vary between clinics. Your clinic will give you a specific timeframe when you book.
