# Nail Changes in Pregnancy

Nails often change during pregnancy due to hormones and increased blood flow. Some people enjoy stronger, faster-growing nails, while others notice brittleness, ridging, or splitting. These changes are normal and usually reverse after birth — though the timing can vary from person to person.

## Why Nails Change {#why-nails-change}

The main driver of nail changes in pregnancy is hormonal. Rising levels of oestrogen and progesterone affect many tissues in the body, including the nail matrix — the area at the base of the nail where new nail cells are produced.

Increased blood flow and circulation, which are a normal part of pregnancy physiology, also affect the nails. Greater circulation can accelerate nail growth. At the same time, the significant nutritional demands of a growing pregnancy can affect the nutrients available for nail health, which may contribute to brittleness or changes in texture.

The direction of change varies widely between individuals. Some people find their nails become noticeably stronger and grow faster than before pregnancy. Others find the opposite — nails that were previously sturdy become prone to splitting or breaking. Both experiences are within the normal range of pregnancy changes.

## Common Nail Changes {#common-nail-changes}

The changes most commonly reported during pregnancy include:

- **Faster nail growth** — nails may need trimming more frequently than usual
- **Harder, stronger nails** — some people notice this particularly in the second trimester
- **Brittle nails that split or break easily** — especially at the tips
- **Vertical ridges** — lines running from the base of the nail to the tip; these are harmless and very common
- **Dry or thickened nails**
- **Onycholysis** — separation of the nail from the nail bed, which is less common but can occur; it usually affects the fingertips and tends to resolve after birth

Nail changes can begin quite early in pregnancy, though many people notice them more from the second trimester onwards.

## Practical Nail Care During Pregnancy

If your nails are becoming brittle or difficult to manage, some simple adjustments can help:

- **Keep nails short** — longer nails are more prone to catching and breaking
- **Wear gloves for housework and washing up** — prolonged exposure to water softens nails and makes them more likely to split
- **Moisturise the nail and cuticle area** — applying a hand cream or cuticle oil regularly helps maintain flexibility
- **Avoid harsh nail products** — some nail polish removers and treatments contain chemicals that can dry or weaken nails
- **Eat a balanced diet** — ensuring adequate protein, iron, and other nutrients supports overall nail health during pregnancy

## Nail Products and Pregnancy Safety

Many people wonder whether nail polish and manicures are safe during pregnancy. In general, occasional use of nail polish in a well-ventilated area is considered low risk. The main concern with nail products is inhaling chemical fumes over a prolonged period, so good ventilation matters more than the specific products used.

If you are having a professional manicure, a well-ventilated salon is preferable. Some people choose to avoid certain nail treatments in the first trimester when the pregnancy is at an earlier stage of development; others continue as normal throughout. This is a personal decision, and your midwife can offer guidance if you have questions.

Gel nails, acrylics, and nail extensions involve stronger chemicals and UV lamps (for curing). If you choose to have these during pregnancy, ensure the salon has good airflow and consider keeping your skin covered during UV curing. There is no established evidence that occasional exposure causes harm, but many people prefer a more cautious approach in pregnancy.

## When to See Your GP

Most nail changes in pregnancy are harmless and resolve on their own after birth. However, there are some situations where it is worth getting your nails checked:

- **Significant separation of the nail from the nail bed** (onycholysis) that is spreading or causing discomfort
- **Discolouration** — yellow, green, or dark nails can sometimes indicate a fungal infection
- **Pain, swelling, or redness around a nail** — these could suggest an infection that needs treatment
- **Nails that look very different from usual** in a way that concerns you

If you notice any of these, speak to your GP. Fungal nail infections, for example, can be treated, but the appropriate treatment during pregnancy depends on how far along you are, so professional advice matters.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Are nail changes in pregnancy normal?

Yes. Changes in nail growth rate, strength, and texture are a common side effect of the hormonal and circulatory changes of pregnancy. They can go in either direction — nails may become stronger or more brittle. Most changes resolve gradually in the months after birth, though the timing varies.

### Can I get manicures while pregnant?

Generally yes. Occasional manicures in a well-ventilated salon are widely considered low risk. The main thing to be mindful of is minimising prolonged exposure to fumes from nail products. If you prefer, you can opt for gentler products or simply keep nails buffed and natural during pregnancy — but there is no requirement to avoid manicures entirely.

### When should I see a doctor about my nails?

See your GP if nails become discoloured (especially yellow or green), if a nail separates significantly from the nail bed, if you notice pain or swelling around the nail, or if you are concerned that what you are seeing does not look like a typical pregnancy change. A GP can assess whether a fungal infection or another cause needs treatment.

Always speak to your midwife or GP if you have concerns about any physical changes during pregnancy.
