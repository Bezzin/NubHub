# Nub Theory: 11-Week Scan Examples

At 11 weeks, nub theory is possible but accuracy is noticeably lower than at 12 or 13 weeks. Here is what the nub looks like at this stage, what you can and cannot reliably read from it, and how to get the most from an early scan image.

## What Is the Nub at 11 Weeks? {#s1}

The "nub" refers to the genital tubercle — a small bud of tissue between the baby's legs that will develop into either a penis and scrotum (boy) or a clitoris and labia (girl). Between approximately 11 and 13 weeks, it is possible to see this structure on an ultrasound scan taken in a true sagittal (side-on) position.

Nub theory proposes that the angle of the nub relative to the baby's spine gives a clue about sex: a nub angled upward at more than roughly 30 degrees suggests a boy, while a nub that runs parallel to the spine or points slightly downward suggests a girl. This principle is rooted in genuine embryological development — the nub does begin differentiating earlier in male foetuses — but accuracy at 11 weeks is meaningfully lower than at 12–13 weeks.

At 11 weeks, the genital tubercle is still in the early stages of differentiation. Both male and female embryos have the same underlying tissue at this point; it is only the direction and pace of that differentiation that differs. This is why 11-week predictions, while possible, carry considerably more uncertainty.

## What 11-Week Nubs Look Like {#s2}

At 11 weeks, the nub is small and the difference between a boy and girl nub is often very subtle. Both can look quite similar at this stage:

- **Boy nubs at 11 weeks:** May show a slight upward tilt away from the spine, but the angle is often not yet pronounced. The nub may look like a small raised bud with a gentle upward direction.
- **Girl nubs at 11 weeks:** Tend to lie flatter, roughly parallel to the spine, sometimes with the tip angling very slightly downward. At 11 weeks this can be hard to distinguish from a mildly angled boy nub.
- **Ambiguous nubs:** A large proportion of 11-week nubs fall into a middle category where the angle is not clearly up or clearly flat. These are genuinely difficult to call, even for experienced readers.

The key challenge at 11 weeks is that the nub is still developing its final position. A nub that looks borderline at 11 weeks may be much clearer — and may point in a slightly different direction — at 12 or 13 weeks.

One thing that can help when reviewing an 11-week image is to look for the overall shape of the nub tip. A forked or stacked appearance at the tip is more characteristic of a boy nub, while a simple, smooth, flat line is more consistent with a girl nub. Even so, these features can be very difficult to make out at 11 weeks, and not all scan images will have the resolution to show them clearly.

## Limitations at 11 Weeks {#s3}

Accuracy at 11 weeks is generally considered to be around 70% under good conditions — meaning roughly 3 in 10 predictions could be wrong. This figure assumes:

- A clear sagittal view of the baby (perfectly side-on, not rotated)
- Good image quality
- A nub that is clearly visible and not obscured by position, shadows, or cord

In practice, many 11-week scan images do not meet all these conditions, and accuracy in real-world images is often lower than under ideal circumstances. Compare this to 12–13 weeks, where accuracy in good-quality sagittal images is generally considered to be meaningfully higher.

Other factors that reduce reliability at 11 weeks:

- The baby may not be in the right position during the scan
- The sonographer's primary focus is medical assessment, not nub angle
- Digital zoom and image compression can make fine details harder to assess
- The cord can run close to the nub and be mistaken for the nub itself

It is also worth noting that sonographers at NHS dating scans are not assessing nub angle — they are focused on measuring the baby and checking for wellbeing. Getting a good nub image at 11 weeks often depends on the baby's position at the time of the scan, which you cannot control.

## Tips for Getting a Readable Image {#s4}

If you want to try nub theory at your scan, the most important factor is the angle of the ultrasound image. You need a true sagittal view — the baby seen perfectly from the side, with the spine running horizontally across the image. Images where the baby is rotated even slightly will make the nub angle appear different from what it actually is, leading to inaccurate readings.

Practical tips:

- **Ask your sonographer** if they can capture a side-on image — they often do this routinely for dating measurements, so a good sagittal image may already be in your set of scan photos.
- **Look for clear spine and skull** in the same image — this is a sign the baby was in a good sagittal position.
- **Avoid over-zooming** on a low-resolution print — this can make the nub appear blurry and harder to read accurately.
- **If the image is ambiguous at 11 weeks,** consider waiting to re-examine the question at your 12-week dating scan if that has not yet happened, or at a private early gender scan at 13+ weeks.
- **Share with an experienced community** — nub theory forums and groups can offer collective experience, though always treat any prediction as a fun guess rather than a definitive answer.

If you had your 11-week scan at a private clinic, the image quality is often slightly better than a printed NHS photo, which can make the nub easier to assess. Even so, manage your expectations at this gestation.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Should I try nub theory at 11 weeks?

You can, but manage your expectations. Accuracy at 11 weeks is around 70% at best, and many images at this stage are genuinely ambiguous. If your image shows a nub that is clearly and strongly angled upward or clearly flat against the spine, that is a more meaningful result. If the angle is borderline, it is worth waiting until 12 weeks or later for a more reliable reading rather than relying on an uncertain early call. Think of an 11-week nub prediction as a fun early guess, not a reliable indicator.

### What does a boy nub look like at 11 weeks?

A boy nub at 11 weeks tends to show a slight upward tilt relative to the baby's spine. However, at 11 weeks this angle is often subtle and not yet as pronounced as it will be at 12–13 weeks. An image showing a clearly raised, upward-pointing nub at 11 weeks is more predictive than one with only a mild tilt. Some 11-week boy nubs may also show the beginnings of a forked or stacked tip, though this detail is often very hard to see at this stage.

### How accurate is nub theory at 11 weeks compared to 12 or 13 weeks?

Accuracy at 11 weeks is generally around 70% under good conditions, compared to meaningfully higher accuracy at 12–13 weeks when the nub has differentiated further. If you have a 12-week dating scan coming up, waiting to read that image — or combining both — will give you a more reliable result. At 13 weeks, nub theory reaches its highest accuracy, as the genital tubercle is much further along in differentiation. Nub theory should always be treated as an enjoyable guess rather than a definitive answer.
