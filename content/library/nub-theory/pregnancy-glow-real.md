# Is the Pregnancy Glow Real?

The pregnancy glow is one of the most talked-about side effects of pregnancy, and yes — it has a genuine biological basis. For many people, a combination of hormonal changes, increased blood volume, and shifts in skin oil production can give the complexion a brighter, rosier, more luminous appearance during pregnancy. Not everyone experiences it in the same way, and some people find their skin changes in less flattering directions, but the glow itself is a real and recognised phenomenon.

## What Causes the Pregnancy Glow {#what-causes-the-glow}

Several things happen to your body during pregnancy that can affect the way your skin looks and feels:

**Increased blood volume.** During pregnancy, your body produces significantly more blood to support the placenta and growing baby. This extra circulation means more blood reaches the small vessels near the surface of the skin, particularly on the face. The result can be a flushed, warm, radiant appearance — the classic "glow."

**Hormonal changes.** Oestrogen and progesterone levels rise steeply in the first trimester and remain elevated throughout pregnancy. These hormones stimulate the sebaceous glands (the oil-producing glands in the skin) to work harder, which can leave skin looking more hydrated and luminous. The downside of this for some people is increased oiliness and pregnancy-related acne or spots.

**Higher metabolic rate.** Your body works harder during pregnancy — your heart beats faster and your metabolism speeds up to support the pregnancy. This increased activity can contribute to a warmer, more energised appearance.

**Sweat gland activity.** The same hormonal changes that increase oil production can also increase sweating slightly, which some people describe as giving their skin a dewy or fresh look — though this is less universally welcomed.

## When Does the Glow Appear {#when-the-glow-appears}

Many people notice changes to their skin from quite early on in the first trimester, though the timing varies a great deal. The classic glow is most often reported in the second trimester — roughly from around 13 to 27 weeks — when morning sickness tends to ease, hormones begin to level out a little, and the body has adjusted to its new state. Energy levels often improve around this time too, which can make people look and feel healthier overall.

For some, the glow comes and goes. Fatigue, nausea, and stress can all affect how skin looks on any given day. If you're deep in first trimester exhaustion, you may not notice the glow until later.

After birth, the glow typically fades as hormone levels drop and blood volume returns to its pre-pregnancy state. Some people also experience postpartum skin changes such as increased hair loss or dryness during this period — again, all driven by the same hormonal shifts running in reverse.

## When the Glow Isn't What You Expected

Pregnancy skin doesn't always behave the way the magazines suggest. While some people genuinely get brighter, clearer skin, others find that the same hormonal processes cause:

- **Acne or spots**, particularly on the chin, jawline, or back
- **Melasma**, sometimes called the "mask of pregnancy" — patches of darker pigmentation on the face
- **Dry or itchy skin**, especially over the growing bump
- **Increased sensitivity** to skincare products

All of these are normal variations. They don't mean anything is wrong with your pregnancy. If you develop severe itching all over your body, particularly on your hands and feet, contact your midwife — this can occasionally be a sign of a condition called obstetric cholestasis that needs to be checked.

## Caring for Your Skin During Pregnancy

If you want to support your skin during pregnancy, a few simple steps can help:

- **Stay hydrated** — drinking enough water supports skin from the inside
- **Use gentle, fragrance-free products** — your skin may be more sensitive than usual
- **Apply SPF daily** — this is always good advice, and particularly important if you're prone to melasma, as sun exposure can worsen pigmentation
- **Eat a varied diet** — plenty of fruit, vegetables, and healthy fats support skin health generally
- **Check products before using them** — some skincare ingredients such as retinoids (vitamin A derivatives) are not recommended during pregnancy; if unsure, ask your midwife or GP

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Does everyone get the pregnancy glow?

No. Hormonal changes affect everyone differently. Some people get radiant, clear skin; others deal with acne, dryness, or pigmentation. Both experiences are entirely normal and don't reflect how healthy your pregnancy is.

### Can I enhance the pregnancy glow?

Gentle skincare, staying well-hydrated, getting enough rest, and eating a balanced diet are all helpful. Avoid harsh or active treatments (such as chemical peels or retinoids) during pregnancy, and always check with your midwife before starting new skincare products.

### Is the pregnancy glow a sign of the baby's gender?

No. The old wives' tale that a glow means you're having a girl (because she "steals your beauty") and dull skin means a boy has no scientific basis whatsoever. The glow — or lack of it — comes from hormones, blood volume, and oil production. It has nothing to do with the baby's sex.

Speak to your midwife or GP if you have concerns about any skin changes during your pregnancy.
