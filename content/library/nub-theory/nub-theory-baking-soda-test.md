# The Baking Soda Gender Test: Does It Work?

The baking soda test is one of the most widely shared old wives' tales for predicting baby gender. It involves mixing your urine with baking soda (bicarbonate of soda) to see whether it fizzes. There is no scientific basis for this test, and its results are no better than chance — but it's still a popular bit of pregnancy fun that many parents enjoy trying.

## What Is the Baking Soda Gender Test?

The test is simple to do at home. You collect a small amount of urine — usually first-morning urine is recommended — and add a spoonful of baking soda to it. According to the old wives' tale:

- **Fizzing or bubbling** is said to mean you're having a **boy**
- **No reaction (or very little fizzing)** is said to mean you're having a **girl**

The reasoning behind it, in folk tradition, is that a baby boy supposedly makes the mother's urine more acidic, and an acidic liquid reacts with baking soda (an alkaline substance) to produce carbon dioxide bubbles. A baby girl, the tale goes, produces less acidic urine, so there is little or no reaction.

## Why the Science Doesn't Support It

The problem is that urine acidity is determined by many factors entirely unrelated to the sex of the baby — including what you've eaten and drunk, how hydrated you are, the time of day, and your kidney function on any given day. Baby sex has no established effect on maternal urine pH.

Baking soda will react with any sufficiently acidic urine regardless of whether you're carrying a boy or a girl. Many people find their urine fizzes regardless, particularly if they're slightly dehydrated or have eaten certain foods. The result is essentially random, giving it roughly a 50% accuracy rate — the same as flipping a coin.

No peer-reviewed research has validated the baking soda test as a gender-prediction method. It has no place in clinical practice and is not used or recommended by midwives or doctors.

## When Is It Genuinely Possible to Find Out Your Baby's Sex?

If you'd like a more meaningful answer about your baby's sex, there are several options available during pregnancy:

- **NIPT (Non-Invasive Prenatal Testing):** A blood test typically offered from around 10 weeks of pregnancy. It analyses fragments of the baby's DNA in your blood and is highly accurate for sex determination. In the UK it is available privately and in some NHS settings for higher-risk pregnancies.
- **Nub theory:** An informal method of reading the genital tubercle (the "nub") on your 12-week dating scan. Accuracy improves with scan quality and gestational age, and is generally considered more reliable than old wives' tales, though it is not a medical test.
- **20-week anomaly scan:** The NHS offers a routine anatomy scan around 18–22 weeks. You can ask the sonographer to tell you the sex at this point, though they will note there is a small margin of error.
- **Private gender scans:** Many private clinics offer gender reveal scans from around 16 weeks onwards.

## Is It Safe to Try the Baking Soda Test?

Yes — baking soda and urine are both harmless substances and there is no safety concern with carrying out the test. It is simply not accurate. If you enjoy it as a bit of pregnancy fun, there's no harm in trying it, provided you treat the result as entertainment rather than fact.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is the baking soda gender test accurate?

No. The baking soda test has no scientific basis and is approximately 50% accurate — the same as guessing. Urine acidity is affected by diet, hydration, and many other factors that have nothing to do with the baby's sex. It is a fun activity, but the result carries no reliable information about whether you're having a boy or a girl.

### Why do people do the baking soda test?

It's a cheap, easy, and harmless activity to try while waiting for a more accurate prediction. Many parents enjoy the guessing game element of pregnancy, and tests like this one give a focus for that excitement. Just don't take the result seriously — it is essentially a coin flip.

### What is the most accurate way to find out baby's gender before the 20-week scan?

NIPT blood testing (available privately from around 10 weeks) is the most accurate early option, using foetal DNA in the mother's blood. Nub theory at the 12-week scan is a popular informal alternative. Both are significantly more informative than old wives' tales like the baking soda test.
