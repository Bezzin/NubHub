# What If There Is No Nub Visible on Scan

You have had your scan, you are hoping to try nub theory to get an early indication of your baby's sex — and then you look at the image and simply cannot see a nub. Or you post it in a forum and get told the image is not readable. This is far more common than you might think, and it is not something to worry about. Here is what it means and what your options are.

## Why the Nub Might Not Be Visible {#why-not-visible}

The nub — formally called the genital tubercle — is a small structure at the base of the baby's spine that begins to develop its characteristic angle during the first trimester. For nub theory to work, the image needs to show the baby in a very specific position. Several factors can make the nub impossible to identify:

**Scan angle is the most common reason.** Nub theory relies entirely on a clear lateral (side-profile) view of the baby. If your baby was facing toward or away from the scanner, or was in a curled position, the nub simply will not appear in the image in a way that can be assessed. This happens in a large proportion of scans — the baby has no reason to hold still in a convenient position.

**Baby was moving.** Even a small amount of movement during the image capture creates blur or distortion that makes the image unreadable for the purpose of nub theory.

**Gestational age is too early.** Before around 11 weeks, the genital tubercle has not yet developed a clear enough angle to assess reliably. If your scan was at 10 weeks or earlier, the nub may simply not be sufficiently differentiated yet.

**Image quality.** Consumer-quality printouts or photos of a screen (rather than digital image files) can lose the resolution needed to identify the small structures involved. Low contrast, shadows, or overlapping structures can all obscure the relevant area.

**Baby's position within the pelvis.** Sometimes the baby is positioned in a way that means the genital area is behind a limb, the umbilical cord, or at the edge of the image — none of which can be predicted or controlled.

## What It Means for Gender Prediction {#meaning-for-prediction}

An unreadable scan means no reliable nub theory prediction can be made from it. That is all it means. It tells you absolutely nothing about your baby's sex, and nothing about your baby's health.

Some people see an "inconclusive" result as disappointing, but it is genuinely preferable to a forced guess from a poor image. A prediction made from an unclear scan is essentially random — it carries no more information than a coin toss. Experienced nub theory readers will always decline to assess an image that is not in the right position, because doing otherwise would simply give false confidence in a meaningless result.

An unreadable nub image is also not a reflection of anything the sonographer did wrong or anything unusual about your baby. It is simply a result of the baby's position at the moment the image was taken.

## What You Can Do Next {#what-next}

If your scan image is inconclusive for nub theory and you are keen to find out your baby's sex before the anatomy scan, you have a few options:

**Wait for the anatomy scan.** The NHS offers a detailed anomaly scan at around 18 to 20 weeks. At this stage, sex can usually be confirmed directly — though this is not always guaranteed, and the NHS may not routinely report the sex in all areas.

**Book a private gender scan.** Many private pregnancy clinics offer gender determination scans from around 16 weeks, when the genitals are more clearly visible. These are generally reliable and are performed by trained sonographers. They typically cost between £50 and £100.

**Consider a blood-based test.** Tests such as NIPT (non-invasive prenatal testing) can determine foetal sex from a blood sample taken from you, usually from around 10 weeks of pregnancy. These are available privately and have a high degree of accuracy. They are not NHS-funded unless there is a clinical reason.

**Try again with a better image.** Occasionally people get a second scan image from a different appointment and find the baby is in a better position. However, there is no guarantee of this, and booking a private scan specifically for a nub theory reading is unlikely to be the most cost-effective route.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Does no visible nub mean something is wrong with my baby?

No. The absence of a visible nub on your scan image is almost always a result of the baby's position or the scan angle. It has no bearing on your baby's health, development, or sex. A nub that cannot be seen is not a nub that is absent — it is simply not captured in the image.

### Can I get another scan to try to see the nub?

You could book a private scan at a clinic, though there is no guarantee the baby will be in the right position the second time either. A more reliable approach is to book a private gender scan from around 16 weeks, when sex can be determined directly rather than inferred from the nub angle.

### Should I pay someone to read an unclear image?

Be cautious. If the image does not show a clear side profile of the baby with the nub area visible, no reader — however experienced — can make a reliable prediction. Any confident prediction given from an unclear image is not based on genuine assessment and should not be trusted. A reputable reader will always tell you when an image is not suitable.
