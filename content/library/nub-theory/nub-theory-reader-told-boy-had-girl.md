# Nub Theory Reader: Told Boy, Had a Girl

Being told "definitely boy" at your 12-week scan — only to hear "it's a girl" at 20 weeks — is a surprisingly common experience in nub theory communities. This is one reader's story, along with a clear explanation of why even confident-sounding predictions can get it wrong.

## The Story {#s1}

Our reader had her 12-week NHS dating scan and noticed the nub appeared to angle upwards in the image. She posted it in a nub theory group and received enthusiastic responses: "definitely boy," "strong boy nub," "I'd stake money on it." She began mentally preparing for a son.

At her 20-week anomaly scan, the sonographer confirmed she was expecting a girl. She was surprised — not upset, but curious. She'd trusted the group's collective confidence and hadn't expected to be in the "wrong" column.

## Why It Was Wrong {#s2}

When the scan image was reviewed more carefully, several factors stood out:

**The gestation was 11 weeks and 5 days.** This matters more than most people realise. At this point the genital tubercle — the nub — is still very early in its development. In girls, the nub gradually flattens and tilts downward over the following weeks. At 11+5 it can still appear slightly elevated, mimicking a male angle.

**The angle was borderline.** The nub appeared to be around 20 degrees from the spine — technically in the "boy" range, but right at the boundary of what's considered ambiguous. Experienced readers acknowledge that anything under 30 degrees is genuinely uncertain. In this image it was called "up" when it was actually a girl nub that simply hadn't flattened yet.

**Image quality affected the reading.** The nub in the image wasn't fully in profile, which can make the angle appear steeper than it is. A true sagittal plane — where the baby is lying perfectly on its side — is required for a reliable reading. Even a slight rotation changes how the nub looks.

**Group consensus amplified confidence.** When one person calls "boy," others often follow. This herd effect in nub theory groups means that an initial confident (but wrong) call can quickly become a unanimous one. Popularity of a prediction doesn't equal accuracy.

## Understanding the Limitations {#s3}

Nub theory is based on the observation that from around 11 weeks, the genital tubercle begins to angle differently in male and female embryos. In boys it angles upward; in girls it remains roughly parallel to the spine or angles slightly downward.

The challenge is that this process is gradual. At 11 weeks even a skilled reader is working with an incomplete picture. The earlier the scan, the less reliable the reading — not because the reader is incompetent, but because the biology simply hasn't declared itself clearly yet.

Community-based nub theory predictions — however enthusiastically worded — are not a medical diagnosis. They are an educated guess based on a still image of a moving structure photographed at a specific moment in development. Even experienced readers who perform well overall will have cases where they get it wrong.

If you're eager to know your baby's sex before the 20-week scan, the most reliable option available from around 10 weeks is a non-invasive prenatal test (NIPT) blood test, which analyses cell-free fetal DNA. Some private providers also offer gender scans from around 16 weeks, when the genitalia are more clearly visible. These carry far less uncertainty than nub readings at 12 weeks.

For most people, nub theory is a bit of fun — a way to engage with the pregnancy and feel involved before 20 weeks. Treating it as a certainty, however, is where things can go wrong emotionally. Prepare for either outcome, and use the 20-week scan as your reliable answer.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### How common is a wrong boy prediction?

Boy predictions based on nub theory are wrong in a meaningful proportion of cases, particularly when the scan is taken before 12 weeks or the image quality is not ideal. The most common reason is reading an ambiguous nub as "up" when it is actually a girl nub that hasn't flattened yet at that stage of development.

### Why do nub theory groups sound so confident if they can be wrong?

Group dynamics play a role — when one person confidently calls a sex, others often agree rather than challenge. Confidence in tone doesn't reflect accuracy. A reliable nub reading requires the right gestation (ideally 12 weeks or later), a true sagittal plane image, and good image quality. Many images posted in groups meet only some of those criteria.

### What's the most reliable way to find out my baby's sex before 20 weeks?

A NIPT blood test, available privately from around 10 weeks, analyses your baby's DNA from your bloodstream and can determine sex with high accuracy. Private gender scans from around 16 weeks are another reliable option. Both are far more accurate than nub theory or old wives' tales.

*Always speak to your midwife or GP if you have questions about your scan results or pregnancy.*
