## What Is a Boy Fork {#boy-fork}

A boy fork describes the genital tubercle appearing as a longer line angled upward away from the spine, sometimes with a small forked or notched tip. In nub theory this raised, forked shape is read as a possible early sign of a boy. The angle is the key part: a steeper upward angle is associated with male development, while the fork itself is a secondary feature some readers look for. It is not a medical diagnosis.

Understanding these basics helps you approach each appointment with realistic expectations and clearer questions for your sonographer or midwife.

## How to Spot a Boy Fork on Your Scan {#spot-boy-fork}

You need a clear side profile image from between about 11 and 14 weeks. Look for the nub angling upward at roughly 30 degrees or more from the spine, often ending in a small forked shape. A flat or downward nub is not a boy fork. Image quality is everything, so blurred or overexposed scans are easy to misread.

Understanding these basics helps you approach each appointment with realistic expectations and clearer questions for your sonographer or midwife.

## FAQ {#faq}

### How accurate is the boy fork sign?

It can be a useful early clue after 12 weeks, but no nub reading is guaranteed. Accuracy depends heavily on the scan angle and clarity.

### Does every boy show a fork?

No. Some boy nubs angle up without a clear fork. The angle matters more than the forked tip.

### Can a girl fork be mistaken for a boy fork?

Yes, especially before 12 weeks when the nub is still developing. Early readings are the least reliable.
