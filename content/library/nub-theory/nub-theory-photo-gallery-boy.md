# Nub Theory Photo Gallery Boy

Curious whether your 12-week scan might be showing a boy? This page walks you through the features people look for in "boy" nub theory examples, so you can compare them with your own scan. Nub theory is a popular bit of fun, but it is not a medical test — it cannot tell you your baby's sex for certain, and even experienced guessers get it wrong. Enjoy it as a guessing game while you wait for a reliable answer.

## What people look for in a "boy" nub

Nub theory looks at the genital tubercle — the small "nub" visible on many scans around 11 to 13 weeks. The idea is that the angle of the nub relative to the baby's spine may hint at sex. In examples that turned out to be boys, people typically point to a few features:

- **An upward angle** — the nub is said to point up away from the spine, often described as around 30 degrees or more.
- **A "stacked" or forked tip** — some boy examples show the nub appearing layered or split at the end.
- **A longer or more prominent nub** — boy nubs are often described as looking more pronounced.

These are patterns enthusiasts describe, not proven medical markers. Two people can look at the same image and disagree, and the same nub can look different depending on how the baby is lying.

## Why the angle is so hard to judge

The single biggest problem with nub theory is the picture itself. For the angle to mean anything, your baby needs to be lying in a clear side-on (sagittal) profile, with the spine and nub both visible in the same plane. If your baby is tilted, curled, or facing a different way, the nub can look angled when it is not — or hidden completely.

Other things that make it unreliable include the stage of pregnancy (earlier scans show less), image quality, and simple guesswork. This is why a nub "prediction" from a photo should never be treated as a result. If you want to know your baby's sex with confidence, the dependable options are a NIPT blood test (often from 10 weeks) that reports sex, or the anomaly scan at around 20 weeks, where a sonographer can look directly if you ask and choose to find out.

## Using these examples sensibly

By all means compare your scan with boy examples and have a guess — it is part of the excitement. Just hold it lightly. A nub that appears to angle upward is "consistent with" a boy guess, but it is not proof, and a flat-looking nub does not rule a boy out. Avoid making firm plans, big purchases, or announcements based on nub theory alone.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I compare my scan to these boy examples?

Yes, and many people enjoy doing exactly that. Look at whether your nub appears to angle upward away from the spine on a clear side-on image. Just remember the comparison is for fun — it is not a diagnosis, and a clear profile shot is essential for it to mean anything at all.

### Do these examples guarantee my baby is a boy?

No. Nub theory is not a reliable way to determine sex, and even confident guesses turn out wrong. Only a sex-reporting blood test (such as NIPT) or a scan where a sonographer checks directly can tell you with confidence.

### When can I find out my baby's sex for certain?

A NIPT blood test can often report sex from around 10 weeks, and the 20-week anomaly scan can usually show it if you ask. Both are far more reliable than reading a nub from a photo.

For anything about your scan or your baby's development, speak to your midwife or sonographer for personal advice.
