# The Shettles Method for Gender Prediction

The Shettles Method is one of the most widely discussed natural gender selection theories. Developed by Dr Landrum Shettles in the 1960s, it suggests that the timing of intercourse relative to ovulation can influence whether you conceive a boy or a girl. While popular with hopeful parents, its scientific backing remains limited.

## What Is the Shettles Method {#what-is-the-shettles-method}

The Shettles Method is based on the idea that X-bearing sperm (which produce girls) and Y-bearing sperm (which produce boys) have different characteristics. According to Shettles, Y-bearing sperm are faster but shorter-lived, while X-bearing sperm are slower but more resilient. By timing intercourse around ovulation, the theory claims you can favour one over the other.

## How It Supposedly Works {#how-it-supposedly-works}

For a boy, Shettles recommended intercourse on the day of ovulation or the day after, when the faster Y-bearing sperm can reach the egg first. For a girl, he suggested intercourse two to four days before ovulation, allowing the longer-lived X-bearing sperm to survive until the egg is released. Positions and other factors were also said to play a role.

## FAQ {#faq}

### Does the Shettles Method work?

Studies have not consistently supported the Shettles Method. Most research suggests the success rate is close to 50 percent, similar to natural odds. Many couples try it anyway as a harmless, low-cost approach.

### Is it safe to try the Shettles Method?

Yes, timing intercourse is safe. There is no medical risk to trying the method, though it may reduce your chances of conceiving in a given cycle if you avoid fertile days.

### Can the Shettles Method replace the nub theory?

No. The Shettles Method is used before conception, while nub theory predicts gender from an ultrasound scan at 11 to 14 weeks. They serve completely different purposes.
