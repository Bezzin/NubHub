# Heart Rate Gender Prediction: Is It Accurate?

The theory that fetal heart rate can reveal the sex of your baby is one of the most enduring gender prediction myths in pregnancy. The claim is simple: a heart rate above 140 bpm means you're carrying a girl, and below 140 bpm means a boy. It sounds almost scientific — after all, it involves an actual measurement. But does the evidence support it?

## The Theory {#s1}

According to this widely circulated old wives' tale: a fetal heart rate above 140 beats per minute (bpm) indicates a girl, while a heart rate below 140 bpm points to a boy. Some versions put the cutoff at 150 bpm, or use slightly different thresholds, but the core idea is the same — that girls' hearts beat faster than boys' in the womb.

The theory gets repeated at every ultrasound appointment, at antenatal classes, and across pregnancy forums. Many people feel it has been confirmed by their own experience, which is understandable — with a 50/50 chance, it will seem right about half the time.

## The Reality {#s2}

The fetal heart rate theory does not hold up to scrutiny. Well-conducted research has consistently found no meaningful difference in fetal heart rate between male and female babies when other factors are accounted for.

Normal fetal heart rate varies considerably depending on gestational age:

- At 6 weeks, the heart rate is often in the range of 100–120 bpm
- By 9–10 weeks, it typically peaks around 170–180 bpm
- From the second trimester onwards, it usually settles between 120 and 160 bpm

Heart rate also changes from moment to moment depending on whether the baby is moving or resting, and varies between individual pregnancies. These natural fluctuations dwarf any theoretical difference between boys and girls. The sex of the baby simply isn't the variable driving the number you see on the monitor.

The accuracy of the heart rate theory is roughly 50% — which is exactly what chance would produce. It is no more reliable than flipping a coin.

## What Actually Determines Fetal Heart Rate?

Fetal heart rate is influenced by:

- **Gestational age** — the biggest factor; heart rate changes significantly across the first and second trimesters
- **Fetal movement** — a moving baby will have a temporarily higher heart rate, just as your own heart rate rises when you move
- **Fetal sleep cycles** — heart rate drops during quiet sleep periods
- **Individual variation** — like all biological measurements, there is a natural range between individual babies

None of these factors are related to the baby's sex. A midwife or sonographer noting your baby's heart rate is checking that it falls within a normal, healthy range for the gestational age — not attempting to guess the sex.

## Reliable Ways to Find Out the Sex

If knowing the sex of your baby matters to you, the genuinely reliable options available in the UK are:

- **Anatomy scan (20-week scan)** — offered on the NHS at around 18–21 weeks. A sonographer looks at the genitalia directly. This is accurate but not 100% guaranteed, as position can make it difficult to see.
- **NIPT (Non-Invasive Prenatal Testing)** — a blood test that analyses cell-free fetal DNA. Available privately from around 10 weeks, and also used in some NHS settings when there is a clinical reason. Very accurate for sex determination.
- **Nub theory** — analysis of the genital tubercle (nub) on the 12-week dating scan. Requires a clear image and is more of an educated prediction than a definitive answer, but can be fun.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Can heart rate predict my baby's gender?

No. The evidence consistently shows no meaningful difference in fetal heart rate between boys and girls. Fetal heart rate is influenced by gestational age, movement, and individual variation — not the baby's sex. The heart rate theory has an accuracy of around 50%, the same as chance. It's a fun piece of folklore, but not a reliable predictor.

### What is a normal fetal heart rate?

Normal fetal heart rate varies with gestational age. From around 10 weeks onwards, a heart rate between 120 and 160 bpm is considered normal. Earlier in pregnancy, rates may be slightly lower at first and then rise. Your midwife or sonographer will tell you if the heart rate is outside the normal range for your baby's gestational age.

### When can the sex of the baby be confirmed accurately?

In the UK, the anatomy scan at around 18–21 weeks is the standard NHS route for checking the baby's sex visually, though it's not always possible depending on position. Privately available NIPT blood tests can determine sex from around 10 weeks of pregnancy and are highly accurate. Nub theory at the 12-week scan is a popular prediction method but is not as definitive.
