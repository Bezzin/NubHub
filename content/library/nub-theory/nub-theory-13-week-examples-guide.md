# Nub Theory: 13-Week Scan Examples

At 13 weeks, nub theory is at its most accurate. The genital tubercle has differentiated significantly from its earlier ambiguous form, making boy and girl nubs noticeably different from each other — and much easier to read in a good sagittal image. Here is what 13-week nubs look like, how to identify key features, and what to watch out for.

## Why 13 Weeks Is the Sweet Spot {#s1}

Between 11 and 13 weeks, the genital tubercle goes through rapid and visible differentiation. At 11 weeks, both boy and girl nubs can look remarkably similar — small, relatively flat, and without obvious distinguishing features. By 13 weeks, the picture is considerably clearer.

In male embryos, the genital tubercle tilts upward and begins the process of forming the penis and scrotum. In female embryos, it remains flat and begins forming the clitoris. By 13 weeks, this divergence is pronounced enough that experienced readers can distinguish between the two with a high degree of accuracy — provided the image is taken in a true sagittal (perfectly side-on) view.

The 12-week NHS dating scan is the most common time people try nub theory in the UK, but babies scanned at the very end of week 12 or at 13 weeks give even clearer results. If you have a private early gender scan at 13 weeks, the image is likely to be among the most readable you will get before an anomaly scan can confirm sex anatomically at around 20 weeks.

## 13-Week Boy Nubs {#s2}

At 13 weeks, a boy nub has several recognisable features:

- **Clear upward angle.** The nub points away from the spine at a noticeable angle — generally described as 30 degrees or more relative to the spine. At 13 weeks, this angle is usually unmistakeable rather than borderline.
- **Elongated shape.** The nub is beginning to elongate as the penis develops, giving it a more prominent, raised appearance compared to earlier weeks.
- **Stacked or forked tip.** Many 13-week boy nubs show a notched or forked appearance at the tip — this is the beginning of the differentiation into the glans and foreskin. This detail can sometimes be seen in a high-quality image.
- **Overall prominence.** A 13-week boy nub typically stands out clearly from the surrounding tissue. It is not subtle.

When reading a 13-week image, a clearly raised nub pointing upward and away from the spine, with an elongated or slightly forked appearance, is a strong indicator of a boy.

## 13-Week Girl Nubs {#s3}

At 13 weeks, a girl nub has equally recognisable features, though in the opposite direction:

- **Flat, parallel orientation.** The nub lies essentially flat against the spine, running parallel to it or angling very slightly downward at the tip. There is no significant upward lift.
- **Three-line or fork appearance at the base.** Some readers describe a 13-week girl nub as showing three short lines at the base — a feature thought to represent the early development of the clitoral hood and labia. This is sometimes called the "fork" sign in girl nubs, though this terminology can cause confusion with the boy nub forked tip, which points upward.
- **Compact and flat.** Overall, a 13-week girl nub is much less prominent than a boy nub. It stays close to the spine and does not lift away from it.

A nub that is completely flat against the spine with no upward angle at all at 13 weeks is very consistently associated with a girl result.

## How to Get the Most from Your 13-Week Image {#s4}

The quality of your reading depends almost entirely on image quality and the baby's position. Even at 13 weeks, a poor sagittal view will produce an unreliable result.

**What makes a good 13-week sagittal image:**

- The baby is seen perfectly from the side — the spine runs horizontally across the image
- The head, torso, and lower body are all visible and not rotated
- The spine and skull dome are both clearly visible in the same image
- The nub is clearly visible between the baby's legs without cord or limbs in the way
- The image is not heavily printed or degraded

**What makes a 13-week image difficult to read:**

- The baby is rotated even slightly (makes the nub angle look different from what it is)
- The nub is obscured by cord, a limb, or the placenta
- The image quality is low or the scan photo has been heavily compressed
- The baby is in a position where the legs are crossed or tucked

If your image is unclear, the most reliable next step is either a repeat private scan at 13+ weeks or simply waiting for the 20-week anomaly scan, where the reporting sonographer can directly confirm the sex anatomically.

Remember that even at 13 weeks, nub theory is not infallible — it is an educated prediction based on the angle of developing tissue, not a diagnostic test. Treat it as an enjoyable, informed guess.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Why is 13 weeks the best time for nub theory?

At 13 weeks, the genital tubercle has reached maximum differentiation within the first trimester. Boy nubs are clearly angled upward and often show elongation and a forked tip, while girl nubs are completely flat against the spine. This degree of differentiation makes the two much easier to distinguish than at 11 or 12 weeks, where borderline cases are far more common. Accuracy in good-quality sagittal images at 13 weeks is generally considered to be higher than at any earlier point — though it is still a prediction rather than a certainty, and the 20-week anomaly scan remains the standard method for confirmed sex determination in the UK.

### Can I get a 13-week private scan specifically for nub theory?

Yes. Many private early pregnancy clinics in the UK offer early gender prediction scans at 13 weeks or from 14 weeks onward. These scans are typically done abdominally and focus on getting a clear sagittal image. Some clinics will specifically mention nub theory as part of what they assess. If you are interested in this, look for a clinic that lists gender prediction from 13 weeks and check that their sonographers are experienced in this area. Private scans of this kind are not part of NHS care and are not medically necessary — they are offered for reassurance or curiosity.

### What if my 13-week image still looks ambiguous?

Some 13-week images are genuinely ambiguous — usually because the baby was not in a clear sagittal position, or because the image quality is not good enough to read the nub angle reliably. If this happens, do not try to force a prediction from an unclear image, as guessing from a poor view is not more reliable than chance. The 20-week anomaly scan can confirm sex directly, and many parents find this a more meaningful reveal regardless of whether they have already tried nub theory.

Speak to your midwife or GP for personalised advice about your scan and pregnancy.
