# Reading Your Ultrasound Report: A Guide for Mums

After your ultrasound scan, you'll usually receive a report with lots of abbreviations and numbers that can look like a foreign language. Here's a plain-English guide to help you understand what it all means.

## Common Abbreviations Explained {#abbreviations}

Here are the most common abbreviations you'll see on your ultrasound report:

- **CRL** — Crown-rump length. The measurement from the top of your baby's head to the bottom of the rump. Used to date the pregnancy in the first trimester.
- **GS** — Gestational sac. The fluid-filled sac in the uterus that contains your baby.
- **YS** — Yolk sac. A small structure inside the gestational sac that provides early nutrition.
- **FP** — Fetal pole. Your baby in the very early stages, before it looks like a baby.
- **FH** — Fetal heart. Usually followed by "seen" or "present" to confirm the heartbeat was detected.
- **FHR** — Fetal heart rate. The number of beats per minute. Normal ranges vary by gestational age.
- **NT** — Nuchal translucency. The fluid-filled space at the back of your baby's neck, measured at the dating scan for chromosomal screening.
- **BPD** — Biparietal diameter. The width of your baby's head. Measured from the second trimester onwards.
- **HC** — Head circumference. The measurement around your baby's head.
- **AC** — Abdominal circumference. The measurement around your baby's tummy.
- **FL** — Femur length. The length of your baby's thigh bone.
- **EFW** — Estimated fetal weight. Calculated from other measurements, used in later pregnancy.
- **AFI** — Amniotic fluid index. A measure of the amount of amniotic fluid.
- **EDD** — Estimated date of delivery. Your due date.
- **LMP** — Last menstrual period. The first day of your last period, used for initial dating.
- **IUP** — Intrauterine pregnancy. Means the pregnancy is correctly located inside the uterus (not ectopic).
- **TA** — Transabdominal. The scan was done through your tummy.
- **TV** — Transvaginal. The scan was done using an internal probe.

## Key Measurements {#measurements}

The report will include several measurements. Here's what they mean:

### CRL (Crown-Rump Length)
This is the key measurement in the first trimester. It's used to date your pregnancy and is accurate to about 5-7 days at the 12-week scan. Typical values:
- 7 weeks: 8-11mm
- 8 weeks: 15-20mm
- 12 weeks: 55-65mm

### NT (Nuchal Translucency)
Measured at the dating scan between 11+2 and 14+1 weeks. A normal NT is typically less than 3.5mm. An NT over 3.5mm doesn't mean your baby has a condition — it means further testing is recommended.

### FHR (Fetal Heart Rate)
The baby's heart rate in beats per minute. Normal ranges:
- 6 weeks: 90-110 bpm
- 7 weeks: 120-150 bpm
- 8-10 weeks: 150-180 bpm
- 12 weeks: 150-170 bpm

## Understanding the Findings {#findings}

The report will include a summary of findings. Here's what common phrases mean:

- **"Single live intrauterine pregnancy"**: One baby, with a heartbeat, correctly located in the uterus. This is what you want to see.
- **"Fetal cardiac activity present"**: The heartbeat was detected. Reassuring.
- **"Fetal pole seen"**: Your baby was visible on the scan.
- **"CRL consistent with dates"**: Your baby measures the expected size for your gestational age.
- **"CRL suggests X weeks Y days"**: Your baby's size indicates this gestational age. If it differs from your dates, your due date may be adjusted.
- **"NT within normal limits"**: The nuchal translucency measurement is normal.
- **"No obvious fetal abnormality seen"**: The sonographer didn't detect any structural issues. Note: this doesn't mean everything is perfect — some issues can't be seen at 12 weeks.
- **"Low risk"**: Your combined screening result indicates a low chance of chromosomal conditions.
- **"Increased risk"**: Your screening result is above the threshold (1 in 150). Further testing will be offered.

## What "Normal" Looks Like {#normal}

A normal first-trimester scan report typically includes:

- Single live intrauterine pregnancy
- CRL consistent with dates (or adjusted dates noted)
- Fetal heart rate within normal range
- NT within normal limits (if screening was done)
- No obvious abnormalities
- Amniotic fluid and placenta appear normal (where visible)

If all of these are noted, your scan was normal and reassuring. The report may also include the sonographer's name, the scan date, and your maternity number.

## When to Ask Questions {#questions}

Ask your midwife or sonographer if:

- **You don't understand a term**: They're used to explaining things in plain English.
- **A measurement seems different from expected**: Ask what it means and whether it's concerning.
- **The report mentions something you weren't told**: Sometimes the report is more detailed than what the sonographer said verbally. Ask for clarification.
- **Your due date has changed**: Ask why and what the new date means for your care.
- **You have an "increased risk" result**: Ask what happens next — you should be offered further testing without pressure.

Never feel embarrassed about asking. Understanding your own healthcare is your right.

## What to Do With Your Report {#do}

- **Keep it in your maternity notes**: Your midwife will usually add it to your handheld notes, but keep a copy for yourself.
- **Share it with your midwife at your next appointment**: They'll go through the findings with you.
- **Don't self-diagnose**: Google can make any finding sound terrifying. Always discuss results with your healthcare team.
- **Keep copies of all reports**: If you move, change hospitals, or need to refer back, having your full scan history is invaluable.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Why does my report say something different from what the sonographer told me?

The report is the formal, written record. The sonographer may have simplified things verbally. If there's a discrepancy that worries you, ask your midwife to explain.

### What does "no obvious abnormality" mean?

It means the sonographer didn't see anything concerning at this scan. It doesn't mean everything is guaranteed to be perfect — some conditions can't be detected until later scans. But it's reassuring for now.

### Should I be worried if my CRL doesn't match my dates?

Not usually. A difference of up to 5-7 days is normal and may result in your due date being adjusted. Larger differences may need a repeat scan, but the most common reason is simply a dating error.
