# What If Baby Measures Smaller Than Expected?

Being told at your scan that your baby is measuring smaller than expected can feel alarming, especially if it catches you off guard. In the majority of cases, there is a straightforward explanation that does not indicate any problem with your baby. This page explains why it happens, what the sonographer will do, and what to look out for going forward.

## How Baby's Size Is Measured at a Scan {#section-1}

At your dating scan (usually between 11 and 14 weeks), the sonographer measures the crown-rump length (CRL) — the distance from the top of your baby's head to the bottom of their spine. This measurement is the most reliable way to estimate gestational age in early pregnancy, and it is what your due date will be based on if there is a discrepancy with your last menstrual period (LMP).

At later scans, different measurements are taken — including head circumference, abdominal circumference, and femur (thigh bone) length — to build a picture of how your baby is growing overall. These measurements are plotted on a growth chart to show whether your baby's growth is following an expected pattern.

## Why Baby Might Measure Small {#section-2}

There are several reasons a baby might measure smaller than expected, and most of them are not cause for alarm:

**Your dates may be slightly off.** This is by far the most common reason. If your menstrual cycle is longer or shorter than 28 days, or if you are unsure of your last period, your estimated due date based on LMP may not be accurate. The scan measurement is considered more reliable, and your due date will be adjusted accordingly.

**Normal biological variation.** Babies, like people, come in different sizes. A baby who measures slightly smaller than the average for their gestational age may simply be naturally petite and perfectly healthy. Growth charts show a range of normal, not a single fixed size.

**The baby's position during the scan.** If your baby is curled up in an awkward position, it can make measurement more challenging and occasionally leads to a slightly lower reading. The sonographer will try different angles and may ask you to move around.

**Fetal growth restriction (FGR).** In a smaller number of cases, a baby measuring small may indicate that the placenta is not providing adequate nutrition and oxygen — a condition called fetal growth restriction. This is more likely to be considered if your baby measures significantly small, if growth appears to have slowed between scans, or if there are other risk factors in your pregnancy. Your midwife and obstetric team will manage this with additional monitoring.

## What Happens After a Small Measurement {#section-3}

What happens next depends on how small the measurement is and at what stage of pregnancy you are:

**Due date adjustment.** At the dating scan, if your CRL suggests you are less far along than your LMP dates indicated, your due date will simply be moved forward to reflect the scan measurements. This is routine and does not mean anything is wrong.

**Follow-up scan.** If there is a meaningful difference between your dates and the scan, or if your baby measures small at a later growth scan, you may be offered a follow-up scan in one to two weeks to check how growth is progressing. Seeing consistent growth between two scans is generally reassuring.

**Referral for additional monitoring.** If there is a concern about fetal growth restriction, your midwife or obstetrician may arrange more frequent scans, Doppler blood flow measurements, or other monitoring. This is precautionary and allows your team to track your baby's wellbeing closely.

In most cases, babies who measure slightly small at one scan are found to be growing normally when reviewed, or simply have a naturally smaller build.

## When to Contact Your Midwife or Team {#section-4}

If you have had a scan result that worries you, always feel free to contact your midwife to ask questions. You should also get in touch promptly if at any point you notice reduced fetal movements, as this is always worth reporting regardless of scan results. If you are ever very concerned and cannot reach your midwife, call your maternity unit directly.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Does measuring small at the dating scan mean miscarriage?

Not at all. The most common reason for measuring small at the dating scan is that your dates were slightly different from what you thought — perhaps your cycle was a little longer than average, meaning ovulation happened later than expected. The sonographer will adjust your estimated due date based on the scan measurement, and that is usually the end of it. A small measurement on its own at the dating scan is not a sign of miscarriage.

### Will I need extra scans if my baby is measuring small?

It depends on how significant the difference is. If your baby is only marginally smaller than expected and your due date has simply been adjusted, you may not need any additional scans beyond your routine schedule. If your baby measures notably small at a later growth scan, or if there is a concern about growth restriction, your team may arrange follow-up scans to monitor progress. Additional scans are a precaution, not necessarily a sign that something is seriously wrong.

### Is a small baby at 12 weeks a problem?

At 12 weeks, a small measurement most often reflects a later conception date than originally estimated rather than a problem with your baby's development. The 12-week scan is partly designed to establish accurate dates. If the sonographer measures your baby and adjusts your due date by a week or two, that is a normal and common outcome. If there is a more significant concern, they will discuss it with you at the appointment.
