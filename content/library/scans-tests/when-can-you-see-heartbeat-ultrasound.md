# When Can You See the Heartbeat on Ultrasound?

Seeing — or hearing — your baby's heartbeat on ultrasound is one of the most emotional moments of early pregnancy. It makes the pregnancy suddenly feel very real. But the question of when you can expect to see it is not always straightforward, and understanding the timeline helps you approach early scans with more confidence.

## When the Heartbeat Becomes Visible

The embryo's heart begins beating very early in pregnancy, but whether it is visible on ultrasound depends on the stage of pregnancy, the type of scan used, and individual variation.

**Around 5–6 weeks** — a heartbeat may be detectable with a transvaginal (internal) ultrasound, but it is not always visible at this stage. The embryo is still extremely small. If no heartbeat is seen at 5–6 weeks, this does not automatically indicate a problem — it is often simply too early.

**6–7 weeks** — a heartbeat is usually visible with a transvaginal ultrasound. At 6 weeks, the embryo measures only a few millimetres, so the flickering of the heartbeat is the clearest visible sign of development.

**7–8 weeks** — a heartbeat should be clearly visible on most transvaginal scans, and often on abdominal ultrasound too, depending on your body and the equipment used.

**8 weeks and beyond** — the heartbeat is generally clearly visible on both transvaginal and abdominal scans, and most sonographers will also let you hear it through the machine's audio output.

## Normal Heart Rate by Week

The fetal heart rate changes as the pregnancy progresses — it starts relatively lower and increases before settling:

| Gestational age | Typical heart rate range |
|---|---|
| 6 weeks | around 90–110 bpm |
| 7 weeks | around 110–150 bpm |
| 8 weeks | around 150–170 bpm |
| 9–12 weeks | around 160–180 bpm |

These are approximate ranges — individual variation exists. A single reading outside these ranges does not necessarily indicate a problem; your sonographer will interpret findings in the full clinical context.

## Why the Heart Rate Changes

In very early pregnancy, the heart rate is relatively slow because the cardiac cells are firing spontaneously before the nervous system takes full control. As the nervous system develops over the first trimester, it begins to regulate the heart more effectively. The rate typically peaks in the late first trimester and then gradually settles throughout the rest of pregnancy.

## Transvaginal vs Abdominal Scans

A transvaginal scan uses a small probe inserted into the vagina to get much closer to the uterus. Because the uterus is low in the pelvis in early pregnancy and the embryo is tiny, transvaginal scanning gives a significantly clearer image than abdominal scanning at 5–8 weeks. This is why early pregnancy scans — especially those before 8 weeks — are often done transvaginally.

If you are offered a transvaginal scan and feel uncertain about it, you can discuss this with the sonographer. It is safe for the pregnancy and should not cause any harm.

## What if the Heartbeat Is Not Visible?

If no heartbeat is seen at your scan, the response will depend on the gestational age and the clinical picture:

- **Before 6 weeks** — it may simply be too early. A follow-up scan in one to two weeks is the standard approach.
- **At 6–7 weeks** — if no heartbeat is seen and the embryo is present but appears small, a further scan is usually recommended before any conclusions are drawn.
- **At 8 weeks or beyond** — the absence of a heartbeat at this stage, with a visible embryo of expected size, is more concerning. The early pregnancy unit will support you with a clear explanation and next steps.

It is worth noting that dating based on the last menstrual period is not always accurate, especially if your cycle is irregular. You may be earlier in pregnancy than you think, which would explain a heart rate that is not yet visible.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What if there is no heartbeat at 6 weeks?

At 6 weeks, it is not unusual to have an inconclusive scan — the embryo may be present but the heartbeat not yet detectable, or the whole pregnancy may be too small to see clearly even on a transvaginal scan. A follow-up scan at 7–8 weeks usually gives a clearer picture. Try not to assume the worst from a 6-week scan alone.

### Does seeing a heartbeat guarantee a healthy pregnancy?

Seeing a heartbeat is a very positive sign and does reduce the risk of miscarriage considerably. However, it cannot guarantee the pregnancy will continue — no scan can do that. What it tells you is that the pregnancy is progressing at that specific moment in time, and that is genuinely reassuring.

### Can I hear the heartbeat at 8 weeks?

Many clinics will turn on the audio so you can hear as well as see the heartbeat from around 8 weeks. Whether this is routine practice depends on the clinic and the type of scan. If you would like to hear it, it is completely reasonable to ask.

Speak to your midwife, GP, or early pregnancy unit for advice specific to your situation and scan findings.
