# 6-Week Private Scan: What You'll See

A 6-week private scan can offer early reassurance, especially if you've had a previous loss, are feeling anxious, or simply want to confirm the pregnancy is in the right place. It's worth knowing in advance that 6 weeks is very early, so what's visible can vary a lot. Understanding what's realistic to see helps you go in with calm expectations rather than worry.

## What You'll See at 6 Weeks

At around 6 weeks, an early scan will usually be done as a transvaginal (internal) ultrasound, because this gives a much clearer picture than a scan over the tummy at this stage. The sonographer is typically looking for:

- **The gestational sac** — the fluid-filled space where the pregnancy is developing.
- **The yolk sac** — a small ring inside the gestational sac, which is an early reassuring sign.
- **The fetal pole** — the first visible sign of the developing baby, which may or may not be seen yet.
- **A heartbeat** — sometimes detectable around this point, but often not visible until a little later.

What shows up depends heavily on exactly how many days pregnant you are, which is why dates being even slightly out can change the picture completely.

## Why Timing Matters So Much

In early pregnancy, a few days makes a big difference. Ovulation and implantation timing vary from person to person, so your "6 weeks" by your last period might actually be 5 weeks and a few days by development. If the scan is on the early side, it's common to see only a sac, or a sac and yolk sac, with no heartbeat yet. This is often completely normal, and many clinics will simply offer a rescan a week or two later to allow the pregnancy to develop further before drawing any conclusions.

For this reason, lots of clinics suggest waiting until 7 to 8 weeks for a first reassurance scan, when a heartbeat is more reliably seen and the result is clearer. If you do choose 6 weeks, try to think of it as a first look rather than a definitive answer.

## What Is Normal vs When to Seek Help

Seeing less than you hoped at 6 weeks is frequently down to timing, not a problem. However, some symptoms always need prompt medical attention regardless of scan plans. Contact your GP, midwife, or early pregnancy unit straight away if you have heavy bleeding, severe or one-sided tummy or shoulder-tip pain, or feel faint or dizzy, as these can be signs of an ectopic pregnancy and need urgent assessment. If you feel it's an emergency, call 999.

A private scan can be reassuring, but it is not a substitute for NHS antenatal care. You should still book your NHS booking appointment with a midwife in the usual way.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is 6 weeks too early for a private scan?

6 weeks is on the early side. You may see a heartbeat, but if your dates are slightly out you might only see a gestational sac. Many clinics recommend waiting until 7 to 8 weeks for a clearer result, and may offer a rescan if it's too early to see much.

### Will I see a heartbeat at 6 weeks?

Possibly. A heartbeat can sometimes be detected around 6 weeks but often isn't visible until closer to 7 weeks. If you don't see one, try not to panic — it may simply be too early, and a follow-up scan a week or so later usually gives a clearer picture.

### Do I still need my NHS scans if I have a private one?

Yes. A private early scan is for reassurance only and doesn't replace NHS care. You'll still be offered your NHS dating scan (around 11 to 14 weeks) and 20-week anomaly scan, and you should still book in with a midwife.

If anything about your pregnancy worries you, speak to your midwife or GP for personal advice.
