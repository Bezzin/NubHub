# Amniocentesis in Pregnancy: What It Involves

Amniocentesis is a diagnostic test offered to some pregnant women to confirm whether a baby has a chromosomal condition. It is more accurate than screening tests because it analyses the baby's own cells. This guide explains why it is offered, what happens during the procedure, and what to expect afterwards.

## Why Amniocentesis Is Offered {#why-offered}

Amniocentesis is usually offered when screening suggests a higher chance of a chromosomal condition, when a previous ultrasound has shown a possible structural problem, when there is a family history of a genetic condition, or when NIPT has returned a positive result. It can detect Down syndrome, Edwards syndrome, Patau syndrome, and a range of other genetic conditions, depending on the tests run on the sample. It is performed after 15 weeks of pregnancy.

## What Happens During the Procedure {#procedure}

Amniocentesis takes around 10 minutes. You will lie down while the sonographer performs an ultrasound to locate your baby and the placenta. A fine needle is then guided through your abdomen into the amniotic sac, and a small sample of amniotic fluid (about 10 to 15ml) is withdrawn. The fluid contains cells from your baby, which are analysed in a laboratory. Local anaesthetic may be used on your skin, and most women describe the sensation as uncomfortable rather than painful.

## Risks and Considerations {#risks}

Amniocentesis carries a small risk of miscarriage, estimated at around 0.5 to 1% when performed by experienced clinicians. Other possible risks include infection, leakage of amniotic fluid, and a small chance of the needle touching the baby, though ultrasound guidance makes this very rare. You will be given detailed counselling before the procedure to help you weigh the benefits and risks. You can choose not to have the test.

## After the Test {#after}

You may feel some mild cramping or soreness for a day or two. You should rest at home, avoid heavy lifting, and avoid strenuous activity for 24 to 48 hours. Contact your midwife or hospital immediately if you experience severe pain, bleeding, fluid leakage, or a temperature. Results for common chromosomal conditions are usually available within three to five days; full results can take up to two weeks.

## FAQ {#faq}

### Is amniocentesis painful?

Most women feel pressure and some mild discomfort rather than sharp pain. Local anaesthetic is used on the skin. You may have cramping afterwards, similar to period pain.

### Can the results tell me other things about my baby?

Yes. As well as chromosomal conditions, amniocentesis can detect certain genetic conditions if there is a known family risk, and sometimes infections. Your consultant will explain what is being tested in your case.
