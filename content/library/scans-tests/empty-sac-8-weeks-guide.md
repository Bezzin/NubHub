# Empty Sac at 8 Weeks: What It Means

Being told that your 8-week scan shows an empty gestational sac is distressing news. Unlike a scan at 5 or 6 weeks — when an empty sac can sometimes be explained by earlier-than-expected dates — by 8 weeks a fetal pole and heartbeat should reliably be visible if the pregnancy is developing normally. An empty sac at this stage is a more definitive finding, though the process of reaching a final diagnosis is careful and compassionate.

## What an Empty Sac at 8 Weeks Usually Means

At 8 weeks of pregnancy, a competent sonographer using a transvaginal ultrasound should be able to see both a fetal pole and a fetal heartbeat. If neither is visible inside a gestational sac of the size expected for this stage, the most likely explanations are:

**Blighted ovum (anembryonic pregnancy).** This is the most common cause. A blighted ovum occurs when a fertilised egg implants and a gestational sac forms, but the embryo either never develops or stops developing at a very early stage — often before it is even visible. The sac continues to grow for a while, driven by pregnancy hormones, which is why symptoms can persist even after the embryo has stopped developing. A blighted ovum is a type of miscarriage, though it may not involve bleeding until later.

**Missed miscarriage.** Also called a silent miscarriage, this is when a pregnancy has ended but the body has not yet recognised it. The sac may contain very early remnants of a fetal pole that has stopped developing. Symptoms often continue because hormone levels have not yet fallen.

**Dates that are significantly earlier than expected.** This is less likely at 8 weeks than at 5 or 6 weeks, but it remains a possibility — particularly if your cycle is irregular or you are uncertain of your last period. In these cases, a follow-up scan is arranged to check for development before a diagnosis is confirmed.

## What Happens After an Empty Sac Finding at 8 Weeks

No responsible clinician will give you a definitive diagnosis of pregnancy loss from a single scan alone — a follow-up is always arranged first.

At 8 weeks, the follow-up scan is typically scheduled 7 to 14 days later. If the sac has grown but remains empty, or has not changed, a diagnosis of blighted ovum or missed miscarriage is confirmed. At this point, your Early Pregnancy Unit (EPU) or hospital team will talk you through your options.

If you are not already being seen by an EPU, your GP can refer you, or you can often self-refer to your local EPU for assessment.

## Your Options After a Confirmed Blighted Ovum or Missed Miscarriage

Once a pregnancy loss is confirmed at 8 weeks, you are typically offered three options. The right choice depends on your personal circumstances, health, preferences, and how quickly you want the process to complete. There is no universally correct choice, and you should not feel pressured.

**Expectant (natural) management.** Waiting for your body to miscarry naturally, without intervention. This can take days or several weeks. It can be the right choice for those who prefer a more natural process, but it can also be emotionally difficult because of the uncertainty about timing.

**Medical management.** Medication (usually misoprostol, given vaginally or under the tongue) is used to trigger the body to pass the pregnancy. This can be done at home or in hospital, depending on your preference and clinical circumstances. It usually results in heavy bleeding and cramping within a few hours.

**Surgical management.** A procedure, usually under general anaesthetic, to remove the contents of the uterus. Options include manual vacuum aspiration (MVA) or surgical management under general anaesthetic (ERPC — evacuation of retained products of conception). This provides the most certainty about completion.

Your EPU team will explain the benefits and considerations of each approach in the context of your specific situation.

## Emotional Support

Receiving an empty sac diagnosis — even when you are still awaiting a follow-up scan — can be devastating. It is completely normal to feel a wide range of emotions: shock, grief, guilt, fear, numbness, or all of these at once. Guilt is particularly common and particularly unfounded — a blighted ovum is a chromosomal event that happens very early and is not caused by anything you did.

Support is available. The Miscarriage Association offers a helpline, online support groups, and a wealth of resources for people going through pregnancy loss. Your EPU team and midwife can also point you towards local support.

If you have a partner or trusted person, lean on them. And be gentle with yourself — there is no right way to feel, and no fixed timeline for processing a loss.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can an empty sac at 8 weeks still turn out OK?

It is much less likely at 8 weeks than at earlier stages. By 8 weeks, a fetal pole and heartbeat should be visible on a transvaginal scan if the pregnancy is developing. An empty sac at this gestation strongly suggests a blighted ovum or missed miscarriage. A follow-up scan is always performed to confirm before any diagnosis is final, but the chance of a different outcome at this stage is low.

### What are my options if a blighted ovum is confirmed?

You will be offered three choices: expectant management (waiting for natural miscarriage), medical management (medication to trigger the process), or surgical management (a procedure to remove the pregnancy). Your EPU team will explain each option in detail, including what to expect physically, the likely timeframe, and the small risks associated with each. The choice is yours to make with their support.

### How long do I have to wait for the follow-up scan?

Typically 7 to 14 days, to allow enough time to confirm whether any development has occurred. Waiting is very hard, but this interval ensures that the diagnosis is accurate before any decisions are made. Your EPU team will make clear what symptoms to watch for in the meantime and will provide contact details if you need to speak to someone before your next appointment.

Speak to your midwife, GP, or Early Pregnancy Unit if you have received an empty sac finding or have any concerns about your scan results.
