# Overactive Thyroid Pregnancy

An overactive thyroid, also called hyperthyroidism, means your thyroid gland produces too much thyroid hormone. It's less common in pregnancy than an underactive thyroid, but it does need careful monitoring. With the right care from your GP, midwife and a specialist, most women with a well-managed thyroid go on to have healthy pregnancies. This guide explains the symptoms, how it's monitored, and when to seek advice.

## What an Overactive Thyroid Means in Pregnancy

Your thyroid is a small gland in your neck that helps control how your body uses energy. When it's overactive, everything tends to speed up. Some pregnancy changes — such as a faster heartbeat or feeling warm — can overlap with thyroid symptoms, which is one reason monitoring with blood tests matters rather than relying on how you feel alone.

If you already know you have an overactive thyroid, tell your maternity team as early as possible, ideally before pregnancy or at your booking appointment. If it's picked up for the first time during pregnancy, you'll usually be referred to a specialist who looks after thyroid conditions in pregnancy.

## Symptoms to Look Out For

Possible signs of an overactive thyroid include:

- A fast or irregular heartbeat or palpitations
- Losing weight or struggling to gain weight despite eating normally
- Feeling anxious, irritable or restless
- Feeling unusually hot or sweating more than usual
- Trembling hands or difficulty sleeping

Because several of these can also be normal in pregnancy, never assume the cause yourself. If you notice these symptoms, mention them to your GP or midwife so they can arrange the right blood tests.

## Monitoring and Treatment

Treatment and monitoring are always tailored to you, so the details below are general and your specialist will guide your care. Many women need regular blood tests through pregnancy so that thyroid hormone levels can be checked and any medication adjusted. Some women take medication that a specialist has confirmed is suitable in pregnancy; the choice and dose are decided by your doctor and may change as pregnancy progresses.

It's important not to stop or change any thyroid medication without medical advice. An untreated or poorly controlled overactive thyroid can increase certain risks in pregnancy, which is exactly why close monitoring and specialist input matter. If you're planning a pregnancy, ask your GP about a review beforehand.

## When to Seek Help

Contact your GP or maternity team if your symptoms get noticeably worse, if you have a very fast or irregular heartbeat, or if you feel unwell and aren't sure why. If you ever feel severely unwell — for example with chest pain, breathing difficulty or a racing heart you can't settle — seek urgent medical help by calling 999 or going to A&E.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How is an overactive thyroid monitored in pregnancy?

Usually through regular blood tests arranged by your GP or specialist, alongside reviews of how you're feeling. The frequency depends on your individual situation, and your medication may be adjusted based on the results.

### Can an overactive thyroid affect my baby?

A well-managed thyroid is the goal precisely because poorly controlled levels can raise certain risks. That's why specialist monitoring is recommended. With good care, many women have healthy pregnancies, so keep all your appointments and follow your team's advice.

### Should I stop my thyroid medication in pregnancy?

No — never stop or change thyroid medication on your own. Speak to your GP or specialist, who will decide whether any changes are needed and make them safely.

For personal advice about your thyroid in pregnancy, speak to your midwife, GP or specialist.
