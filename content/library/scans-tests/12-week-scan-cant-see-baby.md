# What If They Can't See Baby at Your 12-Week Scan?

Hearing that the sonographer "can't see baby" at your 12-week scan is terrifying — but before you panic, there are several reasons this can happen, and many of them are nothing to do with the baby's health. Here's what you need to know.

## Why the Sonographer Might Not See Baby {#why}

There are several reasons a sonographer might struggle to see your baby at 12 weeks, and most of them are benign:

- **You're earlier than you think**: If your periods were irregular or you ovulated late, you might only be 9 or 10 weeks pregnant. At that stage, the baby is much smaller and harder to see clearly on an abdominal scan.
- **Your BMI**: If you carry extra weight around your tummy, the ultrasound has to travel through more tissue, which can make the image less clear. A transvaginal scan usually solves this.
- **A retroverted (tilted) uterus**: Some women have a uterus that tilts backwards rather than forwards. This can make an abdominal scan harder, but again, a transvaginal scan usually gives a clear view.
- **Baby's position**: If your baby is hiding behind your pelvic bone or curled up tightly, the sonographer may not get a good view.
- **Full bladder issues**: If you were asked to have a full bladder and didn't, or if your bladder is so full it's distorting the image, the view may be poor.
- **Equipment or technique**: Occasionally, the sonographer simply needs to try a different approach or a different probe.

## What Happens Next {#next}

If the sonographer can't see your baby on an abdominal scan, they'll usually try one or more of the following:

1. **Switch to a transvaginal scan**: This gives a much clearer view, especially if you're earlier than expected or have a higher BMI. It's the most common next step.
2. **Ask you to empty your bladder**: If your bladder is too full, emptying it can improve the view.
3. **Ask you to wait and walk around**: Sometimes waiting 30 minutes changes the baby's position.
4. **Arrange a repeat scan**: If neither abdominal nor transvaginal scans give a clear view, you may be asked to come back in one to two weeks. By then, the baby will be bigger and easier to see.

## When It Could Be Serious {#serious}

In some cases, not seeing the baby does indicate a problem. These situations are less common but important to be aware of:

- **Missed miscarriage**: This is where the baby has stopped developing but your body hasn't yet recognised the loss. The sonographer may see an empty gestational sac or a baby with no heartbeat. If there's any doubt, you'll be asked to come back in 7-14 days for a repeat scan to confirm.
- **Blighted ovum**: This is a type of missed miscarriage where a gestational sac forms but no embryo develops inside it. Again, a repeat scan is needed to confirm before any decisions are made.
- **Ectopic pregnancy**: At 12 weeks, an ectopic pregnancy (where the baby grows outside the uterus) would usually cause symptoms like pain or bleeding. However, if the sonographer can't see a baby in the uterus and you have symptoms, they'll check your fallopian tubes.

The key point is that **no sonographer will diagnose a loss from a single scan if there's any uncertainty**. They'll always invite you back for a repeat scan to confirm, because dating errors are common and babies can be smaller than expected.

## How to Cope While Waiting {#cope}

Waiting for a repeat scan is agonising. Here are some ways to cope:

- **Don't Google**: Searching for "no baby on scan" will show you worst-case scenarios and increase your anxiety.
- **Lean on someone**: Tell your partner or a close friend how you're feeling.
- **Ask for an earlier repeat**: Some early pregnancy units will scan you again in 7 days rather than 14 if you're very anxious — it's worth asking.
- **Monitor symptoms**: If you develop bleeding, severe pain, or a fever, contact your GP or early pregnancy unit immediately.
- **Look after yourself**: Eat, rest, and be kind to yourself. Stress won't change the outcome.

## Questions to Ask {#questions}

If the sonographer says they can't see your baby, here are good questions to ask:

- "Is it possible I'm not as far along as I thought?"
- "Will you try a transvaginal scan?"
- "What do you think might be the reason?"
- "When should I come back, and what should I do in the meantime?"
- "What symptoms should prompt me to seek urgent help?"

A good sonographer will answer these sensitively and honestly. Don't be afraid to ask — you deserve clear information.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### How common is it not to see the baby at 12 weeks?

It's relatively uncommon but not rare. Most often, it's because you're earlier in your pregnancy than the dates suggest. A transvaginal scan will usually find the baby.

### Will I definitely need a repeat scan?

Not necessarily. If a transvaginal scan gives a clear view, the sonographer can usually complete the scan on the same day. Repeat scans are only needed if the view remains unclear.

### Should I be worried if I have no symptoms?

If you have no bleeding or pain, the chance of a good outcome is higher. Many women who are told the baby can't be seen go on to have perfectly healthy pregnancies — they were simply earlier than expected.
