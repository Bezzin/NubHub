# Protein In Urine Pregnancy

Finding out there is protein in your urine during pregnancy can feel worrying, but in most cases it is nothing serious. A small or trace amount is common, and your midwife uses the dipstick test simply as one of several routine checks to keep an eye on your health. What matters is the bigger picture: the level of protein, your blood pressure, and how you are feeling. This page explains what the test looks for, what is normal, and the signs that mean you should seek advice promptly.

## Why Your Urine Is Tested at Antenatal Appointments

At most antenatal appointments your midwife will ask for a small urine sample and dip a test strip into it. This quick check screens for several things at once, including protein, glucose, and signs of infection. Testing for protein is mainly a way of watching for pre-eclampsia, a condition involving raised blood pressure that usually appears in the second half of pregnancy. Because protein in the urine can be an early clue, your midwife tracks it alongside your blood pressure over time rather than reacting to a single reading in isolation.

## What the Results Can Mean

A trace of protein on its own, especially with normal blood pressure and no symptoms, is usually not a concern. Higher or rising levels may point to a urinary tract infection (UTI), a kidney infection, or pre-eclampsia. Sometimes a sample is simply contaminated, so your midwife may ask you to give another one. If protein is found, you may be offered a follow-up test, a blood pressure check, or blood tests to build a clearer picture. Try not to jump to conclusions from one result; your midwife will interpret it in context.

## When To Seek Help

Contact your midwife or maternity unit promptly if you notice possible signs of pre-eclampsia, particularly from around 20 weeks onwards. These include a severe or persistent headache, problems with your vision such as blurring or flashing lights, sudden swelling of your face, hands, or feet, pain just below your ribs, or feeling generally very unwell. If you have pain or burning when you wee, need to go very often, or have a high temperature, you may have an infection that needs treating. Trust your instincts and ask to be checked if something does not feel right.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is protein in urine always serious?

No. A small or trace amount is common in pregnancy and is often nothing to worry about. Your midwife monitors the level over time, and it is rising protein combined with raised blood pressure or symptoms that needs closer assessment.

### What is pre-eclampsia?

Pre-eclampsia is a pregnancy condition involving high blood pressure, often alongside protein in the urine. It usually develops after 20 weeks and needs monitoring because it can affect you and your baby. Attending your antenatal appointments helps it be picked up early.

### Can a UTI cause protein in my urine?

Yes. A urinary tract or kidney infection can show up as protein, and sometimes other markers, on a dipstick test. If an infection is suspected, your sample may be sent to a lab and you may be offered treatment.

Always speak to your midwife or GP for advice tailored to your own pregnancy.
