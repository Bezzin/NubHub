# What Does a 6 Week Ultrasound Look Like

A 6 week ultrasound is often one of the earliest scans you will have, and it can feel both exciting and nerve-wracking. At this point your baby is tiny — roughly the size of a lentil — and what the scan can show is genuinely limited. Knowing what to expect beforehand can help you feel calmer and avoid unnecessary worry if things look different to what you imagined.

## What You Might See at 6 Weeks {#what-you-see}

At 6 weeks the embryo is only a few millimetres long, so the scan focuses primarily on confirming the pregnancy is in the right place (inside the uterus) and checking that early structures are forming. Here is what may be visible:

**Gestational sac** — This is usually the first thing a sonographer identifies. It appears as a dark, fluid-filled oval shape inside the uterus and is typically visible from around 4.5 to 5 weeks.

**Yolk sac** — A small white ring inside the gestational sac. Its presence is a reassuring sign that development is progressing. It nourishes the embryo before the placenta takes over.

**Fetal pole** — A tiny thickening alongside the yolk sac that represents the earliest visible form of your baby. It may or may not be visible at exactly 6 weeks, depending on how precisely your dates have been calculated.

**Heartbeat** — A flickering movement within the fetal pole indicates cardiac activity. This is sometimes visible from around 6 weeks, though it is more reliably seen at 6.5 to 7 weeks. If dates are off by even a few days, the heartbeat may simply not be detectable yet — this does not automatically signal a problem.

Because everything is so small, the sonographer is largely looking for the right structures in the right place, rather than a fully formed baby picture. The image on screen will likely look like a small white blob in a dark space, which is completely normal.

## What Might Be Too Early to See {#too-early}

It is completely normal for a 6 week scan to leave questions unanswered. Several things may not yet be visible even in a healthy, normally progressing pregnancy:

- **No heartbeat** — Cardiac activity often cannot be seen until at least 6.5 weeks, and sometimes not until 7 weeks. If your dates are based on a longer or irregular cycle, you may be a few days behind where you think you are. A follow-up scan one to two weeks later will usually clarify things.
- **No fetal pole** — If only the gestational sac and yolk sac are visible, this can sometimes be normal if the scan is truly very early. Your sonographer or doctor will advise whether a repeat scan is needed.
- **Measuring slightly smaller than expected** — Embryo size at 6 weeks can vary by a few days even in healthy pregnancies. A small measurement alone is not necessarily cause for concern.

If your sonographer is uncertain about what they can see, they will typically recommend a repeat scan rather than drawing conclusions immediately. This is standard practice and should not be interpreted as bad news.

## How the Scan Is Performed {#how-performed}

At 6 weeks, a transvaginal (internal) ultrasound is almost always used. This is because the embryo is so small that a scan through the abdomen would not provide a clear enough image this early. A thin probe is gently inserted into the vagina, which gives a much closer, clearer view of the uterus.

If you are having this scan privately, you may be asked to have a full bladder or an empty one — your clinic will advise in advance. NHS early pregnancy scans are usually offered if you have symptoms such as bleeding or pain, or if you have had a previous ectopic pregnancy or miscarriage.

After the scan, your sonographer or a doctor will discuss the findings with you. If everything looks as expected, you will usually be advised to continue as normal and attend your NHS booking appointment. If there is anything uncertain, they will arrange a follow-up scan.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Should I see a heartbeat at exactly 6 weeks?

Sometimes, but not always. Cardiac activity is often first visible between 6 and 7 weeks, and it depends heavily on how precisely your dates are calculated. If no heartbeat is seen, a repeat scan is standard — it is not an immediate sign of miscarriage.

### Is a 6 week scan always transvaginal?

Almost always, yes. A transvaginal scan gives a much clearer picture at this early stage because the embryo is too small to see clearly through the abdomen. It is safe in pregnancy and only mildly uncomfortable for most people.

### What if the sac is empty at 6 weeks?

An empty gestational sac — sometimes called a blighted ovum — can be difficult to diagnose at exactly 6 weeks because it may simply be too early to see the embryo yet. A second scan, usually one to two weeks later, is needed before any conclusions are drawn.

### Does a good 6 week scan mean the pregnancy is safe?

A reassuring 6 week scan is a positive sign, but it cannot guarantee the outcome of the pregnancy. Miscarriage risk decreases once a heartbeat is confirmed, but your 12 week dating scan remains the key milestone for overall reassurance.
