## The Role of Progesterone in Early Pregnancy

Progesterone is a hormone produced after ovulation by the corpus luteum, and later by the placenta, that keeps the uterine lining thick and supportive for a growing embryo. In early pregnancy it helps prevent contractions and supports implantation. A single progesterone blood test can be useful alongside hCG: a level above around 25 to 30 nmol/L usually suggests a healthy intrauterine pregnancy, while a level below 10 nmol/L is strongly associated with a non-viable pregnancy or ectopic. Between these values, the picture is less clear and repeat testing or a scan is needed.

## How Progesterone Is Used Alongside hCG

Progesterone is rarely used on its own to judge pregnancy health. Your team will usually look at it together with hCG and sometimes a second hCG taken 48 hours later. A reassuring pattern is a rising hCG with a progesterone above 25 nmol/L. A low progesterone with a slow-rising hCG is more concerning. The NHS does not routinely test progesterone in every pregnancy, but it is commonly offered in early pregnancy units and private clinics when there is bleeding, pain, or a history of loss, to help decide whether to arrange an early scan.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is a normal progesterone level in early pregnancy?**

A level above 25 to 30 nmol/L is generally reassuring. Below 10 nmol/L is strongly associated with a non-viable pregnancy. Values in between need repeat testing and a scan.

**Will the NHS test my progesterone?**

Routine NHS booking bloods do not include progesterone. It is usually tested only if you attend an early pregnancy unit with bleeding, pain, or a history of miscarriage or ectopic pregnancy.

**Can progesterone supplements prevent miscarriage?**

For most women, progesterone supplements do not prevent sporadic miscarriage. However, they may be offered if you have had recurrent miscarriages or bleeding in a current pregnancy with a confirmed heartbeat, based on recent UK guidance.
