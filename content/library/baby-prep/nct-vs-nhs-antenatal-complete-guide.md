# NCT vs NHS Antenatal Classes: Complete Guide

Antenatal classes are one of the most valuable things you can do to prepare for birth and early parenthood — but with both NHS and NCT options available, it can be hard to know which is right for you, or whether you need both. Here is a straightforward comparison to help you decide.

## What Are Antenatal Classes?

Antenatal classes (also called birth preparation or parentcraft classes) cover what to expect during labour and birth, pain relief options, feeding your baby, and early newborn care. They are designed to help you feel informed and confident heading into one of the biggest experiences of your life.

Both NHS and NCT classes cover similar core topics, but they differ significantly in cost, group size, depth of content, and social experience.

## NHS Antenatal Classes

NHS antenatal classes are run by midwives and are free to all pregnant women and their partners. They are delivered through your local hospital or GP surgery, and availability varies depending on where you live.

**What they typically cover:**
- Stages of labour and what to expect
- Pain relief options available at your specific hospital
- When to come into hospital
- Caesarean birth and assisted delivery
- Infant feeding (breastfeeding and bottle feeding)
- Newborn care basics

**Practical details:**
- Free of charge
- Usually 1–3 sessions, sometimes an afternoon or a single day
- Larger groups, often 10–20 couples
- Run by your local NHS midwifery team, so information is specific to your hospital
- Booking is usually done through your midwife

The main advantage of NHS classes is the local, hospital-specific information. You will learn about the facilities at the unit where you plan to give birth, the policies there, and you may even meet some of the midwives who could care for you in labour.

## NCT Antenatal Classes

NCT (National Childbirth Trust) is a charity that runs paid antenatal courses across the UK. Their classes are run by trained NCT practitioners and are widely regarded as comprehensive and high quality.

**What they typically cover:**
- Labour and birth in detail, including breathing and positioning techniques
- Pain relief, including epidurals and natural approaches
- Caesarean and assisted birth
- The immediate postnatal period
- Infant feeding and support
- Partner involvement and emotional preparation
- Early weeks at home with a newborn

**Practical details:**
- Paid — prices typically range from around £100 to over £300 depending on location and format; some bursaries are available for those on lower incomes
- Smaller groups, typically 6–8 couples
- Run over several sessions (usually 3–5 evenings or a weekend course)
- Consistent group throughout, which helps build friendships
- Can be booked directly through the NCT website

The most-cited benefit of NCT classes is not actually the content — it is the people. Because you are in a small, consistent group of couples at the same stage of pregnancy, NCT classes very commonly lead to lasting friendships. For many parents, their NCT group becomes their primary support network through the early months of parenthood.

## Can You Do Both?

Yes — and many parents do. A common approach is to attend NHS classes for the hospital-specific information and to meet local midwives, and NCT classes for deeper preparation and the social connection. If budget is a constraint, the NHS classes are free and cover the essentials well.

## Which Should You Choose?

There is no single right answer, but here are some pointers:

- **If cost is a concern:** NHS classes are free and cover what you need to know.
- **If you are new to the area or don't have many local friends with children:** NCT classes can be particularly valuable for the social connections.
- **If you want detailed, in-depth preparation:** NCT courses tend to go into more depth over more sessions.
- **If you want local hospital-specific information:** NHS classes delivered by your own midwifery team are hard to beat for this.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Are NCT classes worth the money?

For many parents, yes — particularly for the social aspect. NCT groups frequently become tight-knit support networks that last well beyond the newborn stage. The course content is also comprehensive and well structured. If budget allows, most parents who attend NCT classes feel it was a worthwhile investment. If cost is a barrier, the NCT does offer bursaries, and NHS classes provide solid preparation for free.

### What is the difference between NCT and NHS antenatal classes?

The key differences are cost, group size, and depth. NHS classes are free, run by midwives, usually shorter, and provide hospital-specific information. NCT classes are paid, run over multiple sessions with a small consistent group, cover content in more depth, and are particularly valued for the friendships they create.

### When should I book antenatal classes?

NCT courses in particular can fill up quickly, especially in busy areas. It is worth booking by around 20 weeks of pregnancy to secure your preferred dates. NHS classes are usually arranged through your midwife at your booking appointment or later in pregnancy — ask at your antenatal appointments when classes are available locally.

Speak to your midwife for personalised advice on what preparation is right for you and your partner.
