# Midwife-Led Unit vs Obstetric-Led Unit

When choosing where to give birth in the UK, you will often hear about midwife-led units (MLUs) and obstetric-led labour wards. Understanding the difference helps you make the right choice for your pregnancy and birth preferences.

## What is a midwife-led unit? {what-is-mlu}

A midwife-led unit is run by midwives without obstetric doctors routinely present. They are designed for low-risk pregnancies and focus on natural birth with minimal intervention. MLUs can be alongside a labour ward (AMLU) or freestanding (FMU). They typically offer birthing pools, comfortable environments, and more continuity of care. Pain relief options are usually limited to gas and air, water, and pethidine.

## What is an obstetric-led labour ward? {what-is-obstetric}

An obstetric-led labour ward (sometimes called a consultant unit) has doctors, anaesthetists, and neonatal teams on site. It is designed for higher-risk pregnancies or anyone who wants full medical support available. Epidurals, continuous monitoring, induction, and caesarean sections are all available. This is where you will be transferred if complications arise in an MLU or home birth.

## Key differences {key-differences}

MLUs have lower intervention rates, more relaxed environments, and higher rates of normal birth for low-risk pregnancies. Obstetric wards offer immediate access to pain relief, surgery, and specialist care. Research shows low-risk women birthing in MLUs have fewer interventions and similar outcomes for babies compared with obstetric wards.

## How to decide {how-to-decide}

Your midwife will assess your risk throughout pregnancy. If you are low-risk and want a natural birth, an MLU may suit you. If you have a medical condition, previous complicated birth, or want an epidural from the outset, an obstetric ward is recommended. You can also tour both units to compare the environments before deciding.

## FAQ {#faq}

### Can I switch from MLU to obstetric ward during labour?

Yes, transfer is straightforward if you are in an alongside unit. Freestanding units require ambulance transfer, which takes a bit longer.

### Are MLUs safe for first-time mothers?

Yes, for low-risk first pregnancies. Transfer rates are higher for first-time mothers (around 30-40%) but outcomes remain excellent.

### Can I have an epidural in a midwife-led unit?

No, epidurals require anaesthetists. If you want an epidural you should book an obstetric-led ward.
