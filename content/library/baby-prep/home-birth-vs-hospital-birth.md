# Home Birth vs Hospital Birth

Deciding between a home birth and a hospital birth is a personal choice that depends on your medical history, your preferences, and how your pregnancy progresses. Both options are available on the NHS in the UK. This guide compares the two so you can weigh the pros and cons.

## Home birth: the basics {home-birth-basics}

A home birth means you give birth at home with midwives present, supported by NHS community midwives. It is generally offered to women having a straightforward, low-risk pregnancy. You will have two midwives with you during labour, and emergency transfer to hospital is available if needed. Transfer rates are around 10-20% for first-time mothers and lower for subsequent births.

## Hospital birth: the basics {hospital-birth-basics}

Hospital birth takes place in a maternity unit, either on a midwife-led unit or an obstetric labour ward. It offers immediate access to pain relief (including epidural), theatres, and neonatal care if complications arise. Most UK births happen in hospital, and it is the recommended option for higher-risk pregnancies or if you want maximum medical support on hand.

## Comparing the benefits {comparing-benefits}

Home birth benefits include being in your own environment, no travel during labour, continuous midwife support, and faster recovery at home. Hospital birth benefits include immediate access to medical intervention, a wider range of pain relief, and specialist teams if needed. Research shows planned home birth for low-risk pregnancies is as safe as hospital birth for the baby and reduces interventions.

## Who is eligible for home birth? {eligibility}

Home birth is usually offered if you have a low-risk pregnancy, no previous complicated births, and a singleton baby in a head-down position. Conditions like gestational diabetes, pre-eclampsia, previous caesarean, or a breech baby may mean hospital birth is recommended. Your midwife will assess your suitability throughout pregnancy.

## FAQ {#faq}

### Is home birth safe in the UK?

For low-risk pregnancies, studies show planned home birth is as safe as hospital birth for the baby and has lower intervention rates. Emergency transfer is available if needed.

### Can I have an epidural at home?

No, epidurals require an anaesthetist and are only available in hospital. Home births use gas and air, water, and natural pain relief.

### What happens if I need to transfer during a home birth?

An ambulance transfers you to the nearest suitable unit. Most transfers are for slow progress or pain relief rather than emergencies.
