# Car Seat: When to Buy and What to Look For

A car seat is one of the most important purchases you'll make for your baby. Unlike a bouncy chair or a changing mat, it's not optional — you legally cannot take your newborn home from hospital in a car without one. Getting it right before your due date is essential, so here's everything you need to know.

## When to Buy Your Car Seat

Most parents aim to have their car seat sorted by 36 weeks. Babies can arrive early, and you don't want to be sourcing one in a rush while you're in labour or newly postpartum.

Buying a few weeks before your due date also gives you time to:

- Have it professionally fitted in your car
- Practise installing and removing it yourself
- Check compatibility with your pram or travel system if you're using one
- Register the seat with the manufacturer in case of a safety recall

If you're planning a home birth, you'll still need a car seat before your baby is born in case of transfer to hospital, so the 36-week target applies regardless.

## What to Look For

### Safety Standards: i-Size (R129)

Look for a seat that meets the i-Size (UN R129) regulation, which is the current UK and EU safety standard. This replaces the older ECE R44 standard and offers better side impact protection and a more straightforward way to check compatibility with your car. Seats are labelled clearly.

### Rear-Facing for as Long as Possible

Rear-facing is significantly safer for babies and young children. In a rear-facing seat, crash forces are spread across the back, head, and neck — the strongest part of a child's body. The NHS recommends keeping your baby rear-facing for as long as possible, ideally until the upper weight or height limit of the seat is reached.

Most infant carrier seats (Group 0+) accommodate babies from birth up to around 75–85 cm in height. Extended rear-facing (ERF) seats can keep your child rear-facing up to age three or four.

### 5-Point Harness

A 5-point harness — over both shoulders, across both hips, and through the crotch — distributes crash forces most safely for small children. Avoid seats that use just a lap belt for newborns.

### Side Impact Protection

i-Size seats all include side impact testing, but look for additional side wings or energy-absorbing foam in the headrest area. Side impacts are among the most dangerous in real-world crashes.

### Car Compatibility

Not all car seats fit all cars. Always check using the manufacturer's compatibility list before buying, or have the seat tested in your specific car by a trained fitter. Many large retailers offer free fitting checks.

## Getting Your Car Seat Fitted

A professional fit makes a significant difference. Many road safety charities and some retailers offer checking services. An incorrectly installed car seat — even a high-quality one — offers far less protection.

Ask about:
- The ISOFIX anchors in your car (most cars manufactured after 2004 have them)
- Whether you'll need a support leg or top tether
- How to tighten the harness correctly as your baby grows

## What to Avoid

- Second-hand seats with an unknown history — you can't tell if they've been in a crash
- Seats with visible cracks or missing parts
- Seats that don't come with installation instructions

## Frequently Asked Questions

### When should I buy a car seat?

Aim to have your car seat purchased and fitted by 36 weeks pregnant. Babies sometimes arrive before their due date, and you must have a car seat to bring your baby home from hospital. Buying earlier also gives you time to have it properly fitted and to practise installing it yourself.

### Should my baby always be rear-facing?

Yes. Rear-facing is much safer for babies and young children. The NHS recommends keeping your child rear-facing for as long as possible — ideally until they reach the maximum height or weight limit of the seat. Many parents continue rear-facing until age three or four using an extended rear-facing seat.

### Do I need ISOFIX?

ISOFIX provides a rigid, standardised connection between the car seat and the car's chassis, reducing the risk of incorrect installation. It's widely recommended and most i-Size seats use it. Check whether your car has ISOFIX anchor points — if it doesn't, a belt-only seat installed correctly is still safe.

Speak to your midwife or health visitor if you have any concerns about transporting your newborn safely.
