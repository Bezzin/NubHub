# What to Put on Your Baby Registry: Complete List

Building a baby registry for the first time can feel overwhelming — the baby product market is enormous, and it is not always obvious what you actually need versus what is clever marketing. This guide cuts through the noise with a practical UK-focused list of genuine essentials, useful extras, and things that are genuinely not worth adding.

## The Essentials {#section-1}

These are the items you will use from day one and that make a real difference to daily life with a newborn.

**Sleep**
- Crib, moses basket, or next-to-me bedside crib — plus a new, firm, flat mattress that fits snugly (second-hand mattresses are not recommended by the Lullaby Trust for safe sleep)
- At least four to six cellular cotton blankets
- Two to three newborn sleeping bags (once baby is old enough and heavy enough to use one safely)

**Travel**
- Car seat — this must be new or from a trusted source with a full crash history. An infant carrier (Group 0+) or an i-Size infant seat is appropriate for newborns
- Pram or travel system — choose based on where you live (urban pavements vs rural paths) and how you travel

**Feeding**
- If breastfeeding: a good nursing bra, breast pads, lanolin cream, and optionally a manual or electric breast pump (some NHS trusts loan pumps)
- If formula feeding: bottles, a steriliser, and a bottle brush
- Muslins — you will want at least a dozen; they are used for everything

**Changing**
- Changing mat with a lip (wipeable is essential)
- Nappies — a mix of newborn and size one; babies sometimes skip newborn size entirely if they are larger
- Cotton wool balls or reusable cotton pads for the early weeks
- Fragrance-free nappy cream

**Clothing**
- Seven to ten short-sleeved vests (bodysuits) in newborn and 0–3 months
- Seven to ten sleepsuits in newborn and 0–3 months
- A couple of cardigans or jackets
- A hat for outdoor use

**Bathing**
- Baby bath or bath support seat
- A bath thermometer (the elbow test works, but a thermometer removes guesswork)
- Mild, fragrance-free baby wash

**Health and safety**
- Baby nail scissors or a file (newborn nails are surprisingly sharp)
- Digital thermometer

## Useful Extras {#section-2}

These are not strictly necessary, but many parents find them genuinely helpful:

- **Baby monitor** — useful if your home is large or you plan to put baby down in a separate room
- **Baby carrier or sling** — brilliant for keeping hands free; look for a carrier that provides correct hip and spine support
- **Bouncer chair** — gives you somewhere safe to put baby while you eat or shower
- **Nursing pillow** — useful for breastfeeding positioning and later for supported sitting
- **Playmat with arch** — for tummy time and visual stimulation from around six to eight weeks
- **White noise machine or app** — many newborns settle better with consistent background sound
- **Nappy bag** — a dedicated bag with multiple compartments makes outings much smoother

## What to Leave Off {#section-3}

Some products are heavily marketed to new parents but offer little practical benefit:

- **Nappy disposal systems** — a standard bin with a lid works perfectly well
- **Bottle warmers** — a jug of hot water achieves the same result
- **Wipe warmers** — nice in theory, impractical in reality
- **Excessive newborn clothing** — newborns grow extremely quickly; focus on zero to three months rather than stocking up on newborn sizes
- **Elaborate bath seats for newborns** — a simple bath support or held bath is perfectly safe and takes up far less space

## A Note on Second-Hand Items {#section-4}

Many baby items can be safely purchased second-hand — prams, bouncers, high chairs, and clothing are all fine. The main exceptions are car seats (due to crash history concerns) and mattresses (hygiene and safe sleep guidance). Always check that any second-hand item meets current UK safety standards and has not been recalled.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### How many baby clothes do I actually need?

For the newborn stage, aim for around seven to ten vests and seven to ten sleepsuits in both newborn and 0–3 month sizes. Babies go through multiple outfit changes per day, especially in the early weeks, so having enough to get through a couple of days without washing is practical. Avoid buying too many newborn size items — some babies go straight into 0–3 months, particularly if they are born at or above average weight.

### What is the most useful thing to put on a baby registry?

Muslins, sleepsuits, nappies, and feeding equipment tend to be the most genuinely useful items. If you are unsure, adding a gift card option is always appreciated — it lets you buy what you actually need once your baby arrives and you discover their specific preferences. A bouncer or a carrier also tend to come up repeatedly as the item parents wished they had bought sooner.

### Do I need a baby monitor from day one?

Not necessarily. NHS guidance recommends that babies sleep in the same room as a parent for the first six months, which means a monitor is not essential straight away. Many parents find one helpful once their baby moves to their own room, or if their home is large. Audio monitors are perfectly sufficient; video monitors are a personal preference rather than a safety requirement.
