# Medical Management of Miscarriage: What It Involves

Medical management uses medication to help the body pass the pregnancy tissue. It is one of three options for managing a miscarriage — the others being expectant management (waiting for the miscarriage to happen naturally) and surgical management. Understanding what each option involves can help you feel more in control of a very difficult situation.

## What Is Medical Management?

Medical management involves taking medication that causes the uterus to contract and expel the pregnancy tissue. In the UK, the standard regimen uses two medicines:

- **Mifepristone** — taken first, this medicine blocks the hormones that sustain the pregnancy.
- **Misoprostol** — taken 24–48 hours after mifepristone, this causes the uterus to contract and the tissue to pass.

Misoprostol can be taken as tablets placed under the tongue, inside the cheek, or vaginally, depending on what your healthcare team recommends and your own preference.

Some hospitals offer misoprostol alone without mifepristone first — your team will advise which regimen applies to you.

## What to Expect

### In the hours after misoprostol

Most people begin to experience cramping and bleeding within a few hours of taking misoprostol. The bleeding and cramping are typically heavier than a normal period — this is the body passing the pregnancy tissue, and it is expected. You may also notice clots and tissue.

Pain is common and can range from moderate to quite strong. You will be offered pain relief before you leave the hospital or clinic, which usually includes paracetamol and ibuprofen (safe to take once you are no longer pregnant). If pain is severe, contact your healthcare team.

You do not need to try to collect or examine any tissue that passes — simply dispose of it as you would sanitary waste.

### Where you can be

Many people manage medical management at home, which allows you to be in a familiar and private environment. Others prefer to be at hospital, particularly if they live far from emergency care or feel more comfortable with staff nearby. Discuss your preference with your care team — both are generally possible.

### In the days that follow

Bleeding continues for up to one to three weeks after the medication. It should gradually become lighter. Light spotting towards the end of this period is normal.

You may feel emotionally exhausted as well as physically drained. Give yourself time to rest and recover, and accept support from people around you.

## How to Know If It Has Worked

Your hospital or early pregnancy unit will typically arrange a follow-up appointment or ask you to do a urine pregnancy test two to three weeks after treatment to confirm the pregnancy hormone (hCG) has cleared. In some cases, an ultrasound scan is used to confirm the uterus is empty. If tissue remains, further medication or a small procedure may be needed.

## When to Seek Urgent Help

Contact your hospital or call **111** if you experience:

- Bleeding so heavy that you are soaking through more than one thick pad per hour for two consecutive hours
- Severe pain that is not helped by prescribed pain relief
- Signs of infection: high temperature (38°C or above), smelly discharge, or worsening pain several days after treatment
- No bleeding at all within 24 hours of taking misoprostol

Go to A&E or call **999** if you feel faint, collapse, or have severe abdominal pain alongside very heavy bleeding.

## Emotional Recovery

Medical management ends a pregnancy, but it does not end grief. Many people find the physical process is manageable but the emotional aftermath takes longer. This is entirely normal. You do not need to feel a particular way or recover on a particular timeline.

The Miscarriage Association (miscarriageassociation.org.uk) offers free support, including a helpline and online forum. Your GP can also refer you to counselling if you feel you would benefit from it.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is medical management painful?

You will experience cramping that can feel similar to a heavy or painful period, and for some people the cramps are stronger than that. You will be given pain relief before you go home — typically paracetamol and ibuprofen, which can be taken together. If pain is not managed with these, contact your care team. Most people find the worst of the cramping lasts a few hours and then eases significantly, though bleeding continues for several days to weeks.

### How long does bleeding last after medical management?

Bleeding typically lasts one to three weeks. It usually starts heavy with possible clots and tissue in the first day or two, then gradually lightens to spotting. If bleeding remains very heavy (soaking more than a pad per hour), becomes smelly, or is accompanied by fever or worsening pain, contact your GP, the EPU, or call 111. Your period will usually return four to six weeks after the bleeding from the miscarriage has fully stopped.

### Can I try for another pregnancy after medical management?

Most healthcare teams advise waiting until you have had one normal period before trying to conceive again — partly to allow your body to recover and partly to make dating a subsequent pregnancy easier. However, it is worth discussing the timing with your GP or midwife based on your individual situation. There is no evidence that medical management affects future fertility.

Speak to your midwife or GP for advice tailored to your own circumstances and medical history.
