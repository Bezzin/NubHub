# How Is a Missed Miscarriage Diagnosed?

This article explores learn how missed miscarriage is diagnosed with ultrasound scans and hormone tests, what the criteria are, and what happens after diagnosis. We cover what you need to know in plain language.

## The Ultrasound Diagnosis {#section-1}

Missed miscarriage is most often diagnosed by transvaginal ultrasound. The sonographer measures the pregnancy sac and, if present, the embryo, and looks for a heartbeat. A diagnosis is made when the sac is large enough that a heartbeat should be visible but none is found, or when a sac measured at a previous scan has not grown. The guidance in the UK sets thresholds: a missed miscarriage may be diagnosed if the pregnancy sac is at least 25mm with no embryo, or if the embryo is at least 7mm with no heartbeat. Because these measurements carry some uncertainty, and because dating errors can occur, a single scan may not be enough to confirm the diagnosis. In many cases a second scan is arranged seven to fourteen days later to confirm no growth before any management is offered. This cautious approach is designed to avoid ending a viable pregnancy by mistake.

## Hormone Tests and Next Steps {#section-2}

Blood tests for hCG, the pregnancy hormone, can support the diagnosis. A single hCG level is rarely enough: the pattern matters, and a falling or plateauing level over 48 hours is consistent with a non-viable pregnancy. hCG testing is most useful when a scan is inconclusive, for example if the sac is too small to date confidently. After diagnosis, your clinical team should explain your options clearly, give you time to decide, and offer emotional support. Many women feel numb or shocked when told at a scan, so having written information to take home is valuable. You should be offered follow-up and access to counselling if you wish. If you have a history of loss or the team suspects an underlying condition, further tests may be arranged once the miscarriage is complete.

## FAQ {#faq}

### Can a single scan confirm a missed miscarriage?

Not always. If measurements are borderline or dating is uncertain, a second scan seven to fourteen days later is recommended to confirm no growth before any management is offered.

### What hCG pattern suggests a missed miscarriage?

A falling or plateauing hCG level over 48 hours is consistent with a non-viable pregnancy. A single measurement is rarely enough; the trend matters.

### Why is a second scan sometimes needed?

A second scan reduces the risk of ending a viable pregnancy by mistake. Dating errors and measurement uncertainty mean confirmation is safer, especially when measurements are borderline.
