# Pregnancy After an Ectopic: What to Expect

Getting pregnant after an ectopic pregnancy is possible for most people, but the experience can feel very different from a first pregnancy. You may feel anxious, unsure of what monitoring to expect, or worried about the same thing happening again. This guide explains what the NHS pathway typically looks like, what affects your chances, and what symptoms to be aware of.

## Your Chances of Conceiving Again

Most people who have experienced an ectopic pregnancy are able to conceive again. Your individual chances depend on a number of factors, including:

- **Whether both fallopian tubes are intact.** If you were treated medically (with methotrexate) or had surgery that preserved both tubes, your chances of future pregnancy are generally good. If one tube was removed (salpingectomy), you can still conceive naturally using the remaining tube — ovulation alternates between ovaries each cycle, and the remaining tube can pick up eggs released from either side.
- **The health of your remaining tube.** Scarring or damage to the remaining tube can affect fertility and also increases the risk of a further ectopic pregnancy.
- **Your age and overall reproductive health.** These factors affect fertility regardless of ectopic history.

It is sensible to wait until your medical team advises it is safe to try again — typically at least three months after methotrexate treatment, as the drug can affect a developing pregnancy if you conceive too soon. After surgical treatment, your team will advise you individually on when it is safe to try again.

## Extra Monitoring in Your Next Pregnancy

If you have had an ectopic pregnancy, you will be offered closer monitoring at the start of any future pregnancy. This is standard NHS practice and is there to reassure you and catch any problems as early as possible.

You can expect:

- **An early referral to the Early Pregnancy Unit (EPU).** As soon as you have a positive pregnancy test, contact your GP or midwife and mention your history of ectopic pregnancy. They should refer you to the EPU for early monitoring.
- **An early ultrasound scan.** This is usually offered at around 6 to 7 weeks of pregnancy — earlier than the standard NHS dating scan — to confirm the pregnancy is located in the uterus (an intrauterine pregnancy) rather than in a tube or elsewhere.
- **Serial hCG blood tests.** In some cases, the EPU will monitor your pregnancy hormone levels with blood tests taken a few days apart. Rising hCG levels that roughly double every 48 hours are a reassuring sign in early pregnancy.
- **Ongoing support from the EPU.** If anything is unclear on the early scan — for example if the pregnancy is too small to see clearly at the time of the scan — you will usually be asked to return for a repeat scan within a week or two.

Do not wait for your GP or midwife to contact you. If you have had an ectopic pregnancy and get a positive test, contact your GP or EPU directly and mention your history. Acting promptly means you will be scanned and supported earlier.

## Recognising Symptoms of Another Ectopic Pregnancy

Having had one ectopic pregnancy does slightly increase the risk of another, so it is important to be aware of the warning signs — particularly before your early scan has confirmed the location of the pregnancy.

Seek urgent medical attention — call 999 or go to A&E — if you experience:

- Sudden, severe, one-sided abdominal pain
- Shoulder tip pain (a referred pain caused by internal bleeding irritating the diaphragm)
- Feeling faint, dizzy, or collapsing
- Heavy vaginal bleeding with severe pain

Contact your EPU or GP the same day if you have:

- One-sided pelvic or abdominal pain that is not severe
- Light vaginal bleeding or spotting alongside pain
- Feeling unwell in a way that feels different from normal early pregnancy

If you are in any doubt, seek medical advice — it is always better to be checked and reassured than to wait.

## The Emotional Side

The weeks between getting a positive test and having your early scan confirmed can feel agonising. Anxiety after an ectopic pregnancy is very common. You may find yourself hyperaware of every twinge, reluctant to feel hopeful, or struggling to enjoy the pregnancy even once the location has been confirmed.

These feelings are a completely understandable response to what you have been through. Talking to your midwife about how you are feeling gives them the chance to offer support and refer you to perinatal mental health services if needed. Some people also find peer support through organisations like the Ectopic Pregnancy Trust (ectopic.org.uk) helpful — hearing from others who have been through the same experience can reduce the sense of isolation.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What are my chances of a normal pregnancy after an ectopic?

Most people who have had an ectopic pregnancy are able to conceive again and go on to have a successful intrauterine pregnancy. Your individual chances depend on factors like whether both fallopian tubes are intact and the overall health of your reproductive system. If you had one tube removed, you can still conceive naturally using the remaining tube. Your GP or gynaecologist can discuss your specific situation and any fertility referral if you have been trying to conceive for some time without success.

### Will I get extra monitoring in my next pregnancy?

Yes. Having had an ectopic pregnancy means you are entitled to early monitoring in any future pregnancy. As soon as you get a positive pregnancy test, contact your GP or EPU and mention your history. You will usually be offered an early ultrasound scan at around 6 to 7 weeks to confirm the pregnancy is in the right place, and possibly serial hCG blood tests if needed. You do not need to wait for the standard 12-week dating scan before getting support.

### How soon can I try for another pregnancy after an ectopic?

This depends on how your ectopic pregnancy was treated. After methotrexate (medication) treatment, most doctors advise waiting at least three months before trying again, as the drug can affect early fetal development if you conceive too soon. After surgical treatment, your team will advise you individually. Emotionally, many people also need time to process the experience before feeling ready to try again, and there is no pressure to rush.

Speak to your midwife or GP for personalised advice about your situation and what monitoring to expect.
