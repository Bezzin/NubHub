# Surgical Management of Miscarriage (ERPC)

Facing a miscarriage is one of the hardest experiences of pregnancy, and having to make decisions about how it's managed can feel overwhelming. Surgical management — sometimes called ERPC (evacuation of retained products of conception) or SMM (surgical management of miscarriage) — is one of three options you may be offered, alongside expectant management (waiting naturally) and medical management (medication). This guide explains what surgical management involves, so you can make an informed choice with support from your clinical team.

## What Is Surgical Management?

Surgical management is a minor procedure to remove pregnancy tissue from the womb (uterus). It is typically performed under general anaesthetic, though sometimes a local anaesthetic with sedation is used. The procedure itself usually takes between 10 and 20 minutes, and most women are able to go home on the same day.

A small instrument is gently passed through the cervix to remove the pregnancy tissue, usually using gentle suction. You will not feel the procedure itself while under anaesthetic.

Surgical management tends to be offered when:
- You are experiencing heavy or prolonged bleeding
- There are signs of infection
- The expectant or medical approach has not worked
- You personally prefer a quicker, more definitive resolution
- The pregnancy tissue has not passed on its own after a confirmed miscarriage

Your doctor or Early Pregnancy Unit (EPU) team will explain which options are suitable for your specific situation. You do not have to choose surgical management if you prefer another approach, unless it is medically necessary.

## What Happens Before the Procedure

Before the procedure, you will usually have a scan to confirm the findings and a consultation with a doctor or nurse. You'll be asked about any allergies, your general health, and any medications you're taking. You'll need to fast (not eat or drink) for several hours beforehand if you're having a general anaesthetic — the team will give you specific instructions.

You may be given medication (pessaries) beforehand to help soften the cervix and make the procedure easier.

## What Happens Afterwards

After the procedure, you'll be monitored in a recovery area until the anaesthetic has fully worn off. You'll need someone to take you home and stay with you overnight — you won't be able to drive yourself.

**What to expect in the days after:**
- Some bleeding, usually lighter than a period, lasting one to two weeks
- Mild cramping, similar to period pain, for a day or two
- Paracetamol is usually sufficient for pain relief — check with the team which pain relief is appropriate for you
- Your first period after surgical management typically returns within four to six weeks

You'll be given contact details for the EPU in case you have concerns. Go back to the unit or call 111 if you develop a fever, heavy bleeding that soaks more than one pad per hour, severe abdominal pain, or signs of infection such as a foul-smelling discharge.

## Emotional Recovery

Physical recovery after surgical management is generally quicker than with the other approaches, but emotional recovery takes much longer and is just as important. There is no timeline for grief after pregnancy loss. Many women find it helpful to access bereavement support — the Miscarriage Association offers a helpline, online support groups, and written resources. Your GP can also refer you for counselling if you feel you need it.

Some women feel relief at having the process completed; others feel sadness, guilt, or numbness. All of these responses are valid.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is ERPC painful?

The procedure itself is not painful because you're under anaesthetic. Afterwards, you may have mild cramping similar to period pain for a day or two. Paracetamol is usually sufficient, but the clinical team will advise you on what's appropriate before you go home.

### How long is recovery after ERPC?

Physical recovery is usually one to two days. Bleeding typically lasts one to two weeks and is lighter than with medical management. Many women return to work within two to three days, though there is no set rule — take the time you need. Emotional recovery is individual and can take weeks or months.

### When can I try for another pregnancy after surgical management?

Most doctors and midwives advise waiting until after your first period before trying again, which gives your body time to recover and also makes it easier to date a future pregnancy. However, this is something to discuss with your GP, as the right timing depends on your individual circumstances and how you are feeling emotionally.

Speak to your GP, midwife, or the Early Pregnancy Unit team for personal guidance on your options and what is right for your situation.
