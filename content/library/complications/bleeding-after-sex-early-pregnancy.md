# Bleeding After Sex in Early Pregnancy

Noticing bleeding after sex in early pregnancy can be alarming, but it is one of the most common reasons women contact their early pregnancy unit. Light spotting after intercourse is usually harmless, though it is always worth getting checked.

## Why Does Bleeding After Sex Happen?

During pregnancy your cervix becomes softer and more vascular, meaning it has a richer blood supply than usual. Friction during intercourse can irritate this sensitive tissue and cause light spotting, typically pink or brown, which settles within a few hours. This is completely different from the heavier bleeding associated with miscarriage. Using a condom does not prevent this kind of spotting, and changing positions or avoiding deep penetration can reduce the chance of it happening again.

## When to Seek Urgent Help

Contact your maternity unit or GP the same day if the bleeding turns bright red, becomes heavier than a period, or is accompanied by cramping, clots, dizziness, or shoulder-tip pain. These symptoms can sometimes indicate a miscarriage or, more rarely, an ectopic pregnancy. If the bleeding is light and painless, you can usually wait and monitor, but mentioning it at your next appointment is still sensible. Trust your instincts: no concern is too small for your midwife.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### 1. Is it safe to have sex during early pregnancy?

Yes, if your pregnancy is low-risk. Sex does not harm the baby, who is protected by the amniotic sac and uterus.

### 2. How long after sex might spotting last?

Most women find light spotting settles within 24 hours. If it continues beyond a day, contact your midwife.

### 3. Should I avoid sex after bleeding?

It is sensible to wait until the spotting has fully stopped and you have spoken with your healthcare provider.
