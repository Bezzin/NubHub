# Cramping at 8 Weeks Pregnant: When to Worry

Mild cramping at 8 weeks pregnant is common and, in most cases, nothing to worry about. Your uterus is continuing to grow, your ligaments are stretching further, and pregnancy hormones are affecting your digestive system — all of which can produce sensations that feel similar to period cramps or stomach ache. At 8 weeks, the anxiety of early pregnancy is often still running high, and any pain can feel alarming. Here is how to understand what is normal and what warrants medical attention.

## Common Causes of Cramping at 8 Weeks {#section-1}

By 8 weeks, your baby is growing rapidly — roughly the size of a kidney bean — and your body is working hard to support that growth. Several entirely normal processes can cause cramping at this stage:

- **Uterine growth.** Your uterus is continuing to expand. The muscles and ligaments supporting it, including the round ligaments, are under increasing tension, which can produce a pulling, aching, or cramping sensation low in the abdomen.
- **Round ligament stretching.** Round ligament pain tends to become more noticeable from around 8 weeks onwards as the uterus rises and the ligaments that support it lengthen. It can feel like a sharp twinge or a dull ache on one or both sides of the lower abdomen.
- **Digestive changes.** Progesterone slows down digestion during pregnancy to allow more nutrients to be absorbed. This can lead to bloating, trapped wind, and constipation — all of which can create cramping that is not uterine in origin but can be hard to distinguish from it.
- **Gas and bloating.** A very common cause of lower abdominal discomfort at this stage. Moving around gently or passing wind often provides relief.
- **Increased blood flow.** The uterus and pelvis receive significantly increased blood flow during early pregnancy, which can produce a heavy, full, or achy sensation.

Normal cramping at 8 weeks is typically mild, comes and goes, and does not worsen progressively. Many women describe it as similar to period cramps but without the bleeding that would normally accompany them.

## When to Seek Help {#section-2}

While most cramping at 8 weeks is harmless, there are symptoms that should prompt you to contact your GP, midwife, or Early Pregnancy Unit (EPU) without delay:

- **Severe or worsening pain** that does not ease with rest and feels different from ordinary period-type cramps
- **One-sided pain**, particularly if it is sharp, stabbing, or persistent — one-sided pain in early pregnancy should always be assessed to rule out ectopic pregnancy
- **Cramping alongside heavy bleeding** — light spotting in early pregnancy can be normal, but heavy red bleeding accompanied by cramping needs prompt review
- **Shoulder tip pain** — pain felt at the very tip of the shoulder alongside abdominal cramping can be a sign of internal bleeding and requires emergency assessment
- **Feeling faint, dizzy, or very unwell alongside cramping**
- **Cramping with fever or signs of infection**

If you are in any doubt, contact your EPU or call 111. Most EPUs can offer an assessment and early scan if there is clinical concern. You do not have to wait until your 12-week booking scan to seek reassurance.

**Call 999 or go to A&E immediately** if you have severe abdominal pain, heavy bleeding, pain at the tip of your shoulder, or you feel faint or collapse — these can be signs of a ruptured ectopic pregnancy, which is a life-threatening emergency.

## What to Expect at an Early Pregnancy Assessment {#section-3}

If you attend an EPU with cramping at 8 weeks, the team will typically take a history of your symptoms, check your vital signs, and arrange an ultrasound scan. At 8 weeks, a transvaginal (internal) scan gives the clearest view of the pregnancy, though an abdominal scan may also be used. The scan can confirm:

- That the pregnancy is inside the uterus (ruling out ectopic pregnancy)
- That a fetal heartbeat is present
- The size and development of the embryo

Seeing a heartbeat at 8 weeks is genuinely reassuring. If the pregnancy is confirmed to be viable and in the right location, mild cramping is very unlikely to indicate a problem.

## Tips for Managing Mild Cramping at 8 Weeks {#tips}

If your cramping is mild and you are not experiencing any red flag symptoms, the following may help you feel more comfortable:

- **Rest.** Lying down or sitting quietly often helps mild uterine cramps ease.
- **Warmth.** A warm (not hot) bath or a warm wheat bag held against your lower abdomen can be soothing.
- **Paracetamol.** Paracetamol is generally considered safe to take in pregnancy at the recommended dose if you need pain relief. Avoid ibuprofen and aspirin unless specifically advised by a doctor.
- **Stay hydrated.** Dehydration can worsen cramping, so keep up your fluid intake.
- **Gentle movement.** If cramping is digestive in nature, a short gentle walk can help relieve trapped wind and bloating.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Is cramping at 8 weeks normal?

Mild, intermittent cramping at 8 weeks is very common and is usually caused by your uterus growing, ligaments stretching, or digestive changes. As long as cramping is mild and not accompanied by heavy bleeding or severe pain, it is generally not a cause for concern. If you are worried, contact your midwife, GP, or EPU for advice.

### When should I worry about cramping at 8 weeks?

Seek medical advice promptly if cramping is severe, worsening, one-sided, or accompanied by heavy bleeding. Sharp one-sided pain needs urgent assessment to rule out ectopic pregnancy. Call 999 or go to A&E immediately if you have severe pain with bleeding, feel faint, or have pain at the tip of your shoulder.

### Should I go to A&E for cramping at 8 weeks?

For mild cramping without heavy bleeding, contact your GP, midwife, or Early Pregnancy Unit rather than going straight to A&E. They can assess you and arrange a scan if needed. For severe pain, heavy bleeding, one-sided pain, or if you feel very unwell or faint, call 999 or go to A&E immediately.
