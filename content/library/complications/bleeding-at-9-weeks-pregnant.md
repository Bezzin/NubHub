# Bleeding at 9 Weeks Pregnant

Noticing blood when you're 9 weeks pregnant is frightening, and it's completely understandable to feel worried. The important thing to know straight away is that bleeding in early pregnancy is relatively common, and in many cases there is a benign cause — but it always deserves attention. You should always contact your midwife, GP, or Early Pregnancy Unit (EPU) when you experience any bleeding during the first trimester.

## Common Causes of Bleeding at 9 Weeks

There are several reasons bleeding can occur at 9 weeks, and not all of them are serious:

**Cervical changes.** During pregnancy, your cervix becomes more sensitive and has an increased blood supply. Anything that stimulates the cervix — including sexual intercourse, a vaginal examination, or a smear test — can cause a small amount of bleeding. This type of spotting is usually light, short-lived, and not associated with pain. It is not harmful to the pregnancy.

**Subchorionic haematoma.** A subchorionic haematoma is a collection of blood between the wall of the uterus and the gestational sac. It can cause anything from light spotting to a heavier bleed. Many resolve on their own without affecting the pregnancy, though they are monitored by the EPU. An ultrasound scan is needed to diagnose this.

**Implantation-related spotting.** Although true implantation bleeding typically occurs much earlier (around 6–10 days after fertilisation), some light spotting can occur in the very early weeks for other reasons related to the pregnancy establishing itself.

**Infection.** A vaginal infection such as bacterial vaginosis or a urinary tract infection can sometimes cause spotting or light bleeding. These are treatable and should be checked by your GP or midwife.

**Threatened miscarriage.** Unfortunately, bleeding at 9 weeks can sometimes be a sign that a miscarriage is threatened or occurring. This is more likely if the bleeding is heavy (similar to or heavier than a period), bright red, and accompanied by cramping or back pain. A scan at the EPU is the only way to know what is happening.

**Missed miscarriage.** In some cases, a pregnancy stops developing without the person knowing. A missed miscarriage may be discovered on a scan rather than through heavy bleeding — though bleeding can occur as the body begins to pass the pregnancy.

## When to Seek Help

**Contact your GP, midwife, or EPU as soon as possible** if you experience any bleeding at 9 weeks. Even light spotting should be reported so they can assess whether a scan is needed.

**Call 111** if:
- The bleeding is heavier than a light spot and you cannot reach your GP or midwife quickly
- You are unsure what to do and want urgent guidance

**Call 999 or go to A&E immediately** if:
- You are soaking through pads rapidly (very heavy bleeding)
- You have severe, sudden abdominal pain — particularly if it is one-sided
- You feel faint, dizzy, or collapse
- You have shoulder tip pain alongside abdominal pain

One-sided pain combined with bleeding can be a symptom of an ectopic pregnancy. Ectopic pregnancies can be life-threatening if untreated — do not wait to be seen.

## What Happens at the EPU

The Early Pregnancy Unit (EPU) is a specialist service within most NHS hospitals that assesses early pregnancy complications. You may be referred there by your GP, midwife, or 111. Some EPUs also accept self-referrals — it is worth checking with your local hospital.

At the EPU, you will usually have an ultrasound scan (often a transvaginal scan for clearest results at this stage) to check the pregnancy. In many cases, a heartbeat is found and the pregnancy is ongoing — though further monitoring may be recommended. If the news is not what you hoped for, the EPU team are experienced in supporting parents through that too.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is light spotting at 9 weeks always a sign of miscarriage?

No. Light spotting at 9 weeks has several possible causes that are not miscarriage, including cervical sensitivity and subchorionic haematoma. That said, all bleeding in early pregnancy should be assessed by a healthcare professional. A scan is the most reliable way to find out what is happening.

### When should I worry about bleeding at 9 weeks?

Any bleeding warrants a call to your GP, midwife, or EPU. Heavy bleeding, bright red blood, cramping, or one-sided pain are signs you need to be seen urgently — call 111 if you cannot reach your healthcare team quickly, or 999 if symptoms are severe.

### Should I go to A&E or the EPU?

For most cases of early pregnancy bleeding, the EPU is the most appropriate place to go, as they specialise in exactly this. If you have severe pain, very heavy bleeding, feel faint, or are concerned about ectopic pregnancy, go to A&E or call 999.

Speak to your midwife or GP for personal advice about your individual situation.
