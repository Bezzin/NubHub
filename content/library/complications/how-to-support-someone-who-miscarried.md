# How to Support Someone Who Has Had a Miscarriage

When someone you care about experiences a miscarriage, it can be hard to know what to say or do. You might worry about saying the wrong thing, or feel helpless. This guide is here to help you be a genuinely supportive presence — not just in the immediate aftermath, but in the weeks and months that follow.

## Understanding What They're Going Through

Miscarriage is the loss of a pregnancy, and it is a real bereavement. The grief that follows is not proportional to how many weeks the pregnancy had lasted, how many people knew about it, or whether the pregnancy was planned. People grieve differently — some feel devastated immediately, others feel numb at first, and grief can resurface unexpectedly at due dates, anniversaries, or when they see other people's babies.

Partners, family members, and close friends can also grieve the loss. In some relationships, partners may feel they need to stay strong for the person who carried the pregnancy, and can end up without much support themselves. It is worth checking in on everyone affected.

## What to Say

When you don't know what to say, keeping it simple is almost always the right approach. What matters is that you acknowledge the loss rather than glossing over it. Phrases that tend to feel supportive include:

- "I'm so sorry for your loss."
- "I'm here for you — there's no pressure to talk if you don't want to."
- "Take all the time you need."
- "Your feelings are completely valid."
- "I'm thinking of you."
- "Would it help to talk, or would you rather I just sit with you?"
- "I don't know what to say, but I want you to know I care about you."

Sometimes the most powerful thing you can do is simply sit with someone in their pain without trying to fix or minimise it.

## What NOT to Say

Well-meaning comments can inadvertently cause a lot of hurt. Even when said with the best intentions, the following phrases are best avoided:

- "At least you can get pregnant" — this dismisses the specific loss of this specific pregnancy.
- "It wasn't meant to be" — this can feel dismissive and implies there was a reason, when often there isn't one that helps.
- "You can always try again" — grief for this pregnancy is not solved by a future pregnancy.
- "At least it was early" — the length of the pregnancy does not determine the depth of the grief.
- "Everything happens for a reason" — there is rarely a reason that brings comfort in this context.
- "Maybe it's for the best" — this can feel deeply hurtful.
- "I know how you feel" — even if you have experienced a miscarriage yourself, everyone's experience is different.

If you have said something from this list before reading this, don't be hard on yourself. Most of these comments come from a genuine desire to comfort — it's just that they rarely land in the way they're intended.

## Practical Support

Beyond words, practical help can make a real difference, particularly in the days immediately after a miscarriage when the person may be physically exhausted or in pain as well as grieving. Consider:

- **Dropping off food** — a ready meal, some groceries, or a home-cooked dish removes the burden of cooking.
- **Helping with household tasks** — cleaning, laundry, or childcare if they have older children.
- **Keeping in touch regularly** — send a message every few days, not just once. Many people receive a wave of support immediately and then feel forgotten as time goes on.
- **Remembering key dates** — the due date can be particularly painful. A simple message on that day saying "I'm thinking of you" can mean a great deal.
- **Not expecting them to be "over it"** — there is no timeline for grief. Some people feel better after a few weeks; for others it takes much longer.

## When to Suggest Professional Support

If the person you're supporting seems to be struggling significantly — for example, if they are unable to function day-to-day, not eating, or expressing feelings of hopelessness — it may be worth gently suggesting they speak to their GP. Pregnancy loss can lead to depression, anxiety, and complicated grief, all of which benefit from professional support.

The Miscarriage Association (miscarriageassociation.org.uk) offers a helpline, peer support, and a range of resources for anyone affected by pregnancy loss, including partners, family members, and friends.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What should I say to someone who has just miscarried?

Keep it simple and sincere: "I'm so sorry, I'm here for you." Avoid the urge to find a silver lining or a reason. Listen more than you talk, and let them lead the conversation. Your presence and willingness to acknowledge their loss matters far more than finding the perfect words.

### How long should I check in after a miscarriage?

Don't just check in once. Support tends to arrive in a rush immediately after the loss and then drops off quickly. Try to keep checking in for weeks and months afterwards — a quick message costs very little but can mean a great deal. Remember the due date; it is often a particularly difficult day for people who have lost a pregnancy.

### Should I mention the baby or pregnancy by name?

Follow the person's lead. Some people name the baby they have lost and find it comforting when others acknowledge that name. Others prefer not to. If you're unsure, it's fine to ask gently — "Is there anything you'd like me to call the baby, or would you rather I didn't?" — and then do whatever they prefer.
