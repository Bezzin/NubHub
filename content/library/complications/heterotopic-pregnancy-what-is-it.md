# Heterotopic Pregnancy: What Is It?

A heterotopic pregnancy is a rare but serious condition in which two pregnancies exist simultaneously — one inside the uterus (intrauterine) and one outside it (ectopic), most commonly in a fallopian tube. Because there is a visible pregnancy in the uterus, the ectopic component can easily be missed, making early recognition and treatment critically important.

## What Is a Heterotopic Pregnancy?

In a normal pregnancy, a single fertilised egg implants inside the uterus. In an ectopic pregnancy, a fertilised egg implants somewhere outside the uterus — usually in a fallopian tube. A heterotopic pregnancy is when both happen at the same time: one embryo implants in the uterus and a second, separate embryo implants elsewhere (usually a fallopian tube).

The intrauterine pregnancy may be perfectly healthy. The ectopic pregnancy, however, carries the same risks as any ectopic: the tube cannot expand to accommodate a growing pregnancy, and if left untreated it can rupture, causing internal bleeding that is a medical emergency.

## How Common Is It?

Heterotopic pregnancy is extremely rare in women who conceive naturally. It occurs more frequently in women who have undergone assisted reproduction such as IVF, because multiple embryos may be transferred simultaneously and the drugs used to stimulate ovulation increase the chance of more than one egg being fertilised and implanting.

The risk is higher in women with:
- A history of pelvic inflammatory disease or fallopian tube damage
- Previous ectopic pregnancy
- IVF or other assisted conception

## Symptoms to Watch For

The tricky aspect of heterotopic pregnancy is that knowing there is a pregnancy in the uterus can create a false sense of reassurance. Symptoms of the ectopic component can be dismissed as normal pregnancy discomfort.

Signs that warrant urgent assessment include:
- Persistent or worsening one-sided abdominal or pelvic pain
- Shoulder tip pain (caused by internal bleeding irritating the diaphragm)
- Vaginal bleeding, particularly alongside pain
- Dizziness, faintness, or collapse

**If you experience severe one-sided pain with dizziness or shoulder tip pain, call 999 or go to A&E immediately.** These can indicate a ruptured ectopic, which is a life-threatening emergency regardless of whether you have a confirmed pregnancy in the uterus.

## Diagnosis and Treatment

Heterotopic pregnancy is diagnosed by ultrasound. If you have had a positive pregnancy test, a confirmed intrauterine pregnancy on scan, and ongoing pain or bleeding, an experienced sonographer should specifically look for a second gestational sac elsewhere. This is particularly important for women who have had IVF.

Treatment usually involves surgery to remove the ectopic pregnancy while leaving the intrauterine pregnancy undisturbed where possible. Medical treatment with methotrexate (which is used for ectopic pregnancy) is generally not suitable when there is also a viable intrauterine pregnancy, as it could harm that pregnancy.

The outcome for the intrauterine pregnancy depends on how early the heterotopic is diagnosed and treated. Early detection significantly improves the chances of the uterine pregnancy continuing successfully.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How common is heterotopic pregnancy?

Heterotopic pregnancy is very rare in women who conceive naturally. The risk is higher for women who have undergone IVF or other assisted conception because multiple embryos may be involved. Women with a history of fallopian tube damage or previous ectopic pregnancy are also at higher risk. If you are undergoing IVF and experience pain alongside a confirmed intrauterine pregnancy, ask your clinical team to specifically exclude a heterotopic pregnancy.

### Can you have a normal pregnancy and an ectopic at the same time?

Yes — that is precisely what a heterotopic pregnancy is. One embryo implants normally inside the uterus while a second implants outside it. The intrauterine pregnancy may continue to develop normally even after the ectopic is treated, particularly if it is caught early. However, the ectopic component must be treated urgently to prevent rupture and serious internal bleeding.

### What should I do if I think I might have a heterotopic pregnancy?

If you have a confirmed intrauterine pregnancy but are experiencing persistent one-sided pain, unusual bleeding, or feeling faint, seek medical assessment the same day. Contact your Early Pregnancy Unit (EPU), your midwife, or call 111. If symptoms are severe — particularly sudden sharp pain with dizziness or shoulder tip pain — call 999 or go straight to A&E.

Always speak to your midwife or GP if you have concerns about symptoms in early pregnancy.
