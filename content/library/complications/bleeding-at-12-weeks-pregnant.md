# Bleeding at 12 Weeks Pregnant

Noticing any bleeding during pregnancy is frightening, and at 12 weeks — often the week of your dating scan — it can feel especially alarming. The good news is that bleeding at this stage has a range of possible causes, many of which are benign. However, all bleeding in pregnancy should be assessed by a healthcare professional so the cause can be identified and you can be properly supported.

## Common Causes of Bleeding at 12 Weeks {#section-1}

Bleeding at 12 weeks can come from several sources. The most important distinction is between bleeding that originates from the cervix or vaginal area versus bleeding that comes from inside the uterus.

**Cervical sensitivity** is one of the most common reasons for light bleeding at this stage. The cervix becomes much more sensitive and richly supplied with blood vessels during pregnancy. This means it can bleed easily after sexual intercourse, an internal examination, or a transvaginal scan — all without any harm to the baby. If you have just had your dating scan and notice a small amount of spotting or pink discharge afterwards, this is likely the cause.

**Subchorionic haematoma** is a small collection of blood between the uterine wall and the edge of the gestational sac. It can cause spotting or light bleeding and is often discovered incidentally on scan. Many resolve on their own without any treatment needed, though your midwife or doctor will monitor the situation.

**Implantation-related bleeding** can occasionally occur slightly later than expected in some pregnancies, though this is less common at 12 weeks than in the very early weeks.

**Miscarriage** is a possibility that no one wants to consider, but it is important to know that bleeding alone does not confirm miscarriage. Many pregnancies with early bleeding continue without any problem. A scan is the only way to assess the situation properly.

**Ectopic pregnancy** is unlikely at 12 weeks — most ectopic pregnancies are identified earlier — but severe one-sided pain alongside bleeding is always a reason to seek urgent help.

## When to Worry and What to Do {#section-2}

Any bleeding in pregnancy should be assessed, but the urgency depends on the amount and your other symptoms.

**Contact your midwife, GP, or Early Pregnancy Unit (EPU) promptly if:**
- You notice any fresh red bleeding, even if it seems light
- Bleeding follows an internal examination or scan
- You have spotting with no obvious cause
- The bleeding is brown (old blood) and you are unsure why

**Call 999 or go to A&E immediately if:**
- You are experiencing heavy bleeding — soaking a pad or passing clots
- You have severe abdominal pain or one-sided pain
- You feel dizzy, faint, or unwell alongside bleeding

At the EPU, the usual approach is to perform an ultrasound scan to check the baby's heartbeat, assess the position of the placenta, and look for any visible source of bleeding. Most women are reassured by seeing a normal heartbeat and are advised to rest and monitor symptoms. Follow-up appointments may be arranged depending on what the scan shows.

## What Happens at the EPU?

If you are referred to or self-refer to an Early Pregnancy Unit, you can expect:

- A brief consultation to understand your symptoms
- An ultrasound scan, which may be transvaginal for the clearest view
- An explanation of findings and a plan for next steps
- A contact number to call if symptoms worsen before your next appointment

You do not need to wait for a referral from your GP to attend most EPUs — many accept self-referrals. If you are unsure, call 111 and they can advise you on the quickest route to assessment.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is bleeding at 12 weeks always serious?

Not always. Light spotting at 12 weeks — particularly after a scan or intercourse — is often caused by cervical sensitivity and does not indicate a problem with the baby. However, all bleeding should be assessed by a healthcare professional to identify the cause and rule out complications.

### What does it mean if I bleed after my dating scan?

A small amount of pink spotting or brown discharge after a transvaginal scan is very common and usually caused by mild irritation of the sensitive cervix. It typically settles within 24 hours. If it is heavy, bright red, or accompanied by pain, contact your EPU or midwife.

### Should I go to A&E or the EPU?

For mild or moderate bleeding without severe pain, the EPU is usually the most appropriate place to be seen. For heavy bleeding, severe pain, dizziness, or feeling very unwell, call 999 or go to A&E without delay.
