# Coping While Waiting for a Scan After Bleeding

The gap between reporting bleeding and having a scan can feel like the longest days of your life. Whether you are waiting 48 hours or two weeks, the uncertainty is emotionally exhausting. This page offers practical ways to manage the wait and look after your mental health.

## Managing the Anxiety Day by Day

Try to stay occupied with gentle, absorbing activities that do not require intense concentration: a box set, a jigsaw, walking, or catching up with a supportive friend. Set specific times to check for symptoms rather than monitoring constantly, as hyper-vigilance feeds anxiety. Limit online searching, as forums can be reassuring but also triggering. If you need information, use trusted sources like the NHS or Miscarriage Association, and step away if it spirals.

## Practical Self-Care and Support

Keep a simple symptom diary noting the colour and amount of bleeding and any pain, which is useful both for your own peace of mind and for the clinician at your scan. Eat regularly even if you have no appetite, stay hydrated, and accept offers of help with cooking or childcare. If you feel you cannot cope, contact your GP or the Miscarriage Association helpline: reaching out is a strength, not a weakness.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### 1. Should I stay in bed while waiting for my scan?

No, bed rest does not prevent miscarriage. Gentle normal activity is fine unless you have been advised otherwise.

### 2. Is it safe to work while waiting?

Yes, if you feel able. If bleeding is heavy or you are in pain, call in sick and contact your unit.

### 3. Where can I get emotional support right now?

The Miscarriage Association helpline and Tommy's pregnancy support line are excellent UK-based resources.
