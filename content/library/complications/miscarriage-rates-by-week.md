# Miscarriage Rates by Week: What the Statistics Say

This article explores understand miscarriage rates by week of pregnancy, including how risk falls after each milestone and what the numbers really mean for you. We cover what you need to know in plain language.

## Understanding Miscarriage Rates {#section-1}

Miscarriage is the most common complication of early pregnancy, yet the statistics can be both reassuring and confusing. Overall, roughly 10 to 20 percent of known pregnancies end in miscarriage, but this headline figure hides a steep decline in risk as pregnancy progresses. At six weeks, the chance of miscarriage for a pregnancy with a confirmed heartbeat may be around 10 percent. By eight weeks that figure drops to roughly 3 to 5 percent, and by the time you reach twelve weeks with a healthy heartbeat visible on scan, the risk falls to around 1 to 2 percent. These figures come from large cohort studies and are averages: your individual risk depends on age, medical history, and whether you have had previous losses. It is important to remember that most miscarriages happen because of chromosomal problems in the embryo, not because of anything you did or did not do. Statistics are useful for context, but they cannot predict your outcome. Use them as a guide, not a verdict.

## Miscarriage Risk by Week {#section-2}

Week by week, the pattern is broadly as follows. Before six weeks, miscarriage rates are highest and many losses occur before a pregnancy is even recognised. Between six and seven weeks, with a confirmed heartbeat, the risk is roughly 5 to 10 percent. From eight to ten weeks it falls to about 2 to 5 percent. By eleven to twelve weeks, once a scan confirms a normal heartbeat and growth, the risk is around 1 to 2 percent. After twelve weeks, the risk drops below 1 percent and a late miscarriage becomes uncommon. Age matters: women under 35 have a lower baseline risk, while those over 35, and especially over 40, see a higher chance of loss at every stage. Previous miscarriages also raise baseline risk, but even after three losses, many women go on to have a successful pregnancy. If you are worried about your own risk, speak to your midwife or GP, who can take your full history into account rather than relying on averages alone.

## FAQ {#faq}

### What week is miscarriage most common?

Miscarriage is most common in the first six weeks of pregnancy. Many losses happen before a woman even knows she is pregnant. Once a heartbeat is confirmed on scan, the risk falls sharply each week.

### Does the risk drop after a heartbeat is seen?

Yes. After a confirmed heartbeat at six to seven weeks, miscarriage risk drops to around 5 percent, and continues to fall each week. By twelve weeks with a normal scan, the risk is about 1 to 2 percent.

### Do miscarriage rates differ by age?

Yes. Women under 35 have lower risk, while those over 35, and especially over 40, face higher risk at every stage of early pregnancy. This reflects egg quality and chromosomal factors.
