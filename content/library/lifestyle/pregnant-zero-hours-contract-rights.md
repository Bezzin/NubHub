# Pregnant on a Zero-Hours Contract: Your Rights

Being on a zero-hours contract doesn't mean you have no rights when pregnant. In the UK, many of the same protections that apply to permanent employees also apply to you — though the details around maternity pay can differ depending on your earnings and how long you have worked for your employer.

## Your Core Employment Rights {#section-1}

Zero-hours workers are classed as "workers" (and in many cases "employees") under UK employment law, which means you have legal protections from the moment you tell your employer you are pregnant. These include:

**Protection from discrimination.** Pregnancy is a protected characteristic under the Equality Act 2010. Your employer cannot treat you unfavourably because you are pregnant — this includes reducing your hours, passing you over for shifts, or dismissing you.

**The right to a risk assessment.** Your employer must carry out a maternity risk assessment of your working conditions once they know you are pregnant. If there are risks they cannot remove, they must offer you suitable alternative work or, if that is not possible, suspend you on full pay.

**The right to antenatal appointments.** You are entitled to paid time off for antenatal appointments, including midwife visits and scans. You do not need to make this time up.

**The right to maternity leave.** If you have been employed for at least 26 weeks by the qualifying week (the 15th week before your due date), you are entitled to up to 52 weeks of maternity leave. Zero-hours status does not remove this right.

## Maternity Pay on a Zero-Hours Contract {#section-2}

This is where things get more complicated, because your entitlement depends on your earnings and length of service.

**Statutory Maternity Pay (SMP).** To qualify for SMP, you need to have worked for the same employer for at least 26 weeks by the end of the qualifying week, and your average weekly earnings must be at least £123 (2024/25 figure — check the current threshold with GOV.UK). If you qualify, SMP is paid for up to 39 weeks at a rate of 90% of your average earnings for the first six weeks, then a flat weekly rate (currently £184.03 per week) for the remaining 33 weeks.

Because zero-hours contracts often mean variable earnings, your average pay is calculated over an eight-week reference period. If your hours happened to be lower during that period, your SMP entitlement may be affected.

**Maternity Allowance.** If you do not qualify for SMP — because your earnings were too low or you have not worked for the same employer long enough — you may still qualify for Maternity Allowance through the government. You need to have been employed or self-employed for at least 26 weeks in the 66 weeks before your due date, with average weekly earnings of at least £30. Maternity Allowance is claimed through your local Jobcentre Plus rather than through your employer.

## If Your Employer Reduces or Removes Your Shifts {#section-3}

This is a situation that can arise on zero-hours contracts, and it is important to know that it is not acceptable if it is connected to your pregnancy. If you believe your employer has reduced your hours or stopped calling you in because you are pregnant, this is likely to amount to pregnancy discrimination under the Equality Act 2010.

You should keep a record of your shifts before and after you told your employer about your pregnancy. If you believe you have been treated unfairly, you can:

- Raise a grievance directly with your employer in the first instance.
- Contact ACAS (Advisory, Conciliation and Arbitration Service) on 0300 123 1100 for free, confidential advice.
- Make a claim to an employment tribunal. There is no qualifying period of service needed to claim pregnancy discrimination.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Can I get maternity pay on a zero-hours contract?

Yes, if you meet the qualifying conditions. You need to have worked for the same employer for at least 26 weeks by the 15th week before your due date, and your average weekly earnings must meet the minimum threshold. If you do not meet these conditions for Statutory Maternity Pay, you may still be eligible for Maternity Allowance — contact Jobcentre Plus to check and claim.

### Can my employer stop giving me shifts because I am pregnant?

No. Withdrawing or reducing shifts because of your pregnancy is pregnancy discrimination under the Equality Act 2010, and it is unlawful regardless of your contract type. If this happens to you, keep a written record of the changes and seek advice from ACAS. You can bring an employment tribunal claim for pregnancy discrimination without any minimum length of service.

### Do I have to tell my employer I am pregnant?

You are not legally required to disclose your pregnancy, but you must inform your employer at least 15 weeks before your due date if you want to exercise your maternity leave rights. Telling your employer earlier also means you can access the protections around risk assessments and antenatal appointments sooner.
