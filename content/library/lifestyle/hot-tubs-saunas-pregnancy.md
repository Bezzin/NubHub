## Why Heat Matters in Pregnancy {#section-1}

During pregnancy your core body temperature needs to stay within a safe range. Prolonged exposure to high heat, particularly in hot tubs, saunas, and steam rooms, can raise your core temperature significantly. This matters especially in the first trimester, when your baby's neural tube is forming. Research suggests that prolonged hyperthermia in early pregnancy may be associated with an increased risk of neural tube defects, which is why most UK health professionals advise caution.

## Hot Tubs: What the Guidance Says {#section-2}

The NHS advises avoiding hot tub use during pregnancy, particularly in the first 12 weeks. Hot tubs typically maintain water temperatures of 38-40 degrees Celsius, which can raise your body temperature quickly. Unlike a warm bath, which cools as you sit in it, a hot tub stays consistently hot, meaning your core temperature continues to rise. If you used a hot tub before you knew you were pregnant, try not to panic. The risk relates to prolonged and repeated exposure. Speak to your midwife or GP for reassurance.

## Saunas and Steam Rooms {#section-3}

Saunas and steam rooms pose similar concerns because they raise body temperature through sustained ambient heat. Finnish saunas can reach 70-90 degrees Celsius, and steam rooms sit at around 40-45 degrees with high humidity. The combination makes it harder for your body to cool itself through sweating. Most UK guidance recommends avoiding saunas and steam rooms throughout pregnancy, particularly during the first trimester. If you visit a spa, ask staff about pregnancy-friendly alternatives such as warm relaxation rooms.

## Safer Ways to Relax {#section-4}

If you enjoy warmth for relaxation, there are pregnancy-safe options. A warm bath at home, kept below 38 degrees Celsius, is generally considered safe and can ease aches and tension. Keep baths relatively short, around 10-15 minutes, and step out if you feel dizzy or overheated. Swimming in a heated pool is also safe and offers gentle exercise. Prenatal yoga, massage from a qualified pregnancy therapist, and simply resting with a warm (not hot) water bottle can all help you unwind without overheating.

## FAQ {#faq}

### Can I use a hot tub in early pregnancy?

The NHS advises against hot tub use in the first trimester due to the risk of raising your core body temperature. If you used one before knowing you were pregnant, contact your midwife for reassurance.

### Are saunas safe in the second trimester?

Most guidance recommends avoiding saunas throughout pregnancy. Even later on, sustained heat can cause dizziness, dehydration, and low blood pressure, which can affect both you and your baby.

### Is a warm bath safe during pregnancy?

Yes, a warm bath below 38 degrees Celsius is generally safe. Keep it to 10-15 minutes and leave the room if it feels too hot or you feel lightheaded.
