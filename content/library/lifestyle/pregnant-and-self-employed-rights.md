# Pregnant and Self-Employed: Your Rights

If you're self-employed and pregnant, you have different rights and options compared to employed women. You won't qualify for Statutory Maternity Pay, but there is still meaningful financial support available — and knowing what you're entitled to early on gives you time to plan properly.

## Maternity Allowance {#maternity-allowance}

Maternity Allowance (MA) is the main form of maternity pay for self-employed women. It is paid by the government (not your employer) and is available if you have been self-employed and paying National Insurance for at least 26 weeks in the 66 weeks before your baby is due.

The standard rate is set by the government each tax year — check GOV.UK for the current figure, as it is uprated annually. If your earnings are lower than the standard rate, you may receive 90% of your average weekly earnings instead. MA is paid for up to 39 weeks and you can choose when to start it, from as early as 11 weeks before your due date.

To claim, you need to complete form MA1, which you can get from GOV.UK or your local Jobcentre Plus. You will need proof of self-employment (such as self-assessment tax returns or letters from clients) and evidence of your NI record. Your midwife will give you a MATB1 certificate around 20 weeks, which you'll need to include with your claim.

## Antenatal Appointments

Self-employed women do not have the same statutory right to paid time off for antenatal appointments as employees do. However, you can structure your appointments around your working schedule where possible, and your appointments are just as important. Midwife and GP antenatal care through the NHS is free regardless of your employment status.

## Free Healthcare During Pregnancy

All pregnant women in the UK are entitled to free NHS maternity care. You also qualify for free prescriptions and free NHS dental treatment during pregnancy and for 12 months after your baby is born. Apply for a Maternity Exemption Certificate (MATEX) through your midwife or GP — this covers you even if you are self-employed with no employer.

## Other Financial Support {#other-support}

Depending on your income, you may also be entitled to:

- **Universal Credit** — if your income is low, you may qualify during or after pregnancy. Report your pregnancy to the DWP and they may apply a work allowance adjustment.
- **Healthy Start vouchers** — if you're more than 10 weeks pregnant and on certain benefits, you can get vouchers for fruit, vegetables, and milk.
- **Sure Start Maternity Grant** — a one-off payment available if you're on qualifying benefits and having your first child (or multiple babies). Claim within 11 weeks of the birth.
- **Child Benefit** — available to all parents after the baby is born, regardless of employment status.
- **Free childcare hours** — from the term after your child turns 9 months old, working parents (including the self-employed) may be eligible for funded childcare hours. Check your eligibility via GOV.UK's childcare calculator.

## Planning Your Finances

Because there is no employer covering a portion of your maternity period, financial planning matters more when you're self-employed. Consider:

- Setting aside savings during pregnancy to supplement Maternity Allowance
- Reviewing your contracts and notifying key clients of your expected leave dates in advance
- Checking whether you have any income protection or self-employed insurance that covers maternity
- Speaking to an accountant about how maternity leave affects your tax return

There is no legal requirement for self-employed women to stop working at any point during pregnancy, and no minimum or maximum period of leave. Your leave is entirely your own choice — but factor in recovery time after the birth when planning.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Can I get maternity pay if I'm self-employed?

Yes, but not Statutory Maternity Pay (SMP) — that's only for employees. Self-employed women can claim Maternity Allowance instead, paid by the government for up to 39 weeks. You need to have been self-employed and paying National Insurance for at least 26 of the 66 weeks before your due date. Check GOV.UK for the current weekly rate and claim form (MA1).

### Do I have to stop working when pregnant and self-employed?

No. There is no legal requirement to stop working at any point during pregnancy if you are self-employed. Many self-employed women continue working right up until their due date. However, it is worth planning your workload realistically — fatigue, appointments, and recovery after birth all take time. You decide when to start and end your Maternity Allowance period within the permitted window.

### What if I haven't paid enough National Insurance to qualify for Maternity Allowance?

If you don't qualify for Maternity Allowance (for example, because you've only recently become self-employed), you may still be able to claim other support such as Universal Credit or the Sure Start Maternity Grant if your income is low. Check your eligibility on GOV.UK or speak to a benefits adviser at Citizens Advice.

Always speak to your midwife, GP, or a financial adviser for advice tailored to your personal circumstances.
