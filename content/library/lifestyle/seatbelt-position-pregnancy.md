# Seatbelt Position in Pregnancy: Safety Guide

Wearing a seatbelt correctly during pregnancy is essential for your safety and your baby's.

Many pregnant women worry that a seatbelt might harm their baby in an accident — but the opposite is true. Wearing your seatbelt correctly is one of the most important things you can do to keep both yourself and your baby safe when travelling by car. You are legally required to wear one throughout your pregnancy, and the law makes no exception for being pregnant.

## Correct Position {#s1}

The key is knowing exactly where each part of the belt should sit. The rules are straightforward and consistent throughout pregnancy:

**Lap belt:** The lap portion of the belt must go **under your bump**, lying flat across your upper thighs and hips — never across your abdomen or over your bump. If the lap belt sits over your bump, the force of any sudden braking or collision is directed straight at your uterus.

**Diagonal (shoulder) belt:** The diagonal strap should go **between your breasts** and over your shoulder, then diagonally across your chest and to one side. It should never be placed under your arm or behind your back — doing either dramatically reduces the belt's effectiveness and increases the risk of chest and abdominal injury.

Both parts of the belt work together. If either is positioned incorrectly, your protection — and your baby's — is compromised.

## Adjusting as Your Bump Grows {#s2}

As your pregnancy progresses, the correct positioning becomes more important to maintain because your bump grows in exactly the area where an incorrectly positioned belt would rest. A few practical adjustments help:

- **Move your seat back** as your bump grows so you have more space between yourself and the steering wheel. The airbag should not be directly in front of your bump.
- **Adjust the seat height and angle.** Sitting upright (or close to it) makes correct belt positioning easier and more stable.
- **Check the belt each journey.** It can shift during short trips, particularly the lap portion. Take a moment before setting off to confirm both sections are sitting correctly.
- **Use a seatbelt adjuster or clip if helpful.** These small devices attach to the lap section of the belt and hold it below the bump. They are inexpensive and widely available, though not a substitute for correct positioning.

## Tips for Comfortable Car Travel {#s3}

Long journeys in particular can become uncomfortable as pregnancy progresses. These tips help:

- **Take regular breaks.** On longer trips, stop every hour or ninety minutes, get out, and walk around briefly. This promotes circulation and reduces the risk of deep vein thrombosis (DVT), to which pregnant women are more susceptible.
- **Stay hydrated.** Bring water and drink regularly, even if it means more toilet stops.
- **Use a lumbar pillow or rolled-up blanket** behind your lower back if you are experiencing back pain.
- **Consider being a passenger.** If driving becomes very uncomfortable in late pregnancy, let someone else drive. There is no requirement for you to be behind the wheel.
- **Wear compression stockings** on very long journeys to support circulation in your legs.

If you are involved in a road traffic accident, even a minor one, you should contact your midwife or maternity unit the same day — even if you feel fine. A minor impact can occasionally affect the placenta in ways that are not immediately obvious, and your maternity team will want to monitor you and your baby.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Can a seatbelt harm my baby?

No — and importantly, not wearing one is far more dangerous. In a crash, an unrestrained pregnant woman is at much greater risk of serious injury, which directly puts the baby at risk. A correctly worn seatbelt — lap section under the bump, diagonal strap between the breasts — distributes the force of an impact away from the uterus. The concern that a seatbelt will compress the bump is addressed by the correct positioning: the belt goes under and around the bump, not over it.

### Am I exempt from wearing a seatbelt when pregnant in the UK?

No. Pregnant women are not exempt from the UK legal requirement to wear a seatbelt. Exemption certificates (on medical grounds) do exist but are rarely issued, and pregnancy alone does not qualify. The guidance from the NHS, the Department for Transport, and the Royal College of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists is consistent: always wear a seatbelt, correctly positioned, throughout pregnancy.

### What should I do if I'm in a car accident while pregnant?

Contact your midwife or maternity assessment unit (MAU) straight away — even after a minor bump where you feel completely fine. Any impact can cause placental abruption (where the placenta partially separates from the uterine wall), which may not produce obvious symptoms immediately. Your maternity team will typically want to monitor your baby's heartbeat for a period and may carry out an ultrasound. Do not wait to see if symptoms develop; call on the same day.

Always speak to your midwife or GP if you have any concerns about car travel or safety during your pregnancy.
