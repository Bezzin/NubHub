# Single and Pregnant: Support and Advice

Being pregnant on your own is challenging but absolutely doable. Whether you've separated from a partner, chosen to go it alone, or found yourself in unexpected circumstances, you are far from the first person to navigate pregnancy solo — and there is genuine, practical support available to you.

## Practical Support {#section-1}

Telling your midwife early that you're going through pregnancy without a partner is one of the most useful steps you can take. Midwives work with single parents regularly and can make sure you have a little extra attention and information at appointments. Being open also means your maternity team can flag additional resources you might not know about.

Some other practical steps that help:

- **Ask someone to attend scans with you.** A friend, sibling, or parent can come along for the 12-week and 20-week scans. Having another person there means you're not processing the information alone.
- **Get to know your local children's centre.** Many offer antenatal classes, drop-in sessions, and peer support groups specifically welcoming to single parents.
- **Look into a doula.** A doula is a trained birth supporter who provides continuous, non-clinical support during labour and sometimes the postnatal period. Some doulas work on a reduced fee or volunteer basis through schemes like Doula UK's Access Fund — worth exploring if cost is a concern.
- **Build your practical support network now.** Think about who can be on call for lifts to hospital, who could help in the early weeks, and whether you have someone who can come and stay around your due date.

## Financial Support and Benefits {#section-2}

Money is a genuine concern for many single parents, and it's worth understanding what you're entitled to as early as possible. Benefits and entitlements in the UK include:

- **Statutory Maternity Pay (SMP):** If you've been employed for long enough and earn above the lower earnings limit, your employer must pay SMP for up to 39 weeks.
- **Maternity Allowance:** If you don't qualify for SMP — because you're self-employed, have recently changed jobs, or earn below the threshold — you may qualify for Maternity Allowance through the government.
- **Universal Credit:** Single parents may be entitled to Universal Credit to top up income. Eligibility depends on your circumstances, savings, and housing costs.
- **Sure Start Maternity Grant:** A one-off payment available if you're on certain benefits and expecting your first child (or multiple babies).
- **Healthy Start vouchers:** If you're more than 10 weeks pregnant and on qualifying benefits, you can get vouchers to spend on fruit, vegetables, pulses, and milk.
- **Free NHS prescriptions and dental treatment:** All pregnant women in England receive these automatically during pregnancy and for 12 months after birth.

Citizens Advice and your local Jobcentre Plus are both good starting points for checking what you're entitled to based on your specific situation.

## Emotional Support {#section-3}

The emotional side of single pregnancy is real and shouldn't be minimised. You might be managing grief about a relationship ending, the stress of telling people, fear about doing it alone, or simply the exhaustion of carrying worry without a partner to share it with.

Some things that can genuinely help:

- **Connect with other single mums.** Online communities — including UK-specific forums and Facebook groups for single parents — can be a lifeline. Talking to people who are in the same position is different from talking to people who aren't.
- **Don't feel you have to perform fine.** Midwives and GPs are used to supporting people in complex personal situations. You won't be judged for being honest about how you're feeling.
- **Consider counselling.** Your GP can refer you to talking therapy through the IAPT (Improving Access to Psychological Therapies) programme, which includes support for perinatal mental health. Some areas have specialist perinatal mental health teams.
- **Allow yourself the full range of feelings.** It's possible to feel excited and scared, hopeful and sad, capable and overwhelmed — often in the same afternoon. That's not weakness; it's pregnancy.

## At the Birth {#section-4}

You are entitled to have someone with you during labour and birth. You don't need a partner. You can bring a friend, family member, or doula — whoever you feel most safe and supported with. If you have nobody, talk to your midwife team; they will ensure you are looked after and not alone.

It's worth letting the labour ward know in advance that you'll be attending as a single parent so they can make sure your birth preferences are documented and your support person is expected.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### What benefits can I get as a single pregnant woman?

You may be entitled to Statutory Maternity Pay or Maternity Allowance, Universal Credit, Sure Start Maternity Grant (for first babies and multiple births), Healthy Start vouchers, free prescriptions, and free NHS dental treatment during pregnancy and for 12 months after. Entitlement depends on your employment status, income, and other circumstances. Citizens Advice or your local Jobcentre Plus can give you a full picture.

### Can I have someone at my birth if I'm single?

Yes, absolutely. You can bring any person you choose — a friend, parent, sibling, or doula. The choice is entirely yours. Let your midwife team know in advance who you plan to bring so everything runs smoothly on the day.

### Is it safe to go to appointments alone?

Yes. All routine antenatal appointments are designed to be attended by the pregnant person, and many women attend alone for all or most of them. If you'd like company at a particular appointment — especially scans — ask someone you trust. But you will never be turned away or disadvantaged for coming alone.
