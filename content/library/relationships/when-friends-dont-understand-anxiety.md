# When Friends Don't Understand Your Pregnancy Anxiety

Pregnancy anxiety can be relentless — scan worries, birth fears, obsessive symptom-checking, catastrophic thoughts at 3am. And then a friend says "try not to worry" or "stress isn't good for the baby," and you feel both unheard and scolded. When friends don't get it, the loneliness can be as hard as the anxiety itself.

## Why Friends May Not Understand

Most people's image of pregnancy is the glowing bump, not the 4am panic spiral. If your friends haven't been pregnant, or had straightforward experiences, they may genuinely not know what perinatal anxiety feels like. Their dismissiveness is often ignorance, not cruelty — though it still hurts.

Perinatal anxiety is a recognised clinical condition, not simply being a worrier. It can involve intrusive thoughts, physical symptoms like a racing heart, difficulty concentrating, and a constant sense that something is about to go wrong. Without lived experience of this, a well-meaning friend has little frame of reference. They reach for the simplest thing they can think of — "try to relax" — because they want to help and don't know how else to respond.

Understanding that gap doesn't make it hurt less, but it can make it easier to decide how to respond. Most friends are not trying to dismiss you. They just don't have the right tools yet.

## How to Bridge the Gap

There are practical ways to help friends understand and support you better:

- **Be specific about what helps.** "I don't need reassurance that everything's fine — I need someone to sit with me while I feel scared." This tells them exactly what you want, rather than leaving them to guess.
- **Name the condition.** "I've got perinatal anxiety. It's a recognised thing. Logic doesn't switch it off." Giving it a name helps people take it seriously rather than treating it as a mood that can be talked away.
- **Ask for what you don't want.** "Please don't tell me to relax — it makes it worse. Can we just talk about something normal?" Being direct is kinder than leaving a friend to keep making the same mistake.
- **Share an article.** Sometimes a third-party explanation lands better than your own words, particularly if you feel too exhausted or emotional to explain again. Pointing a friend to a page about perinatal anxiety can shift their understanding faster than a conversation.
- **Choose the right moment.** Trying to explain your anxiety mid-panic is rarely productive. A calmer moment, perhaps over a walk or a cup of tea, makes it easier for a friend to really hear you.

Not every friendship will bear the weight of this conversation, and that is all right. You get to choose how much you share and with whom.

## Finding Friends Who Do Get It

Not every friend will be your pregnancy support person, and expecting that of everyone puts too much pressure on you and on them. You may find your best sources of genuine understanding in:

- **Antenatal classes** — meeting others at the same stage who are also navigating fear and uncertainty
- **Perinatal mental health communities** — including NHS-run groups and charity-led peer support
- **Online forums** — places where people are living the same experience in real time, without the need to explain yourself from scratch
- **Pregnancy apps with community features** — which can connect you with others at the same number of weeks

Expanding your circle of support is not a betrayal of existing friendships. It is practical self-care, and most people find that having even one person who truly gets it makes the anxiety easier to carry.

## Protecting Friendships You Value

If a friend is trying but missing the mark, it is worth giving them another chance with clearer guidance. Something like: "When I say I'm anxious, I'm not asking you to fix it. I just want to feel heard." Most genuinely caring people will adjust when they understand what is actually needed.

Consider also that friendships change during pregnancy. Priorities shift, capacity changes, and relationships that feel easy before pregnancy may feel harder now. That does not mean the friendship is over — it may just need to adapt. Some friendships deepen through honest conversation about this kind of difficulty.

## When to Step Back from a Friendship

If a friend repeatedly dismisses your anxiety, makes jokes about it, or leaves you feeling worse after contact, it is reasonable to limit the time you spend with them during pregnancy. You do not need to end the friendship or explain yourself in detail. A simple "I'm needing quieter weekends at the moment" creates space without drama.

Your emotional wellbeing during pregnancy matters. Protecting it is not antisocial — it is sensible.

## Looking After Yourself Beyond Friendship

Untreated perinatal anxiety can persist into postnatal life, affecting your experience of new parenthood as well as pregnancy. If your anxiety is affecting your sleep, appetite, ability to function day to day, or your relationship with your partner, it is worth talking to your midwife or GP. You do not need to be in crisis to ask for help.

NHS talking therapies, including CBT (cognitive behavioural therapy) and counselling, are available during pregnancy and are effective for anxiety. In some cases, medication may be recommended; certain options are considered safe in pregnancy and your GP or midwife can advise you on this. Asking for professional support is not a sign that you cannot cope — it is a sign that you are taking your health seriously.

## A Reminder

Your anxiety is real, even when others cannot see it. The right support exists — you may just need to look in different places to find it. A midwife who listens, a GP who takes you seriously, one friend who simply sits with you without trying to fix anything: these things make a real difference.

Speak to your midwife or GP if your anxiety is affecting your daily life — they can connect you with the right support.
