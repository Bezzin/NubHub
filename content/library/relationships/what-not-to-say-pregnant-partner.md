# What NOT to Say to Your Pregnant Partner

Pregnancy can make communication tricky, and even the most well-meaning comments can land badly. Here is a guide to the phrases to avoid and what to say instead to keep your connection strong during this sensitive time.

## Phrases to Avoid {#section-1}

Communication can be tricky during pregnancy, and some well-meaning comments land badly when hormones are high. Avoid saying, "Are you sure you should be eating that?" which sounds like judgement rather than concern. Steer clear of, "You are being hormonal," which dismisses her feelings as irrational. Never say, "It is probably just in your head," especially when she is describing a real symptom or worry.

Other phrases to avoid include, "My mum never had morning sickness," which invites comparison and guilt, and, "You will snap back after the baby," which puts pressure on her body before she has even given birth. Even seemingly innocent comments like, "You look tired," can sting when she is already feeling rough. The goal is not to walk on eggshells but to communicate with empathy and awareness.

## What to Say Instead {#section-2}

Instead of criticising or comparing, try phrases that validate and support. Say, "How are you feeling today?" to show genuine interest. If she is struggling with nausea, try, "Can I get you anything?" or, "Let me handle dinner tonight." When she expresses anxiety, respond with, "That sounds really worrying, do you want to talk about it?" rather than brushing it off.

Compliment her sincerely and often. Say, "You are doing an amazing job growing our baby," or, "I am so proud of how you are handling this." When in doubt, simply ask, "How can I help?" This puts her in control and shows you are ready to support her on her terms. Thoughtful communication builds trust and helps you both navigate pregnancy as a team.

## FAQ {#faq}

### Is it okay to comment on her body during pregnancy?

Keep comments positive and avoid anything about size or weight. If unsure, simply ask how she is feeling rather than commenting on appearance.

### What if I accidentally say the wrong thing?

Apologise sincerely and ask how you can do better. Pregnancy is a learning curve for both of you, and honest communication helps you recover from slip-ups.

### Should I bring up worries about the pregnancy?

Yes, but gently. Share your concerns at a calm moment and frame them as shared worries rather than problems she needs to fix. This keeps you both on the same side.
