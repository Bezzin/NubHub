# When Your Partner Doesn't Understand Your Pregnancy Anxiety

Pregnancy anxiety is real, common, and often intense — and it can feel especially isolating when the person closest to you doesn't seem to get it. Your partner might be loving and well-meaning, but still manage to say the wrong thing, brush off your worries, or simply go quiet when you need them most. This doesn't have to mean the end of the conversation. It usually means finding a different way to have it.

## Why Partners Struggle to Understand {#section-1}

It's worth approaching this with some compassion — not because your partner's response is acceptable, but because understanding why it happens makes it easier to address.

Partners who aren't pregnant often don't have the same physical relationship with the pregnancy. The hormonal changes, the bodily sensations, the constant awareness that something enormous is happening inside you — they're not feeling any of that. For them, much of the early pregnancy is invisible and abstract. That gap in experience can translate into a gap in understanding.

There's also the problem of how anxiety looks from the outside. To someone who doesn't experience it, anxiety can look like excessive worrying, catastrophising, or even irrationality. A partner who responds with "everything will be fine" genuinely believes that's helpful — they're trying to reassure you. What they don't realise is that reassurance without acknowledgement often lands as dismissal.

Some partners also feel helpless. They want to fix the problem, and when they can't, they withdraw. Others are managing their own anxiety about the pregnancy and find it easier to minimise yours than to sit with the uncertainty.

None of this makes the dismissal okay. But naming the mechanism helps you respond to it rather than just react to it.

## What Pregnancy Anxiety Actually Feels Like {#section-2}

Pregnancy anxiety isn't "just worrying". For many women it involves persistent, intrusive thoughts — often about miscarriage, birth, the baby's health, or your own capacity to cope — that feel very difficult to interrupt. It can involve physical symptoms: a racing heart, tension, nausea that isn't entirely down to morning sickness. It can make it hard to sleep, hard to concentrate, and hard to enjoy a pregnancy that you genuinely want.

Sharing this level of detail with your partner — not just "I'm anxious" but "this is what it actually feels like in my body and my mind" — can shift their understanding significantly. Abstract feelings are harder to empathise with than concrete descriptions.

## How to Communicate More Effectively {#section-3}

Some approaches that tend to work better than others:

- **Use "I feel" rather than "you don't".** "I feel alone when you change the subject" lands differently to "you never listen to me." The former opens a conversation; the latter closes one.
- **Tell them exactly what you need.** Partners often don't know whether you want them to solve the problem, sit with you, hold you, or just listen. Saying "I don't need you to fix this, I just need you to hear me" is specific and actionable in a way that "support me" isn't.
- **Pick the right moment.** Raising difficult emotional conversations when your partner is stressed, distracted, or in the middle of something else rarely goes well. Ask for a dedicated time: "Can we talk tonight? There's something I want to share with you."
- **Invite them in rather than confronting them.** Share an article about perinatal anxiety. Suggest attending a midwife appointment together — hearing a professional validate that anxiety in pregnancy is common and real can do more in five minutes than weeks of conversation at home.
- **Be specific about what dismissal feels like.** If "everything will be fine" makes you feel worse, explain why: "When you say that, I feel like you're not taking what I said seriously." That's information your partner can act on.

## When You Need More Than Your Partner Can Give {#section-4}

Even the most empathetic, well-intentioned partner cannot be your only source of support — and it isn't fair to either of you to expect that. If your anxiety is significant, persistent, or getting in the way of daily life, there are other places to turn.

Your **GP or midwife** can assess perinatal anxiety and refer you to talking therapies or specialist perinatal mental health services if needed. You don't need to reach a crisis point to ask for this.

The **IAPT programme** (Improving Access to Psychological Therapies) offers talking therapy through the NHS, and many areas have perinatal-specific pathways. Ask your GP to refer you.

**PANDAS Foundation** and **Tommy's** both offer support for perinatal mental health, including helplines staffed by people who understand pregnancy anxiety specifically.

Online communities — UK-based forums and groups for anxious pregnant women — can also offer a type of peer understanding that's genuinely different from what a partner can provide.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### How do I explain pregnancy anxiety to my partner?

Be as specific and concrete as possible. Rather than "I feel anxious", try describing what the anxiety actually involves: what thoughts you're having, what it feels like in your body, and what makes it better or worse. Tell them explicitly what kind of support would help — whether that's listening without advising, physical reassurance, or simply not changing the subject. Sharing reputable information about perinatal anxiety can also help a partner who needs to see it acknowledged externally before they can take it seriously.

### What if my partner dismisses my anxiety as silly or overdramatic?

This is hurtful and, if it's a pattern, worth naming directly. Tell your partner that their response makes you feel less safe sharing with them, and that you need to be heard without being judged. If dismissal continues, lean into other support — your midwife, a GP, friends, or a counsellor. You deserve to be heard somewhere, even if you're still working on being heard at home.

### Should we consider couples counselling?

If anxiety and communication difficulties are creating real tension in your relationship, couples counselling during pregnancy is a valid option. It gives both of you a structured space to speak and be heard with a neutral third party. Ask your GP for a referral, or look for a BACP-registered counsellor privately.
