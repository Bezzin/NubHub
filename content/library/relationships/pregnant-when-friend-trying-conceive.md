# Pregnant When a Friend Is Trying to Conceive

You're pregnant. A close friend is trying — month after month, with no success. The dynamic between you has shifted, and you may feel like you're walking through a field of landmines. How do you share your life without causing pain, and how do you handle the guilt that comes with wanting your own happiness?

## The emotional landscape
Your friend may feel genuine joy for you and acute grief for themselves — often simultaneously. They may withdraw, overcompensate, or seem fine and then crumble later. There's no predictable script, because infertility grief is unpredictable.

## How to share news
- **Tell them early and privately.** Don't let them find out via a group announcement.
- **Be direct but gentle.** "I'm pregnant, and I wanted you to hear it from me because I care about you. I know this is complicated."
- **Don't expect a specific reaction.** They may cry, go quiet, or say congratulations flatly. All are OK.
- **Give them an out.** "No need to respond — I just wanted you to know. Take whatever space you need."

## Navigating the friendship
- **Let them set the pace.** If they want to hear about scans, share. If they go quiet, respect it.
- **Don't hide your pregnancy, but don't centre it.** You don't have to pretend you're not expecting, but you also don't have to make every conversation about it.
- **Protect their hard moments.** Baby showers, gender reveals, and birth stories can be excruciating. Invite but make attendance genuinely optional.
- **Don't complain about pregnancy symptoms.** Even legitimate struggles can sting when someone would swap places in a heartbeat.

## Your own feelings
It's OK to feel sad, guilty, or frustrated. You're allowed to want your pregnancy celebrated. Holding space for your friend's pain doesn't require shrinking your own life — just sharing it with care.

## What to avoid
- "You'll be next!" — you don't know that.
- "At least you can focus on yourself / travel / sleep." — not comforting.
- Hiding major news "to protect them" beyond the initial private conversation — it can feel like being excluded from your life.

## A reminder
You can't control their pain, but you can be a safe friend. The fact that you're worrying about this means you already care enough to get it mostly right.
