# 11 Weeks Pregnant: Your Symptoms

At 11 weeks pregnant you are nearly at the end of your first trimester — a real milestone. Your baby is now fully formed in miniature, with all major organs in place and the placenta taking over hormone production. This hormonal shift is often why some symptoms start to ease around now, though every pregnancy is different. Here is what you might experience this week, and what to watch out for.

## Common Symptoms at 11 Weeks {#common-symptoms}

At 11 weeks, you may experience some or none of these symptoms. Every pregnancy is different.

- **Fatigue and tiredness** — still very common as your body works hard, though energy often begins creeping back
- **Nausea and morning sickness** — for many people this starts to ease, but it can persist into the second trimester
- **Breast tenderness** — your breasts may feel heavy, sore, or have visible veins; your bra size may already have changed
- **Frequent urination** — your growing uterus continues to press on the bladder
- **Mood swings** — shifting hormones and tiredness both contribute
- **Bloating and wind** — progesterone slows your digestive system, making bloating and trapped wind common
- **Food cravings or aversions** — these can remain strong throughout the first trimester
- **Heightened sense of smell** — often linked to nausea; may begin to settle soon
- **Mild headaches** — increased blood volume and hormonal changes can trigger them; stay well hydrated
- **Slight bump or thickening** — your uterus is rising out of the pelvis, so some women notice a small, firm bump low down

## Your Baby at 11 Weeks {#your-baby-at-11-weeks}

Your baby is roughly the size of a fig — around 4 to 5 centimetres from head to bottom. Fingers and toes are now separate, tooth buds are forming, and the external genitalia are beginning to develop (though it is still too early to see clearly on a scan). The baby's heart is beating strongly and the kidneys are producing urine. This is also the window during which the nuchal translucency (NT) measurement for the combined screening test can be taken — usually alongside your 12-week dating scan.

## What's Normal at 11 Weeks {#whats-normal}

It is completely normal to have some, all, or none of the symptoms listed above. A sudden improvement in nausea around this time is not a cause for alarm — it often simply reflects the placenta taking over from the corpus luteum. Equally, if your symptoms feel as strong as ever, that is normal too.

Mild, period-like cramps or pulling sensations on one or both sides of your lower abdomen (round ligament pain) are common as your uterus grows. Light spotting after sex can occur because the cervix is more sensitive and blood-rich in pregnancy. Neither of these usually indicates a problem, but always mention them to your midwife.

Your first booking appointment with a community midwife should have happened by now (ideally before ten weeks). If you have not yet had yours, contact your GP surgery or maternity unit to arrange it.

## When to Seek Help {#when-to-worry}

Contact your GP, midwife, or call 111 if you experience:

- Heavy bleeding (soaking a pad) or passing clots
- Severe, one-sided abdominal pain (which can indicate an ectopic pregnancy — call 999 or go to A&E urgently if suspected)
- Fever above 38°C
- Inability to keep any fluids down for 24 hours or more (hyperemesis gravidarum needs medical treatment)
- Painful or burning urination (possible urine infection, which needs prompt treatment in pregnancy)
- Sudden loss of all pregnancy symptoms, particularly after a previous scan confirmed a heartbeat — seek reassurance from your Early Pregnancy Unit (EPU)

## Preparing for Your 12-Week Scan {#preparing-for-your-scan}

Your dating and screening scan is usually offered between 11 weeks and 13 weeks and 6 days. At the scan, the sonographer will check your baby's measurements, look at major structures, and offer the nuchal translucency screening for chromosomal conditions such as Down's syndrome. You will normally need a full bladder, so drink water about an hour before. Bring your scan letter and any maternity notes you have been given.

The scan is also a good moment to confirm your due date and discuss any questions with your midwife before your next appointment.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Is it normal to have no symptoms at 11 weeks?

Yes. Some people sail through the first trimester with minimal symptoms. The absence of symptoms does not mean something is wrong with your pregnancy. If you have previously had a scan confirming a heartbeat and are now symptom-free, this is almost always simply your body adjusting as the placenta matures.

### When will my nausea ease?

Most people find nausea improves between weeks 12 and 16 as first-trimester hormone levels plateau. A minority experience nausea throughout pregnancy. If you are struggling to eat or drink, speak to your midwife or GP — medication is available that is safe to use in pregnancy.

### Should I be worried about cramping at 11 weeks?

Mild, intermittent cramping is common as your uterus grows and your ligaments stretch. It does not usually indicate a problem. Severe cramps, particularly combined with heavy bleeding or one-sided pain, require urgent medical attention — call 111 or go to your nearest EPU or A&E.

Speak to your midwife or GP if you have any concerns about your symptoms this week — no question is too small.
