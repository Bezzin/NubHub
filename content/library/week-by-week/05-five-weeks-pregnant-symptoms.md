# 5 Weeks Pregnant: Your Symptoms

At 5 weeks pregnant, many people are only just discovering they are expecting. Your period is around a week late, a home pregnancy test will almost certainly show a positive result, and your body is already changing rapidly in response to rising levels of hCG (human chorionic gonadotropin) and progesterone. Some people feel very little yet; others are already feeling the full force of early pregnancy symptoms. Both are completely normal.

## Common Symptoms at 5 Weeks {#common-symptoms}

At 5 weeks, you may experience some or none of these symptoms. Every pregnancy is different.

- **Missed period** — the most common first sign, and the reason most people take a pregnancy test
- **Fatigue and tiredness** — often striking and unexpected; your body is diverting enormous energy into building a placenta
- **Nausea** — may be starting to appear, though it often peaks between weeks 6 and 10; it can occur at any time of day, not just in the morning
- **Breast tenderness and changes** — breasts may feel heavy, sore, or tingly; the areolae can begin to darken
- **Frequent urination** — rising hCG increases blood flow to the kidneys, which produce more urine
- **Mood swings** — rapid hormonal shifts can affect emotions significantly
- **Bloating** — progesterone relaxes smooth muscle throughout the body, slowing digestion and causing a bloated feeling
- **Food cravings or aversions** — specific foods may suddenly become irresistible or intolerable
- **Heightened sense of smell** — a very common early symptom, often closely linked to nausea
- **Light spotting (implantation bleeding)** — a small amount of pink or brown discharge around the time of your expected period can occur as the embryo embeds into the uterine lining; this is usually lighter and shorter than a period

## Your Baby at 5 Weeks {#your-baby-at-5-weeks}

At 5 weeks, your baby is roughly the size of a sesame seed — tiny, but already developing at remarkable speed. The embryo has three distinct cell layers (the ectoderm, mesoderm, and endoderm) that will form every organ in the body. The neural tube, which will become the brain and spinal cord, is beginning to form — this is why folic acid in early pregnancy is so important. The heart is also starting to develop, and by the end of this week it may begin to beat, though it is far too early to detect on a standard scan.

The gestational sac may be visible on a transvaginal ultrasound at this stage, but a yolk sac and fetal pole are often not yet visible until around weeks 5 to 6.

## What's Normal at 5 Weeks {#whats-normal}

It is completely normal to have some, all, or none of the symptoms listed above. Symptom intensity varies enormously between people and even between pregnancies in the same person. Having minimal symptoms at 5 weeks does not indicate a problem — the embryo is very small and the placenta is only just beginning to form.

Mild, period-like cramping is also common in early pregnancy as the uterus begins to grow and the embryo continues to implant. This is usually low-grade and intermittent. Light pink or brown spotting that does not fill a pad (often called implantation bleeding) can be normal at this stage, though any bleeding should be mentioned to your GP or midwife.

## When to Seek Help {#when-to-worry}

Contact your GP, midwife, or call 111 if you experience:

- Heavy bleeding — soaking a pad or passing clots — which may indicate a miscarriage or another concern
- Severe, sharp, one-sided abdominal pain, especially with shoulder tip pain or dizziness — this can indicate an ectopic pregnancy, which is a medical emergency; call 999 or go to A&E immediately
- Fever above 38°C
- Inability to keep any fluids down (severe nausea and vomiting may need treatment)
- Burning or pain when urinating (urine infections need prompt treatment in pregnancy)

An ectopic pregnancy occurs when the embryo implants outside the uterus, most commonly in a fallopian tube. It cannot result in a viable pregnancy and requires urgent treatment. Symptoms often appear between weeks 5 and 10, so being aware of the warning signs at this stage is important.

## What to Do at 5 Weeks {#what-to-do-at-5-weeks}

- **Contact your GP** to confirm your pregnancy and get referred to a midwife for your booking appointment, which usually takes place around 8 to 10 weeks
- **Take folic acid** — if you are not already taking it, start a supplement of 400 micrograms per day now (or 5mg if recommended by your GP due to medical history); continue until at least 12 weeks
- **Take vitamin D** — the NHS recommends 10 micrograms daily throughout pregnancy
- **Avoid alcohol, smoking, and recreational drugs** — no safe level of alcohol in pregnancy has been established
- **Review any medications** with your GP or pharmacist — some are not safe in pregnancy
- **Eat as healthily as you can** — if nausea makes this difficult, do not worry; focus on staying hydrated and eating whatever you can manage

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Is it normal to have no symptoms at 5 weeks pregnant?

Yes, completely. Some people have no noticeable symptoms at 5 weeks at all. Others will already be feeling very unwell. Hormone levels vary between individuals, and the absence of symptoms at this early stage does not indicate anything is wrong with your pregnancy. If you are concerned, speak to your GP.

### When will nausea start — and when will it end?

Nausea typically starts between weeks 5 and 7 and peaks around weeks 8 to 10 for most people. It usually eases by the end of the first trimester (around weeks 12 to 14), though a minority of people experience it throughout pregnancy. If nausea is severe and preventing you from keeping food or fluids down, speak to your midwife or GP — there are safe treatments available.

### Should I be worried about cramping at 5 weeks?

Mild, low-level cramping at 5 weeks is very common and usually a normal sign of your uterus beginning to grow. It should not be severe or constant. Cramping accompanied by heavy bleeding, one-sided pain, or pain in the shoulder tip needs urgent medical attention. If you are unsure, it is always better to call 111 or contact your GP for advice.

Speak to your midwife or GP with any questions — the booking appointment is a great opportunity to raise everything that is on your mind.
