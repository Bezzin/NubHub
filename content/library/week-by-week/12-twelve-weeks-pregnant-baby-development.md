# 12 Weeks Pregnant: Baby Development

At 12 weeks pregnant, your baby is approximately the size of a plum — around 5–6 cm from crown to rump. This is a remarkable milestone: your baby has a fully formed, distinctly human shape, reflexes are starting, and almost all of the major organs and structures are in place. The work of the next six months is mostly growth and refinement of what already exists.

## Baby Size at 12 Weeks {#baby-size}

Your baby measures roughly 5–6 cm from the top of the head to the bottom (crown to rump length) and weighs around 14 grams — about the same as a plum. At your 12-week dating scan, the sonographer will take a crown to rump measurement to confirm or adjust your due date.

Growth in the first trimester has been extraordinary. Just eight weeks ago (at conception), your baby was a single fertilised cell.

## Development Milestones {#milestones}

By 12 weeks, a remarkable amount of development is already complete:

**Face and head** — Your baby's face is clearly recognisable. The eyes have moved from the sides of the head towards the front. Eyelids are fused shut (they will open around 26 weeks). The ears are close to their final position. Tiny tooth buds are forming beneath the gums.

**Brain and nervous system** — The brain is developing rapidly. Nerve cells are multiplying at a fast rate. Reflexes are beginning: your baby may already move their fingers, curl their toes, and even make swallowing motions, though you will not feel any of this yet.

**Organs** — All major organs are formed. The liver has begun producing bile, the kidneys are producing urine, and the intestines, which were briefly outside the body in the umbilical cord during development, have now moved back inside the abdomen.

**Heart** — The four-chambered heart has been beating since around 6 weeks and is pumping blood through a small but complete circulatory system. The heartbeat at this stage is typically faster than an adult's — usually somewhere between 120 and 170 beats per minute.

**Sex organs** — External genitalia are forming, though on ultrasound they may not yet be clearly distinguishable as male or female. This is why the 12-week scan is considered the best window for nub theory — the genital tubercle is visible and its angle relative to the spine may give an early clue about sex.

**Limbs** — Fingers and toes are fully separated. Fingernails are beginning to grow. Your baby can flex and move all four limbs.

**Placenta** — By 12 weeks, the placenta is fully formed and has taken over hormone production from the corpus luteum. This is why many women notice their nausea easing around this time — hCG levels, which drove morning sickness, start to level off.

## What You Might Notice {#what-you-notice}

For many women, the end of the first trimester brings some welcome relief. Nausea and extreme fatigue often begin to ease as hCG levels plateau and the placenta takes over. Your uterus is now just above your pubic bone and may be just palpable to the touch, though a visible bump at 12 weeks varies enormously from person to person and pregnancy to pregnancy.

Your 12-week dating scan — usually offered between 11 and 14 weeks on the NHS — gives you a detailed look at your baby's development and screens for Down's syndrome, Edwards' syndrome, and Patau's syndrome as part of the combined screening test.

## FAQ {#faq}

### How big is my baby at 12 weeks?

Your baby is approximately 5–6 cm long (crown to rump) and weighs around 14 grams — roughly the size of a plum. The exact measurement will be confirmed at your dating scan, and the sonographer may adjust your due date by a few days based on this measurement.

### Can I see my baby on a scan at 12 weeks?

Yes. The NHS dating scan is offered between 11 and 14 weeks and gives a clear, detailed view of your baby. You will be able to see the head, body, and limbs, and the sonographer will measure the crown to rump length, check the heartbeat, and look at the nuchal translucency (fluid at the back of the neck) as part of Down's syndrome screening.

### Is 12 weeks a safe milestone?

The risk of miscarriage drops significantly once a heartbeat has been confirmed on scan. Most miscarriages occur in the first 10–11 weeks, so reaching 12 weeks with a healthy scan is genuinely reassuring. That said, some complications can still arise later in pregnancy, so ongoing midwife-led care remains important.

Speak to your midwife or GP for advice specific to your pregnancy.
