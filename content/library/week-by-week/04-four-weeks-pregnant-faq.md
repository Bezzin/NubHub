# 4 Weeks Pregnant: Your FAQs Answered

At 4 weeks pregnant you are right at the point where your period is due — and for many women, this is the week a pregnancy test first turns positive. It is a strange, exciting, and often nerve-wracking time. You may have a million questions swirling around your head, from whether it is too early to test to what you should be eating and doing right now. This guide answers the most common questions women ask at exactly this stage.

## What Is Actually Happening at 4 Weeks?

At 4 weeks, the fertilised egg has implanted into the lining of your uterus and is dividing rapidly. The embryo is tiny — about the size of a poppy seed — but enormous developmental work is already under way. The amniotic sac and yolk sac are forming, and the cells that will become your baby's brain, spinal cord, heart, and other organs are beginning to organise.

Your body is producing human chorionic gonadotrophin (hCG), the hormone detected by pregnancy tests. Levels are rising quickly and will roughly double every couple of days in a healthy early pregnancy.

## What to Do This Week

- **Start folic acid** if you have not already. The NHS recommends 400 micrograms daily from before conception until at least 12 weeks. If you have been prescribed a higher dose (for example, if you have a family history of neural tube defects or take certain medications), continue that dose.
- **Stop alcohol and smoking.** There is no established safe level of alcohol in pregnancy, and stopping smoking reduces risks for both you and your baby.
- **Avoid foods that carry infection risk**, including unpasteurised cheeses, undercooked meat, and raw shellfish.
- **Book a GP appointment** so you can be referred to a midwife and booked into antenatal care. You do not need to rush to A&E unless you have worrying symptoms.
- **Take a prenatal vitamin** that includes folic acid, vitamin D, and iodine. The NHS recommends 10 micrograms of vitamin D daily throughout pregnancy.

## What Is Normal (and What Is Not)

At 4 weeks, many women feel completely normal. Others notice breast tenderness, mild bloating, or unusual fatigue. Light spotting around the time of implantation is also common and does not necessarily mean anything is wrong.

**Seek medical advice promptly if you experience:**
- Heavy bleeding (soaking a pad)
- One-sided pain, especially with dizziness or shoulder tip pain (these can be signs of an ectopic pregnancy)
- Severe abdominal cramping

If you are unsure, call 111 for guidance.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is it too early to take a pregnancy test at 4 weeks?

Four weeks pregnant is the same as the week your period is due, so a standard home pregnancy test should give you a reliable positive result. Some more sensitive tests can detect hCG a few days before your missed period, though the result may be faint. Testing with first morning urine gives the most accurate reading because hCG is most concentrated then. If you get a faint positive, test again in a day or two — the line should get darker as hCG rises.

### Should I have any symptoms at 4 weeks?

Some women notice breast tenderness, fatigue, mild nausea, or a heightened sense of smell. Others feel absolutely nothing different at all. Both are entirely normal at this very early stage. A lack of symptoms does not mean anything is wrong with your pregnancy. Symptoms tend to become more noticeable over the next two to four weeks as hCG levels climb.

### Do I need to see a doctor or midwife straight away?

You do not need to go to A&E or call 999 unless you have worrying symptoms (see above). Book a routine GP appointment so you can be referred for midwife-led antenatal care. Your booking appointment with a midwife will usually take place between 8 and 10 weeks. You can also self-refer directly to a midwife in many NHS areas — ask your GP surgery for the local referral route.

### Can I exercise at 4 weeks pregnant?

Yes. If you were exercising before pregnancy, it is generally safe to continue. Walking, swimming, yoga, and low-impact exercise are all encouraged. Avoid contact sports, activities with a high fall risk, or anything that leaves you breathless and unable to hold a conversation. Listen to your body and ease off if you feel unwell.

Speak to your midwife or GP for advice tailored to your own health and pregnancy.
