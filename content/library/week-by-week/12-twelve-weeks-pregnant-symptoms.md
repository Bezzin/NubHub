# 12 Weeks Pregnant: Your Symptoms

At 12 weeks pregnant, you have reached the end of the first trimester — a milestone many parents-to-be have been eagerly counting down to. This is also when most women in the UK have their dating scan (sometimes called the 12-week scan), which gives you a first proper look at your baby and checks that everything is developing as expected. For many, symptoms begin to ease around this point, though the timeline varies considerably from person to person. You may also be thinking about nub theory — looking at your scan image to try to predict your baby's sex, which becomes more readable from around 12 weeks.

Your baby is now fully formed in miniature, with all major organs, fingers, toes, and even the early development of fingerprints. Although still very small, your baby is moving around — you just won't be able to feel those movements yet. The placenta is now taking over hormone production, which is one reason symptoms often begin to shift around this point.

## Common Symptoms at 12 Weeks {#common-symptoms}

At 12 weeks, you may experience some or none of these symptoms. Every pregnancy is different.

- **Easing nausea** — Many women notice that morning sickness begins to lift around now as hCG levels start to plateau. However, for some it continues well into the second trimester, and that is also normal.
- **Returning energy** — As progesterone production shifts from the corpus luteum to the placenta, many women feel a gradual improvement in their energy levels. The exhaustion of the first trimester often starts to ease.
- **Breast tenderness** — Your breasts may still feel sore and heavier than usual, though the most acute tenderness often softens as the first trimester ends.
- **A visible bump beginning** — Some women, particularly those who have been pregnant before, may notice their abdomen starting to round out. For first-time pregnancies, a bump may not be apparent to others yet.
- **Frequent urination** — This often continues throughout pregnancy, and the pressure on your bladder may intensify again later as baby grows.
- **Mood swings** — Hormonal fluctuations continue, though many women report feeling more emotionally settled as the second trimester approaches.
- **Bloating and digestive changes** — Constipation, wind, and bloating may persist. Staying well hydrated and eating plenty of fibre-rich foods can help.
- **Food cravings or aversions** — These may remain strong or begin to ease — again, both are normal.
- **Heightened sense of smell** — This often begins to improve as the first trimester closes.
- **Headaches** — Hormonal changes and increased blood volume can still contribute to headaches at this stage. Paracetamol at the recommended dose is generally considered safe — speak to your midwife or GP if headaches are frequent or severe.
- **Round ligament pain** — As your uterus grows and rises out of the pelvis, the ligaments supporting it stretch. This can cause brief, sharp pains on one or both sides of your lower abdomen, especially when you change position quickly. It is usually harmless but worth mentioning to your midwife if it concerns you.
- **Skin changes** — Some women notice the beginnings of a linea nigra (a faint, dark line running down the centre of the abdomen), or patches of darker skin on the face (sometimes called the mask of pregnancy, or melasma). These are caused by hormonal changes and are common.

## What's Normal at 12 Weeks {#whats-normal}

It is completely normal to have some, all, or none of these symptoms. Some women feel dramatically better at 12 weeks; others notice little change until later in the second trimester. Both patterns are normal.

Symptom-free days are not a sign that something is wrong. As your placenta takes over hormone production from around this point, it is common to notice fluctuations in how you feel. A day where you feel more like yourself is not a warning sign — it can simply mean your hormone levels are settling.

Your uterus is now just starting to rise above the pelvic bone, which is why a bump may be beginning to show. For many women carrying their first baby, a visible bump does not appear until the second trimester.

## Your 12-Week Scan

Most women in the UK are offered a dating scan between 10 and 14 weeks, with 12 weeks being the most common timing. The scan checks your baby's size, confirms the number of babies, and offers a more accurate due date. At this scan, you may also be offered a nuchal translucency (NT) measurement as part of combined screening for chromosomal conditions — your sonographer will explain this to you.

This is also when nub theory becomes more readable. The nub — the early genital tubercle — is visible on many 12-week scan images and can be used to attempt a gender prediction, though it is not a medical test and cannot be confirmed until your 20-week anomaly scan or via a private blood test.

## When to Seek Help {#when-to-worry}

Contact your GP or midwife if you experience any of the following:

- Heavy bleeding (soaking a pad)
- Severe abdominal pain, especially if one-sided
- Fever above 38°C
- Inability to keep fluids down for 24 hours or more
- Sudden, complete loss of all pregnancy symptoms — especially if you had previously seen a heartbeat on scan
- Pain or burning when urinating, which could indicate a urinary tract infection requiring treatment
- A headache that is severe, sudden, or accompanied by visual disturbances — this should always be assessed promptly

If you experience any pain, pressure, or bleeding around the time of your 12-week scan, contact your midwife or Early Pregnancy Unit (EPU) rather than waiting.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Is it normal to have no symptoms at 12 weeks?

Yes. Some women feel very little by 12 weeks, particularly if their symptoms have naturally eased as the first trimester ends. A symptom-free pregnancy is not automatically a cause for concern. The 12-week scan is the most reliable way to check that your baby is developing well, and your midwife or sonographer will be looking at this carefully.

### When will my symptoms ease?

Many women find that the worst of the nausea and fatigue eases between weeks 12 and 14. Energy levels often return more noticeably from around 14–16 weeks. If you are still struggling with significant nausea beyond 12 weeks, speak to your midwife — there are safe treatment options available that can make a real difference.

### Should I be worried about cramping at 12 weeks?

Mild cramping or stretching sensations can be normal as your uterus continues to grow. However, severe or persistent cramping — particularly one-sided pain or cramping with heavy bleeding — should be assessed promptly. Contact your midwife, call 111, or visit your nearest Early Pregnancy Unit if you are concerned.
