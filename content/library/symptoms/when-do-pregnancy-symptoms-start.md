# When Do Pregnancy Symptoms Start?

One of the most common questions after a positive pregnancy test is: when should I start feeling something? The answer is more varied than you might think. Some women feel symptoms before they even miss a period, while others sail through the first trimester feeling completely normal. Both can be perfectly healthy.

## The Hormone Behind Symptoms {#hormone}

Most early pregnancy symptoms are driven by two key hormones:

- **Human chorionic gonadotropin (hCG)**: Produced by the placenta after implantation. It doubles every 48-72 hours in early pregnancy and is responsible for much of the nausea. It's also what pregnancy tests detect.
- **Progesterone**: Rises dramatically to maintain the uterine lining. It causes fatigue, bloating, constipation, and breast tenderness.

Oestrogen also rises steadily, contributing to breast changes, heightened smell, and mood swings. The interplay of these three hormones creates the full spectrum of pregnancy symptoms.

## Week-by-Week Timeline {#timeline}

### Weeks 1-3 (Before You Know)

Technically you're not pregnant in weeks 1-2 (counting starts from your last period). In week 3, fertilisation and implantation occur. Some women notice very subtle signs:

- Mild cramping (implantation)
- Tiny spotting (implantation bleeding)
- Fatigue
- Heightened smell

Most women don't notice anything at this stage.

### Week 4

A missed period is the classic sign. You may notice:

- Fatigue beginning to set in
- Breast tenderness
- Mild cramping
- Frequent urination starting

A home pregnancy test will usually be positive this week.

### Week 5

Symptoms begin to ramp up:

- Nausea may start (often mild at first)
- Breast tenderness increases
- Fatigue becomes more pronounced
- Food aversions or cravings begin
- Mood swings
- Bloating

### Week 6

This is when most women really start to feel pregnant:

- Morning sickness often kicks in properly
- Nausea and possible vomiting
- Heightened smell becomes noticeable
- Frequent urination
- Headaches
- Constipation or bloating

### Weeks 7-8

Symptoms typically peak in intensity:

- Nausea at its worst for many women
- Extreme fatigue
- Breast changes more visible (darker nipples, veins)
- Emotional ups and downs
- Aversions and cravings stronger

### Weeks 9-10

Often the peak of first-trimester symptoms. hCG levels are at their highest. Many women feel at their worst - but relief is coming.

### Weeks 11-12

For most women, symptoms begin to ease. The placenta takes over hormone production, and hCG levels start to drop. Nausea usually reduces, energy returns, and you may start to feel more like yourself.

### Weeks 13-14

The first trimester ends. Most early symptoms have resolved for the majority of women, though some continue to experience nausea or fatigue into the second trimester.

## The Very First Symptoms {#first-symptoms}

If you're trying to spot symptoms before a missed period, the most commonly reported early signs are:

1. **Fatigue** - sudden, overwhelming tiredness
2. **Breast tenderness** - often before a period is even due
3. **Heightened sense of smell** - suddenly noticing scents you'd usually ignore
4. **Mild cramping** - implantation-related
5. **Frequent urination** - hormones increasing kidney blood flow
6. **Metallic taste** - a strange taste that won't go away

Remember: these can also be caused by stress, illness, or PMS. The only way to know for sure is a pregnancy test.

## Why Some Women Feel Symptoms Earlier {#why-earlier}

Several factors influence when symptoms begin:

- **Multiple pregnancies**: Twins or more mean higher hCG levels and earlier, more intense symptoms.
- **Sensitivity to hormones**: Some women are simply more sensitive to hormonal changes.
- **First pregnancy vs subsequent**: Women who've been pregnant before may notice symptoms earlier because they know what to look for.
- **Body weight**: Higher body fat can affect hormone metabolism.
- **Age**: Older mothers may experience symptoms slightly differently.

## Why Some Women Have No Symptoms {#why-none}

An estimated 20% of women have little to no symptoms in the first trimester. This can be worrying but is usually completely normal. Reasons include:

- Naturally lower hCG levels (still within a healthy range)
- Lower sensitivity to hormonal changes
- Genetics
- The embryo implanted slightly later, meaning hormone rise is delayed

The absence of symptoms does **not** mean your pregnancy is unhealthy. However, if you had symptoms that suddenly disappeared (especially with bleeding or cramping), contact your midwife.

## When to Take a Test {#when-test}

Home pregnancy tests are most reliable from the first day of your missed period (around week 4). Some sensitive tests can detect hCG a few days earlier. For the most accurate result:

- Test with your first morning urine (hCG is most concentrated)
- Wait at least a week after your missed period if you want to be sure
- If negative but symptoms persist, test again in 3-5 days

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Can pregnancy symptoms start before a missed period?

Yes. Some women notice fatigue, breast tenderness, or heightened smell a few days before their period is due. However, these symptoms overlap with PMS, so a test is the only way to confirm.

### Is it normal to have no symptoms at 5 weeks?

Yes. Many women don't feel anything until week 6 or 7. About 20% of women have no symptoms at all in early pregnancy. Read more about [no symptoms at 5 weeks](/symptoms/no-symptoms-5-weeks).

### Do symptoms start earlier with twins?

Often, yes. Multiple pregnancies produce more hCG, which can lead to earlier and more intense symptoms. However, early symptoms alone don't reliably indicate twins - only a scan can confirm this.

### If I had symptoms last time, will I have them this time?

Not necessarily. Every pregnancy is different. You may have more, fewer, or different symptoms in subsequent pregnancies.
