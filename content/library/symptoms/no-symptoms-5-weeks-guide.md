# No Symptoms at 5 Weeks: Is It Normal?

At 5 weeks pregnant, feeling completely normal — no nausea, no sore breasts, no fatigue — is very common. In fact, having no symptoms at this stage is often the norm rather than the exception. If you have just got a positive test and are wondering why you do not feel pregnant yet, this page explains what is happening and what to expect.

## What's Happening at 5 Weeks {#s1}

Five weeks pregnant means you are in the very early days of your pregnancy — roughly one week after a missed period. Your embryo is tiny, around the size of a sesame seed, and is still in the process of implanting fully into the uterine wall. The placenta is only just beginning to form.

Pregnancy symptoms are largely driven by the hormone human chorionic gonadotropin (hCG), which is produced by the developing placenta. At 5 weeks, hCG is rising but has typically not yet reached the levels that cause noticeable symptoms in most women. This is why many women have taken multiple pregnancy tests by this point — they do not feel pregnant and want to reassure themselves the test was real.

Some women do notice very early signs at 5 weeks: mild breast tenderness, needing to wee slightly more often, or a vague sense of tiredness. But many feel nothing at all, and both experiences are completely normal.

## When Symptoms Typically Start {#s2}

For most women, pregnancy symptoms begin or become noticeable between 6 and 8 weeks. This is when hCG levels tend to reach their peak or near-peak, which is why nausea, fatigue, and breast changes are most intense during this window.

Some women experience symptoms earlier, around weeks 4–5. Others do not notice much until closer to 8–10 weeks, and a meaningful proportion of women have very mild symptoms throughout the entire first trimester. All of these patterns can be consistent with a healthy pregnancy.

By week 12–14, hCG starts to decline as the placenta takes over hormone production, and many women find their symptoms ease off or disappear around this time.

## What You Can Do at 5 Weeks {#s3}

While waiting for symptoms (or for your booking appointment), there are a few practical things to focus on:

- **Start taking folic acid** if you have not already — the NHS recommends 400 micrograms daily until at least week 12. Some women are advised to take a higher dose; speak to your GP if you are unsure.
- **Register with a GP** if you have not, and ask to be referred to a midwife to book your antenatal care.
- **Avoid alcohol and smoking** from now.
- **Check any medications** you take with your GP, as some are not safe in pregnancy.
- There is no need to eat for two at this stage — a normal, balanced diet is ideal.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Is it normal to have no symptoms at 5 weeks?

Yes, it is very normal. At 5 weeks, hCG levels are still rising and have not yet reached the point where most women feel noticeably different. Symptoms typically begin between 6 and 8 weeks. The absence of symptoms at 5 weeks does not indicate that anything is wrong with the pregnancy.

### Should I be worried if I feel completely normal at 5 weeks pregnant?

No. Feeling normal at 5 weeks is common and is not a sign that your pregnancy is failing. If you have had a positive pregnancy test, your body is producing hCG. Many women feel fine throughout early pregnancy and go on to have healthy babies. If you are anxious, speak to your GP, who can advise on what early support is available to you.

### When will I start feeling pregnant?

Most women notice symptoms between 6 and 8 weeks. Nausea, fatigue, and breast tenderness are the most commonly reported. However, some women feel very little throughout the first trimester, and some feel symptoms earlier. There is a wide range of normal.
