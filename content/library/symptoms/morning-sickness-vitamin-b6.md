# Vitamin B6 for Morning Sickness

Vitamin B6, also called pyridoxine, has some of the strongest evidence of any non-drug treatment for morning sickness. This page explains the research, safe doses and how to combine it sensibly with prenatal vitamins.

## What the Research Shows

Several randomised trials have found that vitamin B6 modestly reduces nausea in early pregnancy, even when it does not reduce vomiting. The American College of Obstetricians and the Royal College of Obstetricians both recognise it as a reasonable first-line option. The mechanism is not fully understood, but it may relate to how B6 supports neurotransmitter balance. Benefit is often noticed within a week. It is considered safe at low doses, though high doses long-term can cause nerve issues, so stick to recommended amounts.

## Getting the Dose Right

Most studies used 10 to 25mg, taken up to three times daily. Many UK prenatal vitamins already contain some B6, so check the label before adding a separate supplement to avoid doubling up excessively. If your combined intake stays within typical safe ranges, most midwives are comfortable with it. B6 is not a magic fix, and many women benefit from combining it with ginger or acupressure. Speak to your GP if you are unsure about your total intake from all sources.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Is vitamin B6 safe in pregnancy?**

At the doses studied, yes. Stay within the ranges recommended by your midwife and avoid very high doses.

**Q: Can I take B6 with my prenatal vitamin?**

Often yes, but check the label to avoid excessive total intake. Ask your pharmacist if you are unsure.

**Q: Does B6 stop vomiting as well as nausea?**

It tends to help nausea more than vomiting. If vomiting is severe, speak to your GP about additional options.
