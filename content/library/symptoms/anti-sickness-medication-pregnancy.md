# Anti-Sickness Medication in Pregnancy: What's Safe?

If morning sickness is making your life miserable, you may be wondering if medication is an option. The good news is that several anti-sickness medications are considered safe in pregnancy and available on prescription in the UK. This guide covers what's available, how they work, and how to get them.

## When to Consider Medication {#when-meds}

Many women are told to "try natural remedies first" - and while that's reasonable for mild nausea, it's not appropriate for everyone. Consider asking your GP for medication if:

- **You're vomiting 3 or more times per day**
- **You can't keep food or water down**
- **You're losing weight**
- **You feel dizzy, faint, or weak**
- **Your urine is dark or you're urinating less**
- **Nausea is constant and unrelenting**
- **You can't function** - work, care for children, or look after yourself
- **Natural remedies haven't worked** after a few days

You do **not** need to suffer for weeks before asking for medication. If you're struggling, ask early.

## First-Line Medications {#first-line}

These are the most commonly prescribed anti-sickness medications in pregnancy. They're antihistamine-based and have been used safely for decades.

### Cyclizine

- **How it works**: Blocks histamine receptors in the brain
- **Typical dose**: 50mg up to three times daily
- **Side effects**: Drowsiness, dry mouth, dizziness
- **Safety**: Well-established safety record in pregnancy
- **Read more**: [Cyclizine in Pregnancy](/symptoms/cyclizine-pregnancy)

### Promethazine

- **How it works**: Antihistamine that blocks histamine and reduces nausea
- **Typical dose**: 10-25mg every 4-8 hours
- **Side effects**: Drowsiness (can be significant), dry mouth, blurred vision
- **Safety**: Long history of safe use in pregnancy
- **Brand names**: Avomine, Phenergan
- **Read more**: [Promethazine in Pregnancy](/symptoms/promethazine-pregnancy)

### Prochlorperazine

- **How it works**: Dopamine antagonist that blocks nausea signals
- **Typical dose**: 5-10mg up to three times daily
- **Side effects**: Drowsiness, dizziness, dry mouth
- **Available as**: Tablets, buccal tablets (dissolve between gum and lip), suppositories
- **Brand name**: Stemetil

### How to Take Them

- **Start at the lowest dose** and increase if needed
- **Take regularly**, not just when you feel sick - prevention is better than treatment
- **Don't stop suddenly** if you feel better - it's the medication keeping you stable
- **Try different timing** - before bed may help with morning sickness

## Second-Line Medications {#second-line}

If first-line medications don't work, your GP or specialist may prescribe:

### Ondansetron

- **How it works**: 5-HT3 receptor antagonist - blocks serotonin receptors
- **Typical dose**: 4-8mg every 8-12 hours
- **Side effects**: Headache, constipation, drowsiness
- **Safety**: Generally considered safe, though some studies have raised concerns about very slight increased risk of cleft palate (evidence is mixed)
- **Often very effective** when other medications haven't worked
- **Brand name**: Zofran
- **Read more**: [Ondansetron in Pregnancy](/symptoms/ondansetron-pregnancy)

### Metoclopramide

- **How it works**: Dopamine antagonist that also speeds up gastric emptying
- **Typical dose**: 10mg up to three times daily (limited to 5 days at a time)
- **Side effects**: Drowsiness, restlessness, diarrhoea
- **Caution**: Can cause extrapyramidal side effects (muscle movements) - use limited duration
- **Brand name**: Maxolon

### Domperidone

- **How it works**: Dopamine antagonist, similar to metoclopramide
- **Typical dose**: 10mg up to three times daily
- **Side effects**: Dry mouth, headache
- **Less likely** to cause the movement side effects of metoclopramide
- **Brand name**: Motilium

### When Second-Line Is Needed
- First-line medication hasn't worked after 48-72 hours
- You have hyperemesis gravidarum
- You've been admitted to hospital
- Your GP refers you to a specialist

## How to Get a Prescription {#get-prescription}

### From Your GP
1. **Book an appointment** - tell the receptionist it's about pregnancy sickness
2. **Describe your symptoms clearly** - frequency of vomiting, what you can eat/drink
3. **Mention weight loss or dehydration** if relevant
4. **Ask about medication options** - don't wait for them to offer
5. **If they suggest ginger** and you've already tried it, say so

### Free Prescriptions in Pregnancy
All pregnant women are entitled to **free NHS prescriptions**. Ask your GP or midwife for form FW8 to apply for a maternity exemption certificate. This covers you until your baby's first birthday.

### If Your GP Won't Prescribe
- **See a different GP** at your practice
- **Contact your midwife** - she can advocate
- **Call 111** for urgent advice
- **Ask for a referral** to an obstetrician
- **Contact Pregnancy Sickness Support** for advice

## Side Effects {#side-effects}

### Common Side Effects
- **Drowsiness**: Most anti-sickness medications cause some drowsiness. Take care when driving or operating machinery. Taking a larger dose at bedtime can help.
- **Dry mouth**: Sip water, chew sugar-free gum
- **Dizziness**: Stand up slowly
- **Constipation**: Ondansetron can worsen pregnancy constipation. Drink water and eat fibre.
- **Headache**: Common with ondansetron

### Managing Side Effects
- **Take at night** if drowsiness is a problem during the day
- **Start with a lower dose** and increase gradually
- **Combine medications** to reduce individual doses (only under medical guidance)
- **Don't stop suddenly** - symptoms may rebound

### When to Report Side Effects
Contact your GP if you experience:
- Severe drowsiness that affects daily life
- Muscle twitching or involuntary movements (metoclopramide)
- Rapid heartbeat
- Confusion
- Allergic reactions (rash, swelling, breathing difficulty)

## Combination Therapy {#combination}

Many women with significant nausea and vomiting need **more than one medication**. This is normal and evidence-based. Common combinations include:

- **Cyclizine + prochlorperazine**
- **Cyclizine + ondansetron**
- **Promethazine + metoclopramide**
- **Ondansetron + metoclopramide**

Combination therapy works because different medications target different pathways in the brain. Together, they're more effective than either alone.

Your GP or specialist will determine the right combination for you based on:
- Your symptoms
- What you've already tried
- Side effects
- Your medical history

## Over-the-Counter Options {#otc}

### Available Without Prescription
- **Ginger supplements** (up to 1g per day)
- **Acupressure wristbands** (Sea-Bands)
- **Vitamin B6** (pyridoxine) - limited evidence but may help mild nausea

### What's NOT Safe Over-the-Counter
- **Never take over-the-counter anti-sickness medication** without consulting your GP
- Many general anti-sickness drugs are **not safe in pregnancy**
- Always check with a pharmacist or GP before taking anything

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Is it safe to take anti-sickness medication in pregnancy?

Yes, several anti-sickness medications are considered safe in pregnancy and have been used for decades. These include cyclizine, promethazine, prochlorperazine, and ondansetron. Always take medication prescribed by your GP, not over-the-counter products.

### When should I ask for medication for morning sickness?

Ask your GP if you're vomiting 3+ times daily, can't keep food or water down, are losing weight, feel dizzy or weak, or if natural remedies haven't worked after a few days. You don't need to suffer for weeks before asking.

### Will anti-sickness medication harm my baby?

The most commonly prescribed anti-sickness medications have been used safely in pregnancy for decades. The benefits of treating severe nausea and vomiting (preventing dehydration and malnutrition) outweigh the very small risks of medication. Discuss any concerns with your GP.

### Can I take anti-sickness medication throughout my pregnancy?

Most women only need medication for the first trimester. However, some women with HG need medication throughout pregnancy. Your GP will review your medication regularly and reduce it when appropriate.

### What if one medication doesn't work?

If one medication isn't working after 48-72 hours, your GP can prescribe a different one or combine medications. Many women need two or more medications to manage HG. Don't accept being told "there's nothing else" - there are several options.
