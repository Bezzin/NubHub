# Avoiding Morning Sickness Triggers

Much of managing morning sickness is avoiding the things that set it off. While you cannot always eliminate every trigger, identifying your personal ones and reducing your exposure to them can make a real difference to how often and how severely nausea hits. This page helps you do exactly that.

## Common Triggers to Watch For

Every woman's morning sickness is different, but these are among the most frequently reported triggers:

- **Strong cooking smells** — frying, roasting meat, garlic and onions top the list
- **Perfumes and aftershave** — even a favourite scent can become unbearable in early pregnancy
- **Cleaning products** — bleach, disinfectant sprays and washing-up liquid smells are common culprits
- **Stale or stuffy air** — poorly ventilated rooms concentrate odours and heighten nausea
- **Hot environments** — heat slows digestion and increases the intensity of smells around you
- **Going too long without eating** — an empty stomach is one of the most reliable triggers; blood sugar dips fast and nausea follows
- **Strong food flavours** — very sweet, rich, fatty or spicy foods can trigger nausea even when smelled rather than eaten
- **Tiredness and overexertion** — nausea is often worse when you are exhausted; fatigue and sickness feed each other
- **Screen flicker or motion** — some women find scrolling on phones, watching TV, or car journeys worsen symptoms
- **Toothbrushing** — the gag reflex is heightened in pregnancy; toothbrushing in the morning is a very common trigger

## Keeping a Nausea Diary

A few days of simple notes can reveal patterns you would not otherwise notice. Write down what you were doing, eating or smelling in the 20 to 30 minutes before each bout of nausea struck. Note the time of day, your hunger level, and whether you were indoors or outdoors.

After three or four days, review the entries. You may find that nausea spikes just before lunch when your blood sugar has dropped, or that it is worst in the kitchen after breakfast when coffee is being made nearby. Knowing your pattern lets you plan around it rather than being caught off guard.

You do not need anything formal — a few notes in your phone are enough.

## Practical Ways to Reduce Exposure

Once you know your triggers, small adjustments to your daily routine can significantly reduce how often and how severely nausea hits:

- **Ask someone else to cook** when you are at your worst, or batch-cook when you feel better and keep food in the fridge
- **Open windows** while cooking and leave them open for a while afterwards to clear cooking smells
- **Switch to unscented toiletries** — shampoo, shower gel, washing-up liquid and cleaning products all come in fragrance-free versions
- **Avoid hot, crowded spaces** such as busy public transport or overheated offices where possible
- **Eat before you feel hungry** — set a timer if needed and snack every two to three hours; do not let your stomach empty completely
- **Choose cold meals** when hot food smells are a problem; cold food produces far less aroma
- **Prepare food in advance** when you feel relatively well, so that you do not have to cook when nausea is at its worst
- **Travel by a less smelly route** — some women find the underground or buses particularly challenging; a seat near a window or at the front of the bus can help
- **Breathe through your mouth** in environments with strong smells until you can leave

Small changes add up. You do not need to overhaul everything at once — tackle the one or two triggers that affect you most first.

## Triggers at Work

Workplace triggers can be harder to control, but you have more rights than you may realise. Your employer has a duty to consider health and safety risks in pregnancy. If your work involves exposure to strong chemicals, fumes, or you work in catering where cooking smells are unavoidable, speak to your midwife and HR department. Adjustments such as a temporary change of duties or a desk move are reasonable requests.

If nausea is affecting your ability to work, speak to your GP. Pregnancy-related sickness absence is treated separately from normal sick leave under UK employment law and cannot count against you.

## When to Seek Help

Trigger avoidance is a useful self-management strategy for typical morning sickness. However, if nausea and vomiting are so severe that you cannot keep fluids down for more than 24 hours, are losing weight, or are unable to function, you may be experiencing hyperemesis gravidarum (HG). This is a medical condition that requires treatment — speak to your GP or midwife promptly. Do not try to manage HG with dietary changes alone.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What are the most common morning sickness triggers?

Strong cooking smells, perfumes, an empty stomach, hot environments, and cleaning products are among the most widely reported triggers. Triggers are very individual, so keeping a brief diary for a few days is the best way to identify yours.

### Should I keep a nausea diary?

Yes, even a few days of notes can reveal your personal pattern clearly. Note what you were doing, eating or smelling before each bout of nausea. Patterns around time of day and hunger level often emerge quickly.

### Can I avoid all triggers?

Not entirely — some will be unavoidable at work or at home. The goal is to reduce your exposure to the most significant ones. Even partial avoidance can reduce the frequency and severity of nausea meaningfully.
