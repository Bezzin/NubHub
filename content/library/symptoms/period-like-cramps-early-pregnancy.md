# Period-Like Cramps in Early Pregnancy: Normal or Not?

Feeling period-like cramps when you are newly pregnant is one of the most common — and most anxiety-inducing — symptoms. The good news is that mild cramping is usually a sign that your womb is doing exactly what it should: stretching and growing to accommodate your baby. This guide explains what is normal and what warrants a call to your midwife.

## Why You Feel Cramps in Early Pregnancy

From the moment of implantation, your womb starts to change. The muscle thickens, the ligaments stretch, and hormones relax your pelvic joints. All of this can feel like a dull period ache or mild cramps low in your abdomen. You might also feel twinges when you stand up quickly, cough, or roll over in bed. These are normal and usually settle with rest and a warm pack placed on your lower abdomen.

### Implantation

In the very earliest days of pregnancy, implantation itself can cause brief, mild cramping as the fertilised egg embeds into the lining of your womb. This typically happens around six to twelve days after conception, before or around the time you would expect your period. The cramping is usually very mild and short-lived, and may be accompanied by a small amount of light spotting (implantation bleeding).

### Womb Growth and Stretching

As pregnancy progresses through the first trimester, your womb expands to accommodate the growing pregnancy. The round ligaments that support the womb are put under increasing tension and can produce sharp, pulling pains — particularly on one or both sides of your lower abdomen. These round ligament pains are very common and entirely normal, though they can occasionally feel alarming if you are not expecting them.

### Wind and Digestive Changes

Progesterone, the hormone that surges in early pregnancy to support the pregnancy, also slows your digestive system. This can lead to bloating, trapped wind, and cramping sensations that are easy to confuse with womb-related pain. If your cramps shift and resolve after passing wind or opening your bowels, this is most likely the cause.

### Constipation

For the same hormonal reasons, constipation is extremely common in early pregnancy. Straining and a sluggish bowel can produce genuine cramping pain. Drinking plenty of water, eating fibre-rich foods, and gentle movement can all help. Speak to your midwife if constipation is persistent.

## When Cramping Needs Checking

Mild, bilateral cramps that come and go are usually fine. Contact your midwife or early pregnancy unit promptly if any of the following apply:

- Cramps are severe or getting progressively worse
- Pain is one-sided, particularly in the lower abdomen or referred into the shoulder tip
- Cramping is accompanied by vaginal bleeding
- You have a fever or feel generally unwell
- You experience pain or burning on urination alongside cramping (this may indicate a urinary tract infection, which is common in pregnancy and needs treatment)
- You are beyond your first trimester and experience cramping that feels rhythmic or regular

One-sided pain in early pregnancy always warrants prompt assessment to rule out an ectopic pregnancy, a condition where the pregnancy develops outside the womb. Ectopic pregnancy is uncommon but serious. If you have one-sided pain with or without bleeding and cannot reach your midwife or early pregnancy unit quickly, call 111 or attend your nearest early pregnancy unit. If you have severe pain and feel faint or collapse, call 999.

Most women with mild cramping go on to have healthy pregnancies, so try to hold on to that before reaching for the worst-case scenario.

## Managing Mild Cramps at Home

If your cramps are mild and you have no accompanying symptoms that concern you, the following can help:

- Rest — lie down and allow your body to relax
- A warm (not hot) wheat bag or warm water bottle on your lower abdomen
- A warm bath
- Paracetamol, taken according to the packet instructions, is safe in pregnancy for short-term pain relief
- Gentle movement such as a short walk can sometimes relieve trapped wind-related cramps

Avoid ibuprofen and other non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) in the first trimester unless specifically advised by your midwife or doctor, as these are not recommended in early pregnancy.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How long do early pregnancy cramps last?

Cramping can come and go throughout the first trimester as your womb grows and changes. Each episode usually lasts a few minutes to a few hours. If cramping is persistent, severe, or worsening over time rather than settling, contact your early pregnancy unit.

### Are cramps at 6 weeks a sign of miscarriage?

Not on their own. Mild cramps without bleeding are very common at six weeks and are usually caused by normal womb growth and hormonal changes. If cramps are severe or paired with bleeding, contact your midwife or early pregnancy unit for assessment and reassurance.

### Can I take painkillers for pregnancy cramps?

Paracetamol is generally considered safe in pregnancy and can be taken at the recommended dose on the packet for short-term pain relief. Avoid ibuprofen and other NSAIDs in the first trimester unless your midwife or GP has specifically advised you to take them. If you are unsure, ask your pharmacist or midwife.

### Do cramps mean I am going to miscarry?

Cramping alone, without bleeding, is rarely a sign of miscarriage. Many women cramp intermittently throughout the first trimester and go on to have straightforward pregnancies. If you are concerned, your early pregnancy unit can arrange an early scan for reassurance.
