# Is the Pregnancy Glow Real?

The pregnancy glow is one of the most talked about symptoms of expecting a baby. But is it a genuine physiological change or simply a flattering myth? The answer is a bit of both, and it is largely driven by hormones, blood flow and how you care for your skin during these important weeks.

## What Causes the Pregnancy Glow? {#what-causes-the-pregnancy-glow}

During pregnancy your body produces significantly more blood, with blood volume increasing by up to 50 percent by the third trimester. This extra circulation pushes more blood to the skin surface, giving the cheeks a warmer, flushed appearance. At the same time, rising levels of oestrogen and progesterone stimulate the sebaceous glands to produce more oil, which can leave skin looking plump, dewy and well hydrated. The combined effect of increased blood flow and mild oiliness is what many people recognise as the famous glow.

## When Does the Glow Appear? {#when-does-the-glow-appear}

For most women the glow becomes noticeable toward the end of the first trimester, often around weeks 10 to 12, when hormone levels have risen steadily. Some women notice it earlier, while others never experience it at all. Skin changes are highly individual and depend on genetics, baseline skin type and how your body responds to hormonal shifts. If you do not see a glow, it does not mean anything is wrong with your pregnancy.

## How to Support Healthy Skin {#how-to-support-healthy-skin}

Gentle skincare can help you make the most of any natural radiance. Use a mild cleanser twice daily, keep skin well moisturised and apply a broad-spectrum SPF every morning, as pregnancy hormones can make skin more prone to pigmentation and sun damage. Drink plenty of water, eat a balanced diet rich in fruits and vegetables, and avoid harsh active ingredients such as retinoids unless your midwife or GP advises they are safe. Consistency matters far more than expensive products.

## When Skin Changes Need Attention {#when-skin-changes-need-attention}

Not all skin changes in pregnancy are welcome. Some women develop acne, melasma (patchy brown pigmentation), or increased sensitivity. These are usually harmless but can be frustrating. If you notice sudden severe breakouts, widespread rashes, itching without a visible rash, or any new moles that change in shape or colour, contact your midwife or GP. It is always better to check than to worry silently.

## FAQ {#faq}

### Is the pregnancy glow guaranteed?

No. Many women experience a noticeable glow thanks to increased blood flow and oil production, but plenty of others do not. Skin responds differently to pregnancy hormones, so the absence of a glow is completely normal.

### Can I use my usual skincare in pregnancy?

Most gentle products are fine, but avoid retinoids, high-dose salicylic acid and certain essential oils. Check with your midwife or a dermatologist if you are unsure about any ingredient.

### Does the glow last after birth?

The extra blood volume and hormone-driven oiliness settle after delivery, so the glow typically fades over the weeks following birth as your hormone levels normalise.
