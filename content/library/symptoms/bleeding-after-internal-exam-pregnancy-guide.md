# Bleeding After an Internal Exam in Pregnancy

Internal exams — whether for a smear test, a cervical check, or a midwife assessment — can cause light spotting in pregnancy. Seeing blood after any examination is understandably alarming, but in most cases it is a harmless and very common response to a sensitive cervix. This guide explains what happens during an internal exam, why spotting occurs, what is normal, and when you should contact your midwife or GP.

## What Happens During an Internal Exam {#what-happens-during-an-internal-exam}

An internal examination in pregnancy may be carried out for several reasons. A speculum exam is used to take cervical swabs, perform a smear test, or check for signs of infection. A bimanual exam — using gloved fingers — allows a midwife or doctor to assess the size and position of the uterus, particularly in early pregnancy. A transvaginal ultrasound uses a probe inserted into the vagina to get a clearer image of the pregnancy in the first trimester.

All of these are routine procedures. They are designed to be gentle, and your care provider will always explain what they are doing and ask for your consent beforehand.

## Why Bleeding Happens After an Exam in Pregnancy {#why-bleeding-happens}

During pregnancy, especially in the first trimester, your cervix undergoes significant changes. Blood flow to the pelvic region increases markedly to support the growing pregnancy. The cervix becomes softer, more vascular (meaning it has more blood vessels running through it), and more sensitive than it was before pregnancy.

This increased vascularity means that even very gentle contact — a swab, the tip of a speculum, or a probe — can disturb one of the many tiny blood vessels near the surface of the cervix. This is sometimes called a cervical ectropion (or cervical erosion), where the delicate inner lining of the cervix extends to the outer surface and bleeds very easily on contact. Cervical ectropion is extremely common in pregnancy due to hormonal changes and does not indicate anything is wrong with you or your baby.

The result is usually a small amount of pink, red, or brown spotting that appears within a few hours of the exam.

## What Is Normal After an Internal Exam {#what-is-normal}

After an internal examination, the following are generally considered normal and expected:

- **Light pink or brown spotting** appearing within a few hours of the exam
- **Spotting that is lighter than a period** — not soaking a pad
- **No accompanying pain beyond mild cramping**, similar to period discomfort
- **Spotting that settles within 24 to 48 hours**

Brown spotting in particular is a reassuring sign. Brown blood is old blood — it has taken some time to travel through the cervix and vagina, which suggests the bleeding is not fresh or ongoing. Pink spotting is also common and usually nothing to worry about after an exam.

Many women notice spotting after sexual intercourse for the same reason — the cervix is simply more sensitive during pregnancy, and contact of any kind can cause a brief bleed.

## Caring for Yourself Afterwards {#caring-for-yourself-afterwards}

There is no specific treatment needed after light post-exam spotting, but a few sensible steps will help you monitor the situation and stay comfortable:

- **Use a panty liner** rather than a tampon — tampons are not recommended during pregnancy as they can introduce bacteria. A liner lets you track the amount and colour of any discharge or spotting.
- **Note the colour** — pink or brown is reassuring; bright red is worth monitoring more closely.
- **Rest if you feel anxious** — though physically you do not need bed rest, taking it gently for the rest of the day is entirely reasonable.
- **Avoid penetrative intercourse and tampon use** until the spotting has fully stopped.
- **Stay hydrated** and try not to catastrophise. Post-exam spotting is one of the most common reasons women contact their midwife, and it is very rarely a sign of anything serious.

## When to Seek Help {#when-to-seek-help}

While spotting after an internal exam is usually harmless, there are situations where you should contact your midwife, GP, or early pregnancy unit on the same day:

- **The spotting turns to bright red bleeding** that is heavier than light spotting
- **You pass any clots**
- **You develop pain in your abdomen, pelvis, or lower back** — particularly one-sided pain
- **You develop a fever or feel unwell** alongside the bleeding
- **The bleeding does not settle within 48 hours**
- **You feel very anxious and want reassurance** — this is always a valid reason to call

If you experience heavy bleeding — soaking through a pad within an hour — or have severe pain alongside any bleeding, call 999 or go to A&E immediately without waiting. Do not drive yourself.

In the first trimester, your early pregnancy unit (EPU) is the most appropriate place to be assessed if you have concerning bleeding. Your midwife or GP can refer you, and some EPUs accept self-referrals. Call 111 if you are unsure which service to use outside of working hours.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#frequently-asked-questions}

### Is spotting after a cervical smear normal in pregnancy?

Yes. Your cervix becomes more sensitive and more vascular during pregnancy, so light spotting after a smear test is common and usually settles within 24 to 48 hours. Tell your smear nurse or GP that you are pregnant beforehand so they can be as gentle as possible and know to expect some post-exam spotting.

### Can an internal exam cause a miscarriage?

No. A routine, gently performed internal examination does not affect your pregnancy or harm your baby. The cervix is closed and sealed during pregnancy, and the exam does not reach the uterus. Post-exam spotting is caused by surface blood vessels on the cervix, not anything inside the womb.

### Should I cancel my smear test if I am pregnant?

In the UK, smear tests are routinely deferred until three months after birth. If you receive an invitation or reminder for a smear test while pregnant, contact your GP surgery to postpone it. If you have had a smear test early in pregnancy before you knew you were pregnant, or if there are clinical reasons to go ahead, speak to your GP. Any light spotting afterwards is expected and normal.

### What if I have spotting after a transvaginal ultrasound?

A transvaginal ultrasound uses a probe that sits in the vagina near the cervix. Some spotting or light bleeding afterwards is common for the same reasons as any other internal exam — the cervix is sensitive and vascular. Monitor the spotting as described above, and contact your midwife or EPU if it becomes heavier, is accompanied by pain, or does not settle within 48 hours.
