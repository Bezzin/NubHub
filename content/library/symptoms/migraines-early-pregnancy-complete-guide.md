# Migraines in Early Pregnancy: Complete Guide

Migraines can improve, worsen, or appear for the first time in pregnancy. Here's what you need to know about managing them safely and knowing when to get help.

## How Pregnancy Affects Migraines {#s1}

Migraines are closely linked to hormonal fluctuations, which is why pregnancy — with its rapid and significant hormonal changes — can affect them significantly. In the first trimester, many women find their migraines worsen, particularly if they previously experienced migraines around their period. This is thought to be due to the sharp rise in oestrogen in early pregnancy disrupting the patterns your body was used to.

Many women find that migraines improve from the second trimester onwards, once hormone levels stabilise at a higher plateau. Others experience migraines for the first time during pregnancy, with no previous history. Both of these patterns are common and not a cause for alarm on their own.

Common migraine triggers that can be particularly relevant in pregnancy include:

- Dehydration (especially with morning sickness)
- Disrupted sleep
- Skipping meals or low blood sugar
- Bright light or screen glare
- Strong smells
- Stress and tension
- Caffeine withdrawal (if you have cut back since becoming pregnant)

Keeping a brief note of when migraines occur and what you were doing or eating beforehand can help you identify your personal triggers.

## Safe Management in Pregnancy {#s2}

Managing migraines safely in pregnancy requires some care, because several commonly used migraine medications are not recommended during pregnancy.

**What is generally considered safe:**

- **Paracetamol** is the recommended first-line pain relief in pregnancy. Take it at the recommended dose as soon as you feel a migraine coming on — it works better early than once pain is established.
- **Rest in a quiet, dark room** can help significantly. Many people find that sleep is one of the most effective migraine remedies.
- **Cold or warm compress** on your forehead or neck may ease symptoms.
- **Staying well hydrated** is important — drink water regularly and keep up your fluid intake even if you feel nauseous.
- **Regular small meals** can help prevent blood sugar dips that trigger migraines.

**What to avoid without medical advice:**

- **Ibuprofen and aspirin** are generally avoided in pregnancy, particularly in the first and third trimesters.
- **Triptans** (such as sumatriptan) are commonly used for migraines but should only be taken in pregnancy if prescribed and monitored by a specialist. Do not continue taking triptans without discussing this with your GP or obstetrician.
- **Codeine-containing products** are best avoided, especially in later pregnancy.
- **Ergotamine** (found in some older migraine treatments) is not recommended in pregnancy.

If you were prescribed regular migraine medication before becoming pregnant, speak to your GP as soon as possible to review your treatment. Do not stop prescribed medication abruptly without medical advice.

## When to Seek Help

Most migraines during pregnancy, while very unpleasant, are not dangerous. However, a new or very severe headache in pregnancy — particularly in the second or third trimester — can occasionally be a sign of pre-eclampsia or another condition that needs prompt assessment.

**Contact your midwife, GP, or call 111 if:**

- You have a severe headache that is different from your usual migraines
- The headache is accompanied by visual disturbances (flashing lights, blurred vision, or blind spots)
- You have sudden swelling in your hands, face, or feet alongside a headache
- You feel confused, have difficulty speaking, or have weakness on one side of your body
- The headache does not improve with paracetamol and rest
- Your migraines are very frequent and affecting your daily life or ability to eat and drink

**Call 999 or go to A&E immediately** if you have the worst headache of your life, sudden severe pain, or any neurological symptoms such as loss of consciousness, numbness, or severe vision loss.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I take my migraine medication in pregnancy?

Some migraine medications are not safe in pregnancy. Paracetamol is the first-line option. Ibuprofen, triptans, and ergotamine should only be used under medical supervision during pregnancy. If you rely on prescription migraine treatment, talk to your GP as early as possible so they can review your plan and suggest pregnancy-appropriate alternatives. Never stop prescribed medication without consulting your doctor first.

### Will my migraines get worse in pregnancy?

For many women, migraines are more frequent or intense in the first trimester due to hormonal changes. The good news is that for many people they settle down or improve significantly from the second trimester onwards as hormone levels stabilise. However, everyone is different — your midwife or GP can help you manage symptoms throughout.

### Can migraines harm my baby?

Migraines themselves are not known to harm the baby. However, if migraines are causing significant nausea or vomiting and making it difficult to eat, drink, or take your pregnancy vitamins, speak to your midwife or GP so they can offer additional support. A very severe headache with other symptoms (such as visual changes or swelling) should always be assessed promptly to rule out conditions such as pre-eclampsia.

Speak to your midwife or GP for personalised advice about managing migraines safely during your pregnancy.
