# Pelvic Pain in Early Pregnancy

Mild aches and twinges around your pelvis and lower tummy are very common in early pregnancy, and most of the time they are nothing to worry about. As your body adapts to being pregnant, ligaments soften, your uterus begins to grow, and your digestion slows down, all of which can create new and unfamiliar sensations. This guide explains the usual causes, how to ease the discomfort, and the warning signs that mean you should get checked promptly.

## Common Causes of Pelvic Pain

Several normal changes can cause pelvic discomfort in the first trimester. Stretching ligaments as your uterus grows can give brief, pulling aches low down or to the sides, sometimes called round ligament pain. Pregnancy hormones loosen the joints and ligaments around your pelvis to prepare for later pregnancy and birth, which can make the area feel achy or tender. Constipation and trapped wind, both very common early on, can cause cramping and pressure too. Many people also notice mild, period-like cramps in the early weeks as the uterus changes, and this on its own is usually normal.

## Easing the Discomfort

There is often a lot you can do to feel more comfortable. Move position slowly, especially when getting up, turning over in bed, or getting out of the car, and try bending your knees to take the strain off your pelvis. A warm (not hot) bath or a wrapped warm compress can soothe achy muscles. Staying active with gentle walking, keeping well hydrated, and eating plenty of fibre can ease constipation-related cramps. Rest when you can, wear comfortable supportive shoes, and avoid heavy lifting. If you need pain relief, paracetamol is generally considered the first choice in pregnancy when taken as directed, but check with your pharmacist or midwife if you are unsure, and avoid ibuprofen unless a doctor advises it.

## When to Seek Help

While mild aching is usually fine, some symptoms need prompt medical attention. Contact your GP, midwife, or early pregnancy unit, or call NHS 111 for advice, if you have severe or persistent pain, pain that is mainly on one side, pain alongside bleeding, pain with a high temperature, pain or stinging when you wee, or pain with feeling faint, dizzy, or unwell. One-sided pain with shoulder-tip pain, faintness, or bleeding can be a sign of an ectopic pregnancy and should be assessed urgently. If pain is sudden and severe, you feel very unwell, or you collapse, call 999. It is always okay to get checked for reassurance; you will never be wasting anyone's time.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is pelvic pain normal at 8 weeks?

Yes, mild pelvic aching and occasional twinges are common around this stage as your uterus grows and your ligaments and joints soften. Severe pain, one-sided pain, or pain with bleeding or fever should be checked promptly.

### Are mild cramps in early pregnancy a bad sign?

Not usually. Light, period-like cramps are a common early pregnancy sensation as the uterus changes. It is the combination of cramping with heavy bleeding, severe or one-sided pain, or feeling faint that needs urgent assessment.

### What pain relief can I take for pelvic pain in pregnancy?

Paracetamol is generally regarded as the first choice when taken as directed, while ibuprofen and other anti-inflammatories are usually avoided in pregnancy unless a doctor advises them. Check with your pharmacist or midwife if you are unsure.

If your pelvic pain is worrying you or does not settle, speak to your midwife or GP for advice tailored to you.
