# Acupressure Bands for Morning Sickness

Acupressure bands are soft wristbands that press on the P6 point, long used for travel sickness and now widely tried by pregnant women in the UK. This page explains how they work, how to fit them and what to expect.

## How Acupressure Targets Nausea

The P6 or Neiguan point sits on the inner wrist, roughly three finger-widths below the crease. Stimulating it is believed to influence the nerve pathways that trigger nausea. Some clinical trials show modest benefit for pregnancy nausea, while others find little effect, so results genuinely vary between women. The appeal of bands is that they are drug-free, reusable and inexpensive, making them a low-risk option worth trying alongside other strategies. Many UK pharmacies stock them without prescription.

The theory draws from traditional acupuncture, which targets the same P6 point using needles. Acupressure replaces the needle with sustained physical pressure from a small plastic or metal stud sewn into the band. Because acupressure bands carry no drug-related risk, they are generally considered safe to try at any stage of pregnancy without needing to consult a clinician first — though if you have any specific health concerns, it is always reasonable to ask your midwife.

## Fitting and Wearing the Bands

Place the plastic stud on the inside of each wrist, three finger-widths down from the wrist crease, between the two tendons you can feel when you flex your hand. Wear both bands for best results, and keep them on through the times of day you feel worst. Some women find them more useful for mild nausea than for severe vomiting. They can be worn overnight, and unlike medication there is no concern about dose.

Positioning matters more than you might expect. If a band sits too high or too low on the wrist, you may get little benefit. Ask a pharmacist to show you the correct placement if you are unsure. Once in position, the band should feel snug but not so tight that it is uncomfortable or leaves deep marks on your skin. If you notice numbness or tingling in your fingers, loosen the band slightly.

If you see no improvement after a few days of correct use, it is worth trying a different remedy rather than relying on the bands alone. Morning sickness responds differently to different approaches in different women, and combining strategies — such as eating small frequent meals, staying hydrated, and avoiding strong smells — often works better than any single intervention.

## What to Expect from Acupressure Bands {#what-to-expect}

Some women feel relief within minutes of putting the bands on. Others wear them consistently for several days before noticing any difference. A proportion of women find they do not help at all, and this is a perfectly valid outcome — it does not mean you are doing anything wrong.

Acupressure bands are most likely to help if your nausea is mild to moderate. If you are vomiting multiple times a day, struggling to keep fluids down, or losing weight, bands alone are unlikely to be sufficient. In that situation, speak to your GP or midwife promptly. There are safe prescription and non-prescription options for severe pregnancy nausea (sometimes called hyperemesis gravidarum when severe), and you should not feel you have to manage without help.

## Other Remedies to Try Alongside

Acupressure bands work well as part of a broader approach to managing morning sickness. Other strategies that many women find helpful include:

- **Ginger** in the form of ginger biscuits, ginger tea, or ginger capsules — ginger has the most consistent evidence among non-prescription options
- **Vitamin B6** — some women find a B6 supplement reduces nausea; ask your midwife or pharmacist about appropriate amounts
- **Small, frequent meals** — an empty stomach often makes nausea worse
- **Cold or room-temperature foods** — hot food smells can trigger nausea; cold alternatives may be easier to tolerate
- **Fresh air and rest** — fatigue and overheating both worsen symptoms for many women

If none of these measures bring relief, do speak to your GP. Medication for pregnancy nausea is safe and well-established, and you do not need to suffer through the first trimester without support.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How quickly do acupressure bands work?

Some women feel relief within minutes, while others notice no real difference. Give them a fair trial of a few days with correct placement before deciding whether they help you.

### Can I wear the bands all day?

Yes, they are safe for continuous wear. Take them off if the skin becomes sore or irritated, and check that they are not tight enough to cause numbness.

### Do acupressure bands help severe vomiting?

They tend to help mild to moderate nausea more than severe vomiting. If you cannot keep fluids down, contact your GP or midwife promptly — this can lead to dehydration and may need treatment.
