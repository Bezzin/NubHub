# Back Pain in Early Pregnancy

Back pain can start surprisingly early in pregnancy — sometimes before you've even had your first midwife appointment. If you're only a few weeks along and already feeling it in your lower back, you're not alone. This is one of the most common early pregnancy symptoms, and in most cases it's completely normal.

## Causes {#s1}

Several changes in your body during early pregnancy contribute to back pain.

**Relaxin hormone.** Almost immediately after conception, your body begins producing relaxin, a hormone that loosens your ligaments and joints in preparation for birth. This is necessary and helpful, but it can also make your back less stable and more prone to aching — particularly your lower back and the sacroiliac joints on either side of your pelvis.

**Your changing centre of gravity.** As your uterus grows, even slightly in the early weeks, the distribution of weight across your spine and hips begins to shift. Your posture adjusts — often without you noticing — and this puts strain on the muscles of your lower back.

**Muscle fatigue and tension.** Pregnancy is physically demanding from the very start. Fatigue, stress, and poor sleep can cause muscle tension that shows up in the back and shoulders. Many women carry emotional tension physically, and the early weeks of pregnancy — with all the uncertainty and adjustment they bring — can be a stressful time.

**Round ligament discomfort.** The ligaments supporting your uterus stretch as it grows, sometimes causing a sharp or pulling pain in the lower abdomen that can radiate into the back.

## Relief {#s2}

Most early pregnancy back pain can be managed comfortably at home.

- **Warm baths** — A warm (not hot) bath can ease muscle tension. Keep the water at a comfortable, not scalding, temperature.
- **Gentle movement** — Walking, swimming, and pregnancy yoga can all help strengthen the muscles that support your back. Staying active is generally better than resting completely.
- **Good posture** — Try to stand and sit with your pelvis in a neutral position, shoulders relaxed, and weight evenly distributed. Avoid standing with your weight all on one leg for long periods.
- **Supportive shoes** — High heels shift your posture and increase spinal strain. Flat, supportive shoes make a real difference.
- **Pregnancy pillow** — Sleeping on your side with a pillow between your knees takes pressure off your lower back and hips.
- **Paracetamol** — Paracetamol is the painkiller recommended for use in pregnancy when needed. Follow the packet instructions and avoid taking it regularly without discussing it with your GP or midwife first.
- **Avoid heavy lifting** — If you must lift, bend at the knees, keep the load close to your body, and avoid twisting.

A physiotherapist who specialises in women's health or pelvic health can also be enormously helpful — ask your GP or midwife for a referral.

## When to Seek Help {#s3}

Most back pain in early pregnancy is musculoskeletal and not harmful. However, contact your midwife, GP, or call 111 if you experience:

- Severe or sudden back pain that doesn't ease
- Back pain accompanied by vaginal bleeding
- Pain that spreads down your leg (sciatica-like symptoms can sometimes warrant assessment)
- Back pain combined with difficulty urinating or a burning sensation when you wee — this may indicate a urinary tract infection (UTI), which needs treatment in pregnancy
- Fever alongside back pain

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Is back pain normal at 6 weeks pregnant?

Yes. Hormonal changes — particularly the rise of relaxin — start affecting your ligaments and joints very early in pregnancy, often before you've even had your booking appointment. Mild aching in the lower back at six weeks is common and usually nothing to worry about. If the pain is severe, or accompanied by bleeding, contact your midwife or GP.

### Can I use a heat pad for back pain in pregnancy?

A warm heat pad applied to your lower back is generally considered safe during pregnancy. The key word is warm, not hot. Avoid electric blankets set to high, very hot baths, hot tubs, and saunas — these can raise your core body temperature, which is not safe during pregnancy. A microwaveable heat pack at a comfortable temperature is a good option.

### Will the back pain go away?

For many women, back pain eases as the body adjusts to the hormonal changes of the first trimester. For others, it continues through pregnancy as the bump grows and posture changes further. Staying active, practising good posture, and seeing a pelvic health physiotherapist if needed can all help significantly.

Speak to your midwife or GP if back pain is affecting your daily life — they can refer you to appropriate support.
