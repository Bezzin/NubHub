# Pregnancy Anxiety How Common

How common is anxiety in early pregnancy? Statistics and reassurance that you're not alone.

Feeling anxious during pregnancy is far more normal than most people realise. If worry, fear, or a sense of dread has settled over you since seeing that positive test, you are not unusual — and you are certainly not alone. Anxiety is one of the most common experiences in pregnancy, particularly in the first trimester, yet it rarely gets the same attention as morning sickness or fatigue.

## How Many Pregnant Women Experience Anxiety?

Anxiety during pregnancy is genuinely widespread. Research suggests that a significant proportion of pregnant women experience anxiety at some level, with clinically significant anxiety — the kind that interferes with day-to-day life — affecting a meaningful minority. First-trimester rates tend to be higher than later in pregnancy, which makes sense: this is often when you know the least, feel the most uncertain about the pregnancy continuing, and have had very little chance to process what is happening.

Common triggers in early pregnancy include:

- **Fear of miscarriage** — especially if you have experienced one before, or are aware of how common early losses are
- **Uncertainty** — waiting for scans, not feeling pregnant, or not knowing how the pregnancy is progressing
- **Hormonal changes** — the rapid surge in progesterone and oestrogen directly affects mood and can heighten feelings of anxiety
- **Life changes** — the enormity of becoming a parent, worries about finances, relationships, or work can all surface suddenly
- **Physical symptoms** — cramping, spotting, or unfamiliar sensations can trigger alarm even when everything is fine

It is also worth knowing that anxiety and depression often overlap in pregnancy. Many women experience both, and one can make the other feel worse. Neither is a sign of weakness or a failure to be grateful for your pregnancy.

## What Is Normal Versus When to Seek Help

Some level of worry in pregnancy is entirely understandable and healthy — it can motivate you to attend appointments, make lifestyle changes, and prepare thoughtfully for your baby's arrival. The difficulty is when anxiety becomes persistent, overwhelming, or starts to affect your sleep, your relationships, or your ability to function day to day.

Signs it may be time to speak to someone include:

- Worrying that feels uncontrollable or that takes up most of your day
- Difficulty sleeping because of anxious thoughts
- Avoiding pregnancy-related appointments or scan results out of fear
- Panic attacks — episodes of sudden, intense fear accompanied by physical symptoms such as a racing heart, breathlessness, or dizziness
- Feeling constantly on edge, irritable, or unable to relax
- Withdrawing from people you trust

If any of these feel familiar, please speak to your midwife or GP. You do not need to reach a crisis point to ask for support. Perinatal mental health is taken seriously by the NHS, and there are real, effective options available including talking therapies and, where appropriate, medication that is considered safe in pregnancy.

## What Support Is Available in the UK

Your first port of call should always be your midwife or GP. At your booking appointment (usually around 8–10 weeks), your midwife will ask about your mental health — be honest, even if it feels uncomfortable. The information you share is used to connect you with the right support, not to judge you.

Many NHS trusts have dedicated **perinatal mental health teams** who work with women during and after pregnancy. Your midwife or GP can refer you. You can also self-refer to NHS talking therapies (previously known as IAPT) — search "NHS talking therapies" alongside your local area to find your nearest service.

If you are struggling urgently outside of a GP appointment, you can call **111** for advice.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How many pregnant women have anxiety?

Anxiety during pregnancy is very common. Clinically significant anxiety — the level that genuinely impacts daily life — affects a substantial minority of pregnant women, with first-trimester rates particularly high. Many more experience milder but still distressing worry. You are far from alone, and it is worth speaking to your midwife about how you are feeling.

### Is first-trimester anxiety worse than later in pregnancy?

For many women, yes. The first trimester can be the most anxious period because you have so much uncertainty: no visible bump, no felt movements, and often no scan yet to reassure you. Hormonal changes are also at their most intense in these early weeks. For most women, anxiety eases somewhat after the 12-week scan, though it can persist or shift focus throughout pregnancy.

### Can anxiety in pregnancy affect my baby?

Mild to moderate anxiety is very unlikely to harm your baby. Chronic, severe, unmanaged anxiety and stress over a long period is worth discussing with your care team, not because it is certain to cause harm but because good support is available and you deserve to feel as well as possible. Seeking help is the most important thing you can do.

Speak to your midwife or GP for personal advice about managing anxiety during your pregnancy.
