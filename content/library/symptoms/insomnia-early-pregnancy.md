# Insomnia in Early Pregnancy

Can't sleep even though you're exhausted? Insomnia in early pregnancy is common and frustrating. Here's why it happens and what actually helps you get better rest.

## Why Sleep Becomes Difficult {#why}

Several pregnancy changes can wreck your sleep in the first trimester:

- **Frequent weeing**: Rising hormones and a growing uterus mean more night-time trips to the loo.
- **Nausea**: Morning sickness can strike at night, making it hard to settle.
- **Tender breasts**: Growing, sensitive breasts make finding a comfortable position harder.
- **Hormone shifts**: Progesterone can make you sleepy by day but disrupt sleep at night.
- **Vivid dreams**: Hormones and stress can trigger intense dreams that wake you.
- **Anxiety**: Worrying about the pregnancy keeps your mind racing.
- **Fatigue naps**: Daytime sleeping can make night-time rest harder.

## Tips to Improve Your Sleep {#tips}

- **Stick to a routine**: Go to bed and wake up at the same time each day.
- **Limit fluids before bed**: Reduce drinks in the evening to cut night-time weeing.
- **Use a pregnancy pillow**: Supporting your bump and knees eases discomfort.
- **Sleep on your left side**: This improves blood flow and is recommended from early on.
- **Wind down**: Avoid screens for an hour before bed; try reading or a warm bath.
- **Eat early**: Have dinner a few hours before lying down to ease digestion and nausea.
- **Try relaxation**: Deep breathing, meditation or a calming app can help.
- **Keep naps short**: If you nap, limit it to 20 to 30 minutes and avoid late afternoon.

## Safe Sleep Aids in Pregnancy {#safe-aids}

Some sleep aids are safe in pregnancy, while others should be avoided:

- **Safe to try**: Lavender pillow spray, chamomile tea (in moderation), relaxation apps, a warm (not hot) bath before bed
- **Paracetamol**: If pain or discomfort is keeping you awake, paracetamol is generally considered safe — check with your pharmacist
- **Avoid**: Herbal sleep remedies containing valerian or unregulated supplements, sleeping tablets (unless prescribed by your GP), alcohol

Always check with your pharmacist or midwife before taking anything new.

## When to Talk to Your Midwife {#when-midwife}

Mention insomnia to your midwife or GP if:

- You can't sleep for more than a few nights a week
- Insomnia is affecting your daily life or mental health
- It's accompanied by low mood or anxiety
- You're using substances to cope

Persistent insomnia can worsen fatigue and mood, so don't suffer in silence.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Is insomnia normal in early pregnancy?

Yes. Hormone changes, frequent weeing and nausea all contribute. Most women find sleep improves in the second trimester.

### Can I take sleeping tablets in pregnancy?

Only if prescribed by your GP. Most over-the-counter sleep aids are not recommended in pregnancy. Speak to your midwife or GP for safe options.

### Will lack of sleep harm my baby?

Occasional poor sleep doesn't harm your baby. Chronic severe insomnia can affect your wellbeing, so seek support if it's ongoing.
