# Restless Legs in Early Pregnancy

Restless leg syndrome (RLS) is a common and very real part of pregnancy for many women. It can make the evenings and nights frustrating, leaving you with an urge to keep moving your legs just when you want to rest. Although it is more often associated with later pregnancy, some women notice it earlier on. It is usually harmless and tends to ease after birth, but there are practical steps that can make it more bearable in the meantime.

## What RLS Feels Like {#s1}

RLS is best described as an irresistible urge to move your legs, usually worse in the evening or at night and when you are sitting or lying still. The urge is often accompanied by uncomfortable sensations deep in the legs that people describe in different ways, such as crawling, tingling, pulling, aching, or itching. Moving, stretching, or walking tends to bring temporary relief, which is why it can be so disruptive to sleep.

In pregnancy the exact cause is not fully understood, but hormonal changes and low levels of certain nutrients, particularly iron, are thought to play a part. Tiredness and disrupted sleep can also make the sensations feel worse, creating a cycle that is worth trying to break.

## Management {#s2}

There is no single cure, but several self-help measures often help:

- Keep gently active during the day, with regular walking or pregnancy-safe exercise.
- Stretch your calf and leg muscles before bed, and try a warm bath in the evening.
- Massage your legs, or ask your partner to, and try a warm or cool compress to see which suits you.
- Cut back on caffeine, especially in the afternoon and evening, and avoid it close to bedtime.
- Keep a regular sleep routine and try to wind down before bed.

Because low iron can trigger or worsen RLS, it is worth mentioning your symptoms to your midwife, who may check your iron levels. If you are found to be low, treating that can make a real difference. Do not start iron or any other supplement specifically for RLS without advice, as more is not always better.

Avoid medicines for RLS bought over the counter or borrowed from before pregnancy unless a health professional has confirmed they are safe for you now.

## When to seek help

Speak to your midwife or GP if RLS is regularly disturbing your sleep, getting steadily worse, or affecting how you cope during the day. Also mention it if you have other symptoms of low iron, such as feeling unusually tired, breathless, or dizzy, so this can be checked.

## FAQ {#faq}

### Is restless leg syndrome common in pregnancy?

Yes, RLS is a common pregnancy symptom and is often more noticeable later on, although it can start earlier for some women. It is usually harmless and tends to settle after your baby is born. Mentioning it to your midwife is sensible, as low iron can make it worse and is easy to check.

### Will restless legs harm my baby?

No. RLS itself does not harm your baby. The main impact is on your own comfort and sleep, so it is worth trying self-help measures and asking your midwife about your iron levels if it is troubling you.

### What can I do at night when it flares up?

Getting up to walk or stretch, a warm bath before bed, gentle massage, and cutting back on evening caffeine can all help. Building these into a regular wind-down routine often works better than waiting for symptoms to start.

Always speak to your midwife or GP for advice tailored to your own pregnancy.
