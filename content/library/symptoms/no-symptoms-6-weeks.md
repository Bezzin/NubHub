# No Symptoms at 6 Weeks Pregnant - Should I Worry?

Six weeks pregnant and feeling fine? While many women start noticing symptoms around this time, a significant number feel completely normal at 6 weeks. Whether this is your first pregnancy or you've been here before, the absence of symptoms can trigger anxiety. Here's what you need to know.

## Is No Symptoms at 6 Weeks Normal? {#is-normal}

Yes, having no symptoms at 6 weeks is normal. While this is the week when morning sickness often begins, it's also perfectly common to feel nothing at all. Around **20-30% of women** never experience significant pregnancy symptoms, and many more don't have them at exactly 6 weeks.

The "typical" symptom onset is a range, not a fixed point. Morning sickness most commonly starts between weeks 6 and 7, but that means a large proportion of women haven't developed it yet at week 6.

### What Some Women Feel at 6 Weeks

If you do have symptoms at 6 weeks, they might include:
- Nausea (starting mildly)
- Fatigue
- Breast tenderness
- Frequent urination
- Heightened sense of smell
- Bloating
- Mood swings

But having none of these is also entirely normal.

## Why You Might Not Have Symptoms Yet {#why-not}

### hCG Levels Are Still Rising

At 6 weeks, hCG levels typically range from 1,080 to 56,500 mIU/mL - a massive range. If your levels are on the lower end of normal, you may not yet have enough hCG to trigger symptoms. Your levels are likely still rising appropriately, just not high enough to make you feel sick.

### Individual Variation

Every woman's body responds differently to pregnancy hormones. Some are highly sensitive to hormonal changes and feel symptoms early; others have a higher threshold and may never feel much at all.

### Late Ovulation or Implantation

If you ovulated later in your cycle or the embryo implanted a little later, your pregnancy may be a few days "younger" than the textbook 6 weeks. This means your hormone levels are behind the average timeline and symptoms will follow suit.

### Your Body Has Been Here Before

Paradoxically, women who've been pregnant before may feel less anxious about symptom-watching and therefore notice symptoms less. Stress and hyper-awareness can actually amplify the perception of symptoms.

## What's Happening at 6 Weeks {#inside}

Your baby is developing rapidly, even if you feel nothing:

- **Size**: About 6mm - the size of a lentil or a baked bean
- **Heartbeat**: The heart is now beating at around 150-160 beats per minute (visible on an early scan)
- **Neural tube**: Closing and beginning to form the brain and spinal cord
- **Limb buds**: Tiny buds that will become arms and legs are forming
- **Facial features**: Eyes, nose, and mouth are beginning to develop
- **Placenta**: Continuing to develop and starting to take over some hormone production

## When Symptoms Usually Kick In {#when-kick-in}

If you're waiting for symptoms, here's when they typically appear:

| Symptom | When It Usually Starts |
|---------|----------------------|
| Nausea | Week 6-7 (but 20-30% never get it) |
| Fatigue | Week 5-6 |
| Breast tenderness | Week 5-6 |
| Heightened smell | Week 6-7 |
| Food aversions | Week 6-8 |
| Morning sickness (vomiting) | Week 7-8 |

If you're at the start of week 6, symptoms may arrive in the next 7-14 days. Or they may never arrive. Both outcomes are normal.

## When to Contact Your Midwife {#when-contact}

While no symptoms at 6 weeks is usually fine, contact your GP or midwife if:

- **You have bleeding** - especially if it's red, heavy, or accompanied by clots
- **You have abdominal pain** - particularly one-sided pain, which could indicate ectopic pregnancy
- **You have shoulder pain** - this can be a sign of ectopic pregnancy
- **You feel dizzy, faint, or lightheaded**
- **You have a fever** (38°C or above)
- **You've had a previous ectopic pregnancy or miscarriage** and want early monitoring

### The Key Distinction

A gradual lack of symptoms from the start is normal. A sudden disappearance of symptoms that were present (especially with bleeding or pain) is more concerning. If you had symptoms that have vanished, read our guide on [symptoms disappearing at 9 weeks](/symptoms/symptoms-disappearing-9-weeks) or contact your midwife.

## Getting Reassurance {#reassurance}

If you're anxious about having no symptoms, here are your options:

### Early Scan

At 6 weeks, an internal (transvaginal) scan can often detect a heartbeat. This is the gold standard for reassurance. Many private clinics offer early reassurance scans from 6-7 weeks. The NHS may offer an early scan if you have a history of loss or are experiencing bleeding or pain.

### Blood Tests

Your GP can check your hCG levels with two blood tests 48 hours apart. In a healthy pregnancy, hCG should roughly double every 48 hours. This can provide reassurance even before a scan is possible.

### Wait and Watch

If you have no pain or bleeding, the most common advice is simply to wait. Most women who have no symptoms at 6 weeks will either develop them in the coming weeks or remain symptom-free with a healthy pregnancy. Your booking appointment with your midwife (usually around 8-12 weeks) will begin your official antenatal care.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Should I be worried if I have no symptoms at 6 weeks?

No. While 6 weeks is when symptoms often begin, it's completely normal to have none yet. Around 20-30% of women never have significant symptoms. Without bleeding or pain, there's usually no cause for concern.

### Can I have a healthy pregnancy with no symptoms at 6 weeks?

Yes. Many women with no symptoms at 6 weeks go on to have healthy pregnancies and babies. The presence or absence of symptoms is not a reliable predictor of pregnancy health. A scan showing a heartbeat is far more reassuring.

### I'm 6 weeks and my pregnancy symptoms have disappeared - is that OK?

If you previously had symptoms (sore breasts, nausea, fatigue) and they've suddenly vanished, this can sometimes be a sign to investigate. Contact your GP, especially if you also have bleeding or cramping. They may arrange an early scan.

### When should I book my midwife appointment?

Contact your GP as soon as you know you're pregnant. They'll refer you to a midwife for your booking appointment, which typically happens between 8 and 12 weeks. The sooner you're in the system, the better your care.
