# Linea Nigra in Pregnancy: Complete Guide

Linea nigra is the dark vertical line that appears on your belly during pregnancy. It is one of the most common and harmless skin changes in pregnancy, yet it can take many women by surprise if they haven't heard of it before. Here's everything you need to know about what it is, when it appears, and what happens to it after birth.

## What Is Linea Nigra? {#s1}

Linea nigra (Latin for "black line") is a darkened vertical line running from your belly button down towards your pubic bone, and sometimes extending upward past your navel towards your chest. It follows the path of the linea alba — a band of connective tissue that runs down the centre of your abdomen — which is present in everyone but usually invisible due to its pale colour.

During pregnancy, rising levels of hormones — particularly oestrogen and progesterone — stimulate cells called melanocytes to produce more melanin, the pigment that gives skin its colour. This increased melanin production causes the linea alba to darken, turning it into the visible linea nigra. The same hormonal process also causes darkening of the nipples and areolae, and can contribute to the skin condition melasma (sometimes called the "mask of pregnancy") on the face.

Linea nigra is entirely harmless and requires no treatment. It is simply a visible sign of the hormonal changes happening throughout your body.

## When It Appears {#s2}

Linea nigra typically becomes noticeable somewhere between weeks 10 and 20 of pregnancy, though the timing varies considerably between individuals. Some women notice it appearing as early as the end of the first trimester; others don't see it until well into the second trimester, or not at all.

The line tends to become more pronounced as pregnancy progresses and the abdomen grows and stretches. It may also become more vivid in the third trimester as hormone levels remain elevated.

Not every pregnant woman develops a visible linea nigra, and its absence doesn't indicate anything abnormal about your pregnancy.

## What It Looks Like {#s3}

The appearance of linea nigra varies from person to person:

- **Colour**: It ranges from light brown to a much darker brown or near-black, depending on your natural skin tone and the extent of pigmentation
- **Width**: Usually around a centimetre wide, though it can be narrower or slightly broader
- **Length**: Often runs from the pubic bone to the belly button, though in many women it extends above the navel as well
- **Visibility**: It is generally more visible in women with naturally darker skin tones, who produce more melanin overall; women with fair skin may barely notice it

The line may also become more prominent if your skin is exposed to sunlight, as UV exposure further stimulates melanin production. Wearing sunscreen on your bump if you're out in the sun can help minimise this.

## After Pregnancy {#s4}

Linea nigra fades after pregnancy as hormone levels return to their pre-pregnancy state. For most women, it becomes noticeably lighter within a few months of giving birth or stopping breastfeeding (breastfeeding keeps some pregnancy hormones elevated, which can slow the fading process). It may take up to a year to fade completely in some cases.

In women with naturally darker skin tones, the line may not disappear entirely, or may take longer to fade. This is not a cause for concern — it is simply a reflection of how melanin-rich skin responds to hormonal changes.

There is nothing you need to do to make linea nigra fade faster. Avoiding prolonged sun exposure on your bump can prevent it from darkening further, but it will fade in its own time after the hormonal shifts of pregnancy resolve.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Will linea nigra go away after birth?

Yes, for most women it fades within a few months after giving birth. If you are breastfeeding, it may take a little longer because some pregnancy hormones remain elevated during lactation. Women with darker skin tones may find it takes longer to fade, or that a faint line remains, which is completely normal.

### Does linea nigra predict gender?

No. The old wives' tale that the line ending at the belly button indicates a girl, and continuing above it indicates a boy, is a myth with no scientific basis. Linea nigra is caused by hormonal changes affecting melanin production — it has nothing to do with the sex of your baby.

### Is linea nigra the same as stretch marks?

No. They are different skin changes. Linea nigra is a single dark line down the centre of the abdomen caused by pigmentation. Stretch marks (striae gravidarum) are streaks or lines that appear on the abdomen, thighs, breasts, or hips when the skin stretches rapidly. Both are common and harmless.

*Speak to your midwife or GP if you have any concerns about skin changes during pregnancy, or if you notice any changes that seem unusual or accompanied by other symptoms.*
