# No Symptoms at 7 Weeks: Is Baby OK?

By 7 weeks pregnant, many women expect to be feeling the full force of morning sickness and exhaustion — so if you feel relatively normal, it can be unsettling. The honest answer is that having no symptoms at 7 weeks does not mean something is wrong. But it is understandable to feel anxious, and this page explains what is actually happening and what your options are.

## Why Some Women Have No Symptoms at 7 Weeks {#s1}

Seven weeks is around the time when hCG levels are rising towards their peak, so many women do start noticing nausea and fatigue in this window. But not everyone responds to pregnancy hormones in the same way. There are several reasons you might feel well at 7 weeks:

- **Individual hormone sensitivity varies.** Your body may simply be less reactive to rising hCG and oestrogen. This is a biological difference, not a sign that your hormone levels are low or that the pregnancy is struggling.
- **Symptoms may be subtle.** You might be mildly more tired than usual or slightly off certain foods without recognising these as pregnancy symptoms. Women who are busy or distracted sometimes only notice in retrospect.
- **Your symptoms may come later.** Some women do not develop noticeable nausea until 8 or 9 weeks. The timing of symptom onset varies considerably.
- **Some women simply do not get symptoms.** A meaningful proportion of pregnant women have very little or no nausea throughout the first trimester and go on to have healthy pregnancies. This is an accepted variant of normal.

## What the Absence of Symptoms Does — and Does Not — Mean {#s2}

It is worth being honest about what symptom levels can and cannot tell you. The presence of strong symptoms does not guarantee a healthy pregnancy, and the absence of symptoms does not mean the pregnancy is failing. Symptoms are an indirect and unreliable indicator of pregnancy health — they reflect how your body responds to hormones, not whether the embryo is developing well.

The most reliable way to assess how your pregnancy is progressing at 7 weeks is an ultrasound scan. A heartbeat visible on scan at 7–8 weeks is a reassuring sign. If you are anxious and your NHS booking appointment feels far away, a private early reassurance scan is widely available across the UK, typically from around 7 weeks, and usually costs between £50–£120 depending on the clinic.

## When to Contact Your Midwife or GP {#s3}

Having no symptoms on its own is not a reason to call your midwife urgently. However, do contact your midwife or GP if:

- You previously had noticeable symptoms that have suddenly and completely disappeared, combined with pain or bleeding
- You have any bright red bleeding
- You have one-sided or severe abdominal pain

**Call 999 or go to A&E immediately** if you have severe one-sided pain with dizziness, shoulder tip pain, or collapse — these can be signs of an ectopic pregnancy.

If you feel fine with no symptoms and no bleeding or pain, there is no clinical need to contact anyone urgently — though your midwife is always there to talk things through if you are worried.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Can I have a healthy pregnancy with no symptoms at 7 weeks?

Yes. Some women have minimal or no symptoms throughout the first trimester and go on to have uncomplicated pregnancies and healthy babies. The absence of symptoms is not a reliable sign that the pregnancy is in trouble. If you are concerned, the most reassuring thing you can do is book an early scan to see the heartbeat.

### Should I get a scan if I have no symptoms at 7 weeks?

A scan is the most reliable way to see how your pregnancy is progressing, but it is not medically necessary simply because you have no symptoms. Your NHS booking appointment and dating scan will be arranged in due course. If anxiety is significantly affecting your wellbeing, a private reassurance scan from around 7 weeks can provide peace of mind. Speak to your midwife or GP if you are struggling.

### Is it possible to have no symptoms at 7 weeks and then get them later?

Yes. Some women find their symptoms start later than expected — appearing at 8, 9, or even 10 weeks. Others have symptoms that come and go day to day. There is no set timetable, and a delay in symptom onset is not a warning sign.
