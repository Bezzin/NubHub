# Lemon as a Morning Sickness Remedy

Lemon is one of the simplest and most surprisingly effective natural remedies for morning sickness. It costs almost nothing, is safe to use in pregnancy, and can be tried in several different ways depending on whether it is the scent or the taste that you find helpful. This page explains why lemon may ease nausea and how to make the most of it.

## Why Lemon Helps Nausea

The sharp, clean scent of lemon is thought to interrupt nausea signals before they fully register. Scent is processed rapidly by the brain and has a direct effect on the nervous system, which is why aromatherapy approaches for nausea — including lemon — have attracted research interest. Some small studies examining lemon essential oil aromatherapy in pregnancy found that women who inhaled lemon scent reported lower nausea scores compared with a placebo, though the evidence is preliminary and more research is needed. That said, many pregnant women find the scent genuinely effective in the moment, and as it carries no risk, it is a very reasonable first thing to try.

Lemon juice may also offer a mild benefit through taste. The acidic, sharp flavour can cut through the queasy, heavy sensation that characterises pregnancy nausea, and the act of sipping something cold and tart can be grounding when a wave of sickness hits. Staying hydrated is important when nausea is high, and lemon can make plain water significantly more appealing when nothing else sounds drinkable.

## Simple Ways to Use Lemon

There are several practical ways to use lemon for morning sickness:

**Smell a fresh lemon.** Slice or halve a lemon and keep it within reach — on your bedside table, in your bag, or at your desk. When a wave of nausea comes, hold it under your nose and breathe slowly. This is one of the quickest methods to try and requires no preparation.

**Lemon in water.** Add a squeeze of fresh lemon juice to a glass of cold or room-temperature water and sip it slowly throughout the morning. This can make staying hydrated much easier when plain water triggers nausea. Avoid fizzy lemon drinks with high sugar content, as blood sugar dips can worsen nausea.

**Lemon in hot water.** A mug of hot water with a slice of lemon first thing in the morning is a gentle, warming way to start the day if cold drinks do not appeal.

**Lemon in herbal tea.** A squeeze of lemon in chamomile or peppermint tea (both generally considered safe in moderate amounts in pregnancy) combines a couple of nausea remedies in one drink.

**Lemon on food.** A few drops of lemon juice on plain rice, salad, or cold foods can make bland meals more appetising during the phase when very few things appeal. Cold, lemon-flavoured foods tend to have less smell than hot meals, which helps if food odours are a trigger for your nausea.

**Lemon sweets or lemon drops.** Some women find sucking a sharp lemon sweet helpful when nausea comes on suddenly and other options are not available. Choose ones without artificial sweeteners if possible.

What to avoid: highly concentrated lemon supplements, such as tablets or extracts, are not recommended without specific advice from your midwife or GP, as concentrated citrus compounds in supplement form have not been assessed for safety in pregnancy. Stick to lemon as food and drink in normal amounts.

## Lemon in Context: What Else Helps

Lemon works well as part of a broader approach to managing morning sickness. Other commonly used strategies include:

- **Ginger** — in biscuits, tea, or capsules; there is reasonable evidence that ginger reduces pregnancy nausea
- **Small, frequent meals** — keeping something in your stomach helps stabilise blood sugar and reduces acid build-up
- **Cold and bland foods** — plain crackers, rice, and cold snacks tend to have less smell and are easier to tolerate
- **Acupressure wristbands** — worn at the P6 pressure point; some women find them helpful, and they carry no risk
- **Vitamin B6** — your GP can advise on whether a supplement is appropriate if nausea is severe
- **Rest** — fatigue makes nausea worse, so rest whenever you can in the first trimester

If one remedy does not help on its own, try combining a few. Morning sickness is highly individual and what works well for one person may not work at all for another.

## When Morning Sickness Needs Medical Attention

Most morning sickness, while miserable, does not require medical treatment. However, you should contact your GP or midwife if:

- You are unable to keep any food or fluids down for more than 24 hours
- You are losing weight
- You feel very weak, dizzy, or faint
- You are passing very dark urine or not urinating (signs of dehydration)
- Vomiting is severe and affecting your ability to function

A small number of women develop a serious condition called hyperemesis gravidarum (HG), which involves persistent, severe vomiting and requires medical treatment. HG is not the same as ordinary morning sickness — it is a clinical condition that often needs IV fluids and medication. If you think you may have HG, speak to your GP rather than trying to manage it at home with natural remedies alone.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is lemon safe in pregnancy?

Yes. Lemon as fruit, juice, or in food and drink at normal amounts is completely safe during pregnancy. It provides a small amount of vitamin C, which is beneficial. The only thing to avoid is concentrated lemon supplements or extracts, which have not been assessed for safety in pregnancy — stick to lemon as you would eat or drink it ordinarily.

### Does smelling lemon really help with nausea?

Many women find the scent brings fast, if temporary, relief during a wave of sickness. Some small studies have found that lemon aromatherapy reduced self-reported nausea in pregnancy, though the evidence base is limited. As it is completely safe, costs nothing, and is easy to try, it is well worth giving it a go. Keep a halved lemon in a small pot or bag so it is ready when you need it.

### What is the best way to use lemon for morning sickness?

The two most practical approaches are keeping a cut lemon to smell when nausea hits, and adding fresh lemon juice to water to make staying hydrated easier. If neither of those appeals, try a squeeze of lemon in a mild herbal tea, or a sharp lemon sweet when you are out and about. Combine with other strategies — small frequent meals, rest, and ginger — for the best overall effect.
