# Morning Sickness at Night: Why It Happens & How to Cope

The name "morning sickness" is one of the great misnomers of pregnancy. For many women, nausea strikes at night - sometimes only at night. If you're fine all day but retching by 8pm, you're not imagining it, and you're not alone.

## Why Morning Sickness Happens at Night {#why-night}

Despite the name, nausea in pregnancy can happen at any time. Nighttime nausea is particularly common for several reasons:

### Accumulated Fatigue
By evening, you've been up all day. **Fatigue worsens nausea** - they feed each other in a vicious cycle. By nightfall, your body is tired, and tired bodies are more sensitive to nausea triggers.

### Empty Stomach
If you've been eating erratically during the day (common with nausea), your stomach may be empty by evening. **An empty stomach makes nausea worse** because stomach acid irritates the lining with nothing to digest.

### Digestive Slowdown
Pregnancy hormones (especially progesterone) slow digestion throughout the day. By evening, food from lunch may still be sitting in your stomach, causing reflux and nausea.

### Hormone Fluctuations
hCG levels fluctuate throughout the day. While they often peak in the morning (hence "morning" sickness), some women's patterns are different, with peaks in the evening.

### Cooking Smells
Dinner time means cooking smells, which are a major trigger for many women. By the time you've been around food preparation all evening, your nausea may have built up.

### Lying Down
When you lie down (which you do in the evening), gravity stops helping keep stomach contents down. **Acid reflux** is more likely when you're horizontal, causing nausea.

## How Common Is Nighttime Nausea? {#how-common}

Very common. Despite the name:

- **Morning only**: About 30% of women
- **Evening only**: About 15-20% of women
- **All day**: About 30-40% of women
- **No set pattern**: About 10-20% of women

So roughly **half of women** experience nausea in the evening or at night, whether exclusively or alongside daytime symptoms.

### "All-Day Sickness"

Many women find their nausea is actually worse at night than in the morning. The accumulated fatigue, cooking smells, and digestive slowdown all contribute. Some women call this "all-day sickness" - though it often peaks in the evening.

Read our guide to [all-day sickness vs morning sickness](/symptoms/all-day-sickness) for more.

## Triggers That Make Night Worse {#triggers}

### Cooking and Food Smells
The number one trigger for evening nausea. Preparing dinner (or even being in the house while someone else does) can trigger nausea.

### Eating a Large Dinner
A big meal late in the day sits in your stomach longer (thanks to progesterone slowing digestion), causing bloating, reflux, and nausea.

### Lying Down Too Soon After Eating
If you eat dinner at 7pm and lie down at 9pm, the food hasn't had time to digest. It can reflux back up, causing nausea.

### Evening Fatigue
By evening, your energy reserves are depleted. Tiredness amplifies nausea.

### Brushing Teeth
The gag reflex is heightened in pregnancy. Brushing teeth before bed can trigger vomiting.

### Prenatal Vitamins
If you take your prenatal vitamins in the evening, they may trigger nausea. Try taking them in the morning with food instead.

## Practical Tips for Nighttime Nausea {#practical-tips}

### Change Your Eating Pattern
- **Eat your main meal at lunchtime** - lighter dinner
- **Eat small, frequent meals** throughout the day
- **Have a bedtime snack** - plain crackers, toast, or a banana
- **Don't eat large meals** within 2-3 hours of lying down

### Manage Cooking Smells
- **Ask someone else to cook** - this is the #1 tip
- **Use cold foods** - sandwiches, salads (less smell)
- **Cook in bulk** when you feel well, freeze portions
- **Avoid the kitchen** during cooking
- **Open windows** and use extractor fans

### Position Matters
- **Stay upright for 2-3 hours** after eating
- **Prop yourself up with pillows** when you lie down
- **Lie on your left side** - improves digestion
- **Avoid lying flat**

### Bedtime Routine
- **Have dry crackers by your bed** - eat one if you wake up nauseous
- **Keep water nearby** - sip if you wake
- **Try ginger tea** before bed
- **Wear acupressure wristbands** to bed

### Brushing Teeth
- **Use a small-headed, soft toothbrush**
- **Brush at a different time** - try earlier in the evening
- **Use children's toothpaste** (less strong flavour)
- **Try brushing without toothpaste** and use mouthwash instead

### Prenatal Vitamins
- **Take them in the morning** with food, not at night
- **Try taking them at lunch** if morning doesn't work
- **Ask your GP about switching brands** if yours triggers nausea

## Foods That Help at Night {#foods-help}

### Bland, Easy-to-Digest Foods
- **Plain crackers** or digestive biscuits
- **Toast** (plain or with a little butter)
- **Bananas** - easy to digest, contain potassium
- **Plain rice** or plain pasta
- **Boiled potatoes**
- **Porridge** - easy on the stomach

### Foods That Soothe
- **Ginger** - tea, biscuits, sweets, crystallised ginger
- **Peppermint tea** - can settle the stomach
- **Cold foods** - less smell, easier to face
- **Plain yoghurt** - cooling and easy
- **Ice lollies** - hydrating and cooling

### Foods to Avoid in the Evening
- **Spicy foods** - can trigger reflux
- **Fatty or fried foods** - slow to digest
- **Large meals** - sit in your stomach all night
- **Citrus fruits** - can trigger reflux
- **Chocolate** - relaxes the oesophageal valve
- **Caffeine** - can worsen nausea

## When to Seek Help {#when-help}

While nighttime nausea is usually normal, contact your GP if:

- **You're vomiting 3+ times per day** (including at night)
- **You can't keep food or water down**
- **You're losing weight**
- **You feel dizzy, weak, or dehydrated**
- **You're vomiting blood**
- **Nausea is affecting your sleep significantly**

You may need prescription anti-sickness medication. Read our guide to [anti-sickness medication in pregnancy](/symptoms/anti-sickness-medication-pregnancy).

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Why does my morning sickness happen at night?

Nighttime nausea is common due to accumulated fatigue, an empty stomach by evening, digestive slowdown from pregnancy hormones, cooking smells at dinner time, and acid reflux when you lie down. Despite the name, "morning sickness" can happen at any time.

### Is nighttime nausea normal in pregnancy?

Yes. About 15-20% of women have nausea only at night, and many more have it both day and night. The name "morning sickness" is misleading - nausea can strike at any time.

### How do I stop nausea at night in pregnancy?

Eat your main meal at lunchtime, have a bland bedtime snack, stay upright for 2-3 hours after eating, prop yourself up with pillows, avoid cooking smells, and try ginger tea before bed. If nausea is severe, ask your GP about anti-sickness medication.

### Can morning sickness keep me awake?

Yes. Severe nausea can make it hard to fall asleep or wake you up. Keeping crackers by your bed and eating one if you wake can help. If nighttime nausea is significantly affecting your sleep, talk to your GP.

### Should I take my prenatal vitamins at night?

If vitamins trigger nausea, try taking them in the morning with food instead. If they still cause problems, try at lunch. Some women find taking them before bed helps them sleep through any nausea - experiment to find what works for you.
