# Perinatal Mental Health Services in the UK: What's Available

Mental health difficulties during pregnancy and the first year after birth are common. Anxiety, depression, and other conditions can affect anyone, regardless of their circumstances or how much they wanted their baby. The good news is that the UK has dedicated specialist services — and most support is available free through the NHS. Knowing what exists and how to access it makes it easier to reach out.

## What Perinatal Mental Health Means

"Perinatal" refers to the period from early pregnancy through to around one year after birth. Perinatal mental health services cover conditions that arise or worsen during this time, including:

- **Antenatal depression and anxiety** — depression or anxiety during pregnancy
- **Postnatal depression** — which can begin any time in the first year after birth
- **Perinatal OCD** — intrusive thoughts and compulsive behaviours linked to pregnancy or the baby
- **Birth-related PTSD** — following a difficult or traumatic birth experience
- **Postpartum psychosis** — a rare but serious condition requiring urgent care (call 999 or go to A&E immediately if you suspect this)
- **Tokophobia** — severe fear of childbirth

Many of these conditions are treatable and respond well to the right support. Asking for help early generally leads to better outcomes.

## What Services Are Available on the NHS

### Your GP

Your GP is the most accessible first point of contact for any mental health concern. They can assess how you are feeling, prescribe medication if appropriate, and refer you to more specialist services. Do not wait until things feel critical — GPs are used to supporting people through perinatal mental health difficulties, and early conversations tend to lead to faster help.

### Your Midwife

Your midwife will ask about your mental health at every antenatal appointment. Be honest — these questions are not designed to catch you out but to identify whether you need additional support. Your midwife can refer you directly to a perinatal mental health team or other services, and can flag your case for extra attention if needed.

### Specialist Perinatal Mental Health Teams

Most NHS regions in England now have a specialist perinatal mental health team. These are multidisciplinary teams made up of psychiatrists, psychologists, specialist midwives, and other professionals who work specifically with pregnant people and new parents. They offer assessment, therapy, medication reviews, and ongoing support. Referrals usually come from a GP or midwife.

If you are already known to mental health services, it is worth telling your care team you are pregnant so they can consider perinatal specialist input.

### NHS Talking Therapies (formerly IAPT)

NHS Talking Therapies offers Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT) and other evidence-based therapies for anxiety and depression. In many areas you can self-refer directly without needing a GP referral, though waiting times vary. This service is available throughout pregnancy and after birth.

### Health Visitors

Health visitors are nurses or midwives with specialist training who support families from around 28 weeks of pregnancy until a child's fifth birthday. They carry out a routine postnatal mental health assessment and can refer on to other services. If you are struggling after birth, your health visitor is an important contact.

## Charitable and Third-Sector Support

Alongside NHS services, a number of charities offer support:

- **PANDAS Foundation** (pandasfoundation.org.uk) — peer support and a helpline for perinatal mental health difficulties
- **Tommy's** (tommys.org) — information and a free midwife helpline during working hours
- **Mind** (mind.org.uk) — general mental health support including local groups
- **Maternal Mental Health Alliance** (maternalmentalhealthalliance.org) — advocates for services and provides information about what is available in your region
- **Birth Trauma Association** (birthtraumaassociation.org.uk) — support for people affected by traumatic birth experiences

## How to Access Support

The simplest path is:

1. Tell your midwife or GP honestly how you are feeling — even if you find it difficult to put into words
2. They will assess your needs and discuss options with you
3. You may be referred to specialist perinatal mental health services, talking therapies, or both
4. You can also self-refer to NHS Talking Therapies in many areas via nhs.uk
5. If you are in crisis, call 999, go to A&E, or call the Samaritans on 116 123 (free, 24/7)

All NHS perinatal mental health services are free. You do not need to be severely unwell to ask for help.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How do I access perinatal mental health services?

Start by telling your GP or midwife how you are feeling. Be as honest as you can, even if the feelings seem hard to explain. They can refer you to specialist perinatal mental health services, talking therapies, or both. In many areas you can also self-refer to NHS Talking Therapies directly through the NHS website without needing a GP appointment first.

### Is perinatal mental health support free?

Yes. All NHS perinatal mental health services are free, including GP and midwife appointments, specialist team assessments, talking therapies, and medication if prescribed. Charitable services such as PANDAS and Tommy's are also free to access.

### What if I am worried I will be judged or that someone will take my baby?

Many people avoid seeking help because of this fear. It is worth knowing that healthcare professionals are primarily there to support you, and asking for help is generally seen as a positive and protective step. In the vast majority of cases, accessing mental health support does not lead to any involvement from social services. Your midwife or GP can explain what to expect before you share anything you are worried about.

Always speak to your midwife or GP for personal advice about your mental health during pregnancy — early support makes a real difference.
