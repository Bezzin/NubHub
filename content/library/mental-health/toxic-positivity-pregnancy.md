# Toxic Positivity in Pregnancy

"Just enjoy it — you'll miss being pregnant!" "At least you have a baby on the way." "Everything happens for a reason." These phrases sound kind, but they often shut down genuine feelings. That's toxic positivity — the insistence that you must stay positive, even when things are genuinely hard.

## What toxic positivity looks like in pregnancy
It shows up in comments that minimise your pain: dismissing morning sickness as "worth it," glossing over birth fears, or telling you not to worry because "stress harms the baby." The message underneath is: stop feeling bad. The effect is: now you feel bad *and* guilty for feeling bad.

## Why it's harmful
Valid emotions don't disappear when ignored — they go underground. Research on emotional suppression shows it increases anxiety and reduces wellbeing. In pregnancy, pressure to be positive can delay women seeking help for antenatal depression or anxiety because they've been told their feelings are unacceptable.

## How to respond
- **Acknowledge both things.** "I'm grateful AND I'm finding this really hard." Two truths can coexist.
- **Redirect the conversation.** "I know you're trying to help. Right now I just need to vent, not fix it."
- **Protect your boundaries.** Limit time with people who consistently dismiss your feelings.
- **Find validating spaces.** Honest antenatal groups, forums, or a counsellor give you room to be real.

## The difference between support and positivity
Real support sits with you in the hard moment. It says "that sounds awful" before it says "it'll be fine." Positivity without validation is a wall, not a bridge.

## If you're the one being positive
If a pregnant friend is struggling, resist the urge to cheer them up. Ask: "Do you want comfort or solutions?" Often, being heard is the solution.

## A gentler way
You don't have to be positive to be resilient. Acknowledging difficulty is not pessimism — it's honesty, and honesty is where real strength begins.
