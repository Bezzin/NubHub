# Toxic Positivity in Pregnancy

"Just enjoy it — you'll miss being pregnant!" "At least you have a baby on the way." "Everything happens for a reason." These phrases sound kind, but they often shut down genuine feelings. That's toxic positivity — the insistence that you must stay positive, even when things are genuinely hard.

Pregnancy can be wonderful, but it can also be exhausting, frightening, and emotionally complicated. When the people around you respond to your real feelings with relentless optimism, it can leave you feeling more alone than before you spoke.

## What toxic positivity looks like in pregnancy

It shows up in comments that minimise your pain: dismissing morning sickness as "worth it," glossing over birth fears, or telling you not to worry because "stress harms the baby." The message underneath is: stop feeling bad. The effect is: now you feel bad *and* guilty for feeling bad.

Toxic positivity in pregnancy can come from well-meaning people — friends, family members, even healthcare professionals. It is rarely intended to dismiss you. But intent and impact are two different things. When someone responds to your fear of miscarriage with "just think positive thoughts," or to your birth anxiety with "every woman goes through it, you'll be fine," the effect is the same: your experience is dismissed and you are left feeling that something is wrong with you for feeling the way you do.

Common examples include:

- Being told to "just relax" when you express worry about a scan result
- Hearing "at least it's not serious" in response to a difficult symptom or diagnosis
- Being reminded how lucky you are when you are struggling emotionally
- Having birth fears minimised with "women have been doing it forever"
- Being told to enjoy every moment when you are suffering from severe nausea or exhaustion

## Why it's harmful

Valid emotions do not disappear when they are ignored — they go underground. Emotional suppression is associated with increased anxiety and reduced overall wellbeing. In pregnancy, pressure to be positive can delay women seeking help for antenatal depression or anxiety, because they have been told, implicitly or explicitly, that their feelings are unacceptable.

Antenatal depression affects a meaningful number of pregnant women and is at least as common as postnatal depression, yet it receives far less attention. One reason for this is the cultural expectation that pregnancy is a uniformly joyful experience. When that expectation is reinforced by the people around you, it becomes harder to admit you are struggling — and harder still to ask for help.

The experience of having your feelings dismissed can also create distance in relationships that you may need most during this time. If you learn that expressing genuine emotion leads to a cheerful shutdown, you stop expressing genuine emotion. That isolation can compound anxiety and low mood.

## How to respond

Responding to toxic positivity is genuinely difficult, especially when it comes from people you care about. A few approaches that can help:

- **Acknowledge both things.** "I'm grateful AND I'm finding this really hard." Two truths can coexist, and saying this out loud can sometimes help the other person understand that you are not being negative — you are being honest.
- **Redirect the conversation.** "I know you're trying to help. Right now I just need to vent, not fix it." Most people genuinely want to support you and will respond well when you tell them what you actually need.
- **Protect your boundaries.** If certain people consistently respond to your feelings with dismissal, it is reasonable to limit what you share with them. You do not have to perform happiness for anyone.
- **Find validating spaces.** Honest antenatal groups, peer support communities, or a counsellor can give you room to be real. Spaces where mixed feelings are welcome are genuinely valuable during pregnancy.

## The difference between support and positivity

Real support sits with you in the hard moment. It says "that sounds awful" before it says "it'll be fine." Positivity without validation is a wall, not a bridge. The most helpful thing someone can do when you are struggling is not to cheer you up, but to make you feel heard.

A supportive response does not need to fix anything. "That sounds really hard, I'm here" is more valuable than a list of reasons why everything will work out. If you find someone in your life who can offer this, lean on them.

## If you're the one being positive

If a pregnant friend or family member is struggling, resist the urge to immediately cheer them up. Ask first: "Do you want comfort or solutions?" Often, being heard is the solution. Sitting quietly with someone in a hard moment, without rushing to make it better, is a genuinely kind act — and a rarer one than it should be.

## A gentler way

You do not have to be positive to be resilient. Acknowledging difficulty is not pessimism — it is honesty, and honesty is where real strength begins. If you are finding pregnancy hard, in any way, that is a legitimate experience. It does not mean you are ungrateful, anxious, or not ready to be a parent. It means you are human.

If your emotional wellbeing is significantly affected during pregnancy, speak to your midwife or GP. Perinatal mental health support is available on the NHS, and you do not need to be in crisis to ask for it.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Is it normal to have negative feelings during pregnancy?

Yes, completely. Many pregnant women experience fear, sadness, ambivalence, frustration, and anxiety alongside happiness and excitement. Having negative feelings does not mean you are a bad parent or that something is wrong with your pregnancy. If negative feelings are persistent or affecting your daily life, speak to your midwife or GP.

### How do I tell someone their positivity is not helping?

It can feel uncomfortable, but being direct and kind at the same time is possible. Try something like: "I appreciate you trying to help — right now what I really need is just for someone to listen." Most people respond well to being told clearly what you need, rather than being left to guess.

### Where can I get emotional support during pregnancy?

Your midwife is your first point of contact. They can refer you to perinatal mental health services, peer support groups, or NHS Talking Therapies. You can also self-refer to NHS Talking Therapies in most areas. If you are in emotional distress and need to talk to someone urgently, the Samaritans are available 24 hours a day on 116 123.
