# Panic Attacks in Early Pregnancy

A panic attack is a sudden, intense surge of fear that comes with physical symptoms like a racing heart, shortness of breath, and dizziness. Panic attacks can be frightening at any time, but in early pregnancy they carry an added layer of worry — you may fear you are causing harm to your baby, or that the physical symptoms mean something is medically wrong. The reassuring truth is that panic attacks are common in pregnancy, they are treatable, and they do not harm your baby.

## Recognising a panic attack in pregnancy {#section-1}

Typical symptoms include a pounding or racing heartbeat, chest tightness, feeling faint or dizzy, shortness of breath, tingling in the hands or face, sweating, trembling, a sense of unreality, and an overwhelming urge to flee or escape. In pregnancy, some of these sensations can be confused with normal physical changes — your heart rate is naturally higher and your breathing patterns shift — but panic attacks come on suddenly and tend to peak within about ten minutes before gradually subsiding.

One thing that makes panic attacks in early pregnancy particularly distressing is that the physical symptoms can feel like they might be a sign of something going wrong. Chest tightness and breathlessness can feel alarming when you are pregnant. If you have never had a panic attack before, the experience can be very disorienting. It may help to know that panic attacks, however intense, are not medically dangerous for you or your baby.

## Why panic attacks are more common in early pregnancy {#section-2}

Early pregnancy involves rapid hormonal changes — particularly rising levels of oestrogen and progesterone — which can affect mood regulation and increase anxiety. The first trimester is also a time of significant uncertainty: many people are acutely aware of the risk of miscarriage, and for those who have experienced previous loss, anxiety can be especially intense.

Other contributing factors include exhaustion (first trimester fatigue is often severe), the stress of keeping the pregnancy secret before announcing, physical discomfort from nausea and other symptoms, and changes in blood sugar and blood pressure. If you have a history of anxiety disorder or panic disorder, pregnancy can sometimes intensify these tendencies, particularly in the early weeks.

You are not doing anything wrong if you are having panic attacks. They are a sign that your nervous system is under strain, not a character flaw or an indication that you cannot cope with pregnancy.

## Safe ways to cope with a panic attack {#section-3}

When a panic attack begins, the following techniques can help reduce its intensity and duration:

**Slow breathing**: Breathe in through your nose for four counts, hold gently for four counts, then breathe out slowly through your mouth for six counts. Focusing on a longer out-breath helps activate the parasympathetic nervous system and signals to your body that the threat has passed.

**Grounding — the 5-4-3-2-1 technique**: Name five things you can see, four things you can physically feel (the texture of a surface, your feet on the floor), three things you can hear, two things you can smell, and one thing you can taste. This directs attention away from internal panic sensations and back to the present moment.

**Remind yourself it will pass**: Panic attacks are time-limited. Reminding yourself that the feeling will peak and subside — even when it feels relentless — can prevent the spiral of panic about the panic itself, which often prolongs the episode.

**Move to a calm environment if possible**: If you are in a noisy or busy place, moving somewhere quieter can help. Sitting or lying down can also reduce the lightheadedness associated with hyperventilation.

Avoid using panic attacks as a reason to avoid situations or activities. This can strengthen anxiety over time. If avoidance is becoming a pattern, seek professional support.

## Getting support and treatment in the UK {#section-4}

If panic attacks are occurring frequently or significantly affecting your daily life, it is important to tell your midwife or GP. You do not need to manage this alone, and effective support is available.

Your midwife can refer you to a **perinatal mental health team** — specialist services that support pregnant women and new parents with mental health difficulties, available in most areas of the NHS. Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT) is an evidence-based treatment for panic disorder and is recommended by NICE guidelines. It can be delivered during pregnancy and is safe.

Your GP may also be able to refer you to a talking therapies service (IAPT/NHS Talking Therapies) where you can access CBT without a long wait in many areas. Some medications for anxiety are compatible with pregnancy, but this is a decision to make with medical guidance based on your specific circumstances.

If you find yourself calling 111 or attending A&E during a panic attack, you will be assessed appropriately. It is always right to seek help if you are unsure whether symptoms are a panic attack or something that needs urgent attention.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Can a panic attack harm my baby?

No. A brief episode of stress hormones from a panic attack does not harm a healthy developing baby. The main impact of panic attacks is on your own wellbeing and quality of life, which is exactly why seeking treatment matters. Ongoing, unmanaged severe anxiety is worth addressing — but a panic attack itself is not a medical danger to your pregnancy.

### Are panic attacks common in early pregnancy?

Yes, particularly in people with a history of anxiety, panic disorder, or previous pregnancy loss. Hormonal changes, fatigue, uncertainty, and the physical discomfort of the first trimester all create conditions that can increase anxiety. You are far from alone — perinatal mental health services exist precisely because anxiety in pregnancy is a recognised and common experience.

### What if my panic attack symptoms feel like a physical emergency?

If you have chest pain that does not settle, fainting, heavy bleeding, or any symptom that feels different from your usual panic attacks, contact 111 or attend A&E to rule out other causes. It is always better to be checked when in doubt. Once physical causes have been ruled out, your GP or midwife can support you with the anxiety side.
