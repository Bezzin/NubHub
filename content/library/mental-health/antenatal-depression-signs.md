# Antenatal Depression: The Signs to Watch For

Antenatal depression affects around one in ten women in the UK, yet it remains underdiagnosed because symptoms can be mistaken for normal pregnancy tiredness or mood swings. Knowing the signs helps you spot it early and seek support before it escalates. You do not need to feel happy every day during pregnancy — but persistent low mood that lasts more than two weeks is worth discussing with your midwife or GP.

## Common Signs of Antenatal Depression {#section-1}

Signs include persistent sadness, inability to feel excitement about the pregnancy, withdrawing from friends and family, anxiety about the birth, feeling unable to cope, loss of interest in usual activities, changes in sleep or appetite beyond pregnancy norms, and thoughts of self-harm. Any of these lasting more than two weeks warrant a conversation with your midwife or GP.

It is also worth knowing that antenatal depression does not always look like crying. Some women describe it as numbness or feeling detached from their baby and the pregnancy entirely. Others find they become very irritable, snap at people they care about, and feel guilty about it. Low mood in pregnancy can be quiet and internal rather than visibly distressing, which is one reason it goes unrecognised for so long.

Physical symptoms can overlap too. Fatigue, poor sleep, and changes in appetite are all common in pregnancy anyway, which makes it harder to spot when they are driven by depression rather than hormonal or physical changes. If these symptoms feel extreme, persistent, and are affecting your daily life, that is a signal to seek help.

## Risk Factors and When to Seek Help {#section-2}

You are at higher risk if you have a personal or family history of depression, experienced depression in a previous pregnancy, lack a support network, have financial or relationship stress, or are going through a difficult pregnancy. A history of anxiety is also a risk factor, and the two conditions often occur together.

Other situations that can increase vulnerability include an unplanned pregnancy, pregnancy complications such as hyperemesis gravidarum or a threatened miscarriage, a history of trauma or adverse childhood experiences, and significant life stressors coinciding with the pregnancy. You do not need to meet every risk factor to develop antenatal depression — it can affect anyone.

Help is available through NHS perinatal mental health services and your maternity team can refer you. You do not need to wait until things feel unbearable. Talking therapies such as cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) are effective and safe in pregnancy. In some cases, medication may be recommended; your GP can discuss which options are appropriate for your situation. Many women find that simply being heard and assessed brings significant relief.

## What to Expect When You Ask for Help {#seeking-help}

Many women worry about being judged if they admit to struggling during pregnancy. In practice, midwives and GPs are trained to ask about mental health and take it seriously. At your booking appointment and again later in pregnancy, you will typically be asked a couple of brief questions about your mood — this is routine and designed specifically to catch antenatal depression early.

If you need more support, your midwife or GP can refer you to a perinatal mental health team. These are specialist NHS teams who work with women before and after birth, and they can offer therapy, medication advice, and ongoing monitoring. Waiting times vary by area, so raise concerns as early as you can.

If you are having thoughts of harming yourself, speak to your GP or midwife urgently. You can also call NHS 111 or, in a crisis, contact the Samaritans on 116 123 at any time of day.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### How is antenatal depression diagnosed?

Your GP or midwife may use a questionnaire such as the Whooley questions or the Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS) to screen for depression, followed by a fuller assessment if needed. These are short, straightforward questions about how you have been feeling over the past week or two.

### Can antenatal depression develop in the first trimester?

Yes. It can start at any point in pregnancy, and the first trimester is a common time due to hormonal shifts, fatigue, nausea, and the anxiety of early pregnancy when miscarriage risk is at its highest.

### Will I be referred to a specialist?

You may be referred to a perinatal mental health team for specialist support during pregnancy and after birth. The level of support offered depends on how you are feeling and what your care team thinks you need.
