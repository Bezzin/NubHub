# Antenatal Depression: The Signs to Watch For

Antenatal depression affects around one in ten women in the UK, yet it remains underdiagnosed because symptoms can be mistaken for normal pregnancy tiredness or mood swings. Knowing the signs helps you spot it early and seek support before it escalates.

## Common signs of antenatal depression {#section-1}

Signs include persistent sadness, inability to feel excitement about the pregnancy, withdrawing from friends and family, anxiety about the birth, feeling unable to cope, loss of interest in usual activities, changes in sleep or appetite beyond pregnancy norms, and thoughts of self-harm. Any of these lasting more than two weeks warrant a conversation with your midwife or GP.

## Risk factors and when to seek help {#section-2}

You are at higher risk if you have a personal or family history of depression, experienced depression in a previous pregnancy, lack a support network, have financial or relationship stress, or are going through a difficult pregnancy. Help is available through NHS perinatal mental health services and your maternity team can refer you.

## FAQ {#faq}

### How is antenatal depression diagnosed?

Your GP or midwife may use a questionnaire such as the Whooley questions or EPDS to screen for depression, followed by a fuller assessment if needed.

### Can antenatal depression develop in the first trimester?

Yes. It can start at any point in pregnancy, and the first trimester is a common time due to hormonal shifts and uncertainty.

### Will I be referred to a specialist?

You may be referred to a perinatal mental health team for specialist support during pregnancy and after birth.
