# Coping with an Unplanned Pregnancy

An unplanned pregnancy can feel like the ground has vanished beneath you. Whether it's shock, fear, anger, or numbness — whatever you're feeling is valid. This is a big moment, and you deserve clear information and compassionate support, not pressure.

## First, Give Yourself Time

You do not have to decide anything today. The urgency you feel is often emotional, not practical. Unless there is a medical reason to act quickly, give yourself space to breathe and process before making any decision about the pregnancy. Talking to someone you trust, or reaching out to a neutral counselling service, can help you think more clearly without feeling rushed.

It is normal for your feelings to shift day by day in the early period after finding out. Shock can give way to grief, then a flicker of something else entirely — and none of those shifts need to be permanent or decisive.

## Your Options

You have three paths, and each is legitimate:

- **Continuing the pregnancy and parenting.** Support exists — financial, practical, and emotional. Your GP can refer you to antenatal care, and organisations like Family Lives offer practical guidance for parents in unexpected circumstances.
- **Continuing the pregnancy and placing the baby for adoption.** This is a valid, caring choice. An independent social worker or adoption agency can explain how the process works in England, Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland.
- **Ending the pregnancy (abortion).** Legal and accessible in the UK through NHS services. You can self-refer through BPAS or MSI Reproductive Choices, or ask your GP. Services are available at no cost on the NHS.

Information on all three is available from Brook, BPAS, MSI Reproductive Choices, and the Family Planning Association. A neutral options counsellor can help you think things through without pushing you in any direction.

## Processing the Emotions

Unplanned pregnancy can bring grief for the future you expected, anger at circumstances or a partner, fear about money, judgement, or your body, and even relief mixed with guilt. All of these can coexist. You are not a bad person for having complicated feelings.

Some people feel certain quickly; others take weeks. Neither is more valid than the other. There is no correct emotional response to this situation.

## Practical Steps

- **Confirm the pregnancy.** A home test and, if you wish, a scan at a sexual health clinic or GP confirms how many weeks you are — which may be relevant to your decision.
- **Talk to someone safe.** A trusted friend, partner, or counsellor — or a helpline if you would rather stay anonymous. Brook, the Samaritans, and the British Pregnancy Advisory Service all offer confidential support.
- **Write it down.** Listing your fears, your resources, and what matters most can bring surprising clarity. You do not have to share the list with anyone.
- **Avoid pressure.** People with strong views may push their opinions. Your body, your life, your decision — and no one else has the right to make it for you.

## If You're Continuing the Pregnancy

Tell your GP as soon as you feel ready to access antenatal care. If the pregnancy was unplanned, you may find it hard to feel positive for a while — that is common and does not predict how you will feel when your baby arrives or what kind of parent you will be. Many parents who start from a place of shock go on to feel fully bonded and settled.

If anxiety or low mood is significant, let your GP or midwife know at your booking appointment. Perinatal mental health support is available through the NHS and you do not need to manage difficult feelings alone.

## Looking After Yourself

Sleep, eat, and stay hydrated as best you can. Avoid using alcohol or substances as a way to cope — they can increase anxiety and affect how clearly you're able to think. If you feel hopeless, panicked, or unable to function, contact your GP, call 111, or reach a crisis line the same day.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is it normal to feel nothing when you find out you're unintentionally pregnant?

Yes. Emotional numbness or a sense of detachment is a common first response to unexpected news. It does not mean you don't care — it is often the mind's way of protecting itself while you begin to process. Feelings usually arrive in waves over the days and weeks that follow.

### How long do I have to decide what to do?

This depends on which option you are considering. For abortion, earlier is generally safer and offers more options, so it is worth seeking advice promptly even if you are still undecided. For parenting or adoption, there is no set deadline, though accessing antenatal care early is beneficial for your health. A GP or neutral counsellor can help you understand the timelines relevant to your situation.

### What support is available if I continue an unplanned pregnancy?

NHS antenatal care is available regardless of your circumstances. Financial support may include Sure Start Maternity Grant, Child Benefit, and Universal Credit depending on your situation. Local children's centres and organisations such as Home-Start offer practical and emotional support during the early parenting period.
