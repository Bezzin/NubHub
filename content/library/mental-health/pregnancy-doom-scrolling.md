## Why Doom Scrolling Starts in Pregnancy {#section-1}

Pregnancy brings a natural drive to protect your baby, which can lead to searching for reassurance online. Unfortunately, this often tips into doom scrolling, where you endlessly read about worst-case scenarios, complications, and rare risks. Social media algorithms amplify this by showing you more of what you engage with, so reading one worrying story leads to more. What begins as seeking information becomes a cycle that feeds anxiety rather than easing it.

## How It Affects Your Wellbeing {#section-2}

Constant exposure to frightening content can heighten pregnancy anxiety, disrupt sleep, and make it harder to enjoy this time. You may start to expect the worst, second-guess every symptom, and feel unable to switch off. Doom scrolling can also distort your sense of risk, making rare complications feel common. Over time, this affects your mood, relationships, and ability to relax, which is not good for you or your baby.

## Practical Ways to Break the Cycle {#section-3}

Start by setting boundaries. Use screen time limits on social media apps, particularly in the hour before bed. Unfollow accounts that post anxiety-inducing content and follow trusted UK sources such as the NHS and Tommy's instead. Try the stop technique: when you notice yourself scrolling, say stop aloud, close the app, and do something grounding, such as making a cup of tea, stretching, or texting a friend. Replace the habit with a calming activity.

## Healthier Ways to Seek Information {#section-4}

If you have a specific worry, write it down and save it for your next midwife appointment rather than searching late at night. Trusted websites like the NHS pregnancy pages and the Nub Hub guides offer balanced information. If you find yourself repeatedly searching the same question, it may be a sign that anxiety, not lack of information, is the issue. Speaking to your midwife or a perinatal mental health professional can help you address the root cause.

## FAQ {#faq}

### Why do I keep reading scary pregnancy stories?

Pregnancy creates a strong protective instinct, and seeking information is natural. But algorithms show you more of what you engage with, so reading worrying content leads to more of it, creating a cycle.

### How can I stop doom scrolling in pregnancy?

Set screen time limits, unfollow anxiety-inducing accounts, follow trusted sources, and use a stop technique when you notice yourself scrolling. Replacing the habit with a calming activity helps break the cycle.

### When should I seek help for pregnancy anxiety?

If anxiety is affecting your sleep, mood, or daily life, or if you cannot stop worrying despite trying, speak to your midwife or GP. Perinatal mental health support is available and effective.
