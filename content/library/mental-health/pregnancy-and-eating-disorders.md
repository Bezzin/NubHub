# Pregnancy and Eating Disorders: Support Guide

If you have a history of an eating disorder — or are currently struggling with one — pregnancy can bring a uniquely complex set of challenges. Body changes happen quickly, weight gain is expected and encouraged, and food is constantly discussed in antenatal appointments. At the same time, many people find that pregnancy motivates them to seek support they had not sought before. Whatever your situation, you are not alone, and help is available.

## Why Pregnancy Can Be Difficult With an Eating Disorder History {#section-1}

Eating disorders are serious mental health conditions, and pregnancy does not switch those thoughts off. Some of the specific challenges you might face include:

**Body changes happening faster than you expected.** Your body will change significantly during pregnancy, and those changes can feel outside your control. For someone with a history of anorexia, bulimia, binge eating disorder, or orthorexia, this loss of control over your body can be distressing.

**Weight being monitored and discussed.** Midwives and doctors will ask about your weight and nutrition. Routine weigh-ins at appointments, while not always necessary, can be triggering. You can ask your midwife to discuss with you how they handle weight monitoring and whether adjustments can be made.

**Morning sickness creating a complicated relationship with food.** Nausea in early pregnancy can interfere with eating, and for some people, that starts to feel familiar in an uncomfortable way. If nausea is causing you to restrict your intake significantly, let your midwife know.

**Social pressure and unwanted comments.** People may comment on your bump size, your eating, or your body. These comments — however well-intentioned — can be hard to handle if you are already managing difficult thoughts around food and weight.

**Postnatal anxiety about your body.** It is worth being aware that the postnatal period carries its own challenges. Planning for support before and after birth is worthwhile.

## Getting Support During Pregnancy {#section-2}

The most important step you can take is telling your midwife about your eating disorder history, even if you feel your symptoms are currently managed. This is not about being judged — it is about making sure your care plan reflects your needs.

**Perinatal mental health services.** Your midwife can refer you to specialist perinatal mental health support, which exists specifically to support pregnant and postnatal people with mental health conditions, including eating disorders. These teams work closely with maternity care and can provide tailored support.

**Eating disorder specialists.** If you are already under the care of an eating disorder service, make sure they know you are pregnant so they can coordinate care with your maternity team. If you are not currently receiving support but feel you need it, your GP can make a referral to the NHS eating disorder service in your area.

**Beat Eating Disorders.** Beat is the UK's leading eating disorders charity and runs a helpline (0808 801 0677) as well as online support groups. Their support is available to people during pregnancy and in the postnatal period.

**Practical strategies.** Some people find it helpful to shift their focus toward what their body is doing for their baby — not in a pressured way, but as a gentle reframe. Working with a dietitian who has experience in eating disorders and pregnancy can help you meet your nutritional needs without it feeling overwhelming.

## What Is Normal vs When to Seek Help {#section-3}

Many people with eating disorder histories manage pregnancy well with the right support in place. However, there are signs that you should speak to your midwife or GP promptly:

- You are restricting food intake significantly because of fear of weight gain
- You are purging during pregnancy
- You are using exercise compulsively to manage weight gain
- You feel unable to eat enough to sustain your pregnancy
- You are experiencing severe distress about your body or weight that is affecting your daily functioning

These are not signs of weakness — they are signs that you need more support, and there is no shame in asking for it. Your baby's nutrition and your own wellbeing both matter.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Can pregnancy trigger an eating disorder relapse?

Yes, it can. The combination of rapid body changes, weight gain, and the emotional intensity of pregnancy creates conditions that can trigger eating disorder thoughts and behaviours, even in people who have been in recovery for some time. This is why disclosing your history to your midwife early on is so important — it allows your care team to put appropriate support in place before difficulties arise rather than after.

### Is it safe to be pregnant with an eating disorder?

With proper medical support and monitoring, many people with eating disorder histories have healthy pregnancies. However, active eating disorder behaviours during pregnancy can affect your health and your baby's development. This is not said to frighten you, but to encourage you to access support. Your healthcare team will not judge you — they will want to help you and your baby stay well.

### Will my midwife have to tell anyone about my eating disorder history?

Your medical information is confidential within your healthcare team. Disclosing to your midwife does not mean it will be shared beyond your care team without your consent, except in circumstances where there is a serious concern about safety. Most of the time, telling your midwife simply helps them tailor your care.
