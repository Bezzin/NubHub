# Coping with Comments About Your Body in Pregnancy

"You're huge!" "Are you sure there's only one in there?" "You barely look pregnant — is the baby OK?" Pregnancy turns your body into public property, and the comments can start before you've even announced. Learning to handle them protects your mental health and your relationship with your changing body.

## Why people feel entitled to comment

Pregnancy is culturally treated as a shared event. Many people genuinely think they're being affectionate or funny — they remember being pregnant themselves, or they're excited about a new baby in the family, and commentary feels natural to them. But intent doesn't erase impact, and repeated remarks about size, shape, or weight can chip away at body image, especially if you've struggled with it before or if your pregnancy has been anxious or complicated.

There's also an unspoken assumption that a pregnant body is somehow communal. People who would never comment on a stranger's weight in ordinary life feel licensed to do so the moment there's a bump involved. Understanding that this comes from cultural habit rather than genuine malice can help — though it doesn't make the remarks any easier to hear.

## Common comment patterns

- **Size remarks.** "Too big" or "too small" — both feel like judgment, even when dressed up as concern.
- **Weight commentary.** "You're all bump" versus "You've put weight on everywhere." Neither is appropriate.
- **Comparisons.** "My sister was much smaller at that stage" or "You're carrying so differently to last time."
- **Warnings.** "Just wait, it gets worse" or "You'll never get your body back."
- **Unsolicited predictions.** Guessing sex from bump shape, or suggesting twins because you look "big."

## Why they hurt

Your body is changing rapidly and not always in ways you feel in control of. Stretch marks, swelling, the shifting of your centre of gravity, and the general visibility of your body can already feel unfamiliar and exposing. Comments amplify the sense that your body is being watched and graded, which can fuel anxiety and self-consciousness at a time when you're already under significant physical and emotional pressure.

If you have a history of an eating disorder, body dysmorphia, or difficult feelings about your size, unsolicited remarks can be particularly destabilising. It is worth mentioning this to your midwife early in your pregnancy so that appropriate support can be put in place.

## How to respond

You don't have to have the perfect comeback ready. These options cover a range of situations:

- **The neutral redirect.** "Bodies do what they need to in pregnancy. How are you?" — shifts the conversation without confrontation.
- **The honest line.** "I'm trying not to focus on size, so I'd rather not talk about it." — clear and calm.
- **The humour deflection.** "Yep, housing a whole human — it's a big job!" — ends the subject lightly.
- **The firm stop.** "I'm not comfortable with comments about my body. Thanks for understanding." — appropriate for repeat offenders or close family who need clarity.

You don't owe anyone an explanation, and you don't need to soften a boundary to make someone else comfortable.

## Protecting yourself day to day

- **Curate your environment.** If there are serial commentators in your life, limit time with them or change the subject before they start.
- **Focus on function.** Remind yourself what your body is doing: growing a placenta, producing amniotic fluid, adapting your organs, preparing for birth. It is working, not failing.
- **Unfollow comparison content.** Social media accounts showing "perfect bumps" or dramatic body-after-baby posts can distort your sense of normal. Pregnancy bodies vary enormously and all of them are valid.
- **Talk to someone you trust.** A partner, a friend who has been pregnant, or a pregnancy peer support group can help put hurtful comments into context.
- **Tell your midwife or GP.** If body image distress is affecting your mood, your appetite, or your sleep, please mention it at your next appointment. Perinatal mental health support is available on the NHS and your midwife can refer you.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is it normal to find body comments upsetting even when they're meant kindly?

Absolutely. Well-meaning comments can still land badly, especially when you're already navigating a rapidly changing body and fluctuating hormones. Your reaction is valid regardless of the commenter's intent. If remarks are affecting your confidence or mood regularly, speak to your midwife.

### What if the comments are coming from a close family member or partner?

This can feel more difficult to address because the relationship matters to you. It is worth having a calm, private conversation: "I know you don't mean to upset me, but comments about my size make me feel uncomfortable. I'd really appreciate it if we didn't talk about my body that way." Most people respond well when they understand the impact. If the behaviour continues despite a clear request, your midwife or a counsellor can help you find ways to manage it.

### Could comments about bump size mean something is actually wrong?

Your midwife measures your fundal height at routine appointments and will raise any concern about growth. Unsolicited opinions from family members or strangers have no clinical value. If you have a genuine worry about your baby's growth, raise it with your midwife or GP — that is their job, not the job of someone in the supermarket queue.

Your body is doing something remarkable and demanding. It doesn't exist for commentary. You get to decide what is said about it — and to you. If comments are significantly affecting your wellbeing, speak to your midwife or GP who can connect you with the right support.
