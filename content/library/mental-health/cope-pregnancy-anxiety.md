# How to Cope with Pregnancy Anxiety

Pregnancy anxiety is common, but it does not have to dominate your first trimester. A combination of self-help strategies, lifestyle adjustments, and professional support can significantly reduce how anxious you feel. Here are practical steps you can start today.

## Self-help strategies that help {#section-1}

Small, consistent actions make a real difference when anxiety feels overwhelming. None of these require a referral or a waiting list — you can try them from today.

**Limit information overload.** Spending hours reading about pregnancy complications, miscarriage rates, or worst-case scenarios feeds anxiety rather than calming it. Set specific times for checking pregnancy apps rather than scrolling constantly throughout the day. When you catch yourself doom-scrolling, close the app and do something grounding instead.

**Write down your worries.** Anxiety often circles the same thoughts because they feel unresolved. Writing them down externalises them and gives you a chance to challenge them with facts. Ask yourself: is this worry based on something that is actually happening, or is it a "what if"? Many pregnancy worries are about possible futures, not current realities.

**Practise slow breathing daily.** Controlled breathing activates your parasympathetic nervous system and reduces the physical symptoms of anxiety. Try breathing in for four counts, holding for four, and breathing out for six to eight. Doing this for just a few minutes when you feel anxious can bring your heart rate down quickly. There are free guided breathing exercises on the NHS website and on apps such as Calm or Headspace.

**Keep a mood journal.** Tracking your emotional state day to day helps you spot patterns — are you more anxious in the evenings, after a particular trigger, or in certain situations? Patterns are easier to manage than vague background dread.

**Talk to someone you trust.** Keeping anxiety entirely private can amplify it. Telling a partner, friend, or family member how you are feeling is not a burden — it often brings relief and practical support you did not know was available.

**Gentle exercise.** Walking, swimming, and pregnancy yoga all reduce stress hormones and improve mood. Even a 20-minute walk outside each day can make a measurable difference. Always check with your midwife before starting any new exercise during pregnancy.

**Reduce caffeine.** High caffeine intake is not recommended during pregnancy for other reasons, but it also worsens anxiety symptoms. Switching to decaffeinated options can help if you are sensitive to caffeine's effects.

## Professional support options in the UK {#section-2}

Self-help is a good starting point, but if anxiety is persistent, severe, or affecting your daily life, professional support is appropriate and available.

**NHS Talking Therapies (formerly IAPT):** This free NHS service offers cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) and other talking therapies for anxiety and depression. Many areas have perinatal specialists who understand pregnancy-specific worries. You can self-refer at www.nhs.uk/service-search/mental-health — you do not need a GP referral in most areas.

**Your midwife:** At your booking appointment and every subsequent appointment, your midwife will ask about your mental health and wellbeing. Be honest with them. They can refer you to perinatal mental health services, connect you with peer support, or simply listen and signpost further help.

**Perinatal mental health teams:** These NHS teams specialise in supporting women during pregnancy and the first year after birth. Referral is usually through your midwife or GP. If your anxiety is severe, persistent, or has been accompanied by depression, this is the most appropriate level of support.

**Your GP:** Your GP can discuss all the above options, review whether any medication might be appropriate in your situation, and provide onward referrals. You do not have to wait until you are in crisis to see your GP about mental health — earlier intervention is always more effective.

## What is normal anxiety versus when to seek help

It is normal to feel some worry during pregnancy, particularly in the first trimester before a scan has confirmed everything is well. This is different from clinical anxiety, which is persistent, difficult to control, physically distressing, or interfering with your ability to go about your day.

Signs that you should speak to your midwife or GP include: anxiety that is present most of the time and does not respond to self-help strategies; physical symptoms such as rapid heart rate, chest tightness, or difficulty breathing; panic attacks; intrusive or distressing thoughts; or anxiety that is stopping you from eating, sleeping, or functioning normally.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Can exercise reduce pregnancy anxiety?

Yes. Gentle exercise like walking, swimming, or yoga releases endorphins and reduces stress hormones such as cortisol. Most forms of gentle exercise are safe in pregnancy, but it is worth checking with your midwife before starting anything new, particularly if your pregnancy has any complications.

### Should I avoid pregnancy forums if I have anxiety?

If forums increase your anxiety, limit them or choose moderated, supportive spaces with clear community guidelines. Not all online content is accurate or reassuring, and anonymous horror stories can be particularly unhelpful when you are already anxious. Use forums for connection and solidarity rather than medical information.

### How long does it take for coping strategies to work?

Some techniques like slow breathing help almost immediately by reducing the physical symptoms of anxiety. Others, such as journalling or CBT, take a few weeks of consistent practice before you notice a significant shift. Consistency matters more than perfection — doing a small amount daily is more effective than an intensive effort once a week.
