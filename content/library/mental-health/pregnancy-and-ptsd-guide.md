# Pregnancy and PTSD: A Guide

Pregnancy is supposed to be a joyful time, but if you are living with PTSD — whether from a previous traumatic birth, a history of abuse, a medical trauma, or any other experience — it can stir up feelings that are genuinely difficult to manage. You are not alone in this, and you are not making it up. PTSD during pregnancy is recognised by NHS perinatal mental health services, and effective support is available. This guide explains how PTSD can intersect with pregnancy, what kind of care you can ask for, and where to get help.

## How PTSD Affects Pregnancy {#section-1}

Post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD) is a mental health condition that can develop after experiencing or witnessing a traumatic event. In the context of pregnancy, the most common sources of trauma are:

- A previous traumatic birth or emergency delivery
- Pregnancy loss, including miscarriage or stillbirth
- Childhood abuse or neglect
- Sexual trauma
- A serious medical event or illness
- Domestic abuse

Pregnancy can reactivate PTSD symptoms even if you have been managing well for some time. This happens for several reasons. You may be facing medical appointments and physical examinations that feel similar to past traumatic experiences. Your body is changing in ways you cannot fully control, which can feel destabilising if loss of control was part of your original trauma. The emotional stakes of pregnancy — particularly if you have experienced loss before — can bring unresolved feelings to the surface.

Common PTSD symptoms that may intensify during pregnancy include:

- Flashbacks or intrusive memories of a previous trauma
- Nightmares about the trauma or about the pregnancy
- Avoiding antenatal appointments, scans, or examinations
- Feeling emotionally numb or detached from the pregnancy
- Heightened anxiety, hypervigilance, or difficulty sleeping
- Feeling as though something bad will inevitably happen

These are recognised symptoms, not signs of weakness or over-sensitivity. Telling your midwife what you are experiencing is the single most important step you can take.

## Common Triggers During Pregnancy and Birth {#section-2}

Some aspects of antenatal care and birth can be specifically difficult for people with PTSD:

**Internal examinations.** Vaginal examinations can be triggering, particularly for anyone with a history of sexual trauma. You always have the right to decline or pause an examination, and to ask for it to be carried out differently.

**Loss of control during labour.** For many people, the unpredictability of labour — especially emergency situations — can echo traumatic experiences of powerlessness. Having a clear birth plan and a supportive birth partner or doula can help.

**Medical settings and equipment.** Hospitals, monitoring equipment, or the sound of specific alarms may trigger memories for those who experienced medical trauma.

**Scan appointments.** If you have previously had a scan where bad news was delivered, subsequent scans can provoke intense anxiety before and during the appointment.

**Postnatal period.** PTSD can also worsen in the weeks after birth, particularly if the birth itself was traumatic. This is sometimes called birth trauma or postnatal PTSD.

## Getting Support {#section-3}

You do not need to manage PTSD during pregnancy without help. Here is what is available:

**Tell your midwife at your booking appointment.** You do not need to share every detail — simply saying "I have PTSD from a previous experience and I'd like to talk about how we can make my care feel safer" is enough. Good midwives will listen and adapt.

**Ask for a perinatal mental health referral.** Every NHS trust in England has a Perinatal Mental Health team (sometimes called a PMHT or specialist perinatal service). Your GP or midwife can refer you. These teams are specifically trained in supporting mental health during pregnancy and after birth.

**Psychological therapies.** Trauma-focused CBT (cognitive behavioural therapy) and EMDR (eye movement desensitisation and reprocessing) are both recommended for PTSD. These can sometimes be accessed during pregnancy via your GP, a perinatal mental health team, or IAPT services (Improving Access to Psychological Therapies).

**Birth debrief services.** If your PTSD stems from a previous birth, many hospitals now offer birth debrief appointments where you can go through what happened with a midwife or obstetrician. This can help make sense of events and plan differently for a future birth.

**Doula support.** A doula — a trained birth companion who is not a medical professional — can provide continuous, familiar support throughout labour. Having someone present who knows your history and your needs can make a significant difference.

**Organisations that can help:**
- **Birth Trauma Association** (birthtraumaassociation.org.uk) — specialist support for those affected by traumatic births
- **PANDAS Foundation** — perinatal mental health support
- **Mind** — general mental health support and signposting

## What Trauma-Informed Maternity Care Looks Like {#section-4}

Trauma-informed care is an approach where healthcare providers are aware that trauma is common and adapt their care accordingly. In maternity settings, this can mean:

- Asking before any examination and explaining clearly what they are about to do
- Giving you the option to stop or pause at any point
- Avoiding language or phrases that feel clinical or dismissive
- Including your preferences in your birth plan and taking them seriously
- Not surprising you with procedures or interventions without discussion

You are entitled to ask for trauma-informed care. If your current midwife or GP is not responsive to your concerns, you can ask to see a different clinician or request a referral to a specialist perinatal mental health service.

## FAQ {#faq}

### Can pregnancy make PTSD worse?

Yes, it can. Hormonal changes, medical procedures, physical body changes, and the emotional weight of pregnancy can all trigger or intensify PTSD symptoms. This is particularly common for people with a history of previous pregnancy loss or traumatic birth. It does not mean you are not coping — it means you are dealing with a genuinely difficult combination of factors, and professional support is both available and appropriate.

### Is trauma-informed maternity care available on the NHS?

Yes. You have the right to ask for trauma-informed care, and your midwifery team should be able to adapt how they work with you. If you need more specialist support, your midwife or GP can refer you to the NHS Perinatal Mental Health team in your area. You do not need to manage alone, and asking for adjustments to your care is completely reasonable.

### What if I am scared of giving birth because of past trauma?

Fear of childbirth related to past trauma is recognised and taken seriously by NHS maternity services. This is sometimes called tokophobia (secondary tokophobia if it follows a traumatic birth). Raise it with your midwife as early as possible so that you have enough time to access additional support, put a detailed birth plan in place, and explore your options — including whether a different mode of delivery might be appropriate for you.

Always speak to your midwife or GP for personal advice about managing PTSD during your pregnancy — they can connect you with the right support for your individual circumstances.
