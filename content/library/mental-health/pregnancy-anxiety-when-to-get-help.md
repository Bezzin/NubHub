# Pregnancy Anxiety: When to Get Help

Some worry in pregnancy is completely normal, but there is a point at which anxiety becomes a problem that needs professional support. This page helps you recognise the warning signs and explains exactly who to contact in the UK and what kind of help you can expect.

## Signs your anxiety needs professional support {#section-1}

Consider speaking to your GP or midwife if you have persistent worry that does not lift, panic attacks, racing thoughts you cannot switch off, difficulty functioning at work or home, avoidance of pregnancy-related situations (such as scans), physical symptoms like a constant racing heart, or intrusive thoughts about harm. These are all valid reasons to ask for help.

## Who to contact and what happens next {#section-2}

Start with your GP or midwife. They can refer you to a perinatal mental health team, which specialises in supporting women during pregnancy and the first year after birth. Treatment may include talking therapies (such as CBT via NHS Talking Therapies), medication review, or simply regular check-ins. You will not be judged for asking for support.

## FAQ {#faq}

### Will social services be involved if I ask for mental health support?

No. Seeking treatment for anxiety is a sign of good parenting, not a risk factor. Perinatal mental health teams are there to support you, not assess your parenting.

### How long does NHS perinatal mental health support take to access?

Waiting times vary by region, but urgent cases are prioritised. Your GP can expedite a referral if needed.

### Can I self-refer to perinatal mental health services?

In many areas you can self-refer to NHS Talking Therapies. Ask your midwife about local referral pathways.
