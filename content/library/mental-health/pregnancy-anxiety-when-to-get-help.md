# Pregnancy Anxiety: When to Get Help

Some worry in pregnancy is completely normal, but there is a point at which anxiety becomes a problem that needs professional support. This page helps you recognise the warning signs and explains exactly who to contact in the UK and what kind of help you can expect.

## Signs your anxiety needs professional support {#section-1}

Feeling nervous about scans, test results, or labour is a natural part of pregnancy. Anxiety becomes a problem worth addressing when it takes up a significant portion of your day, stops you from doing things you would normally do, or causes you significant distress.

Consider speaking to your GP or midwife if any of the following apply to you:

- **Persistent, uncontrollable worry** — your mind keeps returning to worst-case scenarios even when things are going well, and you cannot distract yourself for long
- **Panic attacks** — sudden intense episodes of fear with physical symptoms such as heart racing, chest tightness, shortness of breath, or dizziness
- **Intrusive thoughts** — unwanted thoughts about harm coming to your baby or yourself, which feel distressing and out of character
- **Avoidance** — you find yourself avoiding pregnancy-related situations such as scans, antenatal appointments, or conversations about birth because the anticipatory anxiety feels unbearable
- **Physical symptoms** — constant tension headaches, a permanently racing heart, poor sleep, or an upset stomach driven by worry rather than pregnancy hormones alone
- **Difficulty functioning** — your anxiety is affecting your work, relationships, or ability to care for yourself day to day
- **Low mood alongside the anxiety** — anxiety and depression often occur together in pregnancy, and this combination benefits from early support

Any one of these experiences is a valid reason to ask for help. You do not need to be in crisis, and you do not need to minimise how you feel before reaching out.

## Who to contact and what happens next {#section-2}

Your first port of call should be your **GP or midwife**. Both are trained to ask about mental wellbeing as part of routine pregnancy care in the UK, but you do not need to wait for them to ask — you can raise it yourself at any appointment.

When you do speak to them, try to be as honest as you can about the frequency and intensity of your symptoms. It can help to keep a brief note beforehand of what you have been experiencing, so nothing gets missed in the moment.

From there, you may be referred to:

- **NHS Talking Therapies** (formerly IAPT) — a talking therapy service available in every area of England offering CBT and other evidence-based therapies. Waiting times vary, but many areas have specific perinatal pathways. You can also self-refer directly without going through your GP first by searching "NHS Talking Therapies" and your local area.
- **A perinatal mental health team** — specialist community teams that support women with more significant mental health needs during pregnancy and in the first year after birth. Referrals are usually made by your GP or midwife.
- **Your community midwife** — midwives are increasingly trained to provide emotional support and can be a valuable ongoing point of contact for lower-level anxiety.

If your anxiety is severe or you are having thoughts of harming yourself, contact your GP urgently, call 111 for advice, or attend your nearest urgent care centre. You can also call the Samaritans on 116 123 at any time.

## What treatment looks like {#section-3}

Treatment for pregnancy anxiety is usually a combination of one or more of the following, tailored to what you need:

**Talking therapies** are the first-line treatment for anxiety in pregnancy. Cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) is the most widely available and has strong evidence behind it. Sessions typically focus on identifying the thought patterns that drive anxiety and practising more balanced responses. Therapy can be delivered face to face, by phone, or online.

**Medication** — some medications for anxiety are considered safe to use during pregnancy, and your doctor can discuss the risks and benefits with you based on your individual situation. If you are already taking medication for anxiety or depression, do not stop without speaking to your GP first, as stopping suddenly can sometimes make things worse.

**Peer support** — connecting with other pregnant women or new parents who have experienced anxiety can reduce feelings of isolation. Your midwife or perinatal team can signpost local groups.

**Self-management strategies** — breathing exercises, mindfulness, reducing caffeine, regular gentle movement, and addressing sleep can all help to manage symptoms alongside formal treatment.

Getting support early tends to lead to better outcomes, both for you and for your baby. Untreated anxiety in pregnancy can affect sleep, nutrition, and enjoyment of the experience — all good reasons to address it sooner rather than later.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Will social services be involved if I ask for mental health support?

No. Seeking treatment for anxiety is a sign of good self-awareness, not a risk factor. Perinatal mental health teams and GPs are there to support you. Referrals to social services only happen in very specific circumstances related to child safeguarding, and simply asking for help with anxiety is not one of them.

### How long does NHS perinatal mental health support take to access?

Waiting times vary by region. NHS Talking Therapies generally has shorter waits and you can self-refer. If your symptoms are urgent, tell your GP or midwife — urgent cases are prioritised. In the meantime, your midwife can often provide interim support and signpost self-help resources.

### Can I self-refer to perinatal mental health services?

You can self-refer to NHS Talking Therapies in most parts of England without needing a GP referral. For specialist perinatal mental health teams, a referral from your GP or midwife is usually required, but your GP can make this quickly if needed. Ask your midwife about the pathways available in your area.
