# Self-Care in Early Pregnancy: Practical Tips

Self-care in early pregnancy isn't a luxury — it's essential. Here are practical tips for looking after yourself.

Early pregnancy can be exhausting, overwhelming, and emotionally complicated — often all at once. You may be keeping a huge secret, managing nausea, worrying constantly, and still trying to show up to work as if nothing has changed. Self-care in the first trimester is not about bubble baths and scented candles (though those are fine too). It is about genuinely tending to your physical and emotional needs during one of the most demanding transitions of your life.

## Physical Self-Care {#s1}

Your body is working extremely hard in the first trimester, even when you cannot see or feel it. The physical demands of building a placenta and supporting rapid early development are real, and listening to your body's signals matters.

**Rest without guilt.** Fatigue in early pregnancy is not laziness — it is a physiological response to surging progesterone. If you need to sleep at 8pm or nap at the weekend, do it. Your body is directing a huge amount of energy inward.

**Eat what you can manage.** If nausea is making healthy eating feel impossible, give yourself permission to eat whatever stays down. Plain crackers, toast, and bland carbohydrates are fine for now. Try small, frequent meals rather than large ones, and keep snacks nearby for when nausea dips.

**Stay hydrated.** Dehydration can worsen headaches and fatigue, and if you are vomiting frequently, it becomes especially important. Sip water steadily throughout the day. Cold water or water with a slice of lemon may be easier to tolerate if plain water feels unappealing.

**Take your prenatal vitamins.** Folic acid (400 micrograms daily) is recommended until at least 12 weeks to support your baby's neural tube development. Vitamin D (10 micrograms daily) is recommended throughout pregnancy. If nausea makes tablets hard to take, try taking them with food or before bed.

**Move gently.** Gentle exercise — a short walk, some light stretching, or a swim — can genuinely lift mood and ease aches. You do not need to maintain a demanding fitness routine. Listen to your body and stop if anything feels uncomfortable.

**Wear comfortable clothing early.** There is no rule that says you have to squeeze into your usual waistbands. Loose, comfortable clothes can make nausea and bloating feel more manageable long before you have a visible bump.

## Emotional Self-Care {#s2}

The emotional experience of early pregnancy is rarely what people expect. Many women feel anxious rather than joyful, ambivalent rather than certain, or simply exhausted by the weight of the secret they are carrying. All of this is normal.

**Give yourself permission to feel however you feel.** There is no right way to experience pregnancy. If you are scared, worried, or even resentful of how hard this is, that does not mean you will be a bad parent. It means you are human.

**Set limits on pregnancy forums and social media.** It can be tempting to spend hours reading other people's experiences, but forums often over-represent the most frightening outcomes. Give yourself a time limit and notice how you feel after spending time online — if it reliably makes you more anxious, it is worth stepping back.

**Talk to someone you trust.** If you are keeping the pregnancy private, carrying the anxiety alone can become very heavy. Even telling one trusted person can bring real relief. If you do not feel ready to tell anyone, a midwife or GP can listen before you are ready to tell family.

**Protect your sleep.** Anxiety tends to spiral at night. If you are lying awake worrying, a short relaxation practice — slow breathing, a body scan, or a gentle audio meditation — can help interrupt the cycle. Several free apps are available that are designed specifically for pregnancy anxiety.

**Do small things that bring you comfort.** Reading, gentle crafts, cooking something you enjoy, time with a pet, time in nature — small pleasures are not trivial when everything feels big and uncertain.

## When to Ask for More Support

Self-care has its limits, and there is no shame in needing more than tips and good intentions. If you are struggling with persistent anxiety, low mood, or intrusive thoughts, speak to your midwife or GP. Perinatal mental health support is available on the NHS, and asking for help early makes a real difference to outcomes. You do not have to be at crisis point to deserve support.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How do I practice self-care in early pregnancy?

Prioritise rest, eat what you can manage, take your folic acid and vitamin D, stay hydrated, and move gently. Emotionally, allow yourself to feel whatever comes up, limit time on anxiety-inducing forums, talk to someone you trust, and protect your sleep. Self-care is not selfish — it is essential for both you and your baby.

### Is it normal to struggle with self-care in early pregnancy?

Completely. Nausea, exhaustion, anxiety, and the pressure of keeping everything secret can make basic self-care genuinely hard. Start small: one thing per day. If you are really struggling, your midwife or GP can help — you do not need to manage alone.

### What vitamins do I need in early pregnancy?

Folic acid (400 micrograms daily) and vitamin D (10 micrograms daily) are the two key supplements recommended by the NHS in early pregnancy. Some women are advised to take a higher dose of folic acid — your GP will advise if this applies to you. A combined prenatal supplement is fine as long as it contains these.

Speak to your midwife or GP for personal advice on looking after yourself during pregnancy.
