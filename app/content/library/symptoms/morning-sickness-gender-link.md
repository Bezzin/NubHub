# Morning Sickness and Gender: Are They Linked?

One of the oldest pregnancy myths is that severe morning sickness means you are carrying a girl, while mild nausea means a boy. But is there any truth behind the folklore? Here is what the evidence actually says about the link between morning sickness and your baby gender.

## What the Research Suggests {#what-the-research-suggests}

Some studies have found a small statistical association between severe nausea and vomiting and carrying a female baby, possibly due to higher hCG levels in pregnancies with girls. However, the effect is modest and nowhere near reliable enough to predict sex. Plenty of women carrying boys have severe nausea, and many carrying girls have none. The overlap between the two groups is far too large for symptoms to be a useful clue.

## Why the Myth Persists {#why-the-myth-persists}

Old wives tales about pregnancy are powerful because they offer certainty in an uncertain time. When roughly half of babies are girls and half are boys, any guess has a 50 percent chance of being right, which can make the tale feel accurate through confirmation bias. People tend to remember the times the myth was right and forget the times it was wrong. In reality, only a scan from around 16 weeks or non-invasive testing can reliably tell you your baby sex.

## What Actually Predicts Severity {#what-actually-predicts-severity}

Severity of morning sickness is more reliably linked to factors such as carrying multiples, a history of motion sickness or nausea with contraception, previous pregnancy nausea, and possibly genetics. Hormone sensitivity plays a bigger role than baby sex. If you had severe nausea before, you are more likely to have it again, regardless of gender.

## When Severe Nausea Needs Attention {#when-severe-nausea-needs-attention}

Whatever the gender, severe nausea and vomiting that prevents you from eating or drinking needs medical assessment. Hyperemesis gravidarum can affect anyone and is not predicted by whether you are having a boy or a girl. If you are struggling, ask for help; effective treatment is available and gender guessing should not delay it.

## FAQ {#faq}

### Does severe morning sickness mean a girl?

There is a slight statistical link, but it is too weak to predict sex. Many women with severe nausea have boys and many with mild nausea have girls.

### Is no morning sickness a sign of a boy?

Not reliably. Nausea varies widely and is influenced by hormones, genetics and sensitivity, not consistently by baby sex.

### When can I find out my baby gender?

A routine scan can often determine sex from around 16 weeks, though it depends on the baby position. Non-invasive prenatal testing can indicate sex earlier, if offered.
