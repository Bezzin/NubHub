# Brown Spotting in Early Pregnancy: What It Means

Seeing brown spotting when you are pregnant can be alarming, but brown is generally the most reassuring colour. Brown blood is simply old blood that has taken longer to travel out of your body. This guide explains the most common causes and helps you decide when a phone call to your midwife is warranted.

## Why Is the Blood Brown?

Fresh blood is bright red. As it sits inside the womb or vagina, it oxidises and turns brown. By the time you see it on your underwear or toilet paper, it has been there a while. Brown spotting often follows implantation, a sensitive cervix after intercourse, or a small amount of hormonal withdrawal bleeding around the time your period was due. The colour alone tells you the bleeding is not fresh, which is reassuring.

Brown discharge can range from a faint pale-brown tinge to a darker, coffee-coloured spotting. Both are within the same category — old blood leaving the body — and neither colour variation on its own is cause for immediate alarm.

## Common Causes of Brown Spotting {#common-causes}

**Implantation bleeding:** When the embryo embeds into the lining of the womb, a small amount of blood can be released. This typically happens around the time your period would have been due, and the blood is often brown by the time you notice it. Not everyone experiences implantation bleeding, and its absence means nothing.

**Hormonal withdrawal:** Some women experience a light bleed around the time their period was due, even when the pregnancy is progressing normally. This is thought to be related to the hormonal shift from your cycle to pregnancy. It can look like a very light period and is usually brown or pink rather than red.

**Post-intercourse spotting:** Your cervix has a much richer blood supply in pregnancy and is considerably softer than usual. This means it can bleed lightly after penetrative sex or an internal examination. The blood that appears on underwear several hours later is often brown.

**Old blood from a previous small bleed:** Sometimes a tiny amount of blood from an earlier bleed takes time to work its way out. You might see brown spotting days after a cramp or bleed that has already resolved, with no ongoing problem.

**Cervical ectropion:** Some women develop a cervical ectropion in pregnancy — an area of softer, more fragile tissue on the cervix. This is harmless but can cause light brown spotting, particularly after sex or an internal examination.

## When Brown Spotting Needs Checking

While brown spotting is usually harmless, you should contact your midwife or early pregnancy unit if:

- It is accompanied by cramping that feels stronger than mild period pain
- It lasts more than a week without easing
- It turns red or pink, or the volume increases
- You have shoulder tip pain, dizziness, or feel faint (these can indicate an ectopic pregnancy — call 999)
- You have a history of ectopic pregnancy or miscarriage and want reassurance
- You feel unwell alongside the spotting

Your midwife may arrange a scan at the early pregnancy unit (EPU) to confirm everything is progressing well. This is purely for peace of mind in most cases, and many women who attend the EPU with brown spotting find a healthy heartbeat on the scan.

If the spotting is very heavy, bright red, or you are passing clots, seek same-day advice from your midwife, GP, or by calling 111.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How long can brown spotting last?

It can last anywhere from a few hours to a week. If it stretches beyond seven days, changes to bright red, or increases in volume, contact your midwife or early pregnancy unit.

### Can brown spotting mean miscarriage?

Brown spotting alone does not mean miscarriage. Many women with healthy pregnancies experience it, particularly in the first trimester. However, if it is paired with significant cramping, a sudden increase in bleeding, or you pass tissue, seek urgent advice from your early pregnancy unit or GP.

### Is brown spotting after sex normal?

Yes. Your cervix is softer and has more blood flow in pregnancy, so light brown spotting after sex is common and usually harmless. If it happens repeatedly or is accompanied by pain, mention it to your midwife so she can examine the cervix and offer reassurance.
