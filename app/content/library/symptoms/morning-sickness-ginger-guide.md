# Ginger for Morning Sickness: A Complete UK Guide

Ginger is one of the most widely researched natural remedies for morning sickness, and many UK midwives suggest it as a first-line option before medication. This guide explains how ginger works, the safest forms to try, and how much is appropriate during pregnancy — including what to do if ginger alone isn't enough.

## Why Ginger Helps Nausea

Ginger contains active compounds called gingerols and shogaols that are thought to support gastric motility — essentially helping your stomach empty more efficiently — and to interact with receptors involved in the nausea reflex. This dual action may explain why many women find it eases the unsettled feeling of morning sickness even when anti-nausea medication isn't immediately available or desired.

The evidence behind ginger is more robust than for many other natural remedies. Multiple systematic reviews have found that ginger modestly but consistently reduces nausea scores in early pregnancy compared with placebo. The Royal College of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists (RCOG) lists ginger as a reasonable first-line option for mild to moderate nausea and vomiting of pregnancy. The NHS also acknowledges ginger as a remedy worth trying before escalating to prescribed medication.

Importantly, ginger works best for nausea rather than vomiting. If you are frequently sick as well as nauseous, you may still benefit from ginger for the background queasy feeling, but you may also need further support from your midwife or GP.

## Practical Ways to Use Ginger

**Ginger tea** is the most accessible starting point. You can buy branded ginger tea bags from any supermarket or pharmacy, or make your own by steeping a few slices of fresh root ginger in boiling water for five minutes. Let it cool slightly before sipping — very hot drinks can feel worse on a sensitive stomach. Try sipping slowly throughout the morning rather than drinking a large cup in one go.

**Ginger biscuits** are the classic bedside remedy. Keeping a packet on your nightstand and eating one or two before getting up can help ease the first wave of nausea that hits many women first thing in the morning. Standard supermarket ginger biscuits do contain real ginger, though typically in small amounts.

**Crystallised ginger** (the sugar-coated dried pieces sold in health food shops and supermarkets) provides a more concentrated hit of ginger flavour. A small piece nibbled slowly can settle queasiness mid-morning or after meals.

**Fresh ginger in food** is another option. Grating a small amount into stir-fries, soups, or even smoothies adds ginger without you having to eat it as a standalone remedy. This works well as the day progresses and you feel well enough to eat small meals.

**Ginger capsules or supplements** are available but carry more uncertainty around concentration and purity. If you want to try capsules, discuss this with your midwife first so they can recommend a reputable product and dosage appropriate for pregnancy.

## How Much Ginger Is Safe in Pregnancy?

Most UK guidance and clinical commentary suggests that up to around 1 gram of ginger per day is considered safe at typical food amounts in pregnancy. This roughly equates to a few cups of ginger tea, several ginger biscuits, or a small piece of crystallised ginger across the day. Spreading intake through the day in small amounts tends to be more comfortable and effective than a single large dose.

Avoid exceeding this general threshold through concentrated capsules without medical advice. There is limited data on very high doses during pregnancy, and more is not necessarily better. If you have any blood-clotting conditions or are taking anticoagulant medication, mention your ginger use to your midwife or GP, as ginger has mild blood-thinning properties.

One side effect to watch for is heartburn. Ginger can relax the lower oesophageal sphincter in some people, which may worsen acid reflux — already a common pregnancy complaint. If you notice reflux increasing after using ginger, reduce the amount or switch to a different remedy.

## When Ginger Isn't Enough

Ginger is a helpful first step, but it has limits. If your nausea is severe, if you are vomiting repeatedly throughout the day, if you cannot keep fluids down for 24 hours or more, or if you are losing weight, you should contact your midwife or GP rather than persisting with natural remedies alone.

Severe nausea and vomiting in pregnancy — known as hyperemesis gravidarum — is a medical condition that can lead to dehydration and nutritional deficiency. It requires proper assessment and may need prescribed medication, IV fluids, or hospital admission. There is no benefit in suffering through it without support, and your midwife or GP will not judge you for asking for help.

Other gentle options that can be used alongside ginger include acupressure wristbands (Sea-Bands), vitamin B6 supplementation (under midwife guidance), eating small amounts frequently, and avoiding strong smells or hot foods that trigger nausea.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is ginger safe in the first trimester?

At typical food amounts — ginger tea, biscuits, or a small piece of crystallised ginger — yes, ginger is considered safe in the first trimester and is supported by UK clinical guidance as a reasonable first-line remedy for mild nausea. Speak to your midwife before taking concentrated ginger capsules, as the dose in supplements varies and hasn't been as thoroughly studied in pregnancy.

### What form of ginger works best for morning sickness?

There is no single best form — whichever you can actually tolerate is the right choice. Tea suits some women; others prefer biscuits or crystallised pieces. Many women find a combination across the day works better than relying on one form. The key is consistency: small amounts regularly tend to work better than a large amount in one go.

### Can ginger cause heartburn in pregnancy?

Yes, in some women. Ginger can relax the lower oesophageal sphincter, which may worsen acid reflux — a symptom many pregnant women already experience. If you notice heartburn increasing after using ginger, reduce the amount or try an alternative remedy, and mention it at your next midwife appointment.
