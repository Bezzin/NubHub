# Cervical Bleeding in Pregnancy: Causes and Reassurance

Any bleeding during pregnancy can feel alarming, and it is always worth getting checked — but not all bleeding signals a problem. Your cervix goes through enormous changes during pregnancy: it becomes softer, engorged with blood, and more sensitive than at any other time. That means even very gentle contact can cause light spotting. Cervical bleeding is one of the most common and least worrying causes of spotting in early and mid pregnancy. This guide explains why it happens, what to do about it, and when to seek further assessment.

## Why Your Cervix Bleeds More in Pregnancy

From very early in pregnancy, rising oestrogen and progesterone cause a significant increase in blood flow to the cervix. This makes the tissue softer, more vascular, and considerably more delicate than usual.

A condition called cervical ectropion — also sometimes called cervical erosion, though this is a misleading term as nothing is actually eroding — is very common in pregnancy. It occurs when the softer, glandular cells that normally line the inside of the cervical canal spread outward onto the visible surface of the cervix. These cells look raw and red, but they are entirely normal. Because they sit close to the surface and are supplied with many small blood vessels, they bleed very easily when touched. Cervical ectropion is not harmful, does not indicate any infection or abnormality, and does not affect your pregnancy or your baby.

## What Cervical Bleeding Looks Like

Cervical bleeding in pregnancy typically appears as:

- Light pink or pale red spotting
- Brown discharge (older blood that has taken time to pass)
- A small amount on tissue after wiping
- Very light spotting noticed in underwear

It generally appears within a few hours of a trigger (such as sex or an internal examination) and settles within 24 to 48 hours. The volume is usually small — far less than a period. If bleeding is heavy, bright red, or continuous, that is a different pattern and warrants prompt assessment.

## Common Triggers

The most frequent causes of cervical bleeding in pregnancy include:

- **Sex (intercourse):** The cervix is easily reached and the friction of penetration is enough to cause light spotting on a sensitised, ectropion-affected cervix.
- **Internal examinations:** A speculum examination at the GP, midwife, or early pregnancy unit involves direct contact with the cervix.
- **Smear tests:** If you have a smear test in early pregnancy (less common but sometimes performed), light bleeding afterwards is not unusual.
- **Straining with a bowel movement:** Increased pressure can occasionally cause very light spotting.

In all of these cases, the blood comes from the surface of the cervix, not from inside the uterus, and poses no risk to the pregnancy.

## Managing Cervical Bleeding

If you notice light spotting after sex or an internal examination:

- **Do not panic.** Light spotting that appears shortly after contact and is pink or brown is usually cervical in origin.
- **Rest for the remainder of the day** if you feel anxious or if it helps you feel more settled.
- **Avoid intercourse** until the bleeding has fully settled. Once it has stopped and your midwife is reassured, you can resume normal sexual activity.
- **Note the colour, volume, and timing** so you can describe it accurately if you speak to a healthcare professional.
- **Mention it at your next midwife appointment.** Even if it has settled completely, it is worth noting in your records. Your midwife may want to examine your cervix to confirm the cause.
- **Stay hydrated and monitor for any other symptoms** such as pain or cramping, which would change the picture.

## When to Seek Help

While cervical bleeding is usually harmless, there are situations where you should contact your midwife, GP, or early pregnancy unit without waiting:

- Bleeding that is heavy or soaking a pad
- Bright red bleeding that has not been triggered by sex or an examination
- Bleeding accompanied by abdominal pain or cramping
- Bleeding alongside one-sided pain (which could indicate an ectopic pregnancy — this is a medical emergency)
- Bleeding with a fever or foul-smelling discharge (may indicate infection)
- Any bleeding that you are worried about, regardless of how light it is

If you experience sudden severe abdominal pain with any bleeding, call 999 or go to A&E immediately.

You can also call NHS 111 at any time for advice on whether you need to be seen urgently.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is bleeding after sex always cervical?

Often, but not always. Cervical ectropion is the most common reason for post-sex spotting in pregnancy, but other causes — including low-lying placenta — can also produce light bleeding. Your midwife or early pregnancy unit can examine your cervix to confirm the source. If bleeding is heavy, painful, or you are concerned, contact your midwife or EPU for assessment rather than assuming a cause.

### Will cervical ectropion affect my pregnancy?

No. Cervical ectropion is a normal hormonal change that is common during pregnancy and does not harm your baby, increase the risk of miscarriage, or indicate any underlying problem. It often resolves on its own after birth when hormone levels return to normal.

### Should I avoid sex if I have cervical bleeding?

Until the bleeding has fully settled, it is sensible to pause intercourse so you can clearly monitor whether it has stopped. Once it has resolved and your midwife is happy there is no other cause, you can resume normal sexual activity. If bleeding recurs consistently after sex, let your midwife know — she may want to examine your cervix to reassure you.
