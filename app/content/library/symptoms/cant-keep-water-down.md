# Can't Keep Water Down in Pregnancy: What to Do

Not being able to keep water down is one of the most alarming experiences in pregnancy. You know you need to stay hydrated, but every sip comes straight back up. This is a key sign of hyperemesis gravidarum (HG) and requires medical treatment - not just willpower or more ginger.

## Why It Happens {#why-happens}

When you can't keep water down, it usually means your nausea and vomiting has progressed beyond normal morning sickness into **hyperemesis gravidarum (HG)**. This is driven by:

- **High hCG levels**: The pregnancy hormone that triggers nausea
- **Hormone sensitivity**: Your body may be particularly reactive to hormonal changes
- **Stomach emptying delays**: Pregnancy hormones slow digestion, so even small sips may trigger vomiting
- **Ketones**: When you can't eat or drink, your body breaks down fat for energy, producing ketones - which actually make nausea worse

### The Vicious Cycle
The inability to keep water down creates a vicious cycle:
1. You can't drink → you become dehydrated
2. Dehydration makes nausea worse
3. Worse nausea makes it harder to drink
4. Ketones build up, worsening nausea further

Breaking this cycle usually requires medical intervention.

## Why It's Dangerous {#why-dangerous}

Dehydration in pregnancy is more dangerous than you might think. It can lead to:

### Immediate Risks
- **Electrolyte imbalances**: Low potassium and sodium can cause heart rhythm problems
- **Ketosis**: Your body breaks down fat, producing ketones that worsen nausea
- **Low blood pressure**: Can cause dizziness and fainting
- **Kidney strain**: Dehydration affects kidney function
- **UTI risk**: Concentrated urine increases urinary tract infection risk

### Pregnancy Risks
- **Reduced amniotic fluid**: Severe dehydration can affect amniotic fluid levels
- **Hospital admission**: You may need IV fluids
- **Impact on baby**: Severe, prolonged dehydration can affect your baby's nutrition

### Signs You're Becoming Dehydrated
- **Dark yellow urine** or no urine for 8+ hours
- **Dry mouth, lips, and tongue**
- **Dizziness when standing**
- **Headache**
- **Fatigue and weakness**
- **Sunken eyes**
- **Skin that doesn't bounce back** when pinched
- **Rapid heartbeat**

## Immediate Steps to Take {#immediate-steps}

If you can't keep water down:

### 1. Contact Your GP or Call 111
- Don't wait - this needs medical attention
- Call 111 if it's out of hours
- Describe your symptoms clearly: "I can't keep any water down"

### 2. Try Tiny Sips
While waiting for help:
- Take **tiny sips** - a teaspoon of water at a time
- Wait 15 minutes between sips
- Try **ice chips** - suck on them slowly
- Try **ice lollies** - they hydrate slowly

### 3. Try Different Fluids
Water isn't the only option:
- **Dioralyte** or other oral rehydration salts (from pharmacies)
- **Flat lemonade** or sports drinks (diluted)
- **Clear broths**
- **Ice cubes made from fruit juice** (diluted)
- **Cold herbal teas** (peppermint or ginger)

### 4. Rest
- **Lie still** - movement can trigger vomiting
- **Stay cool** - heat worsens nausea
- **Avoid smells** - ask someone else to handle food

## What to Try at Home {#try-home}

If you're struggling but not yet at the "can't keep anything down" stage, try:

### Fluid Strategies
- **Sip constantly** rather than drinking large amounts
- **Use a straw** - some women find this easier
- **Try cold fluids** - often better tolerated than warm
- **Ice chips and ice lollies** - slow hydration
- **Electrolyte solutions** (Dioralyte) - available from pharmacies

### Anti-Nausea Strategies
- **Ginger** (if you can stomach it) - tea, biscuits, sweets
- **Acupressure wristbands** (Sea-Bands)
- **Lie on your left side** - can help with digestion
- **Avoid triggers** - strong smells, certain foods, movement
- **Fresh air** - even opening a window can help

### If You Have Medication
- **Take your anti-sickness medication** if you have some
- **Take it at night** if you can't keep it down during the day
- **Ask about dissolvable forms** if tablets won't stay down

## When to Go to Hospital {#when-hospital}

Go to hospital or call 999 if:

### Urgent Signs
- **Can't keep any fluid down for 12-24 hours**
- **No urine for 8+ hours**
- **Dark, concentrated urine**
- **Dizziness or fainting**
- **Confusion or extreme drowsiness**
- **Vomiting blood**
- **Severe abdominal pain**
- **Rapid heartbeat**
- **Chest pain**

### What Hospital Will Do
- **IV fluids** to rehydrate you
- **IV anti-sickness medication**
- **Blood tests** to check electrolytes
- **Urine test** for ketones
- **Monitor your vitals**
- **Possibly an ultrasound** to check the pregnancy

Most women stay **1-3 days** and feel significantly better after IV fluids.

Read our guide on [when to go to hospital with HG](/symptoms/hyperemesis-when-to-hospital) for more detail.

## Medical Treatment {#treatment}

### Medication Options
If you can't keep water down, you likely need prescription medication:

**First-line** (if you haven't tried them):
- Cyclizine
- Promethazine
- Prochlorperazine

**Second-line** (if first-line hasn't worked):
- Ondansetron
- Metoclopramide

**Combination therapy**: Many women need two or more medications.

Read our guide to [anti-sickness medication in pregnancy](/symptoms/anti-sickness-medication-pregnancy) for full details.

### If You Can't Keep Tablets Down
- **Dissolvable tablets** (ondansetron ondispers, prochlorperazine buccal)
- **Suppositories** (promethazine is available as suppositories)
- **IV medication** (in hospital)
- **IM injection** (in hospital or GP surgery)

### Ongoing Treatment
- You may need regular medication for weeks
- Your GP will monitor your weight, urine, and symptoms
- Some women need repeated hospital visits
- This is normal for HG - don't feel like a failure

## Recovery and Prevention {#recovery}

### After Hospital
- **Continue medication** as prescribed
- **Don't stop** when you feel better - it's the medication keeping you stable
- **Sip fluids constantly** - aim for 2 litres per day
- **Eat small amounts** of whatever you can tolerate
- **Rest** - exhaustion makes HG worse

### Preventing Relapse
- **Take medication on schedule** - set alarms
- **Don't skip doses** even if you feel better
- **Stay hydrated** even when you feel OK
- **Ask for help** - don't try to manage alone
- **Monitor for ketones** at home (ask your midwife for strips)

### Long-Term Outlook
- Most HG eases by weeks 14-20
- Some women have symptoms throughout pregnancy
- HG tends to recur in subsequent pregnancies
- Support is available from Pregnancy Sickness Support

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### What should I do if I can't keep water down in pregnancy?

Contact your GP or call 111 immediately. Not being able to keep water down is a sign of hyperemesis gravidarum and needs medical treatment. You may need IV fluids and prescription anti-sickness medication.

### How long can you go without water in pregnancy?

You shouldn't go more than 12-24 hours without keeping any fluids down. Dehydration can develop quickly in pregnancy and is dangerous. If you can't keep any water down for 12 hours, seek medical help.

### Will going to hospital help if I can't keep water down?

Yes. Hospital treatment with IV fluids is usually very effective for dehydration. You'll also receive IV anti-sickness medication, which is often more effective than oral medication. Most women feel much better after a day or two of IV treatment.

### Can I take anti-sickness medication if I can't keep tablets down?

Yes. There are dissolvable tablets, suppositories, and IV options. If you can't keep tablets down, tell your GP - they can prescribe alternatives that don't require you to swallow pills.

### Is not being able to keep water down dangerous for my baby?

With proper treatment, most babies are fine. The danger is untreated dehydration, which can affect amniotic fluid and nutrition. Getting treatment protects both you and your baby. Don't delay seeking help.
