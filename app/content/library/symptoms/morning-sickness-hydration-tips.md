# Hydration Tips for Morning Sickness

Staying hydrated is one of the most important things you can do when morning sickness strikes, but it is also one of the hardest. This page explains why fluids matter and how to get enough when drinking feels impossible.

## Why Hydration Matters {#why-hydration-matters}

Vomiting and poor fluid intake can quickly lead to dehydration, which in turn worsens nausea and can become a serious medical issue. Even mild dehydration can make you feel dizzier, more tired, and more nauseous — creating a cycle that is difficult to break on your own.

In pregnancy your fluid needs are higher than usual, so the gap between what you need and what you can manage can widen quickly when nausea is at its worst. Your kidneys, digestive system, and the placenta all depend on adequate fluid levels to function well.

Recognising early signs of dehydration helps you act before things escalate. Watch for:

- Dark yellow or strong-smelling urine
- Urinating less frequently than usual
- Dizziness or light-headedness
- Dry mouth or lips
- Headache that does not ease with rest

If you notice these signs and cannot keep fluids down, contact your GP or midwife promptly. Severe dehydration — the condition known as hyperemesis gravidarum at its most serious — can require hospital treatment with intravenous fluids.

## Practical Ways to Stay Hydrated {#practical-ways-to-stay-hydrated}

### Sip Little and Often

Drinking a full glass of water in one go is one of the most common triggers for vomiting during morning sickness. Instead, aim for small sips throughout the day — a few mouthfuls every ten to fifteen minutes adds up to a meaningful volume without overwhelming a sensitive stomach.

Keeping a small bottle of water within reach (rather than having to go and find a drink when you feel ill) makes it easier to maintain this habit.

### Find What You Can Tolerate

Plain water is not the only option, and for many women it is one of the harder ones. Try:

- **Cold water** — many women find cold or even iced water easier to tolerate than room temperature.
- **Fizzy water** — the carbonation can settle nausea for some women, though it worsens bloating for others. Try it and see.
- **Ice chips or ice cubes** — sucking on ice is gentler on the stomach than sipping and can be surprisingly effective at maintaining fluid intake.
- **Diluted squash** — a weak, cold squash is often easier to keep down than plain water.
- **Herbal teas** — ginger or peppermint tea (cooled down or iced) are popular choices. Both ginger and peppermint have a long tradition of use for nausea, though evidence for their effectiveness varies.
- **Clear broth or soup** — provides both fluids and a small amount of salt and minerals.
- **Water-rich fruits** — melon, cucumber, strawberries, and grapes contribute to fluid intake without requiring you to drink.

### Oral Rehydration Salts

If you have been vomiting repeatedly and are struggling to keep plain fluids down, an oral rehydration solution (such as Dioralyte, available over the counter at UK pharmacies) can help replace the electrolytes lost through vomiting. These are generally considered safe in pregnancy, but check with your pharmacist or midwife before using them.

### Timing Matters

Many women find their nausea has a pattern — worse first thing in the morning, or worse in the evenings. Identifying your lower-nausea windows and prioritising fluids during those times can help you catch up without fighting your worst symptoms.

Drinking between meals rather than during them also tends to be easier on a sensitive stomach.

## When to Seek Help {#when-to-seek-help}

Contact your GP or midwife the same day if:

- You cannot keep any fluids down for more than 12 hours
- You are vomiting more than three or four times a day consistently
- You notice signs of dehydration (dark urine, dizziness, dry mouth)
- You are losing weight
- You feel so unwell you cannot function day to day

Hyperemesis gravidarum (HG) is a severe form of pregnancy sickness that affects a small number of women and requires medical treatment. It is not a sign of weakness, and you should not try to manage it at home without support. Hospital treatment with intravenous fluids and anti-sickness medication can make a significant difference.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much should I drink with morning sickness?

Aim for around 1.5 to 2 litres of fluid a day in total, taken as small sips throughout the day rather than large amounts at once. This includes fluid from food sources such as fruit and soup. If vomiting is frequent, focus on tolerating any amount of fluid rather than hitting a fixed target — every sip counts.

### What if water makes me feel sick?

You are not alone — plain water is a common trigger for many women with morning sickness. Try cold water, fizzy water, ice chips, diluted squash, herbal tea, or water-rich fruits such as melon. Some women find drinking through a straw helps reduce the amount that hits the stomach at once.

### When should I seek help for dehydration?

Contact your GP or midwife promptly if you cannot keep any fluids down for over 12 hours, if you notice dark urine and dizziness, or if you feel very unwell. Do not wait to see if it improves on its own — dehydration in pregnancy can escalate quickly and respond well to treatment when caught early.
