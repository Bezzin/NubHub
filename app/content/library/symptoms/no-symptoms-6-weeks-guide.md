# No Symptoms at 6 Weeks: Should I Worry?

At 6 weeks, many women start feeling symptoms. But some don't, and that's OK. Having little or no nausea, fatigue, or breast soreness at 6 weeks does not mean your pregnancy is in trouble — it may simply mean your body is handling the hormonal changes quietly. Here is what to know.

## Why You Might Have No Symptoms at 6 Weeks {#s1}

Pregnancy symptoms are driven primarily by rising levels of the hormone hCG (human chorionic gonadotropin), which the placenta begins producing after implantation. Symptoms like nausea, sore breasts, and fatigue tend to appear as hCG rises — but the timing and intensity vary enormously between women, and between pregnancies in the same woman.

Several factors can explain why you might feel nothing much at 6 weeks:

- **Your hCG may still be rising** — hCG levels increase rapidly in early pregnancy, typically doubling every two to three days, and some women only develop noticeable symptoms once levels have climbed higher
- **You may be less sensitive to hormonal changes** — some women's bodies simply respond less dramatically to the hormones of early pregnancy; this is not a sign that anything is wrong
- **Your dates might be slightly off** — if your cycle is longer or shorter than average, or if you ovulated later than expected, you may actually be a little earlier than 6 weeks based on your last period date
- **Some women just do not get many symptoms** — particularly with nausea; it is entirely possible to have a healthy pregnancy with minimal or no morning sickness

## What Symptoms Are Common at 6 Weeks? {#s2}

Just so you have a sense of what is typical, symptoms that often appear around 6 weeks include:

- Nausea (with or without vomiting), often worse in the morning but potentially present at any time
- Sore, heavy, or tingly breasts
- Fatigue — sometimes extreme
- Needing to urinate more frequently
- Bloating or a feeling of fullness
- Heightened sense of smell
- Mild cramping or a pulling sensation in the lower abdomen
- Light-headedness

None of these are guaranteed, and having none of them at 6 weeks is not unusual. Symptoms also tend to come and go — feeling fine one day and awful the next is common and does not indicate anything has changed with the pregnancy.

## When to Seek Reassurance or Medical Advice {#s3}

No symptoms alone is generally not a reason to seek urgent medical advice. However, there are situations where contacting your midwife, GP, or early pregnancy unit (EPU) is appropriate:

- You had significant symptoms (nausea, breast soreness) that have **suddenly stopped completely** — particularly if accompanied by any bleeding or pain. A sudden disappearance of symptoms can sometimes warrant a check, though it is often normal
- You have **one-sided pain**, especially if it is sharp or severe — this can be a sign of an ectopic pregnancy, which is a medical emergency; call 111 or go to A&E if pain is severe
- You have **heavy red bleeding** with or without clots — contact your GP, call 111, or attend your EPU
- You are simply struggling with anxiety and need reassurance — a private early pregnancy scan at 7 or 8 weeks can confirm the presence of a heartbeat and is a legitimate option if worry is affecting your wellbeing

If you are concerned but not experiencing pain or bleeding, it is absolutely reasonable to contact your midwife or GP to talk through how you are feeling.

## The Reassurance Scan Option {#s4}

If the absence of symptoms is causing you significant anxiety, a private **reassurance scan** at 7 or 8 weeks can be very helpful. From around 6.5 to 7 weeks, a heartbeat is typically visible on ultrasound if the pregnancy is progressing. Many private scanning clinics in the UK offer early pregnancy scans from around £70–£120. Your GP or midwife can sometimes refer you to an early pregnancy unit if there is clinical concern, though EPU referrals are generally for women with bleeding or pain rather than symptom absence alone.

Remember that even with a scan, very early pregnancy does not come with guarantees — a visible heartbeat at 7 weeks is genuinely reassuring but not a complete guarantee that all will be well. However, for many women a scan provides meaningful peace of mind.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Should I be worried about no symptoms at 6 weeks?

No, not necessarily. While many women have symptoms at 6 weeks, many others feel very little, and this can be completely normal. Symptoms vary hugely between individuals and are not a reliable indicator of how the pregnancy is progressing. If you are very anxious, speaking to your GP or midwife, or booking a private early scan at 7–8 weeks, can provide useful reassurance.

### Can no symptoms mean a missed miscarriage?

A missed miscarriage (where the pregnancy stops developing but the body does not immediately miscarry) can sometimes cause symptoms to fade. However, lack of symptoms alone is not a reliable indicator of miscarriage — many healthy pregnancies involve minimal symptoms. Signs that warrant medical attention include a sudden and complete disappearance of symptoms that were previously strong, especially alongside bleeding or pain. If you are worried, contact your GP or midwife rather than trying to interpret symptoms (or their absence) alone.

### When do pregnancy symptoms usually peak?

For most women who experience nausea and other first-trimester symptoms, they tend to be at their worst between around 8 and 10 weeks, then gradually ease. Most women notice a significant improvement by around 12 to 14 weeks, though this varies. If your symptoms are mild at 6 weeks, they may still increase over the coming weeks — or they may stay mild throughout.
