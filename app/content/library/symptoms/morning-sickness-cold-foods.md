# Cold Foods for Morning Sickness

When smells trigger nausea, cold foods can be a surprisingly effective option. This page explains why they help, which cold meals and snacks are worth keeping to hand, and how to make cold eating work as part of a practical strategy for getting through the first trimester.

## Why Cold Foods Are Easier {#why-cold-foods-are-easier}

Heat intensifies the aroma of food, and strong smells are one of the most common nausea triggers in pregnancy. The olfactory system becomes hypersensitive in early pregnancy for many women, meaning scents that were previously unnoticeable — cooking meat, garlic, coffee, even toast — can now trigger an immediate wave of nausea.

Cold foods give off much less odour than hot ones. A cold sandwich, a bowl of yogurt, or a piece of fruit produces almost no smell, which removes the sensory trigger before you even take a bite. This is why many women find they can manage a cold meal when the idea of anything hot is impossible.

There are other practical advantages too. Cold foods require little or no cooking, which means less time near a hot stove or oven where smells are concentrated. Preparation is quicker, which reduces the window during which you are exposed to cooking odours. And because cold foods are often simple — crackers, fruit, cold pasta — they tend to be easy to eat in small portions, which suits the little-and-often approach that helps many women manage nausea.

Cold foods are not a cure for morning sickness, but they are a genuinely useful strategy for getting calories in when hot food is not an option.

## Good Cold Food Options to Keep Ready {#good-cold-food-options}

The following cold foods are well tolerated by many women with morning sickness and are easy to keep stocked in the fridge:

**Sandwiches and wraps**
Mild fillings work best — plain chicken, hard cheese, cucumber, or hummus. Avoid very strong-smelling fillings such as tuna or egg mayonnaise if smell is a significant trigger for you. Wraps and pitta bread are a useful alternative to sliced bread if the texture of bread is off-putting.

**Cold pasta or rice**
Cooked pasta or rice cooled and kept in the fridge is one of the most versatile cold options. Add a simple dressing of olive oil and lemon, or eat it plain. Cold pasta with mild pesto or grated parmesan is filling and low in odour.

**Yogurt**
Plain or fruit yogurt is gentle on the stomach, provides protein and calcium, and has almost no odour. Full-fat yogurt is more sustaining than low-fat varieties if your appetite is poor overall. Ensure it is made from pasteurised milk, which all standard supermarket yogurts in the UK are.

**Fruit**
Chilled fruit — watermelon, grapes, melon, apple slices — is refreshing and easy to eat in small quantities. Watermelon is particularly useful as it is mostly water and can contribute to hydration.

**Smoothies**
Blended with cold yogurt, milk, and fruit, smoothies can deliver a reasonable number of calories and some protein without requiring much effort to consume. Keep ingredients pre-portioned in bags in the freezer so they are ready to blend quickly.

**Crackers and cheese**
Plain crackers or oatcakes paired with a mild hard cheese such as cheddar are a classic combination for nausea. The crackers settle the stomach and the cheese provides protein and fat for sustaining energy.

**Cold cucumber, carrot, or celery sticks**
These require no preparation and are easy to graze on throughout the day. Pair with hummus for more sustenance.

**Chilled milk or cold water with lemon**
Some women find cold drinks easier to tolerate than warm ones. Chilled milk can also help to settle stomach acid, which often accompanies nausea in pregnancy.

## Cold Foods and Pregnancy Safety {#cold-foods-and-pregnancy-safety}

Cold foods are safe in pregnancy, but a few standard precautions apply:

- **Avoid unpasteurised soft cheeses** such as brie, camembert, or soft blue cheese — use hard cheeses like cheddar instead
- **Check deli meats are safe** — cold cooked chicken from the fridge at home is fine, but certain processed deli meats such as pâté are best avoided. Pre-packaged sliced cooked chicken, turkey, or ham from a supermarket is generally fine
- **Keep cold foods properly refrigerated** and check use-by dates — bacteria multiply more quickly in food left at room temperature
- **Wash all fruit and salad** before eating, even pre-washed varieties

If you are relying heavily on cold foods because hot food is impossible, that is entirely understandable in the first trimester. Try to include a protein source (cheese, yogurt, chicken, or eggs) alongside carbohydrates to keep your energy more stable.

## Making Cold Eating Practical Day to Day {#making-it-practical}

The key to making cold foods work is preparation when you feel well enough. Spend a few minutes on a good day — or ask a partner or family member — to:

- Batch-cook and cool pasta or rice so it is ready to eat throughout the week
- Pre-slice fruit and keep it in containers in the fridge
- Keep a selection of crackers, cheese, and mild fillings easily accessible
- Have a supply of yogurt pots or smoothie ingredients ready to go

This way, when nausea peaks — often in the morning or late afternoon — you can reach for something without having to prepare it from scratch.

If you are struggling to keep any food down, or if you are losing weight or unable to stay hydrated, speak to your GP or midwife. Severe nausea and vomiting in pregnancy (hyperemesis gravidarum) is a medical condition that needs treatment and is not something you simply have to push through.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#frequently-asked-questions}

### Why do cold foods help morning sickness?

Cold foods give off far less aroma than hot ones. Because heightened sensitivity to smell is one of the most common triggers for pregnancy nausea, reducing odour during eating can make food much easier to tolerate. Cold foods also tend to be simpler in texture and flavour, which suits a queasy stomach.

### What cold foods are good to keep ready for morning sickness?

Sandwiches with mild fillings, cold pasta, yogurt, plain crackers with hard cheese, chilled fruit, and smoothies are all practical and low-odour options. Keeping a few of these prepared in the fridge means you can eat without cooking anything when nausea is at its worst.

### Are cold foods safe to eat in pregnancy?

Yes, as long as you follow standard pregnancy food safety guidance. Stick to pasteurised dairy products, avoid unpasteurised soft cheeses, wash all fruit and salad, and keep cold foods properly refrigerated. Most everyday cold foods — yogurt, hard cheese, cold chicken, fruit, crackers — are safe and appropriate in pregnancy.
