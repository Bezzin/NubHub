# Subchorionic Bleeding in Pregnancy: What to Know

A subchorionic haematoma is a small collection of blood between the pregnancy sac and the wall of your womb. It is a common finding on early scans and a frequent cause of bleeding in the first trimester. While the word sounds frightening, most subchorionic bleeds resolve on their own without affecting the pregnancy. This guide explains what they are and what to expect.

## How Subchorionic Haematomas Form

No one knows exactly why they form, but it is thought to happen when a small blood vessel in the lining of the womb ruptures as the embryo implants. The blood pools between the chorionic membrane and the uterine wall. Your body either reabsorbs it or you bleed it out as dark brown or red spotting. Many women only discover they have one during a routine early scan.

## What Happens Next

Most subchorionic haematomas are small and resolve within a few weeks. Your midwife may arrange a follow-up scan to check it has shrunk. Large haematomas, or those behind the placenta, are monitored more closely as they can slightly increase the risk of complications. If you experience bleeding that increases or turns bright red, contact your early pregnancy unit. Otherwise, rest and wait - your body is usually excellent at clearing it.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Does a subchorionic haematoma mean miscarriage?**

Not usually. Most small haematomas resolve without affecting the pregnancy. Your scan will confirm the size and location.

**Will I need bed rest?**

Bed rest is not proven to help. Most midwives advise avoiding strenuous activity and intercourse until the bleed has settled.

**How is a subchorionic haematoma treated?**

There is no specific treatment - your body reabsorbs it or you pass the blood. Follow-up scans track its size.
