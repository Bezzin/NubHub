# Pregnancy Superfoods: Complete List

Eating well during pregnancy does not have to mean a complicated diet overhaul. Focusing on a handful of particularly nutrient-dense foods — sometimes called superfoods — can make it much easier to get what you and your baby need each day without obsessing over every meal.

These are not exotic or expensive ingredients. Most of them are everyday UK staples that are easy to prepare, widely available, and genuinely useful for your pregnancy.

## Why Certain Foods Stand Out

During pregnancy your body needs more of several key nutrients: folate, iron, calcium, iodine, vitamin D, omega-3 fatty acids, and protein, among others. Some foods deliver several of these in a single serving, which is why they earn the "superfood" label. There is no magic bullet, but regularly including these foods means your nutritional bases are more likely to be covered even on days when your appetite is unpredictable.

## Top Pregnancy Superfoods

**Eggs**
Eggs are one of the most complete foods you can eat in pregnancy. They provide protein, choline (vital for brain development), vitamin D, B12, and healthy fats. Cook them thoroughly — avoid runny or raw eggs unless they carry the British Lion mark, which means they are laid under a salmonella vaccination programme and are considered safe to eat soft or runny.

**Cooked salmon and oily fish**
Oily fish such as salmon, mackerel, and sardines are rich in DHA (an omega-3 fatty acid) that supports your baby's brain and eye development. Aim for up to two portions of oily fish per week — the NHS advises not to exceed this due to naturally occurring pollutants. Salmon is also a good source of vitamin D and protein.

**Spinach and other dark leafy greens**
Spinach, kale, and spring greens are packed with folate, iron, calcium, and vitamin C. Folate is especially important in early pregnancy for neural tube development. Even if you are past the first trimester, keeping folate intake up supports ongoing red blood cell production.

**Sweet potatoes**
Sweet potatoes are an excellent source of beta-carotene, which your body converts into vitamin A. They also provide fibre (helpful if constipation is an issue) and slow-releasing carbohydrates that help maintain energy levels. Unlike liver, which contains preformed vitamin A at levels that can be harmful in pregnancy, the beta-carotene in sweet potatoes is safe.

**Lentils and legumes**
Lentils, chickpeas, and beans are outstanding plant-based sources of protein, iron, and folate. They are also high in fibre, which supports gut health. If you are vegetarian or vegan, lentils in particular are one of the most useful foods to include regularly.

**Yogurt (full-fat)**
Full-fat natural or Greek yogurt provides calcium, protein, and beneficial probiotics. Calcium is essential for your baby's bone and teeth development, and if your intake is low your body will draw calcium from your own bones to meet the baby's needs. Yogurt is also easy to eat even when nausea makes larger meals unappealing.

**Berries**
Strawberries, blueberries, and raspberries are high in antioxidants, vitamin C, and fibre. Vitamin C helps your body absorb non-haem (plant-based) iron more efficiently, so pairing berries or a glass of orange juice with an iron-rich meal is a practical habit worth building.

**Avocado**
Avocado provides folate, potassium, healthy monounsaturated fats, and vitamin K. The fats in avocado also help your body absorb fat-soluble vitamins. It is one of the more calorie-dense options here, which can be helpful if nausea is making it hard to eat enough.

**Nuts and seeds**
Walnuts, almonds, cashews, and pumpkin seeds provide protein, healthy fats, magnesium, and zinc. Walnuts in particular are a good plant-based source of omega-3. A small handful as a snack is a simple way to add nutrients without a full meal.

**Oats**
Oats are rich in slow-releasing carbohydrates, beta-glucan fibre, B vitamins, and iron. They are easy to digest and can be a lifesaver if morning sickness means you need something bland and gentle early in the day. Adding milk, yogurt, and berries turns a bowl of porridge into a genuinely nutritious pregnancy meal.

## Practical Tips for Getting More of These Foods

- Keep a bag of frozen spinach in the freezer — it wilts into pasta, soups, and scrambled eggs without affecting the flavour much.
- Hard-boil a batch of eggs at the start of the week for easy snacks.
- Tinned salmon and sardines count just as well as fresh, and are cheaper.
- Swap white rice or pasta for lentils a couple of times a week in stews and curries.
- Add a handful of mixed nuts to your morning yogurt alongside berries.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What are the best foods to eat in pregnancy?

Focus on: eggs, cooked oily fish (up to two portions a week), leafy greens, lentils, yogurt, berries, nuts, and wholegrains like oats. These provide the nutrients your baby needs for development. Try to eat a varied diet rather than relying on any single food.

### Are there foods that boost baby's brain development?

Certain nutrients play a well-established role in brain development. DHA (found in oily fish such as salmon) and choline (found in eggs) are particularly relevant. Iodine — found in dairy, eggs, and fish — is also critical for the baby's neurological development. Including these regularly as part of a balanced diet is a sensible approach.

### Do I need to change my diet completely?

Not necessarily. If you are already eating a reasonably balanced diet, focusing on the foods above a few times a week — rather than trying to eat all of them every day — is enough for most people. Your prenatal vitamin covers some of the gaps. If you have specific concerns about your diet, your midwife or GP can refer you to a registered dietitian.
