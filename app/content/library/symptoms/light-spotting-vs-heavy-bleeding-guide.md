# Light Spotting vs Heavy Bleeding in Early Pregnancy

One of the most common questions midwives hear is whether the bleeding a woman has noticed is spotting or something more. The distinction matters because it guides what action you should take. This guide explains the difference clearly so you can describe your symptoms accurately and know when to seek help.

## How to Tell the Difference {#how-to-tell-the-difference}

**Spotting** is light enough that you only notice it when you wipe, or see a few drops on your underwear. You do not need a pad. The colour is often brown or pinkish — older blood that has taken time to travel from the cervix. It may appear once and then stop, or come and go over a day or two.

**Light bleeding** might require a thin panty liner but does not soak it. It may be pink or red and can be accompanied by mild cramping.

**Heavy bleeding** is anything that requires a sanitary pad, and is especially concerning if you are soaking through one within an hour. Bright red blood is fresh and more significant than dark or brown blood. The presence of clots, or tissue that looks grey or fleshy, always warrants immediate reporting.

A useful rule of thumb: if you are reaching for a pad, treat it as bleeding rather than spotting, and contact your midwife or early pregnancy unit the same day.

## What Each Might Mean {#what-each-might-mean}

### Spotting

Light spotting in early pregnancy is very common and often benign. The most frequent causes include:

- **Implantation bleeding** — when the fertilised egg embeds in the uterine lining, around 10 to 14 days after conception. It typically lasts just one to two days.
- **Cervical irritation** — the cervix becomes much more sensitive and well supplied with blood during pregnancy. Sex, an internal examination, or even a smear test can cause a small amount of spotting that resolves quickly.
- **Hormonal fluctuations** — around the time your period would have been due, some women notice a small bleed. This is sometimes called a "phantom period" and is harmless.

Spotting alone, without cramping or pain, is usually not a cause for immediate alarm. However, it is always worth mentioning to your midwife — even if it has already stopped — so it can be noted and monitored.

### Bleeding That Needs Assessment

Bleeding that is heavier, brighter red, or accompanied by other symptoms needs to be assessed the same day. Possible causes include:

- **Threatened miscarriage** — the pregnancy is intact but there is some bleeding from the uterine lining. Many pregnancies continue normally after this, but a scan is needed to confirm.
- **Subchorionic haematoma** — a small collection of blood between the placenta and the uterine wall. Often discovered on a scan, and frequently resolves on its own.
- **Ectopic pregnancy** — when the embryo has implanted outside the uterus, usually in a fallopian tube. This is a medical emergency. Signs include one-sided pelvic pain, shoulder tip pain, dizziness, and bleeding. Call 999 if you suspect this.
- **Miscarriage** — heavy bleeding with cramping and the passing of clots or tissue may indicate pregnancy loss. Contact your early pregnancy unit for a scan and support.

Bleeding does not automatically mean pregnancy loss, but it does need a professional assessment. Ring your early pregnancy unit (EPU), NHS 111, or your midwife to describe what you are seeing and get guidance on next steps.

## When to Call 999 Immediately

Call 999 or go straight to A&E if you experience:

- Heavy bleeding that is soaking through a pad quickly
- Severe one-sided pelvic pain
- Shoulder tip pain
- Feeling faint, dizzy, or like you might collapse
- Bleeding with a fever

These symptoms can indicate an ectopic pregnancy rupturing, which is a life-threatening emergency.

## Describing Your Symptoms Accurately

When you call your midwife or EPU, they will ask you to describe the bleeding. It helps to note:

- The colour (brown, pink, or bright red)
- The amount (spotting on paper, needing a liner, needing a pad)
- Whether there are any clots or tissue
- Whether you have any pain, and if so, where
- How many weeks pregnant you are

The more precise you can be, the better they can triage your situation.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Do I need a pad to know if it is heavy bleeding?

If you are using a pad and it is collecting blood rather than just protecting your underwear, treat it as bleeding, not spotting, and contact your midwife or early pregnancy unit the same day. Any soaking through a pad quickly is an emergency — call 999.

### Can spotting turn into heavy bleeding?

Yes, occasionally. If your spotting increases, turns bright red, or you begin passing clots, contact your early pregnancy unit the same day. Do not wait to see if it settles on its own once bleeding becomes heavier.

### Is mild cramping with spotting normal?

Mild period-like cramps alongside light spotting can be normal as your womb grows and adjusts. However, severe or one-sided pain combined with any bleeding needs urgent assessment, as it can be a sign of ectopic pregnancy.
