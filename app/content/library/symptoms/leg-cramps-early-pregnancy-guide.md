# Leg Cramps in Early Pregnancy

Leg cramps are those sudden, gripping muscle spasms that often strike in the calf, foot or thigh. They're very common in pregnancy and tend to be worse later on, but some women notice them in the early weeks too. They can be uncomfortable and often happen at night, yet they're usually harmless. Here's what causes them, what helps, and the warning signs that mean you should get checked.

## Causes {#s1}

The exact cause of pregnancy leg cramps isn't fully understood, but several things are thought to play a part. Changes in your circulation, the extra demands pregnancy places on your body, tiredness and being less active than usual can all contribute. Dehydration and not getting enough of certain minerals may also make cramps more likely.

Cramps often come on at night and can wake you from sleep, leaving the muscle feeling tender afterwards. This pattern is normal and, on its own, isn't a sign that anything is wrong.

## Prevention and Relief {#s2}

There's a lot you can do to ease leg cramps. When a cramp strikes, try gently straightening your leg and pulling your toes up towards your shin, then massage the muscle until it relaxes. Walking around for a few minutes afterwards can help too.

To reduce how often they happen, try these everyday steps:

- Stretch your calf muscles gently before bed.
- Stay well hydrated by drinking water through the day.
- Keep gently active with walking or pregnancy-safe exercise.
- Avoid sitting or standing in the same position for long periods.

Some women find magnesium helpful, but it's best to ask your GP or midwife before starting any supplement so you get advice suited to you.

## When to Seek Help {#s3}

Ordinary leg cramps ease within a few minutes and settle on their own. You should contact your GP or midwife if the pain doesn't go away, if cramping is severe or happening very often, or if it's affecting your daily life.

It's important to know that persistent calf pain is not always a simple cramp. If one leg becomes painful, swollen, warm, red or tender to touch, contact your GP or maternity unit urgently, or call NHS 111 for advice, as these can be signs of a blood clot (DVT) that needs prompt assessment. Trust your instincts — if something feels wrong, it's always worth getting checked.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Can I take magnesium for leg cramps in pregnancy?

Some women find magnesium reduces leg cramps, and it's commonly suggested. As with any supplement in pregnancy, check with your GP or midwife first so you can be sure it's right for you and take a sensible dose.

### Why do leg cramps happen at night?

Night-time cramps are very common in pregnancy and may relate to circulation, tiredness and how you've been positioned in bed. Gentle calf stretches before sleep and staying hydrated during the day can help reduce them.

### Are leg cramps a sign something is wrong?

Usually not — they're a normal part of pregnancy for many women. The exception is pain in one leg with swelling, warmth or redness, which needs urgent medical assessment to rule out a clot.

For personal advice about leg cramps or any pregnancy symptom, speak to your midwife or GP.
