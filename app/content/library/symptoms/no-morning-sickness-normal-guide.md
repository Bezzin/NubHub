# Is No Morning Sickness Normal?

A significant number of pregnant women experience little or no nausea — and yet the absence of morning sickness can feel more worrying than having it. If you're sailing through the first trimester without running to the bathroom, you are not alone, and you are almost certainly not missing a sign that something is wrong.

## Why Some Women Don't Get Morning Sickness {#s1}

Morning sickness is caused by rising levels of human chorionic gonadotropin (hCG) and oestrogen, combined with how sensitive your body is to those hormones. Several factors influence whether you feel sick or not:

- **Hormone sensitivity varies.** Two women can have identical hCG levels and have completely different experiences. Your body's individual response to pregnancy hormones is largely down to biology, not the health of your pregnancy.
- **Genetics play a role.** If your mother or sisters had little or no nausea in pregnancy, you are more likely to follow the same pattern.
- **It is genuinely common.** Estimates suggest that anywhere from one in five to one in four pregnant women have very mild or no nausea at all. You are not in a tiny minority.
- **Timing matters.** If you are very early in pregnancy — five or six weeks — hCG has not yet peaked. Many women who will later feel sick simply haven't reached that point yet.
- **Some pregnancies naturally produce lower hCG.** Lower hCG can still be entirely normal for that individual. The range of normal hCG in early pregnancy is very wide.

## Is No Morning Sickness a Problem? {#s2}

In the vast majority of cases, no. Not having nausea does not mean your pregnancy is less viable. Many people worry because they have heard that morning sickness is a sign of a strong pregnancy — and while it is true that certain conditions associated with pregnancy loss can sometimes cause a drop in symptoms, the absence of symptoms from the beginning is a very different thing.

Healthy pregnancies without any nausea are common. The most reliable way to reassure yourself about your pregnancy's progress is an ultrasound scan, not how sick you feel.

**When to contact your midwife or GP:**
- If you had nausea and it has suddenly disappeared before 10–12 weeks and you have other concerns (such as pain or bleeding), it is worth mentioning to your midwife or GP.
- Sudden loss of symptoms alongside cramping or bleeding should always be assessed promptly — call 111 if you cannot reach your GP or midwife.
- If you feel completely well throughout pregnancy and are simply anxious, a private early reassurance scan (usually available from around 7–8 weeks) can show a heartbeat and provide peace of mind.

## What Is "Normal" in Early Pregnancy? {#s3}

It is worth knowing that morning sickness itself is highly variable even when it does occur. Some women feel mildly queasy only in the morning; others feel sick all day. Some vomit frequently; many feel nauseous but never vomit. Others feel completely fine. All of these patterns can reflect a healthy pregnancy.

Symptoms also come and go. It is normal for nausea to be worse on some days than others, or to improve slightly and then return. The absence of a specific symptom — whether nausea, breast tenderness, or fatigue — is rarely a reliable indicator of pregnancy health on its own.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Is it normal to have no morning sickness?

Yes. A significant proportion of pregnant women have no morning sickness at all and go on to have healthy pregnancies. Not having nausea does not mean your pregnancy is failing or that your hormone levels are low. Every pregnancy is different.

### Should I be worried if I have no symptoms at all?

Not necessarily. Some women have minimal or no symptoms throughout the first trimester and go on to have straightforward pregnancies. If you are concerned, speak to your midwife or GP. A reassurance scan from around 7–8 weeks can show your baby's heartbeat, which is the most reliable early indicator of how a pregnancy is progressing.

### Does no morning sickness mean it's a boy?

No. The idea that no sickness means a boy is an old wives' tale. Research does not support a reliable link between the presence or severity of morning sickness and the sex of the baby.
