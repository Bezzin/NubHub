# Vitamin B6 for Morning Sickness

Vitamin B6, also called pyridoxine, has some of the strongest evidence of any non-drug treatment for morning sickness. This page explains the research, safe doses, and how to combine it sensibly with prenatal vitamins so you get the benefit without exceeding safe limits.

## What the Research Shows {#what-the-research-shows}

Several randomised controlled trials have found that vitamin B6 modestly reduces nausea in early pregnancy, even when it does not significantly reduce vomiting. Both the American College of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists and the Royal College of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists recognise it as a reasonable first-line option for managing mild to moderate pregnancy nausea.

The exact mechanism is not fully understood, but it is thought to relate to B6's role in supporting neurotransmitter production — particularly serotonin — which influences nausea signals. Benefit is often noticed within a week of starting supplementation. It is considered safe at low to moderate doses when used appropriately, though very high doses taken over a long period can cause peripheral neuropathy (nerve problems), so sticking within recommended ranges is important.

It is worth setting realistic expectations: vitamin B6 tends to take the edge off nausea rather than eliminate it entirely. For most women it is one tool among several, not a cure in itself.

## Getting the Dose Right {#getting-the-dose-right}

Most of the studies that showed benefit used doses in the range of 10 to 25 mg, taken two or three times daily. This is a relatively low dose compared to the upper limits set by health authorities, but it is enough to be clinically meaningful in the research.

The important practical point for UK women is that many prenatal multivitamins already contain some vitamin B6. Check your supplement label before adding a separate B6 tablet. If your prenatal already contains 10 mg or more of B6, adding another high-dose B6 supplement on top means your total intake could rise well beyond the studied range. Staying within sensible combined totals is the key consideration — your midwife or pharmacist can advise on what is appropriate for your specific supplements.

## How to Take It {#how-to-take-it}

Taking B6 with food can help reduce any stomach irritation. Spreading doses across the day — for example, morning, afternoon, and evening — tends to work better than taking it all at once, matching how the research trials were typically structured. Some women find it most helpful to take a dose before bed to reduce nausea first thing in the morning.

B6 is not a magic fix, and many women find the best results come from combining it with other evidence-supported strategies. Ginger in various forms, acupressure wristbands, eating small frequent meals, staying hydrated, and avoiding trigger smells are all approaches that can complement a B6 supplement. No single remedy works for everyone, and it often takes some trial and error to find the combination that helps most.

## When to Seek More Help {#when-to-seek-more-help}

Vitamin B6 is suitable for managing mild to moderate nausea, but it is not sufficient for severe pregnancy sickness. If you cannot keep food or fluids down, are losing weight, feeling dizzy or faint, producing very dark urine, or are not functioning day-to-day, you may have hyperemesis gravidarum — a condition that needs proper medical treatment. Contact your GP or midwife promptly in that case; do not try to manage it alone with supplements.

Call 111 if you are concerned and cannot reach your GP or midwife. Go to A&E if you are severely dehydrated, unable to keep any fluids down, or feel very unwell.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#frequently-asked-questions}

### Is vitamin B6 safe in pregnancy?

At the low to moderate doses studied for morning sickness, yes — it is considered safe. The concern with B6 is with very high doses taken for extended periods, which can cause nerve problems. Staying within the ranges your midwife recommends and factoring in what is already in your prenatal vitamin keeps you on safe ground.

### Can I take B6 with my prenatal vitamin?

Often yes, but check both labels first. If your prenatal vitamin already contains B6, you may not need a large additional dose. Your pharmacist can quickly help you work out your combined total and whether it sits within a sensible range.

### Does B6 stop vomiting as well as nausea?

It tends to help nausea more than vomiting. Many women notice nausea eases while vomiting episodes remain about the same. If vomiting is frequent or severe, speak to your GP about prescription antiemetic options that are safe in pregnancy — you do not have to suffer through it untreated.

### How quickly does vitamin B6 work for morning sickness?

Most women who find it helpful notice some improvement within a few days to a week. If you have been taking it consistently for two weeks with no change, it may not be the right tool for your particular nausea, and it is worth speaking to your GP about other options.
