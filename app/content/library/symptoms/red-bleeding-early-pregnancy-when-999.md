# Red Bleeding in Early Pregnancy: When to Call 999

Bright red bleeding in early pregnancy is more concerning than brown or pink spotting. While it does not always mean the worst, it does warrant prompt attention. This guide helps you recognise the red-flag symptoms that need a 999 call, what can wait for a same-day call to your early pregnancy unit, and what to expect when you seek help.

## Red-Flag Symptoms That Need 999

Call 999 immediately — or get someone to take you to A&E without delay — if you experience any of the following:

- **Heavy bleeding** that soaks through a full-size sanitary pad in under an hour
- **Severe or one-sided abdominal pain**, particularly if it is sharp and persistent
- **Shoulder tip pain** — an aching or stabbing sensation at the very tip of your shoulder blade
- **Dizziness, fainting, or feeling like you might pass out**
- **A racing or pounding heartbeat** at rest
- **Pale, clammy skin or feeling suddenly very unwell**

These symptoms together — or even individually alongside bleeding — can indicate an ectopic pregnancy that has ruptured, or internal bleeding from another cause. Both are genuine medical emergencies. Do not drive yourself: call an ambulance or ask someone to take you immediately.

**Why shoulder tip pain matters:** When blood pools inside the abdomen, it can irritate the diaphragm. This is referred as pain to the tip of the shoulder (not the shoulder muscle, but the very point of the shoulder blade). It is a classic warning sign of internal bleeding from an ectopic pregnancy and should never be ignored.

## When to Call Your Early Pregnancy Unit Instead

Not all red bleeding is an emergency. If you are bleeding but the flow is lighter — more like a period or lighter — and you do not have any of the red-flag symptoms listed above, contact your early pregnancy unit (EPU) or midwife the same day. Most EPUs can offer an urgent scan appointment, sometimes on the same day you call.

You should also call your EPU if:
- You have mild cramping alongside the bleeding but no severe pain
- The bleeding has stopped but you are worried
- You have had a previous miscarriage or ectopic pregnancy and are understandably anxious

Many women who experience red bleeding in early pregnancy go on to have healthy pregnancies. Bleeding can occur for a variety of reasons, including implantation, cervical sensitivity, or a small subchorionic haematoma (a pocket of blood near the pregnancy sac). The only way to know what is happening is through assessment, which is why it is important to seek help rather than waiting it out at home.

## What Causes Red Bleeding in Early Pregnancy?

Red bleeding in early pregnancy can have several causes, ranging from harmless to serious:

**Implantation bleeding:** Some women have a small amount of light bleeding around the time the embryo implants into the womb lining, usually around the time of an expected period. This is typically very light and short-lived, and the blood may appear pink or red.

**Cervical sensitivity:** Pregnancy hormones increase blood flow to the cervix, making it more delicate. Some women bleed lightly after sexual intercourse or an internal examination — this is not harmful to the pregnancy.

**Subchorionic haematoma:** A collection of blood between the pregnancy sac and the womb wall. This can cause red or brown bleeding and is usually monitored with scans. Many resolve on their own.

**Miscarriage:** Heavy red bleeding with cramping can indicate a miscarriage, particularly if tissue is passed. This is devastating and deserves immediate compassionate support from your EPU. If you suspect you are miscarrying, contact your EPU or GP the same day.

**Ectopic pregnancy:** When a fertilised egg implants outside the womb — most often in a fallopian tube — it cannot develop normally and poses a serious risk. Bleeding with one-sided pain, dizziness, or shoulder tip pain are the warning signs. This is a medical emergency.

## What Happens When You Seek Help

When you contact your EPU or A&E, they will typically:

- Take a brief history of your symptoms, pregnancy dates, and any relevant medical history
- Arrange a blood test to check your hCG (pregnancy hormone) levels, and in some cases a progesterone level
- Organise an ultrasound scan — this may be a transvaginal scan, which gives clearer images in early pregnancy
- Explain what they can see and what the next steps are

If a heartbeat is visible on scan and the bleeding is settling, this is a reassuring sign. If the scan is inconclusive — which can happen very early in pregnancy — they will usually repeat blood tests 48 hours later to see how hCG levels are changing, and arrange a follow-up scan.

It is normal to feel frightened while waiting for results. If you need emotional support, the Tommy's helpline (available during business hours) and the Miscarriage Association offer trained listeners.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can red bleeding be normal in early pregnancy?

Some red bleeding does occur in pregnancies that continue healthily, including around the time of implantation or due to cervical sensitivity. However, all episodes of red bleeding should be assessed by a midwife or at an early pregnancy unit — it is not something to monitor at home without professional input.

### What does shoulder tip pain mean?

Shoulder tip pain — felt at the very point of the shoulder, not in the muscle — can be a sign of internal bleeding irritating the diaphragm. Combined with red bleeding and dizziness, it is a classic warning sign of a ruptured ectopic pregnancy. Call 999 immediately if you experience this combination of symptoms.

### Should I go to A&E or call my midwife first?

If you have heavy bleeding, severe pain, dizziness, or shoulder tip pain, go straight to A&E or call 999 — do not wait for a midwife callback. If the bleeding is lighter and you feel physically well, call your early pregnancy unit first for same-day assessment. When in doubt, err on the side of going in.
