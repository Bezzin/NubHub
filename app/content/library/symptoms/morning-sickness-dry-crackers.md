# Dry Crackers for Morning Sickness

Dry crackers are probably the most widely recommended morning sickness remedy, and for good reason — they are practical, inexpensive, gentle on a queasy stomach, and easy to keep close at hand at all times. This page explains why crackers help ease pregnancy nausea and how to use them most effectively alongside other strategies.

## Why Crackers Help

Plain dry crackers work for morning sickness for a combination of reasons. They are bland, low in odour, and easy to digest, making them gentle on a stomach that is reacting to almost everything. Unlike hot or heavily flavoured foods, dry crackers produce minimal smell — and for many women in the first trimester, food odour is one of the main triggers for waves of nausea.

Crackers provide a small amount of simple carbohydrate that can help stabilise blood sugar levels. An empty stomach is one of the most reliable triggers for pregnancy nausea, and a plain carbohydrate snack — however small — can take the edge off quickly. The stomach acid that builds up overnight on an empty stomach is also thought to contribute to that horrible first-thing-in-the-morning sickness, and eating something dry and absorbent before you move helps buffer it.

Their greatest practical advantage is convenience. Unlike most foods, crackers can sit beside your bed and be eaten before you even sit up, which matters a great deal when nausea is at its worst in the morning before you have moved.

## Getting the Most from Crackers

**Eat before you get up.** Keep a small pack on your bedside table and eat one or two crackers slowly before you sit up in the morning. Give yourself five to ten minutes before moving, if you can. This early intake of carbohydrate helps settle the stomach acid that has built up overnight and can reduce the sudden wave of nausea that hits the moment you go vertical.

**Carry them with you.** Morning sickness does not always follow a predictable pattern — for many women it strikes at unpredictable times throughout the day. Keeping crackers in your bag, at your desk, or in your car means you can respond to nausea immediately rather than searching for something appropriate.

**Choose the right variety.** Plain, unsalted or lightly salted crackers are usually best. Avoid varieties with strong flavourings such as cheese, onion, or herbs, as the smell can become its own trigger. Cream crackers, rice cakes, oatcakes, and plain water biscuits are all good options. If you have been vomiting, lightly salted crackers can be helpful as they replace a small amount of sodium.

**Sip, do not gulp.** Pair crackers with small sips of water rather than large drinks, which can overfill the stomach and worsen nausea. Room-temperature water is often easier to tolerate than very cold or hot drinks.

**Eat little and often.** Use crackers as part of a little-and-often eating pattern throughout the day. Waiting too long between eating allows blood sugar to dip and stomach acid to build, both of which make nausea worse. Aim to have a small snack every one to two hours if you can manage it, even if it is only a cracker or two.

**Do not rely on them exclusively.** Crackers are a useful tool but they are not nutritionally complete. They work best as a starting point — something to help you get through the worst moments — rather than your entire diet. As your nausea allows, try to introduce other bland, easy-to-tolerate foods alongside them.

## What to Eat Alongside Crackers

When nausea is very severe, a few crackers may be all you can manage, and that is understandable. As things ease slightly, try adding some of these to your cracker-based eating:

- **Plain rice or pasta** — bland, low-odour, and easy to digest
- **Mashed potato** — warm and filling without strong flavours
- **Banana or mild fruit** — provides potassium, which may be depleted if you have been vomiting
- **Plain yoghurt** — a gentle source of protein and calcium
- **Toast or bread** — similar in effect to crackers and easy to tolerate for many women
- **Boiled eggs** — a good source of protein once you can manage something slightly more substantial
- **Mild soup or broth** — helps with hydration as well as providing a small amount of nutrition

The key theme across all of these is blandness and low odour. Cold or room-temperature foods tend to smell less than hot dishes, which can make a meaningful difference when food smells are a primary trigger.

## When Morning Sickness Needs Medical Attention

Crackers and other self-help strategies are appropriate for mild to moderate morning sickness. However, you should speak to your GP or midwife if:

- You cannot keep any food or fluids down for more than 24 hours
- You are losing weight
- You feel faint, weak, or are struggling to function day to day
- Your urine is very dark or you are urinating very infrequently (signs of dehydration)
- Nausea and vomiting are so severe that you cannot eat at all

A small number of pregnant women develop hyperemesis gravidarum (HG), a serious condition involving persistent, severe vomiting that requires medical treatment including fluids and medication. HG is distinct from ordinary morning sickness and cannot be managed with crackers alone. If your symptoms feel extreme or are not improving, please contact your GP rather than continuing to manage at home without support.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Which crackers are best for morning sickness?

Plain, unsalted or lightly salted crackers are generally best. Good options available in UK supermarkets include cream crackers, plain water biscuits, plain rice cakes, and plain oatcakes. Avoid strongly flavoured varieties — the smell of cheese, herb, or onion flavourings can itself become a nausea trigger. If you are also avoiding gluten, plain gluten-free rice cakes or gluten-free crackers work just as well.

### When should I eat crackers for morning sickness?

The most effective time is first thing in the morning, before you sit up or get out of bed. Eating one or two crackers and then resting for a few minutes before moving can significantly reduce the severity of early morning nausea. Keep them beside your bed so they are ready without you needing to go to the kitchen. You should also keep crackers with you through the day and eat a small amount whenever nausea builds — rather than waiting until it becomes overwhelming.

### Can I eat too many crackers?

Crackers are safe in pregnancy, but relying on them as your primary food for an extended period means you are likely missing out on important nutrients. They are very low in protein, iron, folate, and calcium — all of which matter in pregnancy. Use them as a coping tool during the worst of the nausea, and gently add other foods back in as soon as you are able. If you are concerned about your nutritional intake, speak to your midwife or GP.
