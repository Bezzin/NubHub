# Hyperemesis Gravidarum: When to Go to Hospital

If you have hyperemesis gravidarum (HG), knowing when to go to hospital can be confusing. You don't want to waste NHS resources, but you also don't want to ignore dangerous dehydration. This guide will help you recognise when home treatment isn't enough and when you need urgent medical care.

## When to Go to Hospital {#when-hospital}

You should go to hospital if you have any of the following:

### Dehydration Signs
- **Can't keep any fluid down for 12-24 hours**
- **Very dark urine** or **no urine for 8+ hours**
- **Dry mouth, lips, and tongue**
- **Dizziness when standing up**
- **Feeling faint or actually fainting**
- **Sunken eyes**
- **Skin that doesn't bounce back** when pinched

### Severe Vomiting
- **Vomiting 5+ times per day** despite medication
- **Vomiting blood** (red, brown, or coffee-ground appearance)
- **Unable to keep anti-sickness medication down**
- **Vomiting that's getting worse** rather than improving

### Weight Loss
- **Lost 5% or more** of your pre-pregnancy weight
- **Can't eat anything** for 24+ hours

### Other Concerning Symptoms
- **Severe abdominal pain**
- **Confusion or extreme drowsiness**
- **Rapid or irregular heartbeat**
- **Fever** (38°C or above)
- **Chest pain**

## Red Flag Symptoms {#red-flags}

These symptoms require **immediate** medical attention - call 999 or go to A&E:

- **Vomiting blood** - could indicate serious bleeding
- **Severe abdominal pain** - could indicate other complications
- **Confusion or can't stay awake** - severe dehydration
- **Fainting** - dangerously low blood pressure
- **Chest pain** - needs urgent assessment
- **Very rapid heartbeat** at rest - could indicate electrolyte imbalance
- **No urine for 12+ hours** - severe dehydration

These are emergencies. Don't wait - seek help immediately.

## What Happens at Hospital {#what-happens}

Understanding what to expect can reduce anxiety about going to hospital.

### Assessment

When you arrive:
1. **Triage nurse** will assess your symptoms and vital signs
2. **Blood tests** to check electrolytes, kidney function, and blood count
3. **Urine test** to check for ketones (signs of starvation)
4. **Blood pressure and heart rate** monitoring
5. **Possibly an ultrasound** to check the pregnancy (especially if it's your first presentation)

### Treatment

Treatment typically includes:
1. **IV fluids** - to rehydrate you (this is the most important treatment)
2. **IV anti-sickness medication** - given directly into your bloodstream for maximum effect
3. **IV vitamins** - especially thiamine (vitamin B1), which is essential if you've been vomiting a lot
4. **Monitoring** - of your urine output, vital signs, and ketone levels

### Duration of Stay

Most HG admissions are **1-3 days**. You'll usually be discharged when:
- You can keep oral fluids down
- Your ketones have cleared
- Your electrolytes are normal
- You can keep oral anti-sickness medication down

Some women need longer stays or multiple admissions during their pregnancy.

### Which Ward?

You may be admitted to:
- **Gynaecology ward**
- **Early pregnancy unit**
- **Maternity assessment unit**
- **A&E observation unit** (for very short stays)

## How to Prepare for a Hospital Visit {#prepare}

If you have HG, it's wise to have a bag ready in case you need to go to hospital:

### Pack a Bag
- **Your maternity notes** (if you have them)
- **Medication list** - what you're taking and when
- **Phone charger**
- **Comfortable clothes** - loose, easy to remove
- **Sick bowls** for the journey
- **Tissues and lip balm** (lips get dry)
- **Water bottle** (you'll be allowed sips)
- **Snacks** for when you can eat (plain biscuits, crackers)
- **Headphones** for listening to music or podcasts
- **Book or tablet** - you may be bored between treatments

### Before You Go
- **Call your GP or midwife** if it's during the day - they may be able to help without hospital
- **Call 111** if you're not sure if you need to go to hospital - they can advise
- **Have someone drive you** - don't drive yourself if you're vomiting or dizzy
- **Tell the hospital you're pregnant** when you arrive

## After Hospital: What Next? {#after}

### Continue Medication
- Take your anti-sickness medication **as prescribed**
- Don't stop just because you feel better - it's the medication keeping you stable
- If you can't keep medication down, contact your GP immediately

### Follow-Up
- **See your GP** within a few days of discharge
- **Tell your midwife** about your admission
- **Ask about ongoing treatment** - you may need regular check-ins
- **Monitor for ketones** at home with testing strips (ask your midwife)

### Preventing Readmission
- **Sip fluids constantly** - aim for 2 litres per day
- **Take medication on time** - set alarms
- **Eat small amounts** of whatever you can tolerate
- **Rest** - exhaustion makes HG worse
- **Ask for help** - don't try to manage alone

### If You Need to Go Back
It's not uncommon to need multiple hospital admissions with HG. Don't feel like a failure if you need to go back. HG is unpredictable, and sometimes home treatment simply isn't enough.

## When to Call 111 vs 999 {#111-vs-999}

### Call 111 If:
- You're not sure if you need to go to hospital
- You can keep some fluids down but are struggling
- Your symptoms are worsening despite medication
- You need advice about what to do next
- It's out of hours and you can't reach your GP

111 can arrange an urgent GP appointment or direct you to the right service.

### Call 999 or Go to A&E If:
- You're vomiting blood
- You have severe abdominal pain
- You're confused or can't stay awake
- You've fainted
- You have chest pain
- You have no urine for 12+ hours
- You feel critically unwell

Don't hesitate to call 999 if you're seriously unwell. HG can be dangerous if untreated.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### How do I know if I need to go to hospital for HG?

Go to hospital if you can't keep any fluids down for 12-24 hours, have dark or no urine, feel dizzy or faint, are losing weight, or are vomiting blood. When in doubt, call 111 for advice.

### What will they do for me in hospital?

You'll receive IV fluids to rehydrate, IV anti-sickness medication, and monitoring of your electrolytes and urine output. Most women stay 1-3 days until they can keep oral fluids down.

### Will going to hospital harm my baby?

No. Hospital treatment for HG is safe and protects your baby by treating dehydration and malnutrition. Untreated HG is far more dangerous than hospital treatment.

### Can I refuse to go to hospital?

You can refuse treatment, but HG can become dangerous if untreated. Severe dehydration and electrolyte imbalance can harm both you and your baby. If you're advised to go to hospital, it's because you need treatment.

### How many times might I need to be admitted?

Some women need one admission and are then stable. Others need multiple admissions during their pregnancy, especially before week 14. This is normal and not a sign of failure - HG is unpredictable.
