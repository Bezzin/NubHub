# Pregnancy Symptoms: Complete First Trimester Guide

The first twelve weeks of pregnancy bring a wide range of symptoms — some expected, some surprising, and some that nobody warned you about. This guide covers what you might experience, why it happens, what is normal, and when it is worth contacting your midwife or GP.

## The Most Common First Trimester Symptoms {#s1}

**Nausea and morning sickness**
Despite the name, pregnancy nausea can happen at any time of day. It typically starts around weeks 5–6 and often improves by weeks 12–14. A significant number of women have no nausea at all — this is also normal.

**Fatigue**
Profound tiredness is one of the earliest and most common pregnancy symptoms. Your body is doing an enormous amount of work in the first trimester, including building the placenta. Feeling exhausted, even after a full night's sleep, is very common.

**Breast tenderness and changes**
Your breasts may feel sore, heavy, or unusually sensitive from very early in pregnancy. The areolae (the skin around the nipples) may darken, and small bumps called Montgomery glands may become more prominent.

**Frequent urination**
Needing to wee more often can start surprisingly early — sometimes before a missed period — due to hormonal changes affecting the kidneys and increased blood flow.

**Heightened sense of smell**
Many women find that smells they previously enjoyed become unbearable. This can contribute to nausea and food aversions.

**Food aversions and cravings**
Strong aversions to certain foods (often meat, eggs, or coffee) are common. Cravings can appear too, though they are usually less dramatic than popular culture suggests.

**Bloating and constipation**
Progesterone slows digestion, which can cause bloating and constipation from early in pregnancy. Drinking plenty of water and eating fibre-rich foods can help.

**Mild cramping**
Light cramping in early pregnancy is common and is often caused by the uterus stretching and the embryo implanting. It typically feels similar to mild period pain.

**Mood swings**
Rapid hormonal changes can affect your mood significantly. Feeling tearful, irritable, or emotionally up and down is very common and does not reflect on your mental health.

**Headaches**
Hormonal changes and increased blood volume can trigger headaches, particularly in the first trimester.

**Dizziness and lightheadedness**
Blood pressure changes and blood vessel relaxation can cause you to feel dizzy, especially when standing quickly.

## Symptoms That Come and Go {#s2}

It is completely normal for pregnancy symptoms to fluctuate day to day, or even within a single day. Many women worry when symptoms ease off, particularly nausea. A symptom improving or disappearing briefly does not mean something is wrong. Symptoms often come and go throughout the first trimester as hormone levels shift.

## When to Contact Your Midwife or GP {#s3}

Most first trimester symptoms are normal and manageable at home. However, contact your midwife or GP if:

- You are vomiting so frequently that you cannot keep any fluids down (this may be hyperemesis gravidarum and needs treatment)
- You have severe or one-sided abdominal pain
- You have any bright red bleeding
- You experience a sudden, significant loss of symptoms alongside pain or bleeding
- You have a high temperature (38°C or above)
- You feel dizzy and faint regularly

**Call 999 or go to A&E immediately** if you have severe one-sided pain with dizziness and shoulder tip pain — these can be signs of an ectopic pregnancy, which is a medical emergency.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### What are the most common early pregnancy symptoms?

The most commonly reported first trimester symptoms are fatigue, nausea, breast tenderness, frequent urination, and bloating. A missed period is typically the first signal. Many women also notice heightened smell sensitivity and mood changes. Not everyone experiences all of these — the range of normal is very wide.

### When should I worry about pregnancy symptoms?

Most symptoms are normal, but seek prompt help if you experience heavy bleeding, severe one-sided pain, inability to keep fluids down, or a high temperature. Sudden complete disappearance of all symptoms alongside pain or bleeding is also worth getting checked. For anything that concerns you, your midwife or GP is the right first call.

### Is it normal to have no pregnancy symptoms?

Yes. Some women have very few or no noticeable symptoms in the first trimester and go on to have healthy pregnancies. The absence of symptoms alone is not a reliable indicator of a problem.
