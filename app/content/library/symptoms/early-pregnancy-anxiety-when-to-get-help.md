# Early Pregnancy Anxiety: When to Get Help

Feeling anxious in early pregnancy is extremely common — and in many ways, it makes complete sense. You are navigating huge uncertainty, your hormones are shifting rapidly, and the stakes feel enormous. A degree of worry is a normal human response to all of that. But for some women, anxiety in early pregnancy goes beyond ordinary worry and starts to affect daily life in ways that deserve support. Knowing the difference, and knowing where to turn, can make a real difference.

## Why Anxiety Is So Common in Early Pregnancy

The first trimester is a period of intense physical and emotional change. Before the 12-week scan, there is often very little external confirmation that the pregnancy is progressing well. You may be experiencing symptoms that feel alarming (or worrying when they are absent), waiting for appointments that feel very far away, and keeping significant news private from most of the people around you.

On top of that, if you have experienced a previous pregnancy loss, fertility difficulties, or a difficult previous birth, anxiety in a new pregnancy can be especially intense. This is sometimes called pregnancy after loss anxiety, and it is a recognised and understandable response.

Hormonal changes in the first trimester can also directly affect mood and how the brain processes threat and uncertainty, which can amplify anxious feelings even in people who do not normally experience significant anxiety.

## What Normal Pregnancy Worry Looks Like

It is normal to feel:

- Anxious before scan appointments
- Worried when symptoms fluctuate or disappear temporarily
- Nervous about telling people in case something goes wrong
- Uncertain about whether you are making the right choices
- Emotionally up and down from day to day

These feelings tend to come and go, and most people can still eat, sleep, and go about their lives in between worries.

## Signs That Anxiety Has Become Too Much

Anxiety becomes something to seek help for when it is significantly disrupting your day-to-day life. Signs to look out for include:

- **You cannot sleep**, even when physically exhausted, because anxious thoughts keep you awake most nights.
- **You cannot eat properly** due to constant anxiety rather than nausea alone.
- **You are experiencing panic attacks** — sudden intense episodes of fear with physical symptoms such as racing heart, difficulty breathing, or feeling faint.
- **You are constantly scanning for problems** — repeatedly checking for symptoms, googling obsessively, or unable to think about anything else.
- **You cannot function at work or at home** because anxiety is taking up so much mental space.
- **You are avoiding things** — avoiding the toilet in case you see blood, avoiding telling anyone so you do not have to deal with a loss, or avoiding thinking about the future because it feels too frightening.
- **You are having intrusive, unwanted thoughts** about harm coming to the baby or about worst-case scenarios.
- **You cannot feel any positive emotions** about the pregnancy — only dread and fear.

Any of these on their own, if persistent, are worth mentioning to your GP or midwife.

## Where to Turn for Help in the UK

**Your GP or midwife** is the right first step. You do not need to be in crisis or have a diagnosis to ask for help — simply telling them that anxiety is affecting your sleep, your ability to eat, or your daily functioning is enough. They can refer you to perinatal mental health services.

**Perinatal mental health services** are specialist NHS services supporting women with mental health difficulties during pregnancy and after birth. They offer talking therapies including cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT), which is effective for anxiety. Referral is usually via your GP or midwife.

**Your booking appointment** is also a good opportunity — your midwife will routinely ask about your emotional wellbeing using validated questions. Answer honestly; this is not a test you can pass or fail, and your answers will not result in your baby being taken away. The information helps ensure you get the right support.

**If you are in crisis** — experiencing thoughts of harming yourself or others — contact your GP urgently, call 111, or go to your nearest A&E. The Samaritans are also available 24 hours a day on 116 123.

## Self-Help Strategies While You Wait

While you wait for an appointment or referral, some things that can help manage anxiety day to day:

- Limit time spent googling symptoms — set a rule for yourself and stick to it.
- Talk to someone you trust about how you are feeling.
- Try to maintain a gentle routine — regular meals, regular sleep, some fresh air each day.
- Practice slow breathing when anxiety spikes: breathe in for four counts, hold for four, out for six.
- Consider mindfulness apps designed for pregnancy, which can help with anxious thought patterns.

These are not substitutes for professional support when it is needed, but they can help you cope in the short term.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is anxiety in pregnancy normal?

Yes. Some anxiety is completely normal in early pregnancy — you are going through one of the biggest changes of your life with relatively little certainty. Most pregnant women experience periods of worry, particularly in the first trimester before the 12-week scan. Anxiety becomes a concern when it is persistent, overwhelming, preventing you from sleeping or eating, causing panic attacks, or stopping you from functioning in your daily life. At that point, it is worth talking to your GP or midwife.

### How do I access perinatal mental health support in the UK?

Speak to your GP or midwife and tell them that anxiety is affecting your daily life. They can refer you to your local perinatal mental health service, which provides specialist support during pregnancy and up to a year after birth. You can also ask directly about a referral — you do not have to wait to be offered one. In some areas, you can self-refer to IAPT (Improving Access to Psychological Therapies) services for talking therapies, including those with experience in perinatal anxiety.

### Will asking for help affect my care or my baby?

No. Asking for mental health support during pregnancy is a sign of self-awareness and courage, not a reason for concern. Healthcare professionals are there to help you — sharing your feelings honestly with your midwife or GP allows them to put the right support in place. Perinatal mental health services are focused on keeping you well so that you and your baby can thrive together.

Speak to your midwife or GP for personalised advice about your emotional wellbeing during pregnancy.
