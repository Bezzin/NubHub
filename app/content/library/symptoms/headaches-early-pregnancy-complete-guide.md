# Headaches in Early Pregnancy: Complete Guide

Headaches are one of the most common symptoms of early pregnancy. They can range from a mild tension headache that passes with rest and water, to a persistent, debilitating pain that gets in the way of daily life. Understanding what is driving your headaches — and what you can safely do about them — makes them much easier to manage.

## Why Headaches Happen in Early Pregnancy {#s1}

In the first trimester, your body is going through rapid hormonal and physical changes. Several of these contribute directly to headaches:

**Hormonal changes** — the surge in oestrogen and progesterone in early pregnancy affects blood vessels and can trigger headaches in people who are susceptible to them. Hormonal headaches are especially common in the first few weeks.

**Increased blood volume** — your blood volume increases significantly during pregnancy to support the placenta and baby. This places different demands on your cardiovascular system and can cause headaches, particularly during the early weeks of adjustment.

**Dehydration** — many pregnant women are dehydrated without realising it, especially if they are experiencing nausea and vomiting. Dehydration is a very common headache trigger. Drinking enough fluids — around six to eight glasses of water a day — makes a meaningful difference.

**Low blood sugar** — nausea often makes it hard to eat regularly in the first trimester, and going too long without food can cause blood sugar to drop, triggering a headache. Small, frequent meals help.

**Caffeine reduction or withdrawal** — if you have cut down on tea, coffee, or energy drinks since finding out you are pregnant (which is the right thing to do), withdrawal headaches are very common in the first week or two and gradually improve.

**Poor sleep and fatigue** — early pregnancy exhaustion is real, and disrupted sleep contributes to headaches.

**Tension and stress** — the emotional weight of early pregnancy — excitement, anxiety, planning, uncertainty — can cause muscle tension in your neck, shoulders, and scalp, leading to classic tension headaches.

## Safe Treatment Options {#s2}

**Paracetamol** is the pain reliever recommended for use during pregnancy. It is considered safe at the standard adult dose — always follow the dosage instructions on the packet and do not exceed them. Paracetamol works best when taken as soon as a headache starts rather than waiting for it to peak.

**Do not take ibuprofen** or other anti-inflammatory medicines (NSAIDs) during pregnancy unless specifically prescribed by your doctor. These are not considered safe in pregnancy, particularly from 30 weeks onwards.

**Practical self-help measures** to try alongside or instead of paracetamol:

- Drink a large glass of water — dehydration is often a factor
- Eat something if you have not eaten recently
- Lie down in a quiet, darkened room
- Apply a cool or warm compress to your forehead or the back of your neck
- Gently massage your temples, neck, and shoulders
- Try slow, deep breathing or a short mindfulness exercise
- Rest, even if you cannot sleep

If paracetamol and self-help measures are not touching the headache, speak to your GP or midwife before trying anything else.

## When to Seek Help

Most headaches in early pregnancy are benign and will improve with the measures above. However, some headaches warrant prompt attention.

**Contact your midwife or GP if:**
- Your headaches are very severe or unlike any you have had before
- Headaches are persistent and not responding to paracetamol
- You have a headache alongside swelling of your face or hands
- You feel unwell with the headache in a way you cannot explain

**Call 999 or go to A&E immediately if** you develop a sudden, severe "thunderclap" headache — the worst headache of your life, coming on in seconds — as this requires urgent medical assessment.

**Note on pre-eclampsia:** severe headaches are a warning sign of pre-eclampsia, but this condition is most commonly seen after 20 weeks of pregnancy, not in the first trimester. In early pregnancy, headaches are nearly always caused by the hormonal and lifestyle factors listed above.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I take paracetamol for headaches in pregnancy?

Yes. Paracetamol is the recommended painkiller during pregnancy and is considered safe at the standard adult dose. Always read the label and do not take more than the recommended amount. Avoid ibuprofen and aspirin unless they have been specifically prescribed by your doctor.

### When are headaches dangerous in pregnancy?

Severe headaches that do not respond to paracetamol — especially if they come with visual disturbances, upper abdominal pain, sudden swelling of the face or hands, or a very sudden onset — need urgent medical assessment. These can be signs of pre-eclampsia, which is more common after 20 weeks. In the first trimester, dangerous headaches are rare, but always trust your instincts and seek help if something does not feel right.

### Why do I have headaches every day in early pregnancy?

Daily headaches in early pregnancy are usually caused by a combination of factors: hormonal shifts, dehydration, low blood sugar from nausea, disrupted sleep, and possibly caffeine withdrawal. Addressing each trigger systematically — drinking more water, eating small meals regularly, resting, and cutting back on caffeine gradually rather than all at once — usually brings improvement within a week or two. If they do not improve, mention it to your midwife.

Speak to your midwife or GP for advice tailored to your own health and pregnancy.
