# Mood Swings in Early Pregnancy: Complete Guide

Feeling ecstatic one minute and in tears the next? Mood swings in early pregnancy are a perfectly normal part of the journey. Here's a complete guide to what's happening and how to cope.

## Why Mood Swings Happen {#why}

Pregnancy mood swings are largely driven by hormones:

- **Oestrogen and progesterone** surge in the first trimester, affecting brain chemicals that regulate mood.
- **hCG** rises rapidly, which can contribute to emotional sensitivity.
- **Physical exhaustion**: Fatigue from early pregnancy lowers your emotional resilience.
- **Nausea and discomfort**: Feeling unwell makes everything harder to cope with.
- **Life change anxiety**: The realisation that life is about to change can feel overwhelming.
- **Sleep disruption**: Poor sleep makes moods harder to regulate.

These changes are real, not a sign of weakness — your brain is literally being rewired by hormones.

## When Do Mood Swings Start? {#when-start}

Mood swings often begin around weeks 6 to 10, when hormone levels rise most steeply. They're often at their most intense in the first trimester and tend to ease in the second trimester as hormones stabilise and energy returns.

Some women notice emotional shifts even before a positive test, while others experience them more strongly later on. Everyone's experience is different.

## How to Cope With Mood Swings {#cope}

- **Acknowledge your feelings**: It's okay to feel up and down. Naming it helps.
- **Talk to someone**: Your partner, a friend or a family member can be a sounding board.
- **Prioritise rest**: Tiredness makes everything worse. Nap when you can.
- **Eat regularly**: Low blood sugar can worsen irritability and low mood.
- **Get gentle exercise**: A short walk releases feel-good endorphorphins.
- **Reduce stress**: Say no to unnecessary commitments and protect your downtime.
- **Limit caffeine**: Too much can worsen anxiety and jitteriness.

## When to Seek Support {#when-support}

It's normal to feel emotional. But speak to your GP or midwife if:

- Low mood lasts more than two weeks
- You feel unable to cope with daily life
- You have thoughts of harming yourself
- You feel persistently anxious or panicked
- You've lost interest in things you usually enjoy

These could be signs of antenatal depression or anxiety, which are common and treatable. Support is available — you don't have to struggle alone.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### How long do pregnancy mood swings last?

They're usually most intense in the first trimester and often ease from around week 12 onwards. Some women notice mood changes throughout pregnancy.

### Are mood swings a sign of pregnancy?

Yes — unexplained emotional changes can be an early sign, thanks to shifting hormones. However, they're not diagnostic, so take a test if you think you might be pregnant.

### Can mood swings affect my baby?

Everyday mood swings don't harm your baby. Persistent severe low mood or anxiety can affect your wellbeing, though, so do seek support if it's ongoing.
