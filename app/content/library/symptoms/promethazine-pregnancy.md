# Promethazine in Pregnancy: Safety, Dosage & Side Effects

Promethazine is another first-line anti-sickness medication prescribed for nausea and vomiting in pregnancy. Often known by brand names like Avomine or Phenergan, it's been used safely for decades. Here's what you need to know if your GP has prescribed - or may prescribe - promethazine.

## What Is Promethazine? {#what-is}

Promethazine is a first-generation antihistamine that's used to treat nausea, vomiting, motion sickness, allergies, and as a sedative. In pregnancy, it's prescribed for morning sickness and hyperemesis gravidarum (HG).

### Key Facts
- **Drug class**: First-generation antihistamine (H1 receptor antagonist)
- **Brand names**: Avomine, Phenergan (Phenergan is available in different forms)
- **Availability**: Prescription for pregnancy use; Avomine is available over-the-counter for motion sickness but consult your GP before taking in pregnancy
- **Form**: Tablets (10mg, 25mg, 50mg), syrup, suppositories
- **Pregnancy safety**: Considered safe - decades of use

## How It Works {#how-works}

Promethazine blocks histamine (H1) receptors in the brain, which are involved in the vomiting reflex. By blocking these receptors, it reduces nausea and the urge to vomit.

### Why It's Effective in Pregnancy
Pregnancy nausea is driven by hormones (hCG, oestrogen) that affect the brain's vomiting centre. Promethazine helps calm this centre, reducing nausea signals. It also has a sedating effect, which can help with the exhaustion that often accompanies severe nausea.

### How Quickly It Works
- **Oral tablets**: 20-30 minutes to take effect
- **Peak effect**: 2 hours after taking
- **Duration**: 4-6 hours (sometimes longer at higher doses)

## Is Promethazine Safe in Pregnancy? {#safety}

### The Evidence

Promethazine has been used in pregnancy for over 60 years. The safety data is substantial:

- **Large studies** have found no increased risk of birth defects
- **No evidence** of increased miscarriage risk
- **Considered safe** by the UK's RCOG and NICE guidelines
- **Listed as a first-line option** for nausea and vomiting in pregnancy

### Decades of Data

Because promethazine has been used for so long, there's more safety data for it than for many newer medications. This long history provides reassurance about its use in pregnancy.

### The Balance of Risk

As with any medication, the benefits must outweigh the risks. Untreated severe nausea and vomiting can lead to dehydration, weight loss, and nutritional deficiencies - all of which are more dangerous to your pregnancy than the medication.

### When It's Not Suitable
Promethazine may not be suitable if you have:
- Known allergy to promethazine or antihistamines
- Severe liver disease
- Certain types of glaucoma
- Prostate problems (difficulty urinating)
- History of seizures

Always discuss your full medical history with your GP before taking promethazine.

## Dosage {#dosage}

### Standard Dose for Pregnancy Nausea
- **10-25mg every 4-8 hours**, as needed
- **Maximum 100mg per 24 hours**

### Starting Dose
- **Start with 10mg** if you're concerned about drowsiness
- **Increase to 25mg** if 10mg isn't effective
- **Take at bedtime** initially to minimise daytime drowsiness

### For Severe Nausea (HG)
- **25mg every 6-8 hours**
- Often combined with another medication

### Different Forms
- **Tablets**: Most common for pregnancy use
- **Syrup**: Useful if you can't keep tablets down (absorbed faster)
- **Suppositories**: An option if you're vomiting everything orally

### How to Take
- **With food** to reduce stomach irritation
- **Swallow whole** with water
- **Don't crush** tablets

### If You Miss a Dose
- Take as soon as you remember
- Skip if it's nearly time for the next dose
- Never double up

## Side Effects {#side-effects}

### Common Side Effects
- **Drowsiness**: This is the most significant side effect. Promethazine is more sedating than some other antihistamines. Don't drive if affected.
- **Dry mouth**: Sip water, chew sugar-free gum
- **Dizziness**: Stand up slowly
- **Blurred vision**: Usually mild
- **Constipation**: Drink water, eat fibre
- **Difficulty urinating**: Report to your GP if significant

### Less Common Side Effects
- **Headache**
- **Restlessness** (paradoxical effect)
- **Confusion** (more common in higher doses)
- **Skin rash** (allergic reaction - stop and contact GP)
- **Photosensitivity**: Use sunscreen

### Managing Drowsiness
- **Take the largest dose at bedtime**
- **Start with a low dose** and increase gradually
- **Avoid driving** until you know how it affects you
- **Time doses** to allow rest after taking

### When to Contact Your GP
- Severe drowsiness affecting daily life
- Difficulty urinating
- Rapid or irregular heartbeat
- Allergic reaction (rash, swelling, breathing difficulty)
- Confusion or disorientation
- Symptoms not improving after 72 hours

## How to Take It Effectively {#take-effectively}

### Timing
- **Take regularly**, not just when you feel sick
- **Bedtime dose** is often most helpful - reduces morning nausea and drowsiness affects you while you sleep
- **Spread doses evenly** throughout the day

### If You Vomit After Taking It
- **Within 30 minutes**: Take another dose
- **After 30 minutes**: Some will have been absorbed; wait for the next scheduled dose
- **Consistently vomiting doses**: Ask your GP about suppositories or combining with another medication

### Don't Stop Suddenly
If you feel better, it's the medication working. Don't stop abruptly - symptoms may rebound. Talk to your GP about gradually reducing the dose when your symptoms have been controlled for several days.

### Combining with Other Treatments
Promethazine can be safely combined with:
- **Ginger** (tea, supplements)
- **Acupressure wristbands**
- **Vitamin B6**
- **Other anti-sickness medications** (as prescribed)

## Promethazine vs Other Medications {#vs-others}

### Promethazine vs Cyclizine
- Both are antihistamine-based
- Promethazine is **more sedating** than cyclizine
- Cyclizine may be better if drowsiness is a problem
- Promethazine may be better if you're also struggling to sleep
- Effectiveness is similar - some women respond better to one than the other

### Promethazine vs Ondansetron
- Ondansetron works differently (5-HT3 receptor antagonist)
- Ondansetron is **less sedating**
- Ondansetron is often used as a **second-line** option when antihistamines haven't worked
- They can be **combined** for women with severe HG

### Which Will My GP Choose?
Your GP will consider:
- Your symptoms and their severity
- Whether drowsiness is a concern (e.g., if you need to work or drive)
- Your medical history
- What you've already tried
- Whether you need to combine medications

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Is promethazine safe in pregnancy?

Yes. Promethazine has been used to treat nausea and vomiting in pregnancy for over 60 years. Large studies have found no increased risk of birth defects or miscarriage. It's considered safe by RCOG and NICE guidelines.

### Will promethazine make me drowsy?

Yes, drowsiness is the most common side effect of promethazine. It's more sedating than some other anti-sickness medications. Taking the largest dose at bedtime can help manage this.

### Can I buy promethazine over the counter?

Avomine (promethamine) is available over the counter for motion sickness, but you should always consult your GP before taking any medication in pregnancy. For pregnancy nausea, you'll need a prescription to ensure appropriate dosing and monitoring.

### What if promethazine doesn't work?

If promethazine isn't working after 48-72 hours, your GP can add a second medication (like prochlorperazine or ondansetron), switch you to a different medication (like cyclizine), or refer you to a specialist. Don't accept being told there's nothing else.

### Can I take promethazine throughout my pregnancy?

Most women only need anti-sickness medication for the first trimester. However, some women with HG need medication throughout pregnancy. Your GP will review your medication regularly and reduce it when appropriate.
