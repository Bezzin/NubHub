# How to Stop Doom-Scrolling During Pregnancy

If you have found yourself lying awake at 2am reading worst-case scenarios about miscarriage rates, foetal abnormalities, or labour complications — you are not alone. Doom-scrolling during pregnancy is extremely common, and it is not a sign of weakness or irrationality. It is an understandable anxiety response. But it does make things worse, and there are practical ways to break the habit.

## Why Pregnancy Makes You Doom-Scroll {#s1}

Anxiety seeks certainty. When you are pregnant and frightened, your brain tells you that if you can just find enough information, you will be able to prepare for or prevent something going wrong. So you search. And you find stories. And instead of feeling reassured, you feel more frightened — so you search again.

This is sometimes called the "reassurance trap." The temporary relief from finding a reassuring forum post quickly wears off, and within minutes you are looking for the next thing to worry about. The cycle is reinforced every time you reach for your phone.

The internet also has a strong negativity bias: people who have had difficult experiences are far more likely to post about them than people who had straightforward pregnancies. The picture you get from a forum or search engine is not representative of typical outcomes.

## Practical Ways to Stop {#s2}

You do not need to eliminate all information-seeking — that would be impossible and not particularly helpful. The goal is to reduce compulsive, anxiety-driven searching and replace it with something more useful.

**Set a "one search" rule.** If you have a genuine question, look it up once using a reliable source (the NHS website is a good starting point), read what it says, and then close the tab. Do not go looking for forum threads or personal stories.

**Schedule worry time.** This sounds counterintuitive, but it works. Give yourself 10–15 minutes per day — perhaps after lunch — when you are allowed to look things up. Outside that window, if an anxious thought comes, write it down to revisit later rather than searching immediately. Many worries feel much less urgent by the time your window arrives.

**Remove the friction.** Log out of forums, put apps on a different screen or delete them temporarily, and use your phone's screen time limits to restrict certain sites in the evenings.

**Replace the habit, don't just stop it.** When you reach for your phone out of anxiety, have something specific to do instead: a podcast you enjoy, a message to a friend, a breathing exercise. Having a positive replacement is more effective than simply trying to stop.

**Talk to someone.** Sharing your worry with your partner, a friend, or your midwife is almost always more helpful than Googling it. Your midwife can give you information that is relevant to your actual situation, rather than the statistical average.

**Remember what you already know.** If you have had reassuring news — a positive scan, a heartbeat — try to bring that to mind when anxiety spikes. Write it down somewhere you will see it.

## When Anxiety Goes Beyond Doom-Scrolling {#s3}

If you find that anxiety is significantly disrupting your daily life — you cannot sleep, you feel unable to enjoy anything, or the worry is constant — it is worth talking to your GP or midwife. Antenatal anxiety is common and treatable. The NHS can refer you to talking therapies, and your midwife can flag you for additional support from the perinatal mental health team if needed.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Why do I keep doom-scrolling even though it makes me feel worse?

It is an anxiety response — your brain is trying to feel in control by gathering information. The problem is that internet searching feeds the anxiety loop rather than resolving it, because each answer tends to open up more questions or more frightening possibilities. Recognising the pattern is the first step to breaking it.

### How can I stop doom-scrolling during pregnancy?

Set a one-search rule for genuine questions, use reliable sources like the NHS website, schedule a specific "worry window" rather than searching on impulse, and remove apps from easy reach. Replacing the habit with something else — a podcast, a message to a friend — is more effective than just trying to stop cold.

### When should I contact my midwife instead of Googling?

Any time you have a physical concern — bleeding, pain, reduced movement later in pregnancy, or any symptom that worries you — contact your midwife or GP rather than searching online. They can assess your actual situation. Call 111 if you cannot reach your midwife and the concern feels urgent.
