# Right-Sided Pain in Early Pregnancy: Causes and Care

Pain on the lower right side in early pregnancy has a slightly longer list of possible causes than the left, because your appendix also lives on that side. In most cases the cause is benign, but right-sided pain warrants a careful check. This guide explains the common causes and the red flags you should never ignore.

## Benign Causes on the Right {#benign-causes-on-the-right}

The majority of right-sided pain in early pregnancy has a straightforward, harmless explanation. Understanding these can help you describe your symptoms clearly and feel less anxious while you wait to speak to your midwife.

**Round ligament pain** is one of the most common culprits. The round ligaments run from each side of your uterus to your groin, and as your womb grows they pull and stretch — creating a sharp, brief twinge that tends to flare when you stand quickly, roll over, sneeze, or cough. It can occur on either side and does not indicate anything is wrong.

**Corpus luteum cyst on the right ovary** — after ovulation, the follicle that released the egg becomes a structure called the corpus luteum, which produces progesterone to support the early pregnancy. It occasionally forms a small cyst that causes a dull, intermittent ache on whichever side it develops. These almost always resolve on their own by the end of the first trimester and do not harm the pregnancy.

**Constipation and trapped wind** — pregnancy hormones slow the digestive system significantly. Gas and stool can collect in the right side of the bowel, creating pressure and cramping that can feel surprisingly acute. Staying hydrated, eating enough fibre, and moving gently usually helps.

**Implantation** — when the embryo embeds into the uterine lining in very early pregnancy, some women feel a mild one-sided cramp or pulling sensation. This is brief and often accompanied by light spotting.

**General uterine stretching** — as the uterus grows from the size of a pear to much larger over the first trimester, the surrounding muscles and ligaments stretch. This can feel like a generalised ache or pressure low in the abdomen.

## When Right-Sided Pain Needs Urgent Checks {#when-right-sided-pain-needs-urgent-checks}

Right-sided pain in early pregnancy has two additional red flags that make it worth assessing promptly if pain is severe, worsening, or accompanied by other symptoms.

### Ectopic Pregnancy

If the embryo has implanted in the right fallopian tube rather than the uterus, the tube cannot accommodate a growing pregnancy and is at risk of rupturing. Signs of a right-sided ectopic pregnancy include:

- Severe pain on the right side that is persistent or worsening
- Shoulder tip pain (caused by internal bleeding irritating the diaphragm)
- Dizziness, feeling faint, or collapsing
- Any vaginal bleeding alongside the pain
- Feeling very unwell

A ruptured ectopic is a life-threatening emergency. Call 999 immediately if you have these symptoms together.

### Appendicitis

Your appendix sits in the lower right abdomen, and appendicitis can occur at any point during pregnancy. It may present differently in pregnancy because the growing uterus shifts the appendix slightly upward. Classic signs include:

- Pain that begins around the belly button and moves to the lower right
- Nausea and vomiting
- Fever or feeling shivery
- The pain worsening over several hours rather than coming and going

Appendicitis needs urgent surgical assessment. If you suspect it, go to A&E or call 999 — do not wait overnight.

## What to Do If You're Unsure

If your right-sided pain is mild and behaving like round ligament pain or digestive discomfort, it is reasonable to rest, stay hydrated, and monitor it. You can mention it at your next midwife appointment.

If the pain is:
- Moderate to severe
- Persistent for more than a few hours
- Accompanied by bleeding, fever, dizziness, or shoulder pain
- Getting worse rather than better

… then call your early pregnancy unit, NHS 111, or your midwife the same day. Do not wait for a routine appointment if you have any of these features.

## Practical Self-Care for Mild Right-Sided Pain

For benign round ligament or digestive pain, the following tend to help:

- **Move slowly** — avoiding sudden movements reduces round ligament twinges.
- **Rest on your left side** — this can relieve pressure from a corpus luteum cyst or digestive discomfort.
- **Warm (not hot) pack** — a warm wheat bag on the lower abdomen soothes muscle tension. Avoid very hot baths in the first trimester.
- **Increase fluids and dietary fibre** — helps with constipation-related pain.
- **Paracetamol** — safe to take in pregnancy following the dosage on the packet, and can ease mild to moderate cramping.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can appendicitis happen during early pregnancy?

Yes. Pregnancy does not protect you from appendicitis, and it needs urgent assessment just as it would outside of pregnancy. If you have right-sided pain that is worsening, combined with nausea and any fever, seek urgent care at A&E rather than waiting for a GP appointment.

### Is a right-sided twinge at 7 weeks normal?

A brief, sharp twinge on the right at 7 weeks is most likely round ligament pain or a corpus luteum cyst — both entirely normal. If the twinge is severe, persistent, or paired with bleeding or dizziness, contact your early pregnancy unit the same day.

### How is right-sided pain assessed in pregnancy?

Your midwife or doctor will take a detailed history of your symptoms and examine your abdomen. They will likely arrange an ultrasound scan to check the location of the pregnancy and look for any cysts or collections of fluid. Blood tests, including a pregnancy hormone (hCG) level and a full blood count, help rule out ectopic pregnancy and infection. If appendicitis is suspected, you will be referred to a surgical team.
