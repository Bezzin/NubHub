# Can't Eat Properly in First Trimester: What to Do

Struggling to eat anything in the first trimester? You're not alone. Many women find that nausea, food aversions and a sensitive stomach make eating a real challenge. Here's what to do when you can't eat properly in early pregnancy.

## Why Eating Becomes Hard {#why-hard}

The first trimester brings a surge of hormones that can make food unappealing:

- **hCG and oestrogen** rise rapidly, triggering nausea and aversions
- **Progesterone** slows digestion, causing bloating and fullness
- **Heightened smell** makes many foods seem overwhelming
- **Taste changes** can make favourite foods taste odd or metallic
- **Fatigue** saps the energy needed to cook and eat

These changes usually peak between weeks 6 and 10 and ease after week 12 for most women.

## Foods That Are Easier to Tolerate {#easier-foods}

When nothing sounds good, these bland, low-odour foods are often better tolerated:

- **Plain crackers** and dry toast
- **Plain rice** or plain pasta
- **Bananas** and apples
- **Plain yoghurt** or mild cheese
- **Boiled or scrambled eggs** (if smell isn't an issue)
- **Plain biscuits** like Rich Tea or digestives
- **Cold foods** like sandwiches or salads (cold foods smell less)
- **Ginger-based foods** to ease nausea

Avoid spicy, greasy or strong-smelling foods, and steer clear of anything that triggers your nausea.

## Practical Tips to Keep Eating {#practical-tips}

- **Eat before you get up**: Keep crackers by your bed and eat one before standing.
- **Go small and frequent**: Five or six tiny meals are easier than three large ones.
- **Don't wait until you're hungry**: An empty stomach makes nausea worse.
- **Let others cook**: Ask your partner or family to prepare meals so you avoid kitchen smells.
- **Stay hydrated**: Sip water, herbal tea or diluted squash throughout the day.
- **Try cold or room-temperature meals**: Hot food releases more odour.
- **Keep a food diary**: Note what stays down so you can repeat the wins.

## When to Get Help {#when-help}

See your GP or midwife if:

- You can't keep any food or drink down for 24 hours
- You're vomiting multiple times a day
- You're losing weight
- You feel dizzy, weak or faint
- Your urine is dark and you're weeing less than usual

These could be signs of hyperemesis gravidarum or dehydration, which need medical treatment. Your GP may prescribe anti-sickness medication that's safe in pregnancy.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### What if I can only eat one or two foods?

Food aversions are very common. If plain crackers and bananas are all you can manage, eat them. Taking a pregnancy vitamin helps cover nutritional gaps until your appetite returns.

### Will not eating harm my baby?

In the first trimester, your baby is tiny and needs relatively few calories. Staying hydrated and eating small amounts is usually enough. Severe sickness that prevents any intake needs medical review.

### Can I take anti-sickness medication?

Yes. If nausea is stopping you eating, your GP can prescribe safe options such as cyclizine, promethazine or ondansetron. Don't suffer in silence — help is available.
