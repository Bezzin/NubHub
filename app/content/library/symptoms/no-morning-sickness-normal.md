# Is It Normal to Have No Morning Sickness?

If you're pregnant and haven't felt a single wave of nausea, you might be counting yourself lucky - or quietly worrying that something is wrong. The internet is full of stories linking morning sickness to a healthy pregnancy, which can make symptom-free mums-to-be anxious. Let's sort the facts from the fear.

## How Common Is No Morning Sickness? {#how-common}

Approximately **20-30% of pregnant women** experience no morning sickness at all. That's around one in four women who sail through pregnancy without the nausea and vomiting that others dread.

If you're in this group, you are not unusual, you are not "doing pregnancy wrong," and you are not destined to miscarry. Morning sickness - or its absence - is not a reliable indicator of pregnancy health.

### The Numbers

- **70-80%** of women experience some degree of nausea and/or vomiting
- **20-30%** have little to no nausea
- **1-3%** develop hyperemesis gravidarum (severe, debilitating sickness)

## Why Some Women Don't Get Morning Sickness {#why-no}

The exact cause of morning sickness isn't fully understood, but it's linked to hCG (human chorionic gonadotropin), oestrogen, and progesterone levels. Women who don't experience morning sickness may have:

- **Lower or more stable hCG levels**: Still within a healthy range, but not high enough to trigger nausea
- **Lower sensitivity to hormonal changes**: Some bodies simply don't react to hormone surges with nausea
- **Genetic factors**: Research suggests genetics play a role in morning sickness severity
- **Different hormone metabolisms**: How your body breaks down hormones affects symptom severity
- **A baby girl**: Some studies suggest carrying a girl increases morning sickness risk - but this is not definitive and many women carrying girls have no nausea

### Things That Don't Cause Lack of Morning Sickness

It's a myth that no morning sickness means:
- Your baby isn't developing properly
- Your hormone levels are too low
- You're not "really" pregnant
- Your pregnancy is somehow less valid

None of these are true.

## Does No Morning Sickness Mean Higher Miscarriage Risk? {#miscarriage-risk}

This is the fear that drives most women to Google at 2am. The short answer is: **no, not in the way you might think.**

### What the Research Says

Multiple studies have looked at the relationship between morning sickness and miscarriage risk:

- Women who experience morning sickness do have a statistically **lower miscarriage rate** than women who don't. Some studies have found the reduction is significant.
- However, the **vast majority of women who have no morning sickness still have healthy pregnancies and babies.** The increased miscarriage risk for women without morning sickness is small.
- Morning sickness is a sign that hormones are doing their job, but its absence doesn't mean they aren't.

### Why the Association Exists

Morning sickness indicates that hCG levels are rising as expected, which suggests a viable pregnancy. But the absence of morning sickness doesn't mean hCG isn't rising - it may just not be high enough to trigger nausea, or your body may be less sensitive to it.

### The Bottom Line

If you have no morning sickness:
- Your miscarriage risk is slightly higher than women with morning sickness, but still low overall (around 10% in confirmed pregnancies)
- Most women without morning sickness have perfectly healthy pregnancies
- A scan showing a heartbeat at 7-8 weeks provides far more reassurance than symptoms ever can

## Factors That Affect Morning Sickness {#factors}

Several factors influence whether and how severely you experience morning sickness:

### More Likely to Have Morning Sickness
- **First pregnancy**: Hormones are new to your body
- **Multiple pregnancy**: Higher hCG levels
- **History of motion sickness or migraines**: Linked to similar pathways
- **Previous pregnancy with morning sickness**: Likely to recur
- **Female fetus**: Slightly higher risk (though not definitive)
- **Higher body weight**: Some studies suggest a link

### Less Likely to Have Morning Sickness
- **Older mothers**: Some studies suggest less severe symptoms
- **Lower pre-pregnancy weight**: Some studies suggest lower hCG response
- **Genetic predisposition**: Some families simply don't "do" morning sickness
- **Subsequent pregnancies**: May be milder than the first

## When to Be Concerned {#when-concerned}

While no morning sickness is usually fine, you should contact your midwife or GP if:

- **You had morning sickness that suddenly disappeared** (especially before week 9), accompanied by bleeding, cramping, or loss of breast tenderness
- **You have no symptoms at all** and are experiencing bleeding or pain
- **You feel unwell in other ways** - fever, pain, dizziness
- **You had a previous miscarriage** and want reassurance

A scan at your early pregnancy unit can provide the reassurance that symptoms can't.

## How to Respond to Concerned Comments {#responding}

"Well-meaning" friends and family may express surprise or concern that you're not sick. Here's how to handle it:

- **"I'm not sick yet"** - "Not everyone gets morning sickness - about 20% of women don't, and it doesn't indicate a problem."
- **"My friend had no sickness and miscarried"** - "That's sad, but it doesn't mean the two are connected. Most women without morning sickness have healthy pregnancies."
- **"You're just lucky"** - "Yes, I am lucky - and that's a good thing, not a warning sign."

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Can I have a healthy pregnancy with no morning sickness?

Absolutely. Around 20-30% of women have no morning sickness, and the vast majority go on to have healthy pregnancies and babies. A scan showing a heartbeat is far more reassuring than symptoms.

### Does no morning sickness mean I'm having a boy?

The old wives' tale says no morning sickness means a boy. There's a slight statistical tendency for female fetuses to cause more morning sickness, but it's not reliable. Plenty of women carrying boys have severe sickness, and plenty carrying girls have none.

### I had morning sickness with my first but not my second - is that OK?

Yes. Every pregnancy is different. Some women have severe sickness with one pregnancy and none with the next. This is normal and doesn't indicate a problem.

### What if I have no symptoms at all, not just no sickness?

No symptoms at all in early pregnancy is also normal for many women. If you're worried, an early scan can provide reassurance. Read more about [no symptoms at various weeks](/symptoms/no-symptoms-6-weeks).
