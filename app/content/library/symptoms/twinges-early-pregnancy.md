# Twinges Early Pregnancy

Feeling little twinges in your lower tummy in early pregnancy is extremely common, and most of the time it is nothing to worry about. Twinges are quick, sharp or pulling sensations that usually last only a second or two. They are most often caused by your uterus growing, ligaments stretching, trapped wind, or normal changes happening as your pregnancy settles in. As long as they are brief and are not joined by heavy bleeding or severe pain, twinges are generally a normal part of early pregnancy.

## What Causes Twinges in Early Pregnancy?

In the first weeks and months, your body is changing quickly to support your growing baby. Several harmless things can cause twinges:

- Your uterus is expanding, which can cause mild stretching and pulling sensations.
- The ligaments that support your womb are stretching and softening under the influence of pregnancy hormones.
- Trapped wind and constipation are very common in early pregnancy and can cause sharp, fleeting pains.
- The corpus luteum, a small cyst on the ovary that supports early pregnancy, can cause mild one-sided sensations.

These twinges tend to come and go, move around, and ease when you change position or rest. Many people describe them as similar to mild period-type niggles. Feeling them on one side at times is also common and not automatically a cause for concern.

## What Is Normal and What Is Not

Normal twinges are short-lived, mild, and settle on their own. They are not usually accompanied by bleeding, and they do not build into severe or constant pain. Mild cramping that eases with rest is a common feature of early pregnancy.

You should contact your GP, midwife, or local early pregnancy unit if twinges become persistent or severe, if the pain is strong and one-sided, or if you have any vaginal bleeding alongside the pain. Seek urgent medical help by calling 999 or going to A&E if you have severe tummy pain, pain in the tip of your shoulder, feel faint, dizzy, or unwell, as these can be signs of an ectopic pregnancy, which needs prompt assessment. It is always better to be checked and reassured than to wait and worry.

## How to Ease Mild Twinges

For everyday twinges, gentle measures often help. Try changing position slowly, resting when you can, and using a warm (not hot) covered hot water bottle against your lower tummy. Staying well hydrated, eating fibre-rich foods, and keeping gently active can reduce wind and constipation, which are frequent culprits. If discomfort is bothering you and you want pain relief, paracetamol at the recommended dose is generally considered the first choice in pregnancy, but check with a pharmacist or GP first.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Are twinges normal in early pregnancy?

Yes. Quick, sharp or pulling twinges are very common and are usually caused by your uterus growing and ligaments stretching. As long as they are brief and not accompanied by heavy bleeding or severe pain, they are considered a normal part of early pregnancy.

### How do I know if twinges are serious?

Twinges that are mild, short, and come and go are reassuring. Get medical advice if the pain becomes severe, persistent, or strongly one-sided, if you have bleeding, or if you feel faint, dizzy, or have shoulder-tip pain. These warrant prompt assessment to rule out problems such as an ectopic pregnancy.

### Can twinges feel like period pains?

Yes. Many people say early pregnancy twinges feel like mild period-type cramps. This is usually down to your womb expanding. If the cramping becomes intense or is paired with bleeding, contact your midwife or early pregnancy unit.

For advice tailored to your circumstances, speak to your midwife or GP.
