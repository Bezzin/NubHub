# Pink Spotting in Early Pregnancy: Causes and Care

Pink spotting sits between brown and red on the spectrum - it is fresh blood mixed with cervical mucus, diluting the colour. For most women, pink spotting in early pregnancy is a normal sign of hormonal changes or a sensitive cervix. This guide explains what to watch for and when to seek help.

## What Causes Pink Spotting?

Pink spotting most commonly appears after implantation, sexual intercourse, a vaginal exam, or vigorous exercise. The blood mixes with your normal cervical mucus, giving it a pink tinge. It can also happen around week 6 or 7 as your hormones surge and the cervix becomes engorged. The key distinction is that it stays light - spotting, not bleeding.

## Spotting vs Bleeding: Knowing the Difference

Spotting is light enough that you notice it on toilet paper or as a few drops on underwear. Bleeding is anything that requires a sanitary pad. Pink spotting that stays light and resolves within a day or two is usually fine. Pink spotting that increases, turns red, or comes with cramps warrants a call to your early pregnancy unit for assessment.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is pink spotting at 5 weeks normal?**

Yes, it is common around this time due to implantation and hormonal shifts. Mention it to your midwife at your booking appointment.

**Should I rest if I have pink spotting?**

Gentle rest is fine, but bed rest is not proven to prevent miscarriage. Avoid heavy lifting and intercourse until the spotting settles.

**Does pink spotting mean ectopic pregnancy?**

Not on its own. Ectopic pregnancy usually causes pain, shoulder tip ache, and heavier bleeding. Pink spotting alone is rarely a sign.
