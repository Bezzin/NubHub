# Pink Spotting in Early Pregnancy: Causes and Care

Pink spotting sits between brown and red on the spectrum — it is fresh blood mixed with cervical mucus, diluting the colour. For most women, pink spotting in early pregnancy is a normal sign of hormonal changes or a sensitive cervix. This guide explains what causes it, how to tell when it needs attention, and when to seek help.

## What Causes Pink Spotting? {#what-causes-pink-spotting}

Pink spotting in early pregnancy has several common, benign causes:

**Implantation bleeding.** Around the time a fertilised egg implants into the uterine lining — usually between six and twelve days after conception — a small amount of pink or light brown spotting can occur. It is typically very light and lasts no more than a day or two.

**Cervical sensitivity.** During pregnancy, the cervix becomes highly vascular — it has a greatly increased blood supply. This makes it more susceptible to minor irritation from sexual intercourse, a vaginal examination, or even vigorous exercise. Small amounts of fresh blood can then mix with cervical mucus to produce a pink discharge.

**Hormonal fluctuations.** Around weeks 5 to 7, surging pregnancy hormones can cause the cervix to shed a small amount of old lining tissue. This tends to appear as very light pink or brown spotting and is usually nothing to worry about.

**Subchorionic haematoma.** Sometimes a small bleed occurs between the uterine wall and the placenta. This is visible on ultrasound and often resolves on its own. It can produce intermittent pink or brown spotting during the first trimester.

**Infection or irritation.** Less commonly, a vaginal or cervical infection can cause spotting. If the discharge has an unusual smell or is accompanied by itching or burning, mention this to your GP or midwife.

## Spotting vs Bleeding: Knowing the Difference {#spotting-vs-bleeding-knowing-the-difference}

Understanding the distinction can help you judge how urgently you need to act.

**Spotting** is light enough that you notice it on toilet paper when wiping, or as a few small drops on your underwear. It does not flow or require a sanitary pad. Pink spotting that stays light, does not increase in volume, and resolves within a day or two is generally reassuring.

**Bleeding** is anything heavier — enough to wet your underwear or require a pad. If pink discharge is increasing in volume, turning brighter red, or coming with clots, it has moved beyond spotting territory and needs assessment.

Neither spotting nor bleeding automatically means something is wrong, but heavier bleeding — particularly alongside pain — warrants prompt contact with your early pregnancy unit (EPU) or midwife.

## What to Do If You Notice Pink Spotting {#what-to-do-if-you-notice-pink-spotting}

- **Stay calm.** Light pink spotting is common and most often harmless.
- **Note the details.** When did you first notice it? How much is there? Is it getting lighter or heavier? Is there any pain?
- **Avoid intercourse** until the spotting has settled, to avoid further irritation.
- **Rest if it helps you feel calmer**, though bed rest is not proven to prevent miscarriage.
- **Mention it to your midwife.** Even if it stops quickly, it is always worth noting at your next appointment. If you have not had your booking appointment yet and are worried, contact your GP.
- **Contact your EPU** if you are unsure or the spotting is increasing.

## When to Seek Help Urgently {#when-to-seek-help-urgently}

Contact your early pregnancy unit or GP promptly — the same day — if:

- Pink spotting increases and becomes red bleeding
- You have any cramping or pain alongside spotting
- The bleeding requires a pad
- You feel dizzy, faint, or have shoulder tip pain (which can indicate an ectopic pregnancy)
- You are passing clots

**Call 999 or go to A&E immediately** if you have sudden, severe one-sided abdominal pain, heavy bleeding, or feel very unwell. These can be signs of an ectopic pregnancy, which is a medical emergency.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#frequently-asked-questions}

### Is pink spotting at 5 weeks normal?

Yes, it is common around this time due to implantation and rising pregnancy hormone levels. Mention it to your midwife at your booking appointment, or contact your GP if you are concerned or the spotting increases.

### Should I rest if I have pink spotting?

Gentle rest is sensible if it helps you feel calmer and more comfortable. However, bed rest has not been proven to prevent miscarriage or stop spotting. Avoid heavy lifting, strenuous exercise, and intercourse until it has settled.

### Does pink spotting mean ectopic pregnancy?

Not on its own. Ectopic pregnancy typically causes one-sided pelvic pain, shoulder tip pain, and often heavier bleeding. Pink spotting alone is rarely the only sign of an ectopic. However, if spotting comes with any pain — particularly one-sided pain — always seek assessment promptly, as ectopic pregnancy is a serious condition.
