# Protein Snacks for Morning Sickness

Protein is often overlooked in morning sickness advice, yet many women find that including protein at each snack stabilises blood sugar and reduces nausea more effectively than carbohydrates alone. This page explains why protein matters and gives practical, UK-friendly snack ideas you can prepare even when you feel rough.

## Why Protein Eases Nausea

Protein slows digestion and helps keep blood sugar steady, which reduces the dips that trigger nausea. Protein also helps with satiety, meaning you feel less of the emptiness that can bring on a wave of nausea. An empty stomach is one of the most reliable triggers for pregnancy sickness, and protein keeps you feeling fuller for longer than simple carbohydrates alone.

The trick is to combine a little protein with carbohydrate at each snack rather than eating large protein meals, which can feel heavy and hard to digest. Something like a couple of crackers with cheese, or a small yogurt, gives you both the steady energy from carbs and the blood sugar stability from protein. Cold protein foods tend to have fewer strong odours than cooked meat, making them much easier to tolerate when smells are a problem.

It is worth noting that protein needs increase slightly in pregnancy anyway, so prioritising it at snack times supports your baby's development as well as helping manage your symptoms.

## Easy Protein Snack Ideas

These are all easy to prepare, low in strong odour, and widely available in UK supermarkets:

- **Hard-boiled egg** — prepare ahead and keep in the fridge; eat cold with a pinch of salt
- **Cheese and crackers** — mild cheeses such as cheddar or Edam tend to smell less than strong varieties; pasteurised hard cheese is safe in pregnancy
- **Greek yogurt** — high in protein, cooling, and gentle on the stomach; add a drizzle of honey if you can tolerate sweetness
- **Hummus with breadsticks or rice cakes** — plant-based protein that is easy to prepare quickly
- **Small handful of nuts** — almonds, walnuts or cashews are dense in protein and healthy fats; keep a portion-sized bag in your bag
- **Peanut butter on toast or oat cakes** — peanut butter is safe in pregnancy for most women; choose smooth if texture is a problem
- **Cottage cheese** — mild flavour and soft texture; works well on crackers or with cucumber slices
- **A small glass of milk** — if you can tolerate dairy, milk provides both protein and calcium, and many women find it soothing

Avoid strong-smelling cheeses such as blue cheese or brie if odour is a trigger. Also note that unpasteurised soft cheeses and pâté are not recommended during pregnancy.

## How to Fit Protein Snacks Into Your Day

Aim to eat every two to three hours, before hunger — and nausea — build. Prepare snacks in advance when you are feeling relatively well so that when a wave of sickness hits you only have to reach and grab. A small cool bag in your bag or car can be helpful if you are commuting or at work.

Try eating a small protein snack before you even get out of bed in the morning. Keeping crackers or a small pot of nuts on your bedside table and eating them before you sit up is a well-known morning sickness strategy. Adding protein to this, such as a thin scrape of peanut butter on the cracker, can extend how long the comfort lasts.

If food preparation smells are worsening your nausea, ask a partner or family member to prepare protein snacks for you, or rely on ready-to-eat options that require no cooking.

## When Protein Snacks Are Not Enough

For most women, dietary adjustments alongside other strategies such as ginger, small meals, rest and trigger avoidance help manage morning sickness to a tolerable level. However, some women develop hyperemesis gravidarum (HG), a severe form of pregnancy sickness where nausea and vomiting are constant and debilitating.

If you cannot keep any food or fluid down, are losing weight, or feel extremely weak and unable to function, speak to your GP or midwife promptly. HG is a medical condition, not just bad morning sickness, and it can be treated. You should not try to manage it with snacks and home remedies alone.

Signs that you need medical help include:
- Unable to keep fluids down for more than 24 hours
- Feeling dizzy or faint
- Producing very dark or small amounts of urine (a sign of dehydration)
- Vomiting blood or something that looks like coffee grounds — call 999 or go to A&E immediately

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Why does protein help morning sickness?

Protein slows digestion and stabilises blood sugar, both of which help reduce the nausea triggered by blood sugar dips and an empty stomach. Combining protein with a small amount of carbohydrate at every snack is more effective than carbohydrates alone.

### What if meat smells make me feel ill?

Choose cold, low-odour protein snacks such as mild cheese, yogurt, nuts, or peanut butter. Cooked meat smells are among the most common nausea triggers in pregnancy, so sticking to plant-based or dairy protein during the worst weeks is entirely reasonable.

### How much protein should each snack contain?

Around 5 to 10 grams is plenty. The aim is steady, frequent intake throughout the day rather than large amounts at one sitting. A small pot of yogurt, a matchbox-sized piece of cheese, or a small handful of nuts each provide roughly this amount.
