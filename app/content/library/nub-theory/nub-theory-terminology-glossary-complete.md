# Nub Theory Terminology Glossary Complete

If you have ever posted a scan photo to a nub theory community and come back to a response full of words like "stacked," "caudal notch," or "sagittal view," this glossary is for you. Nub theory has its own shorthand that can feel baffling at first. Here is every term explained in plain language.

## What Is Nub Theory?

Nub theory is a method of predicting a baby's sex from an ultrasound scan taken between roughly 11 and 13 weeks of pregnancy. At this stage, all babies have a small protrusion of tissue called the genital tubercle — or "nub" — visible between their legs. The angle and shape of this nub relative to the spine is used to predict whether the baby is a boy or a girl. It is not a medical test, and it is not infallible, but many people enjoy having a guess while they wait for their anatomy scan or a NIPT blood test result.

## Core Terms {#core-terms}

**Nub (genital tubercle)**
The small bud of tissue visible between a baby's legs in the first trimester. In male babies it will develop into a penis; in female babies it becomes the clitoris. Before around 14 weeks, the nub looks broadly similar in both sexes, which is why angle and shape matter so much.

**Sagittal view**
The side-on view of the baby — the view you need to assess the nub. A true sagittal view shows the baby lying flat, with the spine running along the top of the image and the nub pointing towards the bottom. Without a clear sagittal view, a nub reading is not possible.

**Angle of the dip (or nub angle)**
The angle the nub makes in relation to the spine. A nub that points upward at more than roughly 30 degrees from the spine is considered to suggest a boy. A nub that runs parallel to the spine, or dips slightly downward, is considered to suggest a girl. The angle is the core principle of nub theory.

**Stacked**
A description applied to a nub that appears layered or tiered rather than a single smooth line. A stacked nub has a distinct step or second layer at its tip, and is generally read as a boy prediction. It is one of the clearest boy indicators when clearly visible.

**Forked**
A nub where the tip appears to split into two prongs. Forking at the tip is considered a boy indicator by many nub readers, though some forked-looking nubs are later revealed to be girls, so this reading carries more uncertainty than a clearly stacked or high-angle nub.

**Ramped**
A nub where the tip curves or ramps upward. The ramp suggests the nub is rising away from the spine rather than running parallel to it — typically read as a boy sign.

**Flat / parallel**
A nub that runs in the same direction as the spine, with little or no upward angle. A flat or parallel nub is generally read as a girl prediction. The flatter and more horizontal the nub, the stronger the girl reading is considered to be.

**Caudal notch**
The small dip or notch at the base of the spine, visible in a sagittal scan image. Readers use the caudal notch as a reference point to measure the nub's angle accurately. Identifying the caudal notch correctly is important — misidentifying it can lead to an inaccurate angle reading.

**Skull theory**
A separate (and less reliable) method of predicting sex from scan images, based on the shape of the baby's skull. It is sometimes discussed alongside nub theory but is a different technique.

**Ramzi theory**
Another first-trimester prediction method based on the position of the placenta relative to the uterus wall. Again, it is a different approach from nub theory and is assessed on different types of scan images.

**NIPT / Harmony / Panorama**
Non-invasive prenatal tests that analyse fragments of the baby's DNA in the mother's blood. These can give highly accurate sex determination from around 10 weeks. They are medical tests, not predictions, and are far more reliable than nub theory.

**NT scan**
The nuchal translucency scan, usually offered at 11 to 13 weeks on the NHS. This is the scan at which nub theory readings are most commonly attempted because the angle of the nub is clearest at this stage.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### What does "stacked" mean in nub theory?

A stacked nub is one that appears to have a distinct raised or tiered layer at the end, giving it a stepped appearance rather than a smooth single line. It is generally considered a strong boy indicator. However, image clarity and the baby's position can affect whether stacking is genuinely visible or an artefact of the scan angle.

### Can you read a nub without a sagittal view?

No. A true sagittal view — the baby seen perfectly side-on — is essential. If the baby is at an angle, the nub may appear to point up or down when it is actually running straight, which gives a misleading reading. If your scan does not include a clear sagittal view, a nub reading simply is not possible from that image.

### How accurate is nub theory?

Nub theory is not a medical test. When the nub is clearly visible and measured by someone experienced, accuracy rates are often reported as reasonably good in informal community settings — but it is not guaranteed, and there are plenty of cases where predictions are wrong. The most reliable way to find out your baby's sex before the anatomy scan is a NIPT blood test. Always treat a nub theory reading as a fun guess, not a confirmed result.
