# Carrying High vs Low: Does It Predict Gender?

The old wives' tale says carrying high means you're having a girl and carrying low means you're having a boy. It's one of the most repeated bump myths, and it's easy to see why it's fun to play along. The honest answer, though, is that how high or low you carry tells you nothing reliable about your baby's sex. It's a roughly even guess either way, the same odds as flipping a coin.

## The Theory {#s1}

According to the tale, a high, neat bump that sits up near your ribs means a girl, while a low bump that drops towards your hips means a boy. Some versions add that a "wide" bump means a girl and a "ball out front" bump means a boy. These ideas have been passed down for generations, long before scans could tell you anything, so they filled a gap when there was no other way to guess.

The trouble is that none of it stands up. Your baby's sex is set at conception by chromosomes and is completely separate from the size, muscle, and ligament factors that shape your bump. A prediction that's right about half the time isn't really predicting anything — it's just the natural odds.

## The Reality {#s2}

How you carry is mostly about your own body, not your baby's sex. The main things that decide whether you look high or low include:

- **Your abdominal muscle tone.** Firmer tummy muscles tend to hold a bump higher and more compactly.
- **How many pregnancies you've had.** Muscles and ligaments that have stretched before often let later bumps sit lower.
- **Your height and torso length.** A shorter space between your ribs and hips can push a bump forward or up.
- **Your baby's position.** Babies move constantly in early and mid pregnancy, so your bump shape can change week to week, even day to day.

So if your bump looks different from a friend's at the same stage, that's normal and expected. It reflects your build, not whether you're expecting a boy or a girl.

## What Actually Decides Bump Shape {#s3}

If you want to understand your own bump, think about it as a snapshot rather than a fixed shape. Early on, before about 12 weeks, most bumps aren't visible at all, and any "bump" is usually bloating. As pregnancy goes on, the way you carry can shift as your baby grows and changes position and as your core muscles adapt. There's a wide, healthy range, and there's no "right" way to look.

If you ever feel your bump has suddenly changed a lot, or you're worried about your baby's growth, your midwife can measure and reassure you. Bump shape on its own isn't a medical concern, but your midwife is the right person to check if something feels off to you.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Does carrying high or low mean boy or girl?

No. Bump position is determined by your muscle tone, body shape, the number of previous pregnancies you've had, and your baby's position — not by sex. It's roughly an even guess either way, so it isn't a reliable predictor.

### Why does my bump look different from other pregnant women's?

Because everyone's body is different. Your height, build, core strength, and whether you've been pregnant before all affect how you carry. Two people at the exact same stage can look completely different and both be perfectly healthy.

### Is there any reliable way to find out my baby's sex?

The accurate ways are medical: NIPT (a blood test usually from 10 weeks), or seeing the genitals on the 20-week anomaly scan. Bump shape, cravings, and similar tales are just for fun.

If you have any concerns about your bump or your baby's growth, speak to your midwife or GP for personal advice.
