# Nub Theory Reader I Was Told Boy But Had a Girl

Nub theory is one of the most popular ways to try to guess a baby's sex from an early scan image — but it is not infallible. This page looks at what happens when nub predictions go wrong, why errors occur, and how to hold early predictions lightly so you are not caught off guard at your anatomy scan.

## The Prediction {#the-prediction}

One mum shared that her 12-week scan image was sent to a nub theory reader who confidently predicted a boy, pointing to what appeared to be a steep upward angle on the nub. She felt reassured by the confident tone, bought a selection of boy clothes, and told family the news early. At her 20-week anatomy scan, the sonographer confirmed she was expecting a girl. The prediction had been wrong.

Her experience is far from unique. Many parents arrive on nub theory forums after their anatomy scan, sharing the exact same story: a confident early prediction that did not match the anatomy scan result. The surprise can range from mild amusement to genuine emotional adjustment, particularly if purchases have been made or news shared widely.

This is not a criticism of nub theory as a concept — it is a reminder that early sex prediction, even when carried out carefully, carries meaningful uncertainty. The nub itself is a small, developing structure that can be difficult to image clearly, and interpretation depends heavily on the quality and angle of the scan photo.

## Why Predictions Can Be Wrong {#why-wrong}

Several factors make nub theory predictions unreliable, even in experienced hands:

**Scan angle.** The nub angle must be measured accurately relative to the fetal spine. If the baby is rotated even slightly, a nub that appears steep can actually be angled more neutrally — or vice versa. The difference between a "boy angle" and a "girl angle" can be a matter of degrees that are invisible in a standard scan photo.

**Image quality.** Many NHS dating scan images are taken as still frames from video, and the quality is variable. A blurry or compressed image makes accurate measurement much harder. The nub can be partially obscured by the baby's position, the uterine wall, or shadow from the probe.

**Gestational age.** The nub theory window is broadly 11 to 13 weeks. Earlier in this window, the nub has not differentiated as much and is genuinely harder to read. A reading at 11 weeks carries more uncertainty than one at 13 weeks.

**Reader experience.** Nub theory is not a recognised medical technique, and there is no formal qualification for reading nubs. Confidence in a prediction does not equal accuracy. Even experienced community readers who have studied many thousands of images will sometimes be wrong.

**Confirmation bias in reviews.** People who receive a correct prediction are more likely to return to the same reader or forum and share their result. People who received an incorrect prediction may post once and move on. This can create a misleading impression of accuracy in any informal review setting.

## How to Hold a Nub Prediction {#how-to-hold}

The healthiest way to approach any nub theory reading is as an educated guess — entertaining, sometimes accurate, but never a certainty. A few practical approaches help:

- **Wait before purchasing.** Hold off on gender-specific clothing, bedding, or décor until your anatomy scan at around 20 weeks, or until you have had a confirmed result from a medical test such as NIPT or amniocentesis.
- **Be cautious about sharing widely.** Telling extended family or friends the sex based on a nub reading means you may need to correct the information later, which adds an unnecessary layer of stress.
- **Enjoy the speculation for what it is.** Many families find nub theory a fun way to engage with the pregnancy before an official answer is available. That is entirely valid — just hold the prediction lightly.

## FAQ {#faq}

### How common are wrong nub predictions?

No formally published clinical statistics exist for nub theory accuracy rates in casual community settings. Early readings at 11 weeks are wrong often enough that they should never be treated as definitive. Even readings at 13 weeks, when the nub is more developed, can be incorrect due to scan angle and image quality.

### Should I buy gender-specific items after a nub reading?

It is generally wiser to wait until your 20-week anatomy scan or a confirmed medical result before spending money on gendered items. Many parents choose neutral colours and styles in early pregnancy precisely because early predictions — whether nub theory, Ramzi theory, or Chinese gender charts — do not carry clinical certainty.

### Can I trust a reader who sounds very confident?

Confidence in a prediction does not equal accuracy. The most a reader can tell you is what a particular angle looks like on a particular image on a particular day. All of those variables could have pointed differently in a slightly different photo. Treat any prediction, however confidently delivered, as a possibility rather than a fact.
