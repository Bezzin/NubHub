# Nosebleeds in Pregnancy

Nosebleeds are more common in pregnancy than most people expect. The increased blood volume and hormonal changes make the tiny blood vessels in the nose more fragile and prone to bleeding. Most are harmless, but knowing how to manage them and when to seek help matters.

## Why Nosebleeds Happen in Pregnancy {#why-nosebleeds-happen-in-pregnancy}

Pregnancy raises blood volume by up to 50 percent, which puts extra pressure on delicate nasal vessels. Hormones also make mucous membranes swollen and sensitive. Together these factors mean minor irritation can trigger a bleed.

## How to Stop and Prevent Them {#how-to-stop-and-prevent-them}

To stop a nosebleed, pinch the soft part of the nose, lean forward, and breathe through the mouth for ten minutes. Avoid leaning back. To prevent bleeds, keep the nose moist with saline, avoid picking, and use a humidifier. Contact your GP if bleeds are heavy or frequent.

## FAQ {#faq}

### Are nosebleeds in pregnancy dangerous?

Usually not. Most are minor and caused by hormonal and blood volume changes. Heavy, frequent, or hard-to-stop nosebleeds should be checked.

### When should I seek medical help?

If a nosebleed lasts more than 20 minutes, is very heavy, or you also have high blood pressure or a bleeding disorder, contact your GP or maternity unit promptly.

### Can nosebleeds mean something is wrong with the baby?

Rarely. On their own they are usually benign. But combined with other symptoms like severe headache or vision changes, they can signal blood pressure issues, so report them.
