# Bump Shape and Gender: Does It Predict Boy or Girl?

"Carrying high means a girl, carrying low means a boy" — or is it a round bump for a girl and a neat, pointed bump for a boy? There are many versions of this old wives' tale, and they often contradict each other. The truth is that the shape of your bump is determined by your own body and your baby's position, not by whether you are having a boy or a girl. Here is why bumps look so different from person to person, and how to find out your baby's sex reliably.

## The Theory {#s1}

The traditional belief is that a round, wide bump that you "carry all over" means a girl, while a neat, pointed bump that sits forward and low means a boy. Some people swap these around, and others add that carrying high means a girl and carrying low means a boy. The fact that the rules vary so much is the first clue that this is folklore rather than fact.

## The Reality {#s2}

There is no scientific basis for predicting your baby's sex from the shape of your bump. Two people carrying babies of the same sex can have completely different-looking bumps, and the same person can carry differently in each of their pregnancies.

Because the myth gives only two possible answers, it will appear "correct" for roughly half of pregnancies by chance alone — around 50%, no better than guessing. People tend to remember the times the tale was "right" and forget the times it was wrong, which is why it feels more accurate than it is.

## What Actually Shapes Your Bump {#s3}

Lots of genuine factors influence how your bump looks, and none of them relate to your baby's sex:

- Your height, build, and the length of your torso
- Your abdominal muscle tone, and whether this is your first pregnancy (tummy muscles often have more give in later pregnancies, so bumps can show sooner or sit lower)
- The position your baby is lying in, which changes throughout pregnancy
- The amount of amniotic fluid and the position of the placenta
- How far along you are

It is worth remembering that bump size and shape are not a reliable guide to your baby's health, either. If you have any concerns about how you are measuring or growing, your midwife checks this at your appointments. If you would like to know your baby's sex reliably, the mid-pregnancy ultrasound (around 18–21 weeks on the NHS) can often show it, and a blood test such as NIPT from around 10 weeks can indicate it. Enjoy the guessing games, but treat them as fun rather than fact.

## FAQ {#faq}

### Does bump shape predict gender?

No. Bump shape is determined by your body structure, muscle tone, and your baby's position, not by sex. This old wives' tale is right only about half the time, by chance, just like flipping a coin.

### Does carrying high or low mean boy or girl?

No. Whether you carry high or low depends on your build, your abdominal muscles, and your baby's position, not on the baby's sex. Different versions of this tale even contradict each other, which shows it is folklore rather than fact.

### Can bump shape tell me anything about my baby's health?

Bump shape and size are not a reliable guide to your baby's health on their own. Your midwife monitors your baby's growth at your antenatal appointments, so raise any worries with them rather than judging by appearance.

For anything that concerns you about your bump or your baby's growth, speak to your midwife or GP.
