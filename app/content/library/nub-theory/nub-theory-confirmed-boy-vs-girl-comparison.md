# Nub Theory: Confirmed Boy vs Girl Comparison

One of the best ways to understand nub theory is to compare your own scan against examples where the sex was later confirmed by the 20-week anatomy scan or a blood test. Once you know what a clearly male nub and a clearly female nub look like side by side, you can start to read the key features with more confidence — and, just as importantly, recognise when a nub is genuinely ambiguous and a confident prediction is not possible.

## Confirmed Boy Nub {#s1}

In confirmed boy scans, the nub shows a consistent set of features:

**Angle.** The most reliable indicator. A confirmed boy nub rises at a clear upward angle away from the spine — typically described as 30 degrees or more above the horizontal line of the spine. In clear cases this angle is obvious at a glance rather than requiring careful measurement.

**Shape at the tip.** A boy nub often shows a stacked appearance at the tip — two or more lines visible one above the other — sometimes described as "forked." This is because the nub is in the process of developing into the penis and scrotum, and the stacking reflects that early differentiation.

**Length.** Male nubs tend to look more elongated and protrude further from the body than female nubs at the same gestational age.

**Caudal notch.** Some confirmed boy scans show a small visible notch or separation between the nub and the spine, though this feature is not always present and should not be used in isolation.

At 12 weeks and beyond, a clearly angled, stacked, elongated nub is a strong indicator of male sex. At 11 weeks, the same features can still be present but are harder to read because the nub is smaller.

## Confirmed Girl Nub {#s2}

In confirmed girl scans, the nub presents very differently:

**Angle.** A confirmed girl nub lies parallel to the spine or tilts very slightly upward — typically described as fewer than 10 degrees above horizontal, or flat. It does not rise away from the spine the way a boy nub does.

**Shape.** Rather than a stacked or forked tip, a girl nub typically shows three short horizontal lines of roughly equal length — often described as a "trident" or flat fork. The tip does not stack vertically.

**Length.** Girl nubs are generally shorter and sit closer to the body. They tend to blend into the contour of the baby's bottom rather than projecting outward.

**Overall impression.** In clear girl examples, the nub looks low, flat, and flush with the body. Where a boy nub draws the eye upward, a girl nub tends to sit quietly in line with the spine.

## The Key Difference: Angle {#s3}

When comparing confirmed examples, angle is consistently the most reliable differentiator. Shape at the tip (stacked vs flat) is a useful secondary feature but can be harder to read in lower-quality or oblique scan images.

The practical approach when comparing your own scan to confirmed examples:

1. First establish the spine line — the horizontal reference point from which angle is measured.
2. Look at whether the nub rises above that line (boy) or sits level with or below it (girl).
3. Then look at the tip shape as a secondary check.
4. Consider how clear the nub is — a nub that is half-hidden, viewed from an awkward angle, or blurry cannot be reliably compared to confirmed examples.

## When a Comparison Is Not Enough {#s4}

Comparing your scan to confirmed examples works best when your scan image is a true mid-sagittal view (the baby is lying on its side with the spine clearly horizontal), the nub is clearly visible without being obscured, and the baby is at least 11 weeks. When these conditions are not met, even the most experienced nub readers will say a prediction is not possible from that image.

It is also worth remembering that nub theory, even in ideal conditions, is not a diagnostic test. It is a fun and often surprisingly accurate way to guess your baby's sex earlier than the 20-week scan, but it should not be treated as a certainty. Only the 20-week anatomy scan, a NIPT blood test, or an amniocentesis can confirm sex with clinical accuracy.

## FAQ {#faq}

### Can I compare my scan to confirmed examples?

Yes — comparing to confirmed examples is one of the most effective ways to learn nub theory. Look at angle first, then tip shape. The most helpful confirmed examples are those with a clear mid-sagittal view and a sharp, high-resolution image, because these are the conditions under which the features are most readable.

### What if my nub looks somewhere between the confirmed boy and confirmed girl examples?

This is very common, and it usually means the nub is ambiguous from that image. Not every scan produces a clear nub prediction. If your nub does not clearly match either the boy or girl pattern — perhaps the angle is borderline, or the tip shape is unclear — the honest answer is that a confident prediction is not possible from that image. An ambiguous result is not a bad sign for your pregnancy; it simply means the scan conditions did not allow a clear read.

### Does a confirmed comparison mean my prediction is certain?

No. Even a nub that closely matches confirmed boy or confirmed girl examples carries some uncertainty. Nub theory is an informal predictor, not a medical test. The only way to know your baby's sex before birth with clinical certainty is through NIPT blood testing or your 20-week anatomy scan.

Always speak to your midwife or GP for personal advice, and enjoy the guessing in the spirit it is intended — as a fun part of early pregnancy.
