# Vaginal Discharge in Early Pregnancy

An increase in vaginal discharge is one of the earliest and most normal signs of pregnancy. Known as leucorrhoea, this thin, milky discharge helps protect the vagina from infection. Knowing what is normal helps you spot when to seek advice.

## What Is Normal Discharge in Pregnancy? {#what-is-normal-discharge}

Normal pregnancy discharge is thin, milky white or clear, and mild-smelling. It increases due to higher oestrogen levels and greater blood flow to the vaginal area. This is your body's way of keeping the birth canal clean and protected.

You may notice it from very early on — sometimes even before a positive test — and it usually continues throughout all three trimesters. The volume tends to increase as pregnancy progresses. Towards the end of pregnancy, some women notice a heavier or more mucus-like discharge as the cervix begins to soften; this is normal too.

You might also notice a small amount of pink or brown-tinged spotting around the time of implantation (roughly 6–12 days after conception). This is called implantation bleeding and is generally harmless, though any bleeding should be mentioned to your midwife.

## What Changes Discharge in Pregnancy? {#what-changes-discharge}

Several normal pregnancy processes affect discharge:

- **Hormonal changes.** Rising oestrogen increases blood flow to the pelvic area and stimulates the vaginal walls and cervix to produce more fluid.
- **Cervical mucus plug.** From early pregnancy, the cervix produces a thick mucus plug that seals it and protects the uterus from infection. Increased discharge is partly related to this process.
- **Vaginal pH shifts.** The vaginal environment becomes slightly more acidic in pregnancy, which helps suppress harmful bacteria but also makes yeast (thrush) more likely to thrive.

## When to Seek Help {#when-to-seek-help}

See your GP or midwife if discharge changes in any of the following ways:

- **Colour changes.** Green, yellow, or grey discharge is not normal and may indicate bacterial vaginosis (BV) or a sexually transmitted infection (STI).
- **Thick and clumpy.** Cottage-cheese-like white discharge with itching or soreness usually indicates thrush, which is common in pregnancy and treatable.
- **Strong or unpleasant smell.** An unusual odour alongside changed discharge warrants a check-up, even if you feel otherwise well.
- **Watery or very heavy flow.** A sudden gush or continuous watery leakage — particularly in the second or third trimester — may indicate amniotic fluid leaking. Contact your midwife or maternity unit promptly if this happens.
- **Accompanied by bleeding.** Any fresh red bleeding should be reviewed by your midwife or GP, especially in the first trimester.

Most infections found during pregnancy — including thrush, BV, and many STIs — are readily treatable and the sooner they are picked up, the better for both you and your baby.

## Keeping Comfortable {#keeping-comfortable}

- Wear breathable, cotton underwear and avoid tight clothing.
- Use unscented panty liners if you find the increased discharge uncomfortable — change them regularly.
- Wash the external area with plain water; avoid internal douching, which disrupts the natural vaginal balance.
- Avoid perfumed soaps, bubble baths, or scented wipes around the vulva.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Is more discharge normal in early pregnancy?

Yes. Increased discharge is one of the most common early pregnancy symptoms and is usually completely harmless. It is driven by hormonal changes and increased blood flow to the pelvis. As long as it is white, clear, or very faintly creamy and has little or no smell, there is no need for concern.

### When is discharge a concern during pregnancy?

Contact your midwife if discharge becomes green, yellow, or grey; develops a strong or unpleasant smell; is accompanied by itching, burning, or soreness; or if you experience a sudden watery leakage that could be amniotic fluid. Bleeding alongside discharge also warrants prompt assessment.

### Can I use panty liners in pregnancy?

Yes, unscented panty liners are fine to use throughout pregnancy. Avoid scented or deodorising varieties, which can irritate sensitive skin, and avoid internal products like tampons. Never douche during pregnancy, as this can introduce bacteria and disrupt your natural protective environment.
