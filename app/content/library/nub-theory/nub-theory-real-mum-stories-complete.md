# Nub Theory: Real Mum Stories and Results

One of the most useful ways to understand nub theory is through the experiences of people who have actually tried it. These stories illustrate both what it looks like when a prediction lands perfectly and what can go wrong — and why. We've gathered a range of real experiences to give you a balanced, honest picture of what nub theory is actually like in practice.

## Story 1: Right at 13 Weeks {#s1}

Emma had her NHS dating scan at 13 weeks and 2 days. The scan image showed her baby in a clear side-on profile, with the spine visible from neck to tailbone. The nub was angled sharply upward and appeared to have a slightly stacked tip. She posted the image and the community quickly agreed: boy. At her 20-week anomaly scan, the sonographer confirmed it — boy.

What made this prediction reliable? The gestational age was ideal. At 13 weeks, the genital tubercle has had more time to differentiate, making the angle clearer and more meaningful. The image quality was excellent and the nub was unambiguous. When these factors combine, confident readings are reasonable.

Emma described the experience as fun rather than anxious — she treated the community prediction as a curiosity while waiting for her anomaly scan. That approach is probably the healthiest way to engage with nub theory: with enthusiasm, but without staking too much on the result.

## Story 2: Wrong at 11 Weeks {#s2}

Priya had a private early reassurance scan at 11 weeks and 1 day. The nub appeared slightly raised at the tip. The community was cautious — most said "possibly boy, but too early to say" — but she went away hopeful. At her 20-week scan, the baby was a girl.

This is a classic illustration of why gestational age matters so much. At 11 weeks, the genital tubercle in girls and boys can look very similar — both may show a slight upward tilt that later flattens in girls as development progresses. Predictions made before 12 weeks carry noticeably more uncertainty, and even experienced readers will caveat them heavily.

Priya's takeaway was instructive: she said she wished she had paid more attention to the community's caution rather than focusing on the "possibly boy" part. If readers are hedging, that hedge is important information — not noise to be filtered out.

## Story 3: Too Ambiguous to Call {#s3}

Jade's 12-week scan image was shared in our community with high hopes. The problem was that the baby was lying in a slightly curled position, making it difficult to see the spine clearly. Without the spine as a reference baseline, measuring the nub angle accurately is almost impossible. Readers gave varying opinions ranging from girl to boy to "impossible to tell." The community consensus was: not readable.

Jade had a girl. But the honest answer is that no one could have called it from that image — and the right response in cases like this is to acknowledge the limitation rather than guess confidently.

This story illustrates something important about nub theory communities: a good community will tell you when an image cannot be read. If you receive a clear "inconclusive" verdict rather than a prediction, that is the community doing its job well. It would be far worse to receive a confident prediction from an unreadable image.

## Story 4: Right for the Wrong Reasons {#s4}

Chloe's scan at 12+5 showed what looked like a flat nub, leading most readers to say girl. At 20 weeks — girl confirmed. But when Chloe re-examined the image later, she realised the nub had actually been partially obscured by the angle of the baby's legs. She'd received the right answer, but arrived at it through an incomplete assessment. This is a good reminder that a correct prediction and a reliable prediction are not always the same thing.

With a roughly 50/50 baseline probability, any prediction has a reasonable chance of being correct even if made for imperfect reasons. Chloe's story is a good-natured example of how outcomes and methodology can diverge — and why you should focus on whether an image is genuinely readable rather than whether a previous prediction happened to be right.

## Story 5: Changed Mind at 13 Weeks

Sophie's story adds another layer of complexity. Her 12-week scan produced a reading of "likely girl" from most readers. She then had a private scan at 13 weeks because she was anxious about the pregnancy generally — and the new image, taken at a slightly different angle, produced a clear "boy" consensus. At 20 weeks: boy.

This highlights that scan angle and positioning can make a meaningful difference even at the same gestational age. A different day, a different baby position, and a different image can produce a different — and sometimes more reliable — reading. If your first image is inconclusive, a follow-up scan at a slightly later stage may produce a clearer result, though this should always be driven by clinical need rather than gender curiosity alone.

## What These Stories Tell Us {#s5}

Taken together, these experiences point to a few consistent themes:

**Gestational age is the single biggest factor.** Scans taken at 12 weeks or later — and especially between 12 and 13+6 — give the most reliable results. Earlier than 12 weeks, the nub simply hasn't differentiated enough for confident reading.

**Image quality determines readability.** A perfect gestational age is wasted if the baby is in an awkward position. Profile view with the spine clearly visible is non-negotiable.

**Ambiguous images should be called ambiguous.** A good reader will tell you when an image is not readable rather than guessing. If someone is giving you a very confident prediction from a blurry or partially obscured image, treat that confidence with scepticism.

**Correct predictions are not always accurate predictions.** With a roughly 50/50 baseline probability for boy or girl, any guess has a reasonable chance of being right. That's why individual stories — even long runs of correct predictions — don't tell you as much about reliability as the overall image quality and gestational age do.

**How you engage matters.** The people in these stories who had the best experience were those who treated nub theory as one enjoyable part of a much larger pregnancy journey, rather than as a definitive answer. Holding predictions lightly makes the whole experience more enjoyable and protects you emotionally if the prediction turns out to be wrong.

Nub theory can be a fun and interesting way to engage with your pregnancy, especially while you're waiting for the 20-week scan. Just hold predictions lightly, and remember that the anomaly scan or a NIPT test are the only ways to get a confirmed result.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Are nub theory stories reliable evidence?

Individual stories are anecdotal by nature. A story of a correct prediction at 13 weeks is encouraging but tells you nothing definitive about accuracy rates in general. The most meaningful thing these stories do is illustrate the conditions under which predictions tend to be more or less reliable — primarily gestational age and image quality. They are not a substitute for understanding the underlying factors.

### Why do some people get wrong predictions even from clear scans?

Even under ideal conditions, nub theory is not a diagnostic test and carries inherent uncertainty. The nub angle can be genuinely borderline, and individual variation in development means that not every case fits the expected pattern neatly. A wrong result from a clear, well-timed scan is disappointing but not surprising — it is simply part of the margin of uncertainty that exists with any informal gender prediction method.

### What should I do if the community disagrees about my image?

Disagreement in the community is itself a signal. If experienced readers are split, it usually means the image is ambiguous — the nub angle is not clear enough to read confidently one way or the other. In these cases, the honest answer is "inconclusive." Try not to read too much into any particular opinion, and wait for your 20-week anomaly scan for a confirmed result.
