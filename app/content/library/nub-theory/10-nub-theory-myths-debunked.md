# Nub Theory Myths Debunked: Separating Fact from Fiction

Nub theory has grown massively in popularity, and with that popularity comes plenty of misinformation. Let's separate the facts from the fiction.

## Myth 1: Nub Theory Is 100% Accurate {#myth-1}

**Reality:** Nub theory is 80–95% accurate, depending on gestational age.

Even at 13 weeks — the most accurate time — nub theory has a 5–8% error rate. That means roughly 1 in 20 predictions will be wrong. Nub theory is a well-informed prediction, not a definitive diagnosis. Only NIPT (99%+) and diagnostic tests like amniocentesis (100%) can definitively determine sex.

## Myth 2: A Flat Nub Always Means Girl {#myth-2}

**Reality:** A flat nub USUALLY means girl, but there are exceptions.

A boy's nub may occasionally appear flat at 11 weeks if it hasn't angled upward yet. By 12+ weeks, a flat nub is very likely a girl, but at 11 weeks, there's more uncertainty. Always consider the gestational age when interpreting the nub angle.

## Myth 3: The Fork Is Always a Boy Sign {#myth-3}

**Reality:** A forked tip is usually associated with boys, but it's not guaranteed.

The "fork" at the nub tip is a secondary indicator. The primary indicator is always the angle. A forked nub at only 10 degrees is still ambiguous, regardless of what the tip looks like. Don't rely on the fork alone — always check the angle first.

## Myth 4: Nub Theory Works at 8 Weeks {#myth-4}

**Reality:** Nub theory does not work before 11 weeks. The genital tubercle is present from around 9 weeks, but the angle difference between boys and girls doesn't develop until 11+ weeks. Before 11 weeks, all nubs look essentially the same. Anyone claiming to read a nub at 8 weeks is guessing.

## Myth 5: Sonographers Can Confirm Nub Theory {#myth-5}

**Reality:** NHS sonographers do not perform or confirm nub theory. Their job is to measure the baby, check the heartbeat, and perform nuchal translucency screening. They won't assess the nub angle or confirm gender at 12 weeks. Some may offer a casual opinion if asked, but they won't officially confirm sex until the 20-week scan.

## Myth 6: Nub Theory Works on Any Ultrasound Angle {#myth-6}

**Reality:** Nub theory only works on a sagittal (side-profile) view. Face-up, face-down, or bottom views don't show the nub angle correctly. If your scan photo doesn't show a clear side profile, nub theory won't work — no matter how experienced the reader is.

## Myth 7: A Stacked Nub Guarantees a Boy {#myth-7}

**Reality:** Stacking is a strong boy indicator but not a guarantee. Stacking at the nub tip is commonly seen in boys and represents the developing penis and scrotum. However, image artefacts, shadows, and unusual angles can sometimes create a stacked appearance that isn't truly the nub. Always verify with the primary angle measurement.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### What's the biggest myth about nub theory?

The biggest myth is that nub theory is always right. It's not — even at the best time (13 weeks), it's 92–95% accurate, meaning roughly 1 in 20 predictions will be wrong. Nub theory is a prediction, not a guarantee.

### Can nub theory predict gender at 6 weeks?

No. Nub theory is not applicable before 11 weeks. The genital tubercle hasn't developed enough for the angle to be meaningful. Anyone claiming to predict gender from a nub at 6 weeks is guessing. For early prediction, NIPT works from 10 weeks with 99%+ accuracy.

### Is the "stacked" appearance always a boy?

The stacked appearance at the nub tip is strongly associated with boys and represents the developing penis and scrotum. However, it's a secondary indicator — the primary indicator is always the angle. A stacked nub at less than 30 degrees is still ambiguous.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Are nub theory Facebook groups reliable?

Nub theory Facebook groups can be a great place to get multiple opinions on your scan photo. However, the accuracy depends on the experience of the readers and the quality of your image. Always remember that even experienced readers can be wrong 5–8% of the time at 12+ weeks.

### Can nub theory be done from a photo of the screen?

Yes, but image quality matters. A photo taken of the ultrasound screen (rather than a printed scan photo) may have glare, distortion, or reduced clarity. If possible, use the printed photo or a digital image from the scan, not a photo of the screen.
