# The Cabbage Test for Gender Prediction

The cabbage test is one of dozens of old wives' tales that claim to predict your baby's sex at home using everyday items. It involves red cabbage and urine — and while it can be a fun activity, it has no scientific basis and is no more reliable than flipping a coin.

## How the Cabbage Test Works {#section-1}

The test is simple enough to do at home. Here is how it's typically described:

1. Chop half a red cabbage and boil it in water for around 10 minutes
2. Allow the water to cool and strain out the cabbage, keeping the purple-coloured liquid
3. Combine equal parts of the cabbage water and your urine in a clear container
4. Observe the colour change:
   - **Pink or reddish** is said to indicate a boy
   - **Purple or no change** is said to indicate a girl

The colour change is real — red cabbage does change colour in response to pH — but the interpretation as a gender predictor has no scientific foundation whatsoever.

## The Science (or Lack of It) {#section-2}

Red cabbage contains compounds called anthocyanins, which are natural pH indicators. They turn pink or red in acidic conditions and remain purple or turn greenish in alkaline conditions. Urine pH varies naturally depending on what you've eaten, how much water you've drunk, time of day, and various health factors — none of which are related to the sex of your baby.

The sex of your baby is determined by chromosomes established at fertilisation. It does not influence your urine pH in a way that a cabbage test could detect. Any result you get is essentially random, and over a large enough number of tries, the test would be correct roughly half the time — the same as chance.

This is why many people share stories of the test "working" for them. If you do any test that is right 50% of the time with two possible outcomes, half the people who try it will feel it was accurate. The cabbage test has not been studied or validated as a method of sex prediction, and no midwife, GP, or NHS source would suggest using it for that purpose.

## How It Compares to Other Methods {#section-3}

It helps to put the cabbage test in context alongside the options that do carry genuine accuracy:

**Old wives' tales (cabbage test, baking soda test, ring test, heart rate theory, etc.)** — These are all approximately 50% accurate, the same as guessing. They are harmless fun but should not be used to make decisions or form strong expectations about your baby's sex.

**Nub theory at 12 weeks** — Based on the angle of the genital tubercle in an ultrasound scan image. Can be more accurate than chance in ideal conditions (correct gestation, good image quality, experienced reader), but still carries a meaningful error rate, particularly for readings taken before 12 weeks or from lower-quality images.

**Private gender scan (from around 16 weeks)** — A trained sonographer visually identifies the genitalia on ultrasound. This is considerably more reliable than nub theory and is widely available privately in the UK.

**NIPT blood test (from around 10 weeks)** — Analyses fragments of your baby's DNA circulating in your bloodstream. This is the most accurate non-invasive method available before the 20-week scan, with very high accuracy for sex determination. Available privately in the UK.

**20-week anomaly scan** — The NHS scan at around 20 weeks is the standard point at which sex is confirmed on the NHS (if you choose to find out). Accuracy at this stage is high, though it depends on the baby's position.

If knowing your baby's sex before 20 weeks matters to you, a private NIPT or gender scan is a much more reliable option than any home test.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Is the cabbage gender test accurate?

No. The cabbage test has no scientific basis as a method of sex prediction. The colour change it produces is a real chemical reaction — red cabbage reacts to the pH of urine — but urine pH is not related to your baby's sex. The result is essentially random and will be correct roughly half the time, the same as chance.

### Are any home gender tests accurate?

Home urine-based tests such as the cabbage test and the baking soda test are no more accurate than guessing. The only tests with genuine accuracy that can be done outside of a hospital are NIPT blood tests, which analyse fetal DNA from a blood sample. These are available privately in the UK from around 10 weeks. Private gender ultrasound scans from around 16 weeks are also considerably more reliable than home tests.

### Is the cabbage test safe to do during pregnancy?

Yes — boiling cabbage and testing your urine is harmless. The concern with the cabbage test is not safety but accuracy: it simply doesn't work as a gender predictor. If you'd like to try it as a bit of fun, there's no harm in doing so, as long as you don't rely on the result.
