# Nub Theory No Nub Visible Guide

You've received your scan image, you're excited to try nub theory — and then you realise there's simply no nub visible. This is more common than you might think, and it doesn't mean anything has gone wrong. Here's what it means and what you can do next.

## Why the Nub Might Not Be Visible

The "nub" in nub theory refers to the genital tubercle, a small bud of tissue visible between the baby's legs on an ultrasound scan. Whether or not it can be seen clearly depends on several factors:

**Baby's position** is the most common reason. If your baby is lying with their legs tucked towards their body, curled up, or facing away from the probe, the nub will simply be hidden from view. At this early stage — typically between 11 and 14 weeks — babies move around frequently during a scan, and even a few seconds can change how much is visible.

**Gestational age** also plays a role. At 11 weeks, the nub is very small and harder to capture at the right angle. At 13 weeks it is slightly larger and more distinct. If your scan was on the earlier side, there's a greater chance the nub wasn't clearly captured.

**Scan angle and image quality** matter too. Nub theory requires a very specific lateral (side-on) view to assess the angle of the nub relative to the spine. Even if the nub is present, if the scan wasn't captured at this precise angle, a reading simply isn't possible.

**Sonographer focus** is worth considering as well. NHS dating scans are not performed for gender prediction — the sonographer's job is to measure the baby and check development. They won't necessarily position the probe to get a nub-theory-friendly image, and that's entirely normal.

## What It Means for Nub Theory

If the nub isn't visible, nub theory genuinely cannot be applied to your scan. There is no reliable way to make a gender prediction from a scan where the nub region isn't clearly captured in the correct plane. Anyone telling you they can read a nub that isn't visible, or making a prediction based on a poor-quality or wrong-angle image, is not applying the method accurately.

This isn't a failing of your scan — it simply means the conditions weren't right for nub theory on this occasion.

It is also worth remembering that nub theory is not a medically validated method of sex determination. Even under ideal conditions — a clear lateral view, the right gestational age, a well-trained reader — nub theory is not perfectly accurate. It is a fun way to make an early guess, but it should not be relied upon for certainty.

## How Common Is a Hidden Nub?

There is no precise published figure for how often the nub is not visible on a scan, because it depends heavily on how early the scan is taken, the baby's position, the equipment used, and the image captured. What experienced nub theory readers consistently report is that a significant proportion of scan images — particularly those from 11-week scans or from scans taken at non-ideal angles — simply do not show a readable nub. You are not unusual if this is your situation.

## What You Can Do

If you were hoping for a nub theory reading and your current scan doesn't show the nub, you have a few options:

**Wait for the next scan.** If you have a nuchal translucency (NT) scan or an anomaly scan coming up, there may be a better opportunity for a clearer view — though the genital tubercle develops further by 16–20 weeks and nub theory is typically only attempted before 14 weeks.

**Book a private early scan.** Some private clinics in the UK offer early gender scans from around 16 weeks using different techniques, and non-diagnostic gender scans from around 16–20 weeks can often give a clearer result. These are not NHS services and involve a fee, but they are widely available across the UK.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Why can't the sonographer always get a clear nub view?

NHS sonographers are trained to check your baby's health and measure development — capturing a nub-theory-friendly image isn't part of their remit. The baby also needs to be in exactly the right position, lying sideways with legs visible, which doesn't always happen during the few minutes of a scan.

### Can anyone predict the gender if the nub isn't visible?

No — not accurately. If the nub isn't visible, there is no basis for a nub theory prediction. Any "reading" made without a clear nub would be guesswork. Be cautious of online communities or individuals who claim to read a gender from an image where the nub is not clearly shown.

### Does no visible nub mean something is wrong with my baby?

Absolutely not. Not seeing the nub on a scan image is a very normal occurrence and has no bearing on your baby's health or development. It simply means the conditions weren't right for that particular image.

Speak to your midwife or GP if you have any concerns about your scan results.
