# 7 Weeks Pregnant: Your Symptoms

At 7 weeks pregnant, you are firmly in the thick of the first trimester. For many people this is one of the toughest weeks — nausea is often at its worst, exhaustion can feel overwhelming, and you may not yet have told anyone you are pregnant. You may look completely normal on the outside while feeling absolutely terrible on the inside. That is very common and entirely normal.

Your baby is now around the size of a blueberry, with a rapidly developing brain, heart chambers beginning to form, and tiny paddle-shaped limb buds appearing. The pace of development inside the womb is extraordinary, and your body is working hard to support it.

## Common Symptoms at 7 Weeks {#common-symptoms}

At 7 weeks, you may experience some or none of these symptoms. Every pregnancy is different, and the range of normal is wide.

- Fatigue and bone-deep tiredness
- Nausea — with or without vomiting
- Breast tenderness, heaviness, or tingling
- Frequent urination
- Mood swings and emotional sensitivity
- Bloating and digestive changes
- Food cravings or strong aversions
- Heightened sense of smell
- Excess saliva (ptyalism)
- Mild pelvic cramping or stretching sensations
- Headaches
- Dizziness or light-headedness

## What's Normal at 7 Weeks {#whats-normal}

It is completely normal to have some, all, or none of these symptoms. Some people sail through week 7 feeling relatively well; others find it one of the hardest weeks of their lives. Neither experience says anything about how your pregnancy is going.

Having no symptoms at 7 weeks does not mean something is wrong. Nausea and other symptoms are driven by rising hCG (human chorionic gonadotropin) and progesterone levels, and these hormones naturally peak and dip — which is also why symptoms can come and go from one day to the next.

Symptoms fluctuating is also normal. You might feel dreadful on a Monday and much better by Wednesday. This does not indicate a problem.

## Symptoms in Detail {#symptoms-in-detail}

**Exhaustion**: The fatigue at 7 weeks can be unlike anything you have felt before. Your body is producing the placenta, pumping more blood, and running on altered hormone levels around the clock. Rest when you can and do not feel guilty about it.

**Nausea**: Often called morning sickness, it can strike at any time of day or night. Eating small amounts frequently — before you feel hungry — can help. Plain, starchy foods such as crackers, toast, or plain rice are often better tolerated.

**Breast changes**: Rising progesterone and oestrogen cause breast tissue to grow and become more sensitive. Your bra may already feel uncomfortable. A well-fitted, supportive bra (including at night if needed) can help.

**Frequent urination**: Your kidneys are processing more fluid and your uterus is beginning to press on your bladder. This will ease in the second trimester as the uterus rises out of the pelvis.

**Heightened smell**: Many people find that smells they previously enjoyed — coffee, meat, perfume — become overwhelming at 7 weeks. This is thought to be linked to oestrogen and usually improves by the second trimester.

**Mood swings**: Hormonal shifts combined with fatigue, anxiety, and physical discomfort make emotional ups and downs very common. Be kind to yourself.

## When to Contact Your Midwife or GP {#when-to-worry}

Most symptoms at 7 weeks are normal and do not require urgent medical attention. However, contact your GP or midwife promptly — or call NHS 111 — if you experience:

- Heavy bleeding (soaking a pad) or passing clots
- Severe one-sided abdominal or pelvic pain (which can be a sign of ectopic pregnancy)
- A high temperature (above 38°C)
- Inability to keep any fluids down for 24 hours or more — this may indicate hyperemesis gravidarum, which can require treatment
- Sudden disappearance of all pregnancy symptoms, especially after previously seeing a heartbeat on a scan

Call 999 or go to A&E immediately if you have severe abdominal pain with shoulder-tip pain or feel faint, as these can be signs of a ruptured ectopic pregnancy.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Is it normal to have no symptoms at 7 weeks?

Yes, many people have no noticeable symptoms in early pregnancy and go on to have a perfectly healthy pregnancy. Symptoms are driven by hormones, and hormone levels vary considerably from person to person. The absence of symptoms is not a reliable indicator of how your pregnancy is progressing.

### When will my symptoms ease?

For most people, nausea and extreme fatigue begin to ease between weeks 12 and 14 as the first trimester ends and the placenta takes over hormone production. Some people find relief earlier; for others symptoms persist into the second trimester. If nausea is significantly affecting your ability to eat and drink, speak to your GP — there are safe treatments available.

### Should I be worried about cramping at 7 weeks?

Mild, period-like cramping or stretching sensations in the lower abdomen are very common at 7 weeks as your uterus grows and ligaments stretch. This type of cramping is usually normal. However, severe pain — particularly one-sided pain — or cramping accompanied by heavy bleeding should be assessed urgently. If you are unsure, call NHS 111.
