# 4 Weeks Pregnant: Your Symptoms

At 4 weeks pregnant you may not even know you're pregnant yet. Your period is due around now, and the embryo is in the process of implanting into the lining of your uterus. Some people have noticeable symptoms already; others feel completely normal and will not realise they are pregnant until a positive test a few days later. Both experiences are entirely valid.

## Common Symptoms at 4 Weeks {#common-symptoms}

At 4 weeks, you may experience some or none of these symptoms. Every pregnancy is different, and the presence or absence of symptoms tells you very little about how the pregnancy is going.

- **Fatigue and tiredness** — rising progesterone can make you feel more tired than usual, sometimes profoundly so
- **Nausea** — some people notice queasiness from as early as 4 weeks, though it more commonly begins around 6 weeks
- **Breast tenderness** — your breasts may feel sore, heavy, or more sensitive than usual
- **Frequent urination** — increased blood flow to the kidneys means you may need the loo more often
- **Mood swings** — hormonal shifts can make you feel more emotional or irritable
- **Bloating** — progesterone slows digestion, causing a feeling of fullness or bloating
- **Food cravings or aversions** — some people notice changes in their appetite or strong reactions to certain smells
- **Heightened sense of smell** — foods or scents that never bothered you before may suddenly feel overwhelming
- **Mild cramping** — light, period-like cramps can occur as the embryo implants
- **Light spotting (implantation bleeding)** — a small amount of pinkish or brownish spotting around the time your period is due can be implantation bleeding, which is harmless

## What Is Normal at 4 Weeks {#whats-normal}

It is completely normal to have some, all, or none of these symptoms at 4 weeks. Many people have no noticeable symptoms at this stage and only discover they are pregnant because their period does not arrive. Having no symptoms does not mean something is wrong — in fact, symptoms often do not ramp up until hCG levels are higher, typically around 6 to 8 weeks.

Symptoms can also come and go throughout the day. Feeling nauseous in the morning but fine by the afternoon, or noticing breast tenderness that seems to vary, is normal. Hormones fluctuate, and so do the sensations they cause.

Implantation bleeding — if it happens — is typically very light, lasting one to three days, and lighter in colour than a normal period. It can be easy to mistake for an early or light period, which is one reason why some pregnancies are not identified straight away.

## When to Seek Help {#when-to-worry}

Most symptoms at 4 weeks are mild and do not need medical attention. However, contact your GP or midwife if you experience:

- **Heavy bleeding** — soaking a pad or passing clots is not normal at this stage and should always be assessed
- **Severe abdominal pain** — particularly if it is one-sided and persistent, which could rarely indicate an ectopic pregnancy
- **Fever above 38°C** — a true fever in early pregnancy should always be reviewed by a doctor
- **Inability to keep fluids down** — if vomiting is severe and you cannot stay hydrated, seek help
- **Shoulder-tip pain** combined with abdominal pain or bleeding — this can be a sign of internal bleeding and is a medical emergency; call 999

If you have pain and bleeding together, contact your GP, call 111, or attend your nearest Early Pregnancy Unit (EPU) for assessment.

## Registering Your Pregnancy

If you have had a positive test, now is a good time to register with your GP to begin antenatal care. In the UK, your booking appointment with a midwife usually takes place between 8 and 12 weeks. There is no need to rush, but getting the referral in place early means you will be seen in good time.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Is it normal to have no symptoms at 4 weeks?

Yes. Many people have no noticeable symptoms at 4 weeks pregnant. This does not mean anything is wrong. Pregnancy hormones are still building at this stage, and for many people symptoms do not become obvious until 6 to 8 weeks. A positive pregnancy test is a more reliable indicator than symptoms alone.

### When will my symptoms ease?

Most people find that early symptoms — particularly nausea and fatigue — begin to ease between weeks 12 and 14 as the first trimester ends. For some this happens earlier, for others it takes a little longer. If symptoms are severe and affecting your daily life before then, speak to your midwife or GP.

### Should I be worried about cramping at 4 weeks?

Mild, period-like cramping at 4 weeks is common and is often caused by implantation. It is not usually a cause for concern. If cramping is severe, one-sided, or accompanied by heavy bleeding, contact your GP or call 111 for advice.
