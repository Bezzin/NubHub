# 13 Weeks Pregnant: Your Symptoms

At 13 weeks pregnant you are at the very end of the first trimester — and for many people, this week marks a genuine turning point. The relentless nausea that defined the past few weeks may begin to ease. Your energy may start to return. Many people find week 13 feels like emerging from a fog.

Your baby is now around the size of a peach, with all their major organs in place and fingers and toes fully separated. The placenta is taking over hormone production from the corpus luteum, which is one reason why symptoms often shift around this stage.

You may also have had — or be about to have — your dating scan or nuchal translucency (NT) scan, which is a major milestone for many families.

## Common Symptoms at 13 Weeks {#common-symptoms}

At 13 weeks you may still be experiencing first trimester symptoms, though they are often beginning to ease. Every pregnancy is different.

- Reducing nausea for many (though not all) people
- Returning energy levels
- Breast tenderness — often less intense than earlier weeks
- A visible or feeling bump beginning to appear
- Increased appetite as nausea fades
- Bloating and wind
- Mild round ligament pain as the uterus grows
- Constipation
- Headaches
- Vivid dreams
- Increased vaginal discharge (leukorrhea)

## What's Normal at 13 Weeks {#whats-normal}

Week 13 sits right at the transition between the first and second trimester, so what is "normal" covers a wide range of experiences. Some people feel dramatically better this week; others find their symptoms persist for several more weeks. Both are normal.

It is also completely normal to still feel anxious at 13 weeks, even if your scan went well. The transition out of the first trimester does not automatically mean the anxiety lifts — give yourself time.

If your nausea has not eased by week 13, do not assume something is wrong. A portion of people experience nausea throughout pregnancy. If it is severe enough to prevent you eating and drinking adequately, speak to your GP — there are treatments available.

Having no symptoms at 13 weeks is also normal. This does not indicate that your pregnancy has stopped progressing.

## Symptoms in Detail {#symptoms-in-detail}

**Improving energy**: As the placenta takes over from the corpus luteum, progesterone levels stabilise and many people feel noticeably less exhausted. You may feel almost like yourself again — though growing a baby remains hard work.

**Increased appetite**: With nausea easing, many people discover they are suddenly hungry again. You do not need to "eat for two" — the NHS advises that you do not need any extra calories in the first trimester and only a modest increase in the third. Focus on balanced, nutritious meals.

**Round ligament pain**: You may notice twinges or brief sharp pains on one or both sides of your lower abdomen, particularly when you move quickly, sneeze, or cough. This is caused by the ligaments supporting your growing uterus stretching. It is normal and harmless.

**Visible bump**: Depending on your body shape, how many pregnancies you have had, and how your uterus is positioned, you may be starting to show. Some people have a noticeable bump at 13 weeks; others will not show until much later. Both are normal.

**Vaginal discharge**: An increase in milky white or clear discharge is very common throughout pregnancy. As long as it has no strong odour and is not accompanied by itching, soreness, or unusual colour, this is a normal pregnancy change. If you have any of those additional symptoms, mention it to your midwife as it could indicate thrush or another infection that can be safely treated in pregnancy.

**Constipation**: Progesterone relaxes smooth muscle throughout the body, which slows digestion. Drinking plenty of water, eating foods high in fibre, and taking gentle exercise can all help. Speak to your GP before taking any laxatives during pregnancy.

## New Symptoms That Can Appear at 13 Weeks {#new-symptoms}

As you move into the second trimester, some new symptoms may emerge:

- **Skin changes**: Hormonal shifts can cause changes in pigmentation — including linea nigra (a dark line down the abdomen) and melasma (dark patches on the face). These are harmless and usually fade after birth.
- **Nasal congestion**: Increased blood volume can cause the mucous membranes in your nose to swell, leading to a blocked nose or nosebleeds. This is called pregnancy rhinitis and is harmless.
- **Gum sensitivity**: Hormones make gum tissue more sensitive. Bleeding gums when brushing are common in pregnancy. Keep up with dental care — NHS dental treatment is free during pregnancy and for 12 months after birth.

## When to Contact Your Midwife or GP {#when-to-worry}

Contact your midwife or GP, or call NHS 111, if you experience:

- Heavy bleeding (soaking a pad) or passing clots
- Severe abdominal pain or one-sided pain
- A high temperature (above 38°C)
- Inability to keep fluids down for 24 hours or more
- Burning or pain when urinating (which may indicate a urinary tract infection — these are common in pregnancy and need treatment)
- Unusual vaginal discharge with odour, itching, or discolouration

Call 999 or go to A&E immediately for severe abdominal pain with dizziness, fainting, or shoulder-tip pain.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Is it normal to have no symptoms at 13 weeks?

Yes. Many people find their symptoms ease considerably by 13 weeks, and some have very few symptoms from early on. This does not indicate that anything is wrong. If you have had a reassuring scan recently, this is additional grounds for reassurance — though it is also normal to feel anxious regardless.

### When will my symptoms ease?

Most people find that nausea and first trimester fatigue begin to ease between weeks 12 and 16. If you are at 13 weeks and still feeling unwell, hang in there — relief is usually not far off. If nausea is severe, speak to your GP, as safe treatment options exist.

### Should I be worried about cramping at 13 weeks?

Mild cramping or stretching sensations at 13 weeks are often caused by round ligament pain as the uterus grows, or simply by normal uterine stretching. This type of cramping is usually brief and settles on its own. Severe pain, persistent cramping, or cramping accompanied by heavy bleeding should always be assessed by a healthcare professional. Call NHS 111 if you are unsure.
