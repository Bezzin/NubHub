# 5 Weeks Pregnant: Your FAQs Answered

Five weeks pregnant is still very early days — you've likely just had a positive pregnancy test, and a mixture of excitement and anxiety is completely normal. At this stage, common questions include: when do I see a midwife? Is cramping normal? What if I have no symptoms? Can I still exercise? This page answers the questions that come up most often at week five.

## What's Happening at 5 Weeks?

Your baby is tiny — roughly the size of a sesame seed. The embryo is forming into three distinct layers that will become all of its organs, bones, muscles, and skin. The heart is beginning to develop and may already be flickering, though it is usually too early to see on an ultrasound scan.

Meanwhile, your body is producing large amounts of hCG (the pregnancy hormone detected by tests), which is what causes early pregnancy symptoms. Progesterone is also rising, which helps maintain the pregnancy and often causes tiredness and breast tenderness.

## Overview

Here is a quick summary of what to do at 5 weeks:

- Contact your GP to start the process of booking your first midwife appointment
- Make sure you are taking folic acid (400 mcg daily) — if you haven't started already, start now
- Avoid alcohol, limit caffeine to under 200 mg per day, and be cautious about foods to avoid in pregnancy (see the NHS guidance)
- Rest when you need to — fatigue is real and early
- Mild cramping and breast tenderness are normal
- You can continue moderate exercise

## When Should I Book My First Midwife Appointment?

Contact your GP as soon as you have a positive test. Your GP will refer you to midwifery care. The first formal midwife appointment — called the 'booking appointment' — usually happens at around 8 to 10 weeks. It is a longer appointment (often an hour or more) where your midwife takes a full medical history, arranges blood tests, and discusses your care pathway.

You don't need to wait until 8 weeks to contact your GP. The earlier you register, the sooner things can be organised. If you have any medical conditions (such as diabetes, epilepsy, or a blood pressure condition), it's particularly important to be in touch quickly so your care can be tailored.

## What Symptoms Are Normal at 5 Weeks?

Symptoms vary hugely between people. Some women feel very rough from week 5; others feel nothing at all. Both are normal. Common symptoms at 5 weeks include:

- **Breast tenderness or soreness** — one of the earliest and most consistent signs
- **Mild cramping or a pulling sensation** — your uterus is beginning to grow and the ligaments around it are stretching
- **Fatigue** — often significant, even exhausting, in early pregnancy
- **Nausea** — may start as early as 4–5 weeks, though for many people it peaks later
- **Frequent urination** — hormonal changes affect the kidneys
- **Bloating** — progesterone relaxes smooth muscle, slowing digestion
- **Emotional changes** — mood swings, tearfulness, or heightened anxiety are all common

Having no symptoms at 5 weeks is also completely normal. Symptom presence and intensity don't reliably indicate how your pregnancy is progressing.

## Is Cramping Normal at 5 Weeks?

Mild, period-like cramping is normal at 5 weeks. Your uterus is growing, and implantation (if it hasn't fully completed) can also cause some cramping and light spotting. This is sometimes called implantation bleeding and is usually pink or brown rather than red.

However, you should contact your GP or call 111 if you experience:
- Cramping that is severe or getting worse
- Cramping accompanied by heavy or bright red bleeding
- One-sided pain, especially with dizziness or shoulder tip pain (which can be a sign of ectopic pregnancy)

If you have severe one-sided pain with dizziness or fainting, **call 999 or go to A&E immediately**, as these can be signs of a ruptured ectopic pregnancy, which is a medical emergency.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### When should I book my first midwife appointment?

Contact your GP as soon as you have a positive test. They'll refer you to midwifery care, and your booking appointment will typically be arranged for around 8–10 weeks. You don't need to wait — the sooner you register, the sooner your care is in place.

### Is cramping normal at 5 weeks?

Mild cramping is normal — your uterus is starting to grow and your ligaments are stretching. Severe pain, cramping with heavy or bright red bleeding, or one-sided pain with dizziness all need prompt medical attention. If you're ever unsure, call 111 for guidance.

### What if I have no symptoms at 5 weeks?

Having no symptoms at 5 weeks is completely normal. Symptoms vary enormously between pregnancies and between people. The absence of nausea, breast tenderness, or fatigue at this stage does not mean anything is wrong. Many healthy pregnancies have very few early symptoms.
