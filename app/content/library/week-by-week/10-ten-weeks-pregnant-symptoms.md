# 10 Weeks Pregnant: Your Symptoms

At 10 weeks pregnant, your baby has just crossed an important milestone — they are now officially called a foetus rather than an embryo, and all of the major organs and body systems are formed, even if they are still very small and developing fast. You are two-thirds of the way through the first trimester, and for many women this week brings a mix of ongoing symptoms alongside the first hints that the most intense phase may be beginning to ease.

## Common Symptoms at 10 Weeks {#common-symptoms}

Every pregnancy is different, and you may experience some, all, or none of these symptoms. Their presence or absence does not indicate how well the pregnancy is progressing.

- **Nausea and morning sickness** — still very common at 10 weeks, though some women notice a slight easing. For others it remains at its peak. Nausea can strike at any time of day despite the name.
- **Fatigue** — growing a baby is hard work. The hormone progesterone has a sedating effect, and your blood volume is increasing rapidly, which can leave you feeling exhausted even after a full night's sleep.
- **Breast tenderness and fullness** — breasts may feel heavier, more sensitive, and visibly larger as they begin preparing for feeding.
- **Frequent urination** — your kidneys are working harder and your growing uterus is beginning to press on your bladder.
- **Bloating and digestive changes** — progesterone slows digestion, which can cause bloating, constipation, and trapped wind.
- **Food cravings and aversions** — strong likes and dislikes are very common in the first trimester; aversions often ease as the weeks go on.
- **Heightened sense of smell** — many women find certain smells overwhelming at this stage, which can contribute to nausea.
- **Mood swings** — hormonal fluctuations affect mood, and anxiety about the pregnancy is also very common before the 12-week scan.
- **Mild cramping and round ligament discomfort** — as your uterus grows, you may feel occasional pulling or stretching sensations low in the abdomen.
- **Visible veins** — blue veins may become more prominent on your breasts and abdomen as blood supply increases.

## Your Baby at 10 Weeks {#your-baby}

At 10 weeks your baby is roughly the size of a strawberry. They have a recognisable human form — a head, tiny arms and legs with fingers and toes, and all major internal organs in place and beginning to function. The heart has been beating for several weeks now. Bones are starting to harden from soft cartilage, and the face is becoming more defined. Tiny fingernails and hair follicles are forming.

## What's Normal at 10 Weeks {#whats-normal}

It is completely normal at 10 weeks to:

- Feel very sick some days and almost fine on others — symptoms fluctuate with hormone levels
- Have no visible bump yet, or just a hint of bloating rather than a genuine pregnancy bump
- Feel anxious, particularly if you are waiting for your 12-week scan
- Experience different symptoms in this pregnancy compared with a previous one
- Notice your symptoms feel slightly different from week to week

It is also normal to have very few symptoms at 10 weeks. A quiet week does not mean something is wrong. Symptoms tend to ease naturally as the placenta takes over hormone production later in the first trimester.

## When to Contact Your Midwife or GP {#when-to-worry}

Most symptoms at 10 weeks are normal pregnancy experiences, but contact your midwife, GP, or maternity triage promptly if you notice:

- Heavy vaginal bleeding (more than light spotting)
- Severe abdominal pain or one-sided pain that does not ease
- A fever above 38°C
- Inability to keep any fluids down for 24 hours or more (a condition called hyperemesis gravidarum can cause dangerous dehydration and needs treatment)
- A sudden complete disappearance of symptoms that previously felt strong, especially if accompanied by any bleeding

If you are ever in doubt about a symptom, call your midwife or NHS 111. It is always better to check.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Is it normal to have no symptoms at 10 weeks pregnant?

Yes. Some women have very few or no noticeable symptoms throughout the first trimester and go on to have completely healthy pregnancies. The presence and severity of symptoms depends on hormone levels, which vary from person to person and from pregnancy to pregnancy. If you have had a scan confirming a heartbeat and have no bleeding or pain, there is no reason to worry about a lack of symptoms.

### When will morning sickness ease?

For most women, nausea and vomiting begin to improve between weeks 12 and 16, as the placenta takes over from the corpus luteum in producing hormones and levels begin to stabilise. Some women feel better much sooner; a smaller number continue to experience nausea into the second trimester. If sickness is severe and stopping you eating or drinking, speak to your GP — effective treatments are available.

### Should I be worried about cramping at 10 weeks?

Mild, occasional cramping is very common in early pregnancy and is usually caused by the uterus growing and stretching, or by round ligament discomfort. It is not normally a cause for concern on its own. However, severe cramping, cramping that gets progressively worse, or cramping accompanied by heavy bleeding or one-sided pain should always be assessed by a medical professional promptly.

Speak to your midwife at your booking appointment if you have any questions about what to expect this week — they can reassure you and make sure everything is on track for your 12-week scan.
