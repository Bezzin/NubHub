# Compression Socks for Pregnancy Travel

Yes — for most pregnant travellers, graduated compression socks are a simple, safe way to reduce swelling and lower the risk of a blood clot (deep vein thrombosis, or DVT) on longer journeys. Pregnancy naturally makes your blood more likely to clot, and sitting still for hours adds to that risk, so compression socks are a sensible precaution. This guide explains when you actually need them, how to choose a pair, and when to check with your midwife first.

## Do You Need Them? {#s1}

Pregnancy increases your risk of DVT compared with not being pregnant, and long periods of sitting still make blood pool in your legs. For long-haul flights, and especially journeys of around four hours or more, the NHS suggests wearing graduated compression (flight) socks to help keep your blood moving. They are also worth wearing on long car, coach or train trips where you can't move around freely.

Compression socks work by gently squeezing your lower legs more tightly at the ankle than at the calf, which encourages blood to flow back up towards your heart rather than collecting in your feet and ankles. As a bonus, many people find they ease the puffy, achy, heavy-leg feeling that is common in pregnancy.

Even with compression socks on, keep doing the basics on a long journey: move your feet and ankles regularly, stand up and walk about when it is safe to, and drink plenty of water. If you have had a blood clot before, have a clotting disorder, or your midwife has told you that you are higher risk, talk to your midwife or GP before you travel — you may need additional advice or treatment rather than socks alone.

## Choosing Socks {#s2}

Not all "compression" socks are the same, so it is worth choosing carefully:

- **Graduated, medical-grade compression** — look for socks designed for travel or flights, sometimes labelled around 15–20 mmHg. Graduated means firmer at the ankle and looser towards the top.
- **The correct size** — measure your ankle and calf and follow the brand's sizing chart. Pregnancy swelling can change your size, so re-measure if needed.
- **Snug but not painful** — they should feel supportive, not so tight that they dig in, leave deep marks, or cut off circulation.
- **Put them on early** — pull them on before you start travelling, ideally first thing while any overnight swelling is at its lowest.
- **Choose an easy fabric** — breathable, seam-friendly socks are more comfortable for hours of wear.

Stop wearing them and seek advice if they cause numbness, tingling, skin colour changes, or pain that doesn't settle.

## When to seek help

Compression socks lower risk but don't remove it. Get urgent medical advice — call 111, or 999 if symptoms are severe — if during or after travel you notice pain, swelling, redness, warmth or tenderness in one leg (often the calf), as these can be signs of a DVT. Seek emergency help (999) for chest pain, breathlessness or coughing up blood, which can signal a clot on the lung.

## FAQ {#faq}

### Are compression socks safe in pregnancy?

Yes. Graduated compression socks are widely recommended for long-distance travel in pregnancy. They help reduce swelling and lower the risk of a blood clot, and they don't harm your baby. Choose a properly fitted, graduated pair (often around 15–20 mmHg).

### How long should I wear them when travelling?

Put them on before you set off and keep them on for the whole journey, including any long waits. On very long trips you can keep them on afterwards if your legs feel swollen, but take them off to sleep unless your midwife or doctor has advised otherwise.

### Do I still need to move around if I wear them?

Yes. Socks work best combined with regular movement — flex your ankles, walk about when it is safe, and stay hydrated. Compression alone is not a substitute for keeping active on a long journey.

If you are higher risk or unsure, speak to your midwife or GP for advice tailored to your pregnancy before you travel.
