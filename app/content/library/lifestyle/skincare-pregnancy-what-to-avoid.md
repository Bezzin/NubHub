# Skincare in Pregnancy: What to Avoid

## Why Skincare Matters in Pregnancy {#section-1}

Your skin changes significantly during pregnancy due to hormonal shifts, increased blood volume, and stretching as your bump grows. Many women develop pigmentation changes, stretch marks, acne, or dryness. While a good skincare routine can help you feel comfortable, some common ingredients are not recommended during pregnancy because they may be absorbed through the skin and affect your baby.

## Ingredients to Avoid {#section-2}

Retinoids, including retinol and prescription retinoids, are generally avoided in pregnancy due to a theoretical risk of birth defects. High-dose salicylic acid, particularly in chemical peels or leave-on products, should also be avoided. Hydroquinone, used for skin lightening, has limited safety data and is best avoided. Phthalates and parabens, while not strictly prohibited, are worth minimising as a precaution. Always check labels and ask your pharmacist if you are unsure.

## Safer Alternatives {#section-3}

Plenty of pregnancy-safe skincare ingredients can keep your skin healthy. Vitamin C is a good alternative for brightening and is considered safe. Hyaluronic acid provides hydration without concern. Glycerin, ceramides, and niacinamide are all gentle and effective. For acne, azelaic acid is generally considered safe in pregnancy and can help with both breakouts and pigmentation. Sunscreen is essential, as pregnancy hormones can worsen pigmentation, so choose a broad-spectrum SPF daily.

## Common Pregnancy Skin Changes {#section-4}

Melasma, sometimes called the mask of pregnancy, causes brown patches on the face and is worsened by sun exposure. Stretch marks are common as your skin stretches, and while creams cannot prevent them, keeping skin moisturised can reduce itching. Pregnancy acne is also common due to increased oil production. Most skin changes fade after birth, but a gentle, consistent routine can help manage symptoms in the meantime.

## FAQ {#faq}

### Can I use retinol while pregnant?

Retinoids, including retinol, are generally avoided during pregnancy due to a theoretical risk to the baby. Switch to vitamin C or niacinamide instead.

### Is salicylic acid safe in pregnancy?

Low concentrations in wash-off products are usually considered fine, but high-dose or leave-on salicylic acid should be avoided. Ask your pharmacist for advice.

### What can I use for pregnancy acne?

Azelaic acid and glycolic acid are generally considered safe options for pregnancy acne. Avoid retinoids and high-dose salicylic acid.
