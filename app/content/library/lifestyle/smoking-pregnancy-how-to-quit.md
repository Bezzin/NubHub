# Smoking in Pregnancy: How to Quit

This article explores a practical, supportive guide to quitting smoking in pregnancy, including the risks, NHS support, nicotine replacement, and staying smoke-free. We cover what you need to know in plain language.

## Why Quitting Matters {#section-1}

Smoking in pregnancy is one of the most modifiable risk factors for complications. It raises the chance of miscarriage, ectopic pregnancy, premature birth, low birth weight, and stillbirth, and is linked to sudden infant death syndrome and respiratory problems in childhood. The carbon monoxide in smoke reduces oxygen reaching your baby, and many of the thousands of chemicals in cigarettes cross the placenta. The good news is that quitting at any stage helps: stopping before 15 weeks brings the biggest benefits, but quitting later still improves outcomes. Even cutting down helps, though quitting is the goal. Many women feel guilty or judged when asked about smoking, but clinicians ask to offer help, not to criticise. The NHS is there to support you, and every cigarette avoided is a step forward.

## NHS Support and Nicotine Replacement {#section-2}

The NHS offers free stop-smoking services in pregnancy, often with a specialist advisor who can see you at home or by phone. Ask your midwife for a referral, or search for your local service. Support usually combines behavioural sessions with nicotine replacement therapy, such as patches, gum, or lozenges, which are considered safer than smoking because they avoid the harmful chemicals in smoke. Varenicline is not generally used in pregnancy. Patches may be used, and short-acting forms like gum or lozenges can help with cravings. Vaping is sometimes used as a harm reduction tool, but is not risk-free and should be discussed with your midwife. Set a quit date, plan for cravings, and tell people close to you so they can support you, including by not smoking around you. Many women find that pregnancy itself provides strong motivation.

## FAQ {#faq}

### Is nicotine replacement safe in pregnancy?

Nicotine replacement is considered safer than continuing to smoke because it avoids the harmful chemicals in cigarettes. Patches, gum, and lozenges can be used; discuss the best option with your midwife.

### Does quitting later in pregnancy still help?

Yes. Quitting at any stage improves outcomes. Stopping before 15 weeks brings the biggest benefits, but quitting later still reduces risks of prematurity and low birth weight.

### Will I be judged for smoking in pregnancy?

NHS staff ask about smoking to offer support, not to criticise. Specialist pregnancy stop-smoking services are non-judgemental and focused on helping you quit.
