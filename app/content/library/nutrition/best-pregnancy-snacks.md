# Best Pregnancy Snacks: Healthy Ideas for Mums-to-Be

Snacking in pregnancy is not just allowed — it's encouraged. Eating little and often helps maintain stable blood sugar, manage morning sickness, and keeps you energised. Here are the best pregnancy-friendly snacks, organised by what they offer.

## Why Snacking Matters in Pregnancy {#why}

Snacking in pregnancy serves several purposes:

- **Stabilises blood sugar**: Eating every 2-3 hours prevents dips that cause dizziness, fatigue, and nausea
- **Manages morning sickness**: An empty stomach makes nausea worse. Regular snacking keeps something in your stomach
- **Supports nutrient intake**: Snacks are an opportunity to add more vitamins and minerals to your day
- **Provides sustained energy**: Pregnancy is tiring. Nutritious snacks keep you going
- **Prevents overeating at meals**: If you're not starving at mealtimes, you're less likely to overeat or choose unhealthy options

The key is choosing snacks that are **nutrient-dense** — providing vitamins, minerals, and protein rather than just empty calories.

## Snacks for Morning Sickness {#sickness}

When nausea strikes, these bland, gentle snacks are your friends:

- **Plain crackers** (Ritz, cream crackers, Rich Tea biscuits)
- **Dry toast** with a little butter
- **Plain biscuits** (digestives, oatcakes)
- **Ginger biscuits** or ginger snaps
- **Rice cakes** (plain or with a thin scrape of butter)
- **Plain rice** or plain pasta (if you can face it)
- **Banana** (easy to digest, contains B6)
- **Apple sauce** (plain, no sugar)
- **Boiled new potatoes** (cooled, plain)
- **Melon** (cold and refreshing)
- **Ice lollies** (if you can't face solid food)
- **Plain popcorn** (unsalted, unbuttered)

## High-Iron Snacks {#iron}

These snacks help boost your iron intake:

- **Dried apricots** (a handful, about 5-6) — sweet and iron-rich
- **Pumpkin seeds** (a small handful) — sprinkle with a squeeze of lemon for vitamin C
- **Dark chocolate** (2-3 squares, 70% or higher) — contains iron and is a treat
- **Fortified cereal** (dry, as a handful) — many cereals are fortified with iron
- **Nut butter on wholegrain toast** — peanut or almond butter with iron from the bread
- **Houmous with red pepper sticks** — chickpeas for iron, pepper for vitamin C
- **Trail mix** (dried fruit, nuts, and seeds) — concentrated iron source

## High-Calcium Snacks {#calcium}

These snacks support your baby's bone development:

- **Yoghurt** (plain or fruit, pasteurised) — a pot gives about 200mg calcium
- **Cheese and crackers** (hard cheese like cheddar) — calcium-rich and satisfying
- **Milk** (a glass, or in a smoothie) — the simplest calcium source
- **Fortified plant milk** (oat or soya) — if you're dairy-free
- **Almonds** (a handful, about 20) — 60mg calcium per handful
- **Dried figs** (3-4 figs) — surprisingly calcium-rich
- **Tahini on rice cakes** — sesame seeds are a good calcium source
- **Sardines on toast** (with the bones mashed in) — calcium and protein
- **Cheese and apple slices** — a surprisingly tasty combination
- **Fortified orange juice** (some brands add calcium)

## High-Protein Snacks {#protein}

Protein snacks keep you full and support your baby's growth:

- **Boiled egg** (British Lion Code) — 6g protein, easy to prepare
- **Cheese and crackers** — protein and calcium in one
- **Greek yoghurt** — 17g protein per pot, more than regular yoghurt
- **Houmous and vegetable sticks** — protein from chickpeas
- **Peanut butter on toast** — a pregnancy classic
- **Handful of nuts** (almonds, walnuts, cashews) — protein and healthy fats
- **Edamame beans** (steamed, with a little salt) — 17g protein per cup
- **Cottage cheese** (with pineapple or on a rice cake) — high in protein
- **Lentil dip with pitta** — protein from lentils and grains
- **Tofu cubes** (marinated and baked) — great plant protein
- **Mozzarella and tomato** (pasteurised mozzarella) — protein and calcium
- **Chicken strips** (cold, plain) — lean protein
- **A small tin of tuna** (on crackers or salad) — protein and omega-3 (limit tuna to 4 tins/week)

## Quick On-the-Go Snacks {#on-the-go}

For busy days, these snacks travel well:

- **Banana** — comes in its own packaging, easy to eat anywhere
- **Nuts and seeds** — fill a small container and carry it with you
- **Fruit** (apple, pear, grapes, clementines) — portable and nutritious
- **Cheese and crackers** — pack them separately and assemble on the go
- **Yoghurt tube or pouch** — easy to eat without a spoon
- **Rice cakes** — light, portable, and won't get squished
- **Dried fruit** (apricots, raisins, figs) — concentrated nutrition
- **Granola bar** (check it's pregnancy-safe, no caffeine or herbs)
- **Popcorn** (plain, in a bag) — a whole grain snack
- **Mini cheese portions** (individually wrapped) — handy calcium
- **Smoothie** (in a travel cup) — nutrition in a drink

## Snacks to Avoid {#avoid}

Not all snacks are pregnancy-safe. Avoid:

- **Soft mould-ripened cheese snacks** (brie, camembert) — listeria risk
- **Raw or undercooked meat snacks** (salami, chorizo, prosciutto) — toxoplasmosis risk unless cooked
- **Pâté on crackers** — listeria risk
- **Raw fish snacks** (sushi with raw fish, unless previously frozen)
- **Unpasteurised milk or cheese** — listeria risk
- **Raw egg snacks** (mousse, raw cake batter) — salmonella risk
- **High-sugar snacks** (cakes, biscuits, sweets) — fine in moderation but not nutritious
- **High-salt snacks** (crisps, salted nuts) — can cause fluid retention
- **Caffeinated snacks** (chocolate with high caffeine, energy bars with caffeine)
- **Energy drinks** — caffeine and other stimulants

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### How many snacks should I eat in pregnancy?

Most pregnant women benefit from 2-3 snacks per day, spaced between meals. If you have morning sickness, you may prefer 5-6 small snacks instead of meals. Listen to your body — eat when you're hungry.

### Are crackers OK for morning sickness?

Yes, plain crackers are one of the best morning sickness snacks. Keep some by your bed and eat 2-3 before getting up. Dry, bland, and easy to digest.

### Can I eat chocolate in pregnancy?

Yes, in moderation. Dark chocolate (70%+) actually provides iron and antioxidants. Just be mindful of the caffeine content — a 50g bar of dark chocolate has about 25mg caffeine, which counts towards your 200mg daily limit.
