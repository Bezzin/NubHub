# How Many Extra Calories Do You Need in the First Trimester?

Many women assume pregnancy means eating more from day one, but the first trimester has different rules. This guide explains how many extra calories you actually need in early pregnancy, what key nutrients to prioritise, and what to do if morning sickness is making it difficult to eat at all.

## First Trimester Calorie Needs

In the first trimester, most healthy women do not need any additional calories beyond their usual daily intake. This surprises many people, but your baby in the first twelve weeks is still very small — the energy demands on your body have not yet increased significantly. The widely cited advice to "eat for two" does not apply in early pregnancy and can sometimes lead to unnecessary weight gain that becomes harder to manage later.

Current UK guidance, in line with NHS recommendations, is that extra calories are not needed until the third trimester, when an additional 200 calories per day is suggested for most women. Even that figure is modest — equivalent to a small bowl of porridge or a slice of toast with peanut butter. In the first and second trimesters, no formal calorie increase is recommended for women with a healthy pre-pregnancy weight.

If you feel hungrier than usual, it is entirely reasonable to have a small nutritious snack between meals. Hormonal changes in early pregnancy can genuinely increase appetite, and ignoring real hunger is not helpful. The key is to respond with nutrient-dense choices rather than simply eating more of everything.

## What to Focus on Instead of Calories

Because your calorie needs are unchanged in the first trimester but your nutrient needs are elevated, the quality of what you eat matters far more than the quantity. Your baby's most critical developmental stages — neural tube closure, organ formation, and early cell division — are happening in these early weeks, and they depend on specific nutrients rather than extra energy.

A balanced first-trimester diet should include:

- **Plenty of fruit and vegetables** — aim for a variety of colours to cover a wide range of vitamins and antioxidants
- **Wholegrains** such as oats, wholemeal bread, and brown rice for sustained energy and fibre (helpful against constipation, which is common in early pregnancy)
- **Lean protein** from eggs, poultry, fish (within recommended limits), pulses, and dairy
- **Dairy or fortified alternatives** for calcium, which is essential for your baby's bone development and your own bone health
- **Healthy fats** from avocado, nuts, seeds, and oily fish (up to two portions a week) for the fatty acids that support fetal brain development

Staying well hydrated is equally important. Aim for around six to eight glasses of water or other fluids a day, and more if you are vomiting.

## Key Nutrients in the First Trimester

**Folate (folic acid):** The NHS recommends 400 micrograms of folic acid daily from before conception through to twelve weeks of pregnancy. This helps prevent neural tube defects such as spina bifida. Good food sources include leafy green vegetables, fortified breakfast cereals, and legumes, but a daily supplement is the most reliable way to reach the recommended amount. Women with certain risk factors may be prescribed a higher dose by their GP.

**Vitamin D:** The NHS recommends 10 micrograms of vitamin D daily throughout pregnancy (and breastfeeding). Most people in the UK cannot get enough from sunlight alone, particularly in autumn and winter, so a supplement is advisable. Vitamin D supports calcium absorption and fetal bone development.

**Iron:** Your blood volume increases significantly during pregnancy, raising your iron needs. Good sources include red meat, fortified cereals, beans and lentils, and dark leafy greens. Eating iron-rich foods alongside a source of vitamin C (such as a glass of orange juice) improves absorption.

**Iodine:** Important for fetal thyroid development and brain growth. Found in dairy products, fish, and eggs. Many UK prenatal vitamins include iodine; check yours does.

A good-quality prenatal supplement helps cover the gaps, but it should not replace a varied diet — it works alongside food, not instead of it.

## When Morning Sickness Gets in the Way

For many women, following nutritional advice in the first trimester is easier said than done when nausea and vomiting make eating feel impossible. If this is you, the most important things are:

- Eat whatever you can manage, even if it is plain crackers, toast, or plain rice
- Keep sipping fluids regularly to avoid dehydration — small sips often is better than trying to drink large amounts at once
- Don't force yourself to eat large meals; small, frequent amounts are usually better tolerated
- A prenatal vitamin will help ensure you are not missing critical nutrients even when your diet is restricted

Your baby draws on your body's existing stores for the nutrients it needs, which means short-term dietary restrictions due to morning sickness rarely cause harm. However, if you are unable to keep any food or fluid down for more than 24 hours, or if you are losing weight rapidly, contact your midwife or GP. Severe nausea and vomiting (hyperemesis gravidarum) is a medical condition and needs treatment.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Do I need extra calories in the first trimester?

For most women, no. UK guidance does not recommend any additional calorie intake in the first trimester. Your baby is very small and calorie demands have not yet increased. Focus on eating nutritious foods rather than eating more overall.

### Why am I so hungry in early pregnancy if I do not need extra calories?

Hormonal changes — particularly rising progesterone and hCG — can increase appetite and alter your metabolism in early pregnancy. Responding with a small, nutritious snack is perfectly reasonable. The hunger is real; it just doesn't mean you need to double your portions.

### What if morning sickness makes it hard to eat enough in the first trimester?

Eat small, frequent amounts of whatever you can manage and prioritise staying hydrated. Your baby will draw on your body's nutritional reserves during this period. If you cannot keep fluids down at all, or if symptoms are severe, speak to your midwife or GP — you should not have to manage alone.
