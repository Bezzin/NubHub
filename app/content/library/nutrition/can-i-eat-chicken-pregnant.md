# Can I Eat Chicken When Pregnant?

Yes — chicken is safe to eat during pregnancy, and it is actually one of the most useful foods you can include in your diet. It is an excellent source of protein and B vitamins, both important for your baby's growth. The key rule is simple: chicken must always be cooked thoroughly before you eat it.

## Quick Answer

**Chicken in pregnancy: Yes — cook thoroughly until no pink remains and juices run clear**

Chicken is safe to eat throughout pregnancy provided it is properly cooked. You should not eat chicken that is pink in the middle or has any pink juices. Well-cooked chicken is a nutritious, versatile, and safe protein source for pregnant women.

## Is Chicken Safe in Pregnancy? {#is-it-safe}

Yes — cooked chicken is safe throughout pregnancy. The concern with chicken is bacterial contamination, particularly *Campylobacter* and *Salmonella*. Both bacteria can be present in raw or undercooked chicken and can cause food poisoning, which is more dangerous in pregnancy because your immune system is naturally suppressed.

*Campylobacter* is actually the most common cause of food poisoning in the UK. In pregnancy, a severe episode of food poisoning can cause dehydration and, in rare cases, may lead to complications. This is why thorough cooking is essential — heat destroys these bacteria reliably.

Raw chicken can also carry *Listeria monocytogenes*, though this is less common. Listeria can cross the placenta and pose a risk to the baby, which is why the NHS advises following careful food hygiene around all raw meat.

## NHS Guidance on Chicken in Pregnancy {#nhs-guidance}

The NHS advises that chicken is safe to eat in pregnancy as long as it is cooked all the way through. Specifically:

- There should be no pink meat anywhere in the chicken — this applies to breast, thigh, drumstick, and any minced chicken products
- The juices should run clear when you pierce the thickest part with a skewer or knife
- The meat should be steaming hot throughout

The NHS also recommends:
- Washing your hands thoroughly after handling raw chicken
- Using separate chopping boards and utensils for raw chicken and other foods
- Not washing raw chicken before cooking — this can splash bacteria around your kitchen sink and worktop

Chicken that has been fully cooked and then chilled and reheated is also safe, provided it is piping hot all the way through when served. Do not reheat chicken more than once.

## How to Enjoy Chicken Safely During Pregnancy {#how-to-eat-safely}

Chicken is one of the most versatile and nutritious foods you can eat in pregnancy. Here are some practical tips:

- **Always check the centre.** When cooking chicken pieces or a whole bird, cut into the thickest part to check there is no pink remaining before serving.
- **Use a food thermometer if in doubt.** Chicken is safe to eat when its internal temperature reaches 75°C at the thickest point.
- **Store raw chicken safely.** Keep raw chicken in a sealed container on the bottom shelf of the fridge so it cannot drip onto other foods.
- **Cold cooked chicken is fine.** Chicken that has been properly cooked, cooled, and refrigerated promptly is safe to eat cold — in sandwiches or salads, for example.
- **Pre-packaged cooked chicken is safe.** Supermarket cooked chicken (rotisserie-style, cooked slices) is safe in pregnancy provided it is within its use-by date and has been stored correctly.
- **Avoid pink or bloody chicken in restaurants.** If chicken arrives undercooked at a restaurant, send it back. Do not eat it to avoid causing a fuss.

## Nutritional Benefits of Chicken in Pregnancy

Chicken is a brilliant food during pregnancy for several reasons:

- **Protein.** Protein is essential for your baby's cell growth and tissue development. Chicken is a rich, complete source of protein.
- **Iron.** Chicken contains haem iron, the form most easily absorbed by the body, which supports your increased blood volume during pregnancy.
- **B vitamins.** Chicken is high in B3 (niacin) and B6, which support energy metabolism and your baby's brain development.
- **Choline.** Chicken liver (eaten in strict moderation) is very high in choline, which supports fetal brain development — though liver must be limited in pregnancy due to its high vitamin A content.
- **Zinc.** Zinc supports immune function and is important for your baby's growth.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### What if I accidentally ate undercooked chicken while pregnant?

Try not to panic — a single exposure does not guarantee illness. Monitor yourself for symptoms of food poisoning over the next few days: nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea, stomach cramps, and fever. If you develop a fever above 38°C, bloody diarrhoea, or feel very unwell, contact your GP or call 111 promptly. Mention that you are pregnant so they can assess you appropriately.

### Is cold cooked chicken safe in pregnancy?

Yes — cold cooked chicken is safe in pregnancy provided it was properly cooked and then stored in the fridge promptly. Eat it within two days of cooking and check that it smells fresh. Do not leave cooked chicken at room temperature for more than two hours before refrigerating.

### Is chicken safe to eat in the first trimester?

Yes, the same rules apply throughout pregnancy: cook chicken thoroughly until no pink remains and the juices run clear. In the first trimester, when nausea may be strong, cold or lightly seasoned chicken — in a wrap or salad — can be a palatable way to keep up your protein intake even if strong smells are putting you off cooking.
