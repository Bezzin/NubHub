# What to Eat in the First Trimester

The first trimester (weeks 1-12) is when your baby's organs are forming, making nutrition especially important. But with morning sickness, food aversions, and exhaustion to contend with, eating well can be a challenge. Here's how to nourish yourself and your baby in those crucial early weeks.

## Why the First Trimester Matters {#why}

The first 12 weeks are when your baby's neural tube, brain, spinal cord, heart, and other major organs develop. This is why **folic acid is crucial** in the first trimester — it prevents neural tube defects like spina bifida, which occur in the very early weeks, often before you even know you're pregnant.

Your baby is growing rapidly, even though they're still tiny. By 12 weeks, your baby is about 5-6cm long with a beating heart, forming limbs, and developing organs. All of this requires nutrients from you.

However, you don't need extra calories in the first trimester. Your baby is small enough that your normal calorie intake (around 2,000 per day) is sufficient. Focus on the quality of your food, not the quantity.

## Key Nutrients for the First Trimester {#nutrients}

### Folic Acid — The Most Important
- **Why**: Prevents neural tube defects (spina bifida, anencephaly)
- **How much**: 400mcg supplement daily until 12 weeks (5mg if high risk)
- **Food sources**: Spinach, kale, broccoli, fortified cereals, lentils, chickpeas, beans
- **Tip**: Take your supplement with food to reduce nausea

### Iron
- **Why**: Your blood volume increases in pregnancy; iron makes haemoglobin to carry oxygen
- **How much**: 14.8mg per day
- **Food sources**: Lean red meat, dark leafy greens, beans, fortified cereals, dried apricots
- **Tip**: Eat iron-rich foods with vitamin C (like orange juice) to boost absorption. Avoid tea and coffee with iron-rich meals (they reduce absorption)

### Vitamin D
- **Why**: Helps absorb calcium for your baby's bones; supports your immune system
- **How much**: 10mcg daily supplement throughout pregnancy
- **Food sources**: Oily fish, eggs, fortified foods. (Sunlight is the main source, but UK levels are often low)
- **Tip**: Take it with a meal containing fat for best absorption

### Calcium
- **Why**: Your baby's bones and teeth are developing
- **How much**: 700mg per day
- **Food sources**: Milk, yoghurt, hard cheese, fortified plant milks, leafy greens, tofu, sardines

### Omega-3
- **Why**: Supports brain and eye development
- **Food sources**: Salmon, mackerel, sardines, walnuts, chia seeds, flaxseeds
- **Tip**: Eat 2 portions of fish per week, including 1 oily fish

### Vitamin B6
- **Why**: May help reduce nausea; supports brain development
- **Food sources**: Poultry, fish, potatoes, bananas, nuts, seeds

## Foods to Focus On {#focus}

Try to include these nutrient-rich foods regularly:

- **Leafy green vegetables**: Spinach, kale, broccoli — packed with folate, iron, and calcium
- **Fortified cereals**: Many breakfast cereals are fortified with folic acid and iron — an easy win
- **Lean protein**: Chicken, turkey, eggs, beans, lentils, tofu
- **Oily fish**: Salmon, mackerel, sardines (limit to 2 portions per week)
- **Dairy (pasteurised)**: Milk, yoghurt, hard cheese — for calcium and protein
- **Wholegrains**: Brown rice, wholewheat bread, oats — for fibre and B vitamins
- **Colourful fruit and vegetables**: Berries, citrus, peppers, carrots — for vitamins and antioxidants
- **Nuts and seeds**: Walnuts, almonds, chia seeds, flaxseeds — for healthy fats and minerals

## Foods to Avoid {#avoid}

In the first trimester, avoiding certain foods is especially important because your baby's organs are forming:

- Soft mould-ripened and blue cheeses
- Raw or undercooked meat and fish
- Liver and liver products (too much vitamin A)
- Raw eggs (unless British Lion Code stamped)
- Unpasteurised milk and dairy
- Shark, swordfish, and marlin
- Limit caffeine to 200mg/day
- Avoid alcohol completely

## Managing Morning Sickness Through Diet {#sickness}

Morning sickness affects up to 80% of pregnant women, usually peaking around weeks 8-10. Diet can help manage it:

- **Eat little and often**: Small, frequent meals prevent an empty stomach, which worsens nausea
- **Bland, plain foods**: Crackers, toast, rice, plain biscuits — easy to keep down
- **Ginger**: Ginger tea, ginger biscuits, or ginger capsules can reduce nausea (don't exceed 1g per day)
- **Cold foods**: Often easier than hot foods because they have less smell
- **Stay hydrated**: Sip water throughout the day. Try ice lollies if you can't face drinks
- **Avoid triggers**: Strong smells, spicy foods, fatty foods. Everyone's triggers are different
- **Eat before getting up**: Keep crackers by your bed and eat a few before standing

See our full [morning sickness foods guide](/nutrition/best-foods-morning-sickness) for more tips.

## When Eating Is Hard {#hard}

If morning sickness is severe (hyperemesis gravidarum), you may struggle to eat anything. In this case:

- **Prioritise hydration**: Water is more important than food. Sip constantly.
- **Eat whatever you can keep down**: If only crackers and ginger ale work, that's fine for now.
- **Take your folic acid**: Even if you can't eat, try to take your supplement. If you can't keep it down, ask your GP about alternatives.
- **Talk to your GP**: If you can't keep any food or water down, you may need anti-sickness medication or IV fluids. Don't suffer in silence.
- **Don't worry about nutrition short-term**: Your baby will take what they need from your stores. A few weeks of poor eating won't harm them — getting through the sickness is the priority.

## Sample First Trimester Day {#sample}

Here's an example of a balanced first-trimester day:

- **Breakfast**: Bowl of fortified cereal with milk and a handful of berries + folic acid supplement
- **Mid-morning snack**: A banana and a handful of almonds
- **Lunch**: Chicken and salad sandwich on wholegrain bread, with a glass of orange juice (for vitamin C with iron)
- **Afternoon snack**: A yoghurt and a few crackers (if nausea strikes, just the crackers)
- **Dinner**: Grilled salmon with brown rice and steamed broccoli + vitamin D supplement
- **Evening**: Ginger tea and a piece of fruit

If morning sickness hits, swap to: crackers, toast, plain rice, soup, and cold foods throughout the day.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Do I need extra calories in the first trimester?

No. You don't need extra calories in the first 12 weeks. Your baby is small enough that your normal intake is sufficient. Extra calories are needed from the second trimester onwards.

### What if I can't eat vegetables due to food aversions?

Don't worry. Food aversions are common and temporary. Focus on what you can eat, and try hiding veg in soups or smoothies. Your prenatal vitamins will help fill the gaps. Most aversions ease by the second trimester.

### Should I take folic acid even if I eat lots of folate-rich foods?

Yes. The NHS recommends a 400mcg folic acid supplement regardless of your diet, because it's hard to get enough from food alone, and the synthetic form is more easily absorbed. Continue until 12 weeks.
