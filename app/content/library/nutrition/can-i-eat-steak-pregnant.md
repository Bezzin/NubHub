# Can I Eat Steak When Pregnant?

Can you eat steak when pregnant? The short answer: **Yes — but it must be cooked well-done with no pink in the middle**. A steak craving during pregnancy is perfectly understandable, and you absolutely do not have to give up red meat. You just need to be a little more careful about how it is cooked.

## Quick Answer

**Steak in pregnancy: Yes — well-done only**

Steak is safe to eat during pregnancy provided it is cooked all the way through. There should be no pink or red in the middle, and the juices should run clear. This applies to all cuts — fillet, sirloin, rump, ribeye, and any other variety.

## Why Does Steak Need to Be Well-Done in Pregnancy? {#is-it-safe}

The reason you need to avoid rare and medium-rare steak during pregnancy comes down to two specific food safety risks.

**Toxoplasmosis** is a parasitic infection caused by *Toxoplasma gondii*. The parasite can survive on the surface of meat and — in the case of steaks that are not cooked through — on the inside too. In healthy adults, toxoplasmosis often causes no symptoms at all or a mild flu-like illness. During pregnancy, however, it can in some cases cross the placenta and affect the developing baby, which is why the NHS recommends avoiding undercooked meat entirely.

**E. coli and other bacteria** can also be present in raw or undercooked beef. Thorough cooking destroys harmful bacteria. Whole cuts of beef (like steaks) carry bacteria mainly on the outer surface, but the heat from well-done cooking ensures those are eliminated.

It is worth knowing that burgers, mince, and rolled joints carry a higher risk than whole-cut steaks because the surface bacteria get mixed throughout during processing. These must also be cooked through with no pinkness.

## NHS Guidance on Steak {#nhs-guidance}

The NHS advises that red meat — including beef, lamb, and pork — is safe to eat during pregnancy as long as it is cooked thoroughly. This means no rare or medium-rare options. The meat should have no pink or red in the centre and the juices should run clear when pierced.

The NHS also recommends washing hands thoroughly after handling raw meat and keeping raw meat stored separately from ready-to-eat food in the fridge.

Steak is actually a helpful source of iron and protein during pregnancy. Iron needs increase during pregnancy because your body is producing more blood, and red meat provides haem iron, which is readily absorbed by the body. Eating it alongside vitamin C-rich foods such as a tomato salsa or peppers can further support iron absorption.

## How to Eat Steak Safely {#how-to-eat-safely}

When cooking steak at home or ordering in a restaurant, here is what to keep in mind:

- **Ask for well-done** — no pink in the middle, juices run clear. If you are in a restaurant, it is completely reasonable to specify this.
- **Check it before eating** — cut into the thickest part to confirm no pink remains. If it is still pink, send it back or continue cooking.
- **Use separate utensils** — do not let utensils or plates that touched raw steak come into contact with cooked steak.
- **Store raw meat safely** — keep it in the fridge on the bottom shelf, covered, and use within the use-by date.
- **Reheat leftovers fully** — if eating leftover steak, heat it all the way through until steaming hot throughout.

Steak cooked well-done can still be delicious. Marinating before cooking and resting the meat for a few minutes after cooking helps retain juiciness even at a higher internal temperature.

## What About Steak at Restaurants?

Eating out is perfectly fine — just be clear with your server that you are pregnant and need your steak well-done. Most kitchens will accommodate this without any issue. If you are at a steak house where rare is the norm, do not feel embarrassed to make the request.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### What if I accidentally ate undercooked steak while pregnant?

Try not to worry. The risk of infection from a single exposure is generally low, and most healthy pregnant women who eat undercooked meat on one occasion will not develop toxoplasmosis or any other illness. Watch for symptoms over the following weeks, such as swollen glands, fever, muscle aches, or tiredness. If you develop any of these, or feel concerned, contact your GP or midwife and let them know what happened. They can advise on whether any monitoring is needed.

### Is steak safe in the first trimester?

Yes, the same rules apply throughout all three trimesters. Steak is safe as long as it is cooked well-done. There is no trimester during which the guidance changes — well-done steak is always the safe choice throughout pregnancy.

### Can I eat steak pies or steak in casseroles?

Yes. Steak used in pies, casseroles, stews, and slow-cooked dishes is generally cooked very thoroughly as part of the cooking process. As long as the meat is piping hot all the way through when served, these are safe to eat in pregnancy.
