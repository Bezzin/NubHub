# Choline in Pregnancy: The Underrated Nutrient

Choline is one of the quietest workers in pregnancy nutrition. It rarely gets the attention that folic acid or iron do, yet it plays an important part in your baby's developing brain and nervous system. Because demand for choline rises while you are pregnant, and because many prenatal vitamins contain little or none of it, it is worth knowing where it comes from and how to make sure you are getting enough through your diet.

## Why Choline Matters {#s1}

Choline supports several processes that matter most in early and ongoing pregnancy. It contributes to your baby's brain development and memory pathways, helps with the healthy formation of the neural tube (the early structure that becomes the brain and spinal cord), and is involved in the way cells are built and signals are passed between them. Your own body uses choline too, including in the liver, so pregnancy stretches the supply in two directions.

Your need for choline increases during pregnancy, with a commonly cited target of around 450mg per day, and a little more while breastfeeding. Many people in the UK do not reach this amount, partly because the richest sources are foods some pregnant women eat less of, such as eggs. The good news is that choline is found in everyday foods, so closing the gap is usually about a few sensible choices rather than anything complicated.

## Sources {#s2}

Some of the richest sources of choline are eggs (especially the yolk), meat, poultry, fish, and dairy foods. One egg provides roughly 147mg of choline, which makes eggs one of the easiest ways to top up. Make sure eggs are cooked until both the white and yolk are firm, unless they carry the British Lion mark, in which case runny yolks are considered lower risk in pregnancy.

Plant sources are more modest but still useful, and helpful if you eat little or no animal produce. These include soya beans and other beans, nuts, and some vegetables such as broccoli and Brussels sprouts. If you follow a vegan or vegetarian diet, it is worth paying particular attention to choline and discussing it with your midwife, as reaching the target from plants alone takes more planning.

## When to consider a supplement

Diet is the first place to look, and for many people a varied diet that includes eggs and dairy will cover most of their choline needs. If your prenatal vitamin does not contain choline, you do not necessarily need a separate supplement, but you may want to be more deliberate about choline-rich foods. If you cannot eat eggs, follow a plant-based diet, or are simply unsure whether you are getting enough, ask your midwife or GP before adding a standalone choline supplement, as they can advise on what is appropriate for you.

## FAQ {#faq}

### Does my prenatal vitamin have enough choline?

Many prenatal vitamins contain little or no choline, so it is worth checking the label. If yours does not include it, focus on choline-rich foods such as eggs, dairy, fish, and meat, or ask your midwife whether a separate supplement would suit you.

### Can I get enough choline without eating eggs?

Yes, but it takes more planning. Beans and soya, nuts, dairy, fish, meat, and some vegetables all contribute. If you avoid eggs or follow a plant-based diet, discuss your overall intake with your midwife so you can fill any gaps safely.

### Is too much choline a concern in pregnancy?

For most people eating a normal diet, getting too much choline is not a realistic worry. Very high doses from supplements are not recommended, so if you are considering a standalone product, check the amount and ask a health professional first.

Always speak to your midwife or GP for advice tailored to your own pregnancy.
