# High Dose Folic Acid 5mg: Who Needs It

Most pregnant women take 400mcg of folic acid each day, but some need a much higher 5mg dose. This prescription-strength supplement is given to women with specific risk factors that increase the chance of neural tube defects. In this guide we explain who needs the 5mg dose, why it matters, and how to get it.

## Who Needs the 5mg Dose? {#who-needs}

The NHS recommends a 5mg daily dose of folic acid for women who have a higher risk of a pregnancy affected by a neural tube defect. You may be advised to take 5mg if any of the following apply to you: you have had a previous pregnancy affected by a neural tube defect; you or your partner have a neural tube defect, or there is a family history; you have diabetes (type 1 or type 2); you are taking anti-seizure medication such as carbamazepine or valproate; you have coeliac disease, inflammatory bowel disease, or other conditions affecting nutrient absorption; you have a higher body mass index (BMI of 30 or above); or you have sickle cell anaemia or thalassaemia.

## Why a Higher Dose? {#why-higher}

These conditions either increase the rate at which your body uses folate, reduce how well you absorb it, or independently raise the risk of neural tube defects. A standard 400mcg supplement may not be enough to build sufficient folate stores in these situations. The 5mg dose ensures your body has the folate it needs during those crucial early weeks, when the neural tube is forming.

## How to Get the 5mg Dose {#how-to-get}

Unlike the 400mcg tablets available over the counter in pharmacies and supermarkets, the 5mg dose is prescription-only. Speak to your GP before you start trying to conceive if you know you have a risk factor. They will assess your medical history and prescribe the higher dose if appropriate. If your pregnancy is unplanned, contact your GP as soon as you find out so the prescription can be arranged promptly.

## Safety and Side Effects {#safety}

The 5mg dose is considered safe for the women who need it, and the benefits far outweigh the risks in this group. Some women experience mild nausea or bloating. Rarely, high doses can mask a vitamin B12 deficiency, which is why it should only be taken under medical supervision. Your GP will review your overall health and any other medications before prescribing.

## FAQ {#faq}

### Can I just take several 400mcg tablets to reach 5mg?

No. The 5mg tablet is a single prescription formulation. Taking multiple over-the-counter tablets is not recommended because they contain other ingredients and you would exceed safe limits of additional vitamins. Always use the prescribed dose.

### Should I continue 5mg throughout pregnancy?

Your clinician will advise. Most women stop at 12 weeks, but those with ongoing absorption issues or specific medical conditions may continue. Follow your own care plan.
