# Can I Eat Liver When Pregnant?

Can you eat liver when pregnant? The short answer: **No — avoid liver and liver products during pregnancy**. This includes all types of liver (chicken, beef, lamb, and pork), as well as liver pâté and haggis. The reason is not food poisoning but rather an excess of vitamin A, which in large amounts can cause harm to a developing baby.

## Quick Answer

**Liver in pregnancy: No**

Liver is very high in a form of vitamin A called retinol. Consuming too much retinol during pregnancy is linked to a risk of harm to the developing baby, particularly in the first trimester when organs are forming. The NHS advises pregnant women to avoid all liver and liver products for this reason.

## Why Is Liver Unsafe in Pregnancy? {#is-it-safe}

Unlike most other foods that are restricted in pregnancy due to bacterial risks such as listeria or salmonella, liver is avoided for a completely different reason: vitamin A toxicity.

Vitamin A is an essential nutrient and you do need some during pregnancy. However, there are two forms: retinol (found in animal products) and beta-carotene (found in plant-based foods like carrots and sweet potato). Beta-carotene from food is safe in any amount during pregnancy because the body only converts it to vitamin A as needed. Retinol, on the other hand, can accumulate to levels that are harmful to a developing baby if consumed in excess.

Liver contains very high concentrations of retinol — far more than your body needs on a given day. This is why the NHS advises avoiding it entirely during pregnancy rather than suggesting a small amount is acceptable.

The same caution applies to supplements containing retinol. Check your prenatal vitamin label — most pregnancy-specific vitamins contain beta-carotene rather than retinol precisely because of this concern. Avoid any supplement that lists retinol or fish liver oil (such as cod liver oil) unless specifically advised by your doctor.

## NHS Guidance on Liver {#nhs-guidance}

The NHS advises that you should avoid eating liver and liver products such as liver pâté and liver sausage during pregnancy. Haggis, which contains liver, should also be avoided.

This advice applies throughout pregnancy — not just in the first trimester, though the first trimester is the period of greatest concern as the baby's organs are forming.

There is no known safe amount of liver that is recommended for pregnant women, so the guidance is to avoid it altogether rather than try to limit portions.

## What to Eat Instead {#how-to-eat-safely}

Liver is a nutrient-dense food and is particularly rich in iron and folate, which are important during pregnancy. You can get both of these nutrients safely from other sources:

- **Iron**: Red meat (well-cooked), fortified breakfast cereals, beans and lentils, spinach, and dried apricots are all good sources. Eating iron-rich foods alongside a source of vitamin C (such as a glass of orange juice) helps your body absorb the iron more efficiently.
- **Folate**: Leafy green vegetables, fortified bread and cereals, and chickpeas are good dietary sources. Most pregnant women are also advised to take a folic acid supplement, particularly in the first 12 weeks.
- **Protein**: Any cooked meat, fish, eggs, dairy, legumes, and tofu are safe protein alternatives.

If you have concerns about getting enough iron or other nutrients during pregnancy, your midwife can arrange a blood test to check your levels and advise on any supplements you may need.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### What if I accidentally ate liver while pregnant?

Try not to panic. A single small portion of liver is unlikely to cause harm. The concern with liver is cumulative excess of vitamin A over time, rather than a single exposure creating immediate risk. Avoid eating it again during your pregnancy and mention it to your midwife at your next appointment if you are worried. Do not take any extra supplements without medical advice in the meantime.

### Is liver safe in the first trimester?

No — the NHS advises avoiding liver throughout pregnancy, but the first trimester is the period of greatest concern because this is when the baby's major organs, including the brain and spinal cord, are developing. If you accidentally ate liver before you knew you were pregnant, speak to your midwife for reassurance.

### What about liver-flavoured crisps or products that contain tiny amounts of liver extract?

The NHS guidance focuses on eating liver or liver products directly. Highly processed foods with only a trace of liver flavouring are unlikely to contain meaningful amounts of retinol, but if you are unsure about any specific product, checking with your midwife is always a sensible step.
