# Best Pregnancy Snacks: Nutritious Ideas for Every Trimester

When you are pregnant, hunger can strike at any moment. Choosing the right pregnancy snacks helps you stay energised, manage morning sickness, and provide your growing baby with essential nutrients. This guide covers the best pregnancy snacks that are both delicious and nourishing, whether you are in your first trimester battling nausea or approaching your third trimester with a growing appetite.

## Why Snacking Matters During Pregnancy

Pregnancy increases your body's demand for energy, vitamins, and minerals. Rather than relying solely on three large meals, eating smaller amounts more regularly throughout the day can help stabilise blood sugar, reduce nausea, and prevent the energy dips that are so common in pregnancy — particularly in the first and third trimesters.

The best approach is to aim for snacks that combine protein, complex carbohydrates, and healthy fats. This combination keeps you fuller for longer, supports steady energy levels, and avoids the sugar highs and crashes that can leave you feeling worse. Nutrient-dense options also support your baby's development, supplying folate, iron, calcium, and omega-3 fatty acids during crucial growth stages.

From a practical standpoint, keeping healthy snacks within reach — in your bag, at your desk, or on your bedside table — makes it much easier to resist less nutritious choices when cravings strike unexpectedly.

## Top Pregnancy Snack Ideas by Category

**Protein-rich snacks**
Hard-boiled eggs are one of the most convenient high-protein options and contain choline, which supports fetal brain development. Greek yoghurt (full-fat, pasteurised) with a handful of berries provides protein and calcium. A small portion of cottage cheese on wholegrain crackers or edamame beans straight from the freezer (defrosted or briefly microwaved) are both satisfying and nutritious.

**Fruit and vegetables**
Apple slices with a tablespoon of nut butter (peanut, almond, or cashew) combines natural sugars with protein and healthy fats. Carrot sticks and cucumber with hummus is filling and supplies folate. A small handful of grapes, a sliced banana, or a bowl of berries are all quick options when you need something sweet but wholesome.

**Wholegrains and slow-release carbohydrates**
Oatcakes or wholegrain crackers with a slice of hard cheese (such as cheddar or edam) provide sustained energy. Wholemeal toast with mashed avocado and a sprinkle of seeds is excellent in the second and third trimesters when appetite increases. A small bowl of porridge made with milk can double as a snack and is an outstanding source of iron and calcium.

**Healthy fats and nuts**
A small handful of mixed unsalted nuts — such as walnuts, almonds, or Brazil nuts — provides healthy fats and a range of minerals. Walnuts in particular are a plant-based source of omega-3 fatty acids. Avoid flavoured or heavily salted varieties, which are fine occasionally but shouldn't be an everyday snack.

**Quick smoothies**
Blending a banana, a handful of spinach, some frozen berries, and full-fat milk or a fortified plant alternative creates a nutrient-dense drink in under two minutes. Smoothies are especially useful if nausea makes solid food unappealing.

## Snacks for Specific Pregnancy Challenges

**If you have morning sickness:** Dry crackers, plain rice cakes, ginger biscuits, or plain breadsticks eaten little and often can help settle your stomach, especially first thing in the morning before getting up. Keeping plain crackers on your bedside table to nibble before rising is a trick many midwives recommend.

**If you have heartburn or reflux:** Avoid spicy or acidic snacks. Opt instead for oatcakes, plain yoghurt, or a small portion of nuts. Eating smaller amounts more frequently is more comfortable than large snacks.

**If you have gestational diabetes:** Your midwife or dietitian will give you personalised advice, but generally low-glycaemic snacks such as nuts, cheese, hard-boiled eggs, and vegetables with hummus are better choices than fruit juice, biscuits, or high-sugar foods.

**If you are very tired:** Snacks combining iron-rich foods with vitamin C can help combat pregnancy fatigue. Try fortified wholegrain cereal with a glass of orange juice, or iron-fortified bread with a squeeze of lemon on the side.

## Snacks to Limit or Avoid

Some foods that seem like healthy snack choices carry specific risks in pregnancy. Unpasteurised cheeses (such as some imported soft cheeses) should be avoided because of the risk of listeria. Cold ready-to-eat meats such as pâté and deli slices also carry this risk. High-mercury fish (shark, swordfish, marlin) should not be eaten at all. Liver-based snacks are high in vitamin A and should be avoided. Keep caffeine-containing snacks — including chocolate — within the recommended daily caffeine limit your midwife will advise you on.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How many snacks should I eat during pregnancy?

Most pregnant women benefit from two to three nutrient-rich snacks a day alongside balanced meals. The exact number depends on your appetite, your trimester, and whether you have any conditions such as gestational diabetes. Listen to your body — genuine hunger is a valid guide.

### Are snacks safe in pregnancy if I have gestational diabetes?

Yes, but your choices matter more than usual. Focus on low-glycaemic options such as nuts, cheese, non-starchy vegetables with hummus, and plain yoghurt. Avoid high-sugar snacks, fruit juices, and refined carbohydrates. Always work with your midwife or a registered dietitian for a plan tailored to your glucose levels.

### What snacks help with morning sickness?

Dry crackers, ginger-based biscuits, plain rice cakes, and bland foods eaten little and often are the most widely recommended options. Cold foods tend to have less smell and can be easier to tolerate than hot meals. If you cannot keep any food or fluid down for 24 hours or more, contact your midwife or GP — severe nausea (hyperemesis gravidarum) needs medical assessment.
