# Thyroid Function in Pregnancy: What Is Checked

Why thyroid function matters in pregnancy, what TSH and free T4 results mean, and how thyroid conditions are managed in the UK.

## Why Thyroid Function Is Checked in Pregnancy

Your thyroid gland produces hormones that control metabolism and are vital for your baby brain development, especially in the first 12 weeks before the baby own thyroid works. An underactive thyroid (hypothyroidism) is more common in pregnancy and can increase the risk of miscarriage, preterm birth and developmental issues if untreated. An overactive thyroid (hyperthyroidism) is less common but can also cause complications. The NHS does not screen every pregnant person for thyroid function, but testing is routine if you have symptoms, a history of thyroid disease, or type 1 diabetes.

## Reading Your Thyroid Results

A thyroid function test measures TSH (thyroid stimulating hormone) and free T4. In pregnancy, normal TSH is slightly lower, especially in the first trimester, because hCG stimulates the thyroid. A high TSH with low free T4 suggests hypothyroidism and usually means starting or increasing levothyroxine. A low or suppressed TSH with high free T4 suggests hyperthyroidism and may need referral to an endocrinologist. Your dose of levothyroxine often needs to increase in early pregnancy, so testing is repeated every four to six weeks if you have a known thyroid condition.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Will my thyroid be tested at booking?**

Only if you have symptoms, a personal or family history of thyroid disease, type 1 diabetes, or previous pregnancy loss. Universal thyroid screening is not currently NHS policy, though many private clinics include it.

**Is levothyroxine safe in pregnancy?**

Yes, levothyroxine is a copy of the hormone your body makes naturally and is considered safe in pregnancy. Most people with hypothyroidism need a higher dose in the first trimester.

**Can pregnancy cause thyroid problems to go away after birth?**

Some people develop postpartum thyroiditis, a temporary inflammation of the thyroid after birth. It can cause a brief overactive phase followed by an underactive phase, and usually resolves within a year, though some go on to develop permanent hypothyroidism.
