# 8-Week Ultrasound: What to Expect

An 8-week ultrasound is a common time for a first scan — whether that is an NHS early pregnancy scan, a private reassurance scan, or an early pregnancy unit (EPU) appointment. Knowing what the sonographer is looking for, and what the images actually show, can make the experience much less nerve-wracking.

## What You Will See at 8 Weeks

At 8 weeks, the pregnancy is still very small, but an ultrasound can show a good amount of detail. Here is what to expect to see:

**Gestational sac** — the fluid-filled sac that surrounds and protects the developing baby. At 8 weeks this should be clearly visible and well-defined.

**Yolk sac** — a small, round structure inside the gestational sac that has been providing nutrients to the embryo. It is clearly visible at 8 weeks and is a reassuring sign.

**The embryo (fetal pole)** — at 8 weeks, the embryo typically measures around 16–20 mm from crown to rump. This is small enough to fit on your thumbnail, but on the ultrasound screen the structure is clearly identifiable.

**A visible heartbeat** — at 8 weeks, a flickering heartbeat should be clearly visible. The normal rate at this stage is roughly 150–170 beats per minute, which is much faster than an adult's heart and completely normal. The sonographer may let you hear it too.

**Early limb development** — tiny limb buds — the beginnings of arms and legs — should be visible at 8 weeks, though they look like small bumps rather than distinct limbs at this stage.

**Early facial structures** — the areas that will become the face are forming, though distinct facial features are not visible yet.

## What the Sonographer Checks

The sonographer is not just showing you a picture — they are carrying out a clinical assessment. At an 8-week scan they will:

- **Confirm the pregnancy is intrauterine** — confirming the embryo is growing inside the uterus, not in a fallopian tube (ectopic pregnancy)
- **Confirm a heartbeat** — and note whether the rate is within a normal range
- **Measure the embryo** — the crown-rump length (CRL) measurement is used to confirm or adjust your gestational age and estimated due date
- **Check for multiple pregnancies** — twins or higher-order multiples may be identified at this stage
- **Assess the gestational sac and yolk sac** — their size and appearance provide information about how the pregnancy is progressing

## Transvaginal vs Abdominal Scans

At 8 weeks, the clinic may use a transvaginal (internal) probe, an abdominal probe, or both. A transvaginal scan often gives a clearer image this early, because the uterus is still low in the pelvis and the abdominal wall can reduce image quality. You will usually be told which type is planned before the scan begins, and you can ask questions or raise any concerns.

If a transvaginal scan is offered, it is safe and should not cause any harm to the pregnancy. It involves a small, smooth probe being inserted into the vagina. It can feel uncomfortable but should not be painful. You can decline and opt for abdominal only, though the images may be less clear.

## What the 8-Week Scan Cannot Tell You

An 8-week scan cannot reliably identify structural abnormalities — this is the purpose of the 20-week anomaly scan. It also cannot tell you the sex of the baby. The scan is focused on confirming the pregnancy is progressing and measuring development at this stage.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is an 8-week scan internal?

It depends on the clinic. Some use a transvaginal (internal) scan at 8 weeks for the clearest image; others use abdominal ultrasound. Both are routine and safe. The sonographer will explain the approach before starting, and you can ask questions.

### What is a normal heart rate at 8 weeks?

A normal fetal heart rate at 8 weeks is approximately 150–170 beats per minute. This sounds very fast compared to an adult heart, but it is entirely normal at this stage of development. The rate will gradually settle as the pregnancy progresses.

### What if the sonographer cannot find a heartbeat at 8 weeks?

If no heartbeat is found at 8 weeks, the sonographer and doctor will discuss the findings with you carefully. In some cases a follow-up scan is recommended before a conclusion is drawn. This is an incredibly anxious situation, and the early pregnancy unit team is experienced in supporting you through it, whatever the outcome.

Speak to your midwife or GP for advice personalised to your pregnancy.
