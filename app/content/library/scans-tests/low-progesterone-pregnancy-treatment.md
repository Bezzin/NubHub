# Low Progesterone in Pregnancy: Treatment Options

When low progesterone is treated in UK pregnancy, what supplements are available, and what the evidence says about their use.

## When Low Progesterone Is Treated

Progesterone treatment is not offered for every low reading, because a low level often simply reflects a pregnancy that is already failing rather than causing the failure. However, there are clear situations where treatment is recommended in the UK. The PRISM and Promise trials showed that progesterone pessaries can improve outcomes for people with bleeding in early pregnancy who have a confirmed heartbeat and a history of one or more miscarriages. Treatment is also offered in certain assisted conception pregnancies, and sometimes in people with a short luteal phase or previous preterm birth.

## What Treatment Looks Like in Practice

If you are offered progesterone, it usually comes as a vaginal pessary or capsule (such as cyclogest or utrogestan) taken once or twice daily, sometimes as an intramuscular injection in specific cases. Treatment typically starts as soon as low progesterone or bleeding is identified and continues until 12 to 16 weeks, when the placenta takes over hormone production. Side effects are usually mild and can include vaginal discharge, bloating and breast tenderness. Your team will arrange a follow-up scan to confirm the pregnancy is continuing before extending treatment.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can progesterone save a failing pregnancy?**

If the pregnancy is already failing for chromosomal reasons, progesterone will not save it. Treatment helps in specific situations, such as bleeding with a confirmed heartbeat and a history of miscarriage.

**Are progesterone pessaries safe for the baby?**

Progesterone used in pregnancy is considered safe and has been studied extensively. Large UK trials found no significant harm to babies from progesterone treatment in early pregnancy.

**Do I need a prescription for progesterone in the UK?**

Yes. Progesterone pessaries and injections are prescription-only. Your GP, midwife, fertility clinic, or early pregnancy unit can prescribe them if you meet the clinical criteria.
