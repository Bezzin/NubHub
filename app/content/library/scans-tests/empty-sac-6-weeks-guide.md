# Empty Sac at 6 Weeks: What Happens Next?

Seeing an empty gestational sac on an early scan at six weeks is one of the most anxious moments in early pregnancy. It is frightening, but it does not automatically mean something is wrong. At six weeks, the embryo is tiny — often smaller than a grain of rice — and whether or not it is visible depends heavily on scan equipment, your exact dates, and the position of the sac. This page explains what an empty sac at six weeks really means, what happens next, and when to be concerned.

## Why the Sac Might Be Empty at 6 Weeks

There are several reasons a gestational sac can look empty at this stage:

**Your dates may be slightly off.** A sac can look empty if you are actually closer to five weeks than six. Even a difference of a few days matters enormously at this early stage. If your last period was irregular, or you ovulated later than usual, your true gestational age could be less than you think.

**The fetal pole simply hasn't developed enough to be visible yet.** A fetal pole — the very first signs of the embryo — is typically detectable from around six weeks on a transvaginal scan, but it can sometimes remain hidden until six and a half or seven weeks. Transabdominal scans (over the tummy) are less sensitive than transvaginal scans, so if you had an abdominal scan, this is an especially common reason for a seemingly empty sac.

**Scan equipment and operator experience.** Early pregnancy scanning requires specialist training and high-resolution equipment. A sac that appears empty on a basic machine may show a fetal pole clearly on a higher-resolution scanner at an Early Pregnancy Unit (EPU).

**A blighted ovum (anembryonic pregnancy).** In some cases, the fertilised egg begins to develop a sac but no embryo forms inside it. This is called a blighted ovum or anembryonic pregnancy. Importantly, this can only be diagnosed after one or more follow-up scans — a single empty sac at six weeks is never enough to confirm this on its own.

## What Happens Next

If you have a scan at six weeks and the sac appears empty, the clinician will almost always recommend a repeat scan rather than making any immediate diagnosis. You will typically be asked to return to the Early Pregnancy Unit (EPU) in one to two weeks.

At the follow-up scan — usually at seven or eight weeks — the most common outcomes are:

- A fetal pole and heartbeat are now visible, meaning your pregnancy is progressing normally
- A fetal pole is visible without a heartbeat yet, in which case another scan may be needed
- The sac remains empty and has not grown, or has collapsed, which may lead to a diagnosis of blighted ovum or early pregnancy loss

It is important not to draw conclusions between scans. The waiting period is genuinely difficult, but acting on the basis of a single early scan that showed an empty sac is not advisable.

## If You Are Having Pain or Heavy Bleeding

An empty sac alone does not usually require emergency care. However, if you develop severe one-sided pain, shoulder tip pain, heavy bleeding, or feel faint or dizzy, you should seek immediate help. These symptoms can indicate an ectopic pregnancy, which requires urgent treatment. Call 999 or go to A&E if you have any of these symptoms.

For light spotting or mild cramping without the above, contact your EPU or call 111 for guidance.

## FAQ

### Is an empty sac at 6 weeks normal?

It can be completely normal. At six weeks — especially if your dates are even slightly off — it may simply be too early to see the fetal pole. A follow-up scan at seven to eight weeks usually gives a much clearer picture. No diagnosis should be made from a single empty sac scan at six weeks.

### What is a blighted ovum?

A blighted ovum (also called an anembryonic pregnancy) is when a gestational sac develops but no embryo forms inside it. The sac may grow for a time because pregnancy hormones are still rising, which is why symptoms like nausea can continue. It is only diagnosed after repeat scans confirm there is no embryo developing. It is never diagnosed from a single six-week scan alone.

### Should I go to A&E if my scan showed an empty sac?

Not unless you have symptoms of an ectopic pregnancy — severe one-sided pain, shoulder tip pain, heavy bleeding, or feeling faint. For an empty sac without those symptoms, contact your EPU or midwife and follow their advice on when to return for a repeat scan.

Always speak to your midwife or GP for personal advice about your individual scan findings.
