# Can Children Come to the 12-Week Scan?

If you already have children, you may want to bring them along to your 12-week scan so they can see their new sibling on the screen. But whether this is allowed depends entirely on your hospital's policy.

## Do Hospitals Allow Children? {#allow}

There's no single national policy on children at scans — every NHS trust sets its own rules. Broadly, hospitals fall into three categories:

1. **Children welcome**: Some hospitals happily allow children of any age into the scan room, especially if an adult is there to look after them.
2. **Children welcome with supervision**: Some hospitals allow children only if you bring another adult (not the partner already watching the scan) to supervise them. This is because the sonographer needs you to stay still and can't have you chasing a toddler around.
3. **No children**: Some hospitals don't allow children in the scan room at all, due to space, safety, and distraction concerns.

You'll need to check your specific hospital's policy. The easiest way is to ring the ultrasound department or ask your midwife.

## Why Some Hospitals Say No {#why-no}

The restrictions aren't there to be unkind. There are good reasons behind them:

- **Distraction**: The sonographer is performing a clinical examination. They're measuring your baby, checking development, and screening for chromosomal conditions. A noisy or restless child can break their concentration, potentially causing them to miss something.
- **Space**: Scan rooms are small. Between the ultrasound machine, couch, and sonographer, there isn't always room for a pushchair or an extra small person.
- **Safety**: Ultrasound rooms contain expensive equipment and trailing cables. Young children could hurt themselves or damage equipment.
- **Emotional sensitivity**: If the sonographer finds something concerning, they need to discuss it with you privately. Having children present can make difficult conversations harder.
- **Other patients**: The ultrasound department serves other patients too — some may be having scans for worrying reasons. Having children running around isn't appropriate for everyone.

## How to Find Out Your Hospital's Policy {#check}

Don't wait until the day of the scan to find out. Here's how to check:

- **Ask your midwife** at your booking appointment — they'll usually know the local policy.
- **Ring the ultrasound department** directly — the number is often on your appointment letter.
- **Check the hospital's website** — some trusts publish their scan policies online.
- **Look at your appointment letter** — some include information about who can attend.

If the policy is unclear, assume children aren't allowed and arrange childcare. It's better to be safe than turned away on the day.

## Preparing Your Child for the Scan {#prepare}

If your hospital does allow children and you decide to bring them, a little preparation helps:

- **Explain what will happen**: Tell them Mummy will lie on a bed and a doctor will put cold jelly on her tummy to see the baby. Keep it simple.
- **Use age-appropriate language**: For toddlers, "we're going to see the baby on TV" works. For older children, you can explain more.
- **Manage expectations**: Explain they'll need to sit or stand quietly. Promise a treat afterwards.
- **Bring entertainment**: Snacks, a drink, a small toy, or a tablet with headphones can keep them occupied.
- **Bring another adult**: If possible, bring someone whose sole job is looking after the child. This frees you to focus on the scan.

## What to Bring If Children Are Allowed {#bring}

- A small bag with snacks and a drink
- A favourite toy or comfort item
- A tablet or phone with headphones and a pre-loaded show
- Wipes for sticky fingers
- A pushchair if your child is small — but check the hospital allows pushchairs in the scan room, as some don't due to space

## Alternatives If Children Aren't Allowed {#alternatives}

If your hospital says no children, don't feel you're shutting them out. There are other ways to involve siblings:

- **Show them the scan photo afterwards**: Let them hold it and talk about what they can see.
- **Video call your partner from the waiting room**: If your partner is in the scan room and the hospital allows it, your child could watch from the waiting area on a phone.
- **Book a private scan**: Private clinics are often more child-friendly, with larger rooms and a more relaxed atmosphere.
- **Wait for the 20-week scan**: Some hospitals have different policies for the anomaly scan, so you could try bringing them then instead.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Can I bring my toddler to the 12-week scan?

It depends on your hospital. Some allow toddlers, some don't. Ring ahead to check, and if they do allow it, bring another adult to supervise your child.

### What if my child cries during the scan?

If your child becomes distressed, the sonographer may ask you to pause the scan while you comfort them. If it's a persistent problem, you may need to reschedule. Bringing another adult to help can prevent this.

### Are private scans more child-friendly?

Generally yes. Private clinics tend to have larger rooms, more relaxed policies, and a focus on the experience. Many actively welcome siblings.
