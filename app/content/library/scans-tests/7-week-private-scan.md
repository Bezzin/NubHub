# 7-Week Private Scan: What You'll See

A 7-week private scan is one of the best times for early reassurance. The heartbeat is usually visible, the baby has grown enough to measure, and you're likely to leave with a clear picture of how things are progressing.

## What You'll See at 7 Weeks {#see}

At 7 weeks, your baby is about 10mm long — roughly the size of a blueberry. On the ultrasound screen, you'll see:

- **The gestational sac**: A dark circle in the uterus, containing the baby.
- **The yolk sac**: A small white circle, still visible and providing nutrients.
- **The fetal pole**: Your baby, now looking like a small curved shape rather than just a blob. You may be able to make out a head and a tail end.
- **The heartbeat**: A rapid flicker within the fetal pole. This is the moment most women are waiting for — and at 7 weeks, it's usually there.

The baby still doesn't look like a baby. It's more like a tiny kidney bean or a comma. But the heartbeat is clearly visible, and that's what matters most at this stage.

## The Heartbeat at 7 Weeks {#heartbeat}

At 7 weeks, the fetal heart rate is typically between **90 and 120 beats per minute** (bpm). This might seem very fast — and it is. Your baby's heart beats much faster than yours, which is completely normal.

The heartbeat starts around 6 weeks at roughly 90-110 bpm and accelerates by about 3 bpm per day, reaching a peak of around 170-180 bpm around 9-10 weeks before gradually slowing down. If your sonographer tells you the heart rate, don't worry if it seems high — a fast heart rate in early pregnancy is a good sign.

If the heartbeat is slow (below 80 bpm) at 7 weeks, the sonographer may ask you to come back for a repeat scan in a week to check it's sped up. A slow heart rate doesn't always mean the worst, but it does warrant follow-up.

## How the Scan Is Done {#how}

At 7 weeks, the scan may be **abdominal or transvaginal**, depending on the clinic and your body type:

- **Transvaginal**: Still the most common at 7 weeks, especially if you're slim or the baby is low in the pelvis. Gives the clearest image.
- **Abdominal**: Some clinics try abdominal first, especially if you have a full bladder. If the view is clear, no need for transvaginal.

For a transvaginal scan, you'll need an empty bladder. For an abdominal scan, you may be asked to drink water beforehand. The sonographer will explain what they're doing throughout.

## What the Sonographer Measures {#measures}

At 7 weeks, the sonographer will:

- **Measure the crown-rump length (CRL)**: From the top of the head to the bottom. This gives an accurate estimate of how far along you are, with an accuracy of about 3-5 days at this stage.
- **Check the heartbeat**: Confirming it's present and measuring the rate.
- **Check the gestational sac and yolk sac**: Ensuring they look normal and are the right size for your dates.
- **Count babies**: Twins can be clearly seen at 7 weeks. The sonographer will check for more than one gestational sac or fetal pole.
- **Confirm the pregnancy is in the uterus**: Ruling out ectopic pregnancy.

## What If Something Looks Wrong? {#wrong}

Most 7-week scans show a healthy pregnancy, but sometimes concerns arise:

- **No heartbeat**: If the fetal pole is large enough that a heartbeat should be visible but isn't, this could indicate a missed miscarriage. However, if the fetal pole is small, it may simply be too early. A repeat scan in 7-14 days will clarify.
- **Slow heartbeat**: Below 80 bpm at 7 weeks warrants a repeat scan in a week. Sometimes the heart has only just started beating and will speed up.
- **Small fetal pole**: If the CRL is smaller than expected, your dates may need adjusting. A repeat scan will check for growth.
- **Empty sac**: If the gestational sac is present but no fetal pole is seen, you may be earlier than expected, or it could be a blighted ovum. A repeat scan is essential before any diagnosis.

If any of these come up, the sonographer will explain gently and arrange a follow-up. Never accept a diagnosis of miscarriage from a single scan if there's any uncertainty — a repeat scan is standard practice.

## Why 7 Weeks Is a Popular Time {#popular}

Seven weeks has become a sweet spot for private early scans because:

- **The heartbeat is usually visible**: Unlike 6 weeks, where it's hit and miss.
- **The baby is measurable**: The CRL is accurate enough to date the pregnancy.
- **Results are usually conclusive**: You're less likely to need a repeat scan than at 6 weeks.
- **It's early enough for reassurance**: For women who can't wait until 12 weeks, 7 weeks is early enough to ease anxiety.
- **It rules out ectopic pregnancy**: This is a key safety check that can't wait.

If you're deciding between 6 and 7 weeks, 7 weeks is almost always the better choice for peace of mind.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Can you see twins at 7 weeks?

Yes. By 7 weeks, two separate fetal poles with heartbeats are usually visible if you're having twins. The sonographer will check for multiples.

### Is the heartbeat at 7 weeks a guarantee of a healthy pregnancy?

Seeing a heartbeat at 7 weeks is very reassuring. The chance of miscarriage drops significantly once a heartbeat is confirmed. However, it's not a guarantee — miscarriage can still occur. Most pregnancies with a confirmed heartbeat at 7 weeks do continue successfully.

### Will I get a photo at 7 weeks?

Most private clinics include at least one photo, though at 7 weeks the baby is so small that the photo may not look like much. Some clinics offer digital images you can access on your phone.
