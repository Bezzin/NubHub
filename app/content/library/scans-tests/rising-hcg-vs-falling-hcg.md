# Rising hCG vs Falling hCG: What It Means

What rising, plateauing and falling hCG levels tell you about early pregnancy viability, and when to seek further investigation.

## What a Rising hCG Level Tells You

In a healthy intrauterine pregnancy, hCG usually rises by at least 49 percent every 48 hours in the first few weeks, and often closer to doubles. A steadily rising level that follows this pattern is reassuring and suggests the pregnancy is developing. However, a rise that falls short of doubling is not automatically bad news, because normal pregnancies can have slower rises and still progress. Your medical team will look at at least two readings, and usually three, before drawing conclusions, and will arrange an early scan once hCG is high enough to see a gestational sac (usually above 1,500 to 2,000 IU/L).

## What a Falling or Plateauing hCG Level Means

A falling hCG level usually means the pregnancy is not continuing, either because of a miscarriage or, less commonly, an ectopic pregnancy that has stopped growing. A level that plateaus, rising by less than around 35 percent in 48 hours, is a cause for concern and warrants investigation. Importantly, a slow or falling rise can sometimes indicate an ectopic pregnancy even before symptoms appear, so your team may arrange an early scan and repeat bloods even if you feel well. Never rely on hCG alone, always follow up with ultrasound when levels allow.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How fast should hCG rise in early pregnancy?**

In a healthy pregnancy hCG typically rises by at least 49 percent every 48 hours. It does not always exactly double, and slower rises can still be normal, especially after 6 weeks.

**Can hCG fall and then rise again?**

Rarely, hCG can dip and recover, but a confirmed fall usually means the pregnancy is not viable. If you see this pattern, ask your team for a repeat test and an early scan to rule out ectopic pregnancy.

**Should I keep testing hCG at home?**

Home urine tests only show whether hCG is present, not the level. Blood tests at a clinic are the only way to track the actual number, and over-testing at home can cause unnecessary anxiety.
