# Slow Heartbeat at 6 Weeks: What's Normal?

Seeing a heartbeat on an early scan is a huge relief — but if the sonographer mentions it's on the slower side, it's natural to feel anxious. A slow fetal heartbeat at 6 weeks is not always cause for alarm, and in many cases it resolves on its own. Here's what you need to know about what's normal, what warrants monitoring, and what to expect next.

## Normal Heart Rate at 6 Weeks

The fetal heart begins beating from around 6 weeks of pregnancy. At this very early stage, the rate is naturally slower than it will be in the weeks that follow:

- At around 6 weeks, a heart rate of **90–110 beats per minute (bpm)** is generally considered within the normal range
- The heart rate increases rapidly as the pregnancy progresses, typically reaching 140–170 bpm by around 8–10 weeks
- Exact normal ranges can vary slightly depending on the source — your sonographer or early pregnancy unit (EPU) will interpret the reading in the context of your specific scan

Because the heart has only very recently started beating at this stage, a reading at the lower end of normal is not automatically worrying. The timing of exactly when you are scanned within week 6 also matters — at 6+0 (6 weeks exactly) the heart may only just be detectable, whereas at 6+6 a slightly higher rate is expected.

## When a Slow Heartbeat May Need Monitoring

A heart rate below approximately 80 bpm at 6 weeks or beyond is generally considered lower than expected and is more likely to prompt a follow-up scan. However, even in these cases:

- The measurement may have been taken at a slightly earlier gestation than estimated
- Scan measurements at this stage carry a margin of uncertainty
- A single slow reading is not necessarily a definitive sign of a problem

If the sonographer expresses concern, they will usually arrange for you to return within one to two weeks for a repeat scan. This is standard practice at UK early pregnancy units and does not necessarily mean something is wrong — it is simply the appropriate next step to monitor progress.

## What Happens at the Follow-Up Scan

A repeat scan at 7–8 weeks gives a much clearer picture. By this point:

- The heartbeat is usually much easier to see and measure accurately
- The rate should have increased significantly if the pregnancy is progressing well
- The size of the embryo (CRL — crown-rump length) can be compared to expected measurements for the gestation

Many pregnancies that show a slow heartbeat at 6 weeks go on to have a perfectly normal 8-week scan. Others may show that the heartbeat has not continued, which would indicate a missed miscarriage. A follow-up scan is the only way to know for certain, and waiting for it can feel extremely hard emotionally. Lean on your midwife, GP, or the EPU team for support during this time.

## Practical Guidance While You Wait

If you are waiting for a follow-up scan after a slow heartbeat was noted:

- **Contact your early pregnancy unit (EPU)** if you develop heavy bleeding, severe one-sided pain, or feel unwell — these symptoms require prompt assessment
- **Call 111** if you are unsure whether your symptoms need urgent attention outside of normal hours
- Try to avoid reading too deeply into forums or online statistics, as individual outcomes vary widely and numbers can feel alarming out of context
- It is completely normal to feel anxious — speak to your midwife or GP about how you are feeling

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What's a normal heart rate at 6 weeks?

At 6 weeks, a fetal heart rate of around 90–110 bpm is generally considered within the normal range. The heart has only just started beating at this stage, so a rate at the lower end of normal is not unusual. The rate increases steadily over the following weeks as the pregnancy develops.

### Can a slow heartbeat at 6 weeks improve?

Yes. Many pregnancies where a slow heartbeat is seen at 6 weeks go on to show a normal rate at a follow-up scan one to two weeks later. The early pregnancy unit will usually arrange a repeat scan to check progress and will be able to give you a clearer picture once more time has passed.

### Should I go to A&E if I'm worried about my baby's heartbeat?

In most cases, the early pregnancy unit (EPU) is the right place to go if you have concerns, rather than A&E. EPUs have specialist staff and equipment for early pregnancy scans. If you are experiencing heavy bleeding, severe pain, or feel very unwell, call 999 or go to A&E immediately. For non-emergency concerns outside of EPU hours, call 111 for guidance.

*Speak to your midwife or GP for personalised advice about your scan results or any concerns during early pregnancy.*
