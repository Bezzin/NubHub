# BMI and Pregnancy: What You Need to Know

Your BMI (Body Mass Index) is recorded at your booking appointment and plays a practical role in shaping the care you receive throughout pregnancy. Understanding what the numbers mean — and what extra support may be offered — helps you feel informed and prepared.

## What BMI Is and How It Is Measured

BMI is a simple calculation based on your height and weight. Most NHS trusts use the following ranges:

- **Under 18.5** — underweight
- **18.5–24.9** — healthy weight
- **25–29.9** — overweight
- **30 or above** — obese (further divided into categories at 35+ and 40+)

Your midwife will calculate your BMI at your booking appointment, usually between 8 and 10 weeks. The figure recorded at booking is used for planning your care — it is not recalculated later in pregnancy, because weight naturally increases as your baby grows.

BMI is a screening tool, not a complete picture of your health. It does not account for muscle mass, bone density, or how weight is distributed. Your midwife will consider your BMI alongside your full medical history.

## What Happens If Your BMI Is High

If your BMI is 30 or above, your care will be adapted to manage some additional risks. This does not mean your pregnancy will automatically be complicated — many people with a higher BMI have straightforward pregnancies — but some conditions are more likely and your team will monitor for them proactively.

**You may be offered or recommended:**

- **Low-dose aspirin** — taken from around 12 weeks, this reduces the risk of pre-eclampsia in people with a BMI of 30 or above. Your midwife or GP will advise on this at or after your booking appointment.
- **Gestational diabetes screening** — you will usually be offered an oral glucose tolerance test (OGTT) between 24 and 28 weeks. This is a routine blood test done at your hospital or GP surgery.
- **Additional growth scans** — extra ultrasound scans in the third trimester to monitor your baby's growth and wellbeing, as a higher BMI can sometimes make it harder to assess growth by hand measurement.
- **Consultant-led care** — depending on your BMI and other factors, your care may be led by an obstetrician rather than a midwife alone. This is a precaution, not a sign that something is wrong.
- **Discussions about birth options** — if your BMI is 40 or above, you may be advised that a consultant unit is safer than a midwife-led unit or home birth, because of the slightly higher chance of requiring intervention. This is worth discussing openly with your team.

If you are offered aspirin, it is important to take it as directed — it is a low dose (usually 75–150 mg) and specifically prescribed to reduce pre-eclampsia risk. Do not take additional aspirin or ibuprofen on top of this without checking with your midwife first.

## What Happens If Your BMI Is Low

If your BMI is under 18.5, your midwife will also keep a closer eye on your pregnancy. Risks associated with a low BMI include a higher chance of the baby being smaller than expected and a slightly increased risk of preterm birth. You may be referred to a dietitian for tailored nutritional advice and offered additional growth scans to monitor your baby's development.

## What You Can Do

Being open with your midwife about your health, diet, and any concerns is the most helpful thing you can do. Regardless of your starting BMI, gentle movement appropriate to your fitness level, balanced nutrition, and attending all your appointments will support a healthy pregnancy.

You do not need to "diet" during pregnancy to lower your BMI — this is not recommended. The focus is on eating nourishing food and moving your body safely, not on weight loss.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Will a high BMI affect my pregnancy care?

Yes, but in a practical and supportive way. If your BMI is 30 or above, your midwife will likely offer you additional monitoring such as growth scans and screening for gestational diabetes. You may also be prescribed low-dose aspirin to reduce pre-eclampsia risk. Your team will create a personalised care plan with you.

### Can I have a healthy pregnancy with a high BMI?

Absolutely. Many people with a BMI above 30 have healthy, uncomplicated pregnancies. The extra monitoring is precautionary — it means any changes are caught early. Being open with your midwife, attending all your appointments, and following any recommendations will give your pregnancy the best possible foundation.

### What if I am unhappy with how my BMI is being discussed?

You are entitled to respectful, non-judgmental care. If you feel your BMI is being raised in a way that feels harmful rather than helpful, you can ask your midwife to focus on the specific clinical steps being recommended rather than the number itself. You can also request a referral to a specialist midwife if your trust has one for higher-BMI pregnancies.

Always speak to your midwife or GP for advice that is personalised to your situation and medical history.
