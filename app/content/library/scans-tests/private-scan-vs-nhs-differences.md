# Private Scan vs NHS Scan Differences

When you are pregnant in the UK, you will be offered a set of free NHS scans as part of your routine antenatal care. Many parents also choose to book private scans alongside these. Understanding what each option offers — and where the differences lie — helps you make informed decisions and approach every appointment with realistic expectations.

## What NHS Scans Cover {#nhs-scans}

NHS pregnancy scans are free and are provided as part of the standard maternity care pathway. Most women are offered two routine ultrasound scans:

- **The dating scan (also called the booking scan)**, usually offered between 10 and 14 weeks. This confirms your pregnancy, checks for a heartbeat, gives you an estimated due date, and screens for chromosomal conditions as part of the combined screening test (which also uses a blood test). Twins or multiples are also identified at this point.
- **The 20-week anatomy scan (also called the anomaly scan)**, offered between 18 and 21 weeks. This is a detailed medical examination of your baby's organs, limbs, brain, spine, and face. It checks for a range of physical conditions. It is not routinely used to reveal the sex of the baby in all NHS trusts, though some will tell you if you ask.

NHS sonographers are trained medical professionals. Their focus is on clinical accuracy — they are looking for signs that may need monitoring or further investigation. Appointments are typically brief (often 20 to 30 minutes), and you may not have a great deal of time to simply look at your baby or ask general questions. You will usually receive one or two printed scan images to take home.

Some NHS trusts also offer additional scans if there is a clinical reason — for example, if your baby's growth needs monitoring, if there was an unclear finding at the anatomy scan, or if you have a condition such as gestational diabetes that requires closer observation. These extra NHS scans are always arranged through your midwife or obstetric team, not independently.

It is worth knowing that waiting times for NHS scans can vary by trust. Your booking-appointment midwife will explain when you can expect your appointments and how you will receive your letter or invitation.

## What Private Scans Add {#private-scans}

Private pregnancy scans are paid appointments offered by independent clinics. They can complement your NHS care, though they do not replace it. Common types of private scan include:

- **Early reassurance scans**, typically from 6 to 10 weeks, for parents who want confirmation of a heartbeat before their NHS dating scan. These can be particularly helpful after a previous pregnancy loss or if you are experiencing anxiety about the pregnancy.
- **Gender scans**, usually from around 16 weeks, which aim to identify the sex of your baby. The accuracy improves with gestational age.
- **3D and 4D bonding scans**, often booked from around 24 weeks, which produce lifelike images and short video clips of your baby's face and movements.
- **Well-being checks**, which can be booked at any stage to check the baby's growth, movements, position, and amniotic fluid levels.
- **Nub theory scans**, offered by some specialist clinics from around 12 weeks, which attempt to predict the sex of the baby by examining the angle of the genital tubercle on the scan image.

Private clinics typically offer longer appointment times, a more relaxed atmosphere, more images and videos to take home, and the option to bring additional family members or older children. Many parents appreciate the extra time to bond and ask questions in an unhurried setting.

It is important to check that any private clinic you use employs qualified, registered sonographers. In the UK, sonographers should be registered with the Health and Care Professions Council (HCPC). Reputable clinics will be happy to confirm their staff qualifications on request. Some private clinics also work alongside consultant obstetricians who can review findings if something unexpected is identified.

Private scans do carry an out-of-pocket cost, and prices vary considerably between clinics and scan types. It is sensible to check what is included — some clinics charge per image, while others include a package of prints and digital copies.

## Key Differences at a Glance {#key-differences}

| | NHS Scan | Private Scan |
|---|---|---|
| Cost | Free | Paid (varies by clinic and scan type) |
| Purpose | Medical screening | Reassurance, bonding, gender |
| Booking | Through your midwife or GP | Directly with the clinic |
| Appointment length | Shorter | Typically longer |
| Images provided | One or two prints | Multiple prints, often digital or video too |
| Gender reveal | Not routinely offered | Available from around 16 weeks |
| Extra family members | Usually limited | Often welcomed |
| Replaces the other? | No | No |

Both types of scan have their own value. NHS scans are medically essential. Private scans are an optional extra that many parents find reassuring and enjoyable, but they are not a substitute for your standard antenatal care. If a private scan identifies anything that causes concern, you should always follow up with your midwife or GP rather than relying on a private sonographer to manage a clinical finding.

## What to Expect at Each Type of Appointment

At an NHS scan, you will usually be called from a waiting area, asked to confirm your details, and then scanned by a sonographer in a clinical room. The sonographer may not narrate everything they are doing in real time, as they need to focus on measurements and documentation. If they identify anything that requires further review, they will let you know and arrange the appropriate follow-up. Try not to read too much into silence during an NHS scan — it is usually just concentration.

At a private scan, the atmosphere is generally more conversational. The sonographer will typically talk you through what you are seeing on screen, point out features of your baby, and take time to capture good images. Some clinics play music, dim the lights for a more intimate experience, and offer refreshments in a waiting lounge. The pace is simply different — private clinics are designed with the bonding experience in mind, while NHS departments are designed around throughput and clinical safety.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Do I still need NHS scans if I go private?

Yes. Private scans are extras and do not replace the medical screening the NHS provides. The dating scan and the 20-week anomaly scan are the two most important clinical checks of your pregnancy, and both should be attended regardless of any private appointments you book.

### Are private scans medically checked?

Reputable private clinics employ qualified, registered sonographers. However, private scans are not always carried out in a clinical setting with the same equipment used by NHS units. If a private scan identifies something that concerns you, your sonographer should advise you to follow up with your midwife or GP. They cannot make formal diagnoses.

### How much do private scans cost?

Prices vary between clinics and the type of scan. A basic early reassurance scan typically starts from around £50 to £80. A gender scan may cost a similar amount. 3D or 4D bonding packages can cost considerably more depending on the length of the appointment and what is included. It is worth reading reviews and checking credentials before booking, as quality varies.

### Can I have a private scan very early in pregnancy?

Some clinics offer scans from 6 weeks of pregnancy, usually using an internal (transvaginal) scan for clearer images at that stage. A heartbeat is often visible from around 6 to 7 weeks, though not always — this is normal and does not necessarily indicate a problem. A repeat scan a week later can help clarify the situation if no heartbeat is seen at an early scan.
