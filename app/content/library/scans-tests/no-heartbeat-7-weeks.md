# No Heartbeat at 7 Weeks: Should I Worry?

Not seeing a heartbeat at 7 weeks is more concerning than at 6 weeks, but it still doesn't automatically mean the worst. There are several possible explanations, and the process for confirming what's happening is careful and deliberate.

## What No Heartbeat at 7 Weeks Means {#means}

At 7 weeks, a heartbeat is usually visible on a transvaginal scan. The heart starts beating at about 6 weeks, and by 7 weeks it's typically strong enough to detect. So not seeing one at 7 weeks is more concerning than at 6 weeks — but it's not a definitive diagnosis on its own.

There are two main possibilities:

1. **You're earlier than expected**: Your dates are off by a few days, and the heart has only just started beating or hasn't started yet. This is still common at 7 weeks, especially with irregular cycles.
2. **The pregnancy has stopped developing**: A missed miscarriage, where the baby has stopped growing but your body hasn't yet recognised the loss.

The only way to tell the difference is a **repeat scan** after 7-14 days to see if the baby has grown and whether a heartbeat has appeared.

## Could You Be Earlier Than You Think? {#earlier}

It's very possible. Dating in early pregnancy is based on your last menstrual period (LMP), but this assumes a 28-day cycle with ovulation on day 14. If your cycle is longer or you ovulated late, you could be several days less far along than you think:

- **35-day cycle**: You probably ovulated around day 21, meaning you're about a week less far along than standard dating suggests.
- **Irregular cycles**: If your periods are unpredictable, your due date could be off by up to 2 weeks.
- **Late ovulation**: Even with a regular cycle, ovulation can occasionally be delayed by stress, illness, or travel.

If your sonographer measures the CRL and it's smaller than expected for 7 weeks (for example, it measures 5-6 weeks), this strongly suggests you're simply earlier than expected, and a heartbeat may appear at your repeat scan.

## When to Be Concerned {#concerned}

There are specific criteria that make a missed miscarriage more likely:

- **CRL of 7mm or more with no heartbeat**: This meets the diagnostic criteria for a missed miscarriage. However, a repeat scan is still recommended to confirm.
- **Gestational sac of 25mm or more with no embryo**: This suggests an anembryonic pregnancy (blighted ovum), where the sac developed but no baby formed. Again, a repeat scan confirms.
- **CRL that hasn't grown on repeat scan**: If the baby is the same size at a repeat scan 7-14 days later, with no heartbeat, this confirms a missed miscarriage.
- **No heartbeat at two separate scans**: If both scans show no heartbeat and no growth, the diagnosis is confirmed.

If none of these criteria are met — for example, the CRL is small and the sac looks normal — the most likely explanation is that you're earlier than expected.

## What Happens Next {#next}

If no heartbeat is seen at 7 weeks, the sonographer will:

1. **Measure the CRL and sac size carefully**: This helps determine whether you're earlier than expected or whether there's a concern.
2. **Check the gestational sac and yolk sac**: If they look normal and are correctly sized, that's reassuring.
3. **Arrange a repeat scan**: Usually in 7-14 days. The sonographer won't make a diagnosis from a single scan if there's uncertainty.
4. **Check your blood type**: If you're RhD negative, you may need anti-D after certain procedures, so this is noted.
5. **Advise on symptoms to watch for**: Bleeding, pain, or fever should prompt urgent contact with your GP or EPU.

## The Repeat Scan {#repeat}

The repeat scan is the key moment. At this scan:

- **If the baby has grown and a heartbeat is visible**: Wonderful news — your pregnancy is progressing. You were simply earlier than expected at the first scan.
- **If the baby hasn't grown and there's still no heartbeat**: This confirms a missed miscarriage. Your healthcare team will discuss your options with you sensitively.
- **If the baby has grown but there's still no heartbeat**: This is an unusual situation. The sonographer may arrange a further scan or refer you to a fetal medicine specialist.

Most women who had a small CRL at the first scan go on to see a heartbeat at the repeat scan. The outcome depends heavily on the measurements at the first scan.

## Coping Strategies {#cope}

Waiting is the hardest part. Here are some ways to cope:

- **Limit Googling**: The internet will show you the worst stories. The statistics are actually more reassuring than the forums suggest.
- **Lean on support**: Tell your partner or a trusted friend how you're feeling.
- **Ask for a 7-day repeat**: If the waiting is unbearable, some EPUs will scan you in 7 days rather than 14.
- **Monitor for symptoms**: Bleeding or cramping should prompt you to contact your EPU. If you feel fine, take heart from that.
- **Take time off**: If you can, take a few days off work to rest and process your emotions.
- **Prepare for both outcomes**: It's OK to hope while also being prepared for bad news.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### What are the chances the heartbeat will appear at the repeat scan?

If your CRL was small at the first scan and the sac looked normal, the chances are good — the majority of these cases turn out to be dating errors. If the CRL was 7mm or more with no heartbeat, the chances are unfortunately lower.

### Can you have a healthy pregnancy after no heartbeat at 7 weeks?

Yes, absolutely — if the reason was a dating error. Many women who are told there's no heartbeat at 7 weeks go on to see a healthy heartbeat at their repeat scan and have normal pregnancies.

### Should I stop taking folic acid?

No. Keep taking your folic acid and prenatal vitamins until you know for certain what's happening. Stopping them won't change the outcome, and if the pregnancy is continuing, you still need them.
