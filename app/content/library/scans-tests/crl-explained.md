# CRL (Crown-Rump Length) Explained

CRL — crown-rump length — is one of the most important measurements in early pregnancy. It's how your sonographer dates your pregnancy and checks your baby is growing as expected. Here's what it means and why it matters.

## What Is CRL? {#what}

CRL stands for **crown-rump length**. It's the measurement of your baby from the top of the head (crown) to the bottom of the buttocks (rump). It doesn't include the legs, which aren't developed enough to measure in the first trimester.

CRL is the standard measurement used to date pregnancies in the first trimester. It's taken at your dating scan (and at any early private scan) to estimate how far along you are and to check your baby is growing as expected.

The CRL is measured in millimetres and compared against standard charts. Each gestational age has an expected CRL range, and the sonographer uses this to confirm your due date.

## How CRL Is Measured {#how}

During your scan, the sonographer will:

1. **Find the best view of your baby**: They'll position the probe so your baby is clearly visible and as straight as possible.
2. **Take the measurement**: Using the ultrasound machine's calipers (electronic measuring tools), they'll measure from the top of the head to the bottom of the rump.
3. **Repeat the measurement**: Most sonographers take at least two or three measurements to ensure accuracy.
4. **Calculate the gestational age**: The ultrasound machine uses the CRL to calculate how many weeks and days pregnant you are.

The measurement is most accurate when your baby is lying fairly straight. If the baby is curled up, the measurement may be too small. The sonographer may wait for the baby to stretch out or ask you to change position to get a better view.

## CRL by Gestational Age {#by-week}

Here's a guide to typical CRL measurements at different gestational ages:

| Gestational Age | Typical CRL |
|-----------------|-------------|
| 6 weeks | 4-6mm |
| 7 weeks | 8-11mm |
| 8 weeks | 15-20mm |
| 9 weeks | 22-30mm |
| 10 weeks | 31-40mm |
| 11 weeks | 41-54mm |
| 12 weeks | 55-65mm |
| 13 weeks | 66-78mm |
| 14 weeks | 79-90mm |

These are average ranges. Individual babies vary, and a measurement slightly outside these ranges isn't necessarily a problem.

## Why CRL Matters {#why}

CRL is important for several reasons:

- **Dating the pregnancy**: In the first trimester, CRL is the most accurate way to estimate how far along you are. It's more reliable than counting from your last menstrual period, especially if your cycle is irregular.
- **Setting your due date**: Once your CRL is measured at the dating scan, your due date is set. This due date stays with you for the rest of your pregnancy, even if later scans give slightly different measurements.
- **Checking growth**: At early scans, the CRL confirms your baby is growing as expected. A significantly smaller or larger CRL may indicate a dating error or, less commonly, a growth issue.
- **Screening eligibility**: The NT (nuchal translucency) screening for chromosomal conditions is only available between 11+2 and 14+1 weeks. The CRL confirms you're within this window.

## How Accurate Is CRL? {#accuracy}

CRL is the **most accurate way to date a pregnancy in the first trimester**:

- **At 6-8 weeks**: CRL is accurate to about **3-5 days**.
- **At 11-14 weeks (dating scan)**: CRL is accurate to about **5-7 days**.
- **After 14 weeks**: CRL becomes less accurate because babies start growing at different rates. Other measurements (head circumference, femur length) are used instead.

Because CRL is most accurate in the first trimester, this is why the NHS dating scan is done between 11 and 14 weeks. Once the due date is set at this scan, it's not changed, even if later scans suggest a different date.

## What If CRL Is Smaller or Bigger? {#different}

If your baby's CRL is different from expected:

- **Smaller than expected (up to 5-7 days)**: Usually a dating error. Your due date may be adjusted. This is very common with irregular cycles.
- **Smaller than expected (more than 7 days)**: May indicate a dating error or, less commonly, a growth concern. A repeat scan may be arranged.
- **Bigger than expected (up to 5-7 days)**: Usually a dating error. Your due date may be adjusted.
- **Bigger than expected (more than 7 days)**: May indicate a dating error. Rarely, it could suggest gestational diabetes or another condition, but this is typically investigated later.

The most common reason for a CRL that doesn't match your dates is simply that your dates are wrong — not that something is wrong with your baby.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### What's a normal CRL at 12 weeks?

At 12 weeks, the typical CRL is about 55-65mm. The exact measurement varies between babies, and a range of about 10mm is normal.

### Can CRL predict the baby's birth weight?

No. CRL in the first trimester doesn't predict birth weight. Birth weight depends on genetics, maternal health, placental function, and many other factors throughout pregnancy.

### Why wasn't my due date changed after my scan?

If your CRL was within 5-7 days of your original dates, your due date stays as it was. The NHS only adjusts the due date if there's a significant difference, because minor variations are normal.
