# No Heartbeat at 6 Weeks What It Means

Leaving a scan without hearing the words "there's the heartbeat" is one of the most frightening experiences in early pregnancy. If you are six weeks along and no heartbeat was seen, please know that this is often not the end of the story. At six weeks, the window for detecting cardiac activity is narrow and highly dependent on exact gestational age — a difference of just a few days can be the difference between seeing a heartbeat and not.

## Why a Heartbeat May Not Be Visible at 6 Weeks {#why-not-visible}

The embryonic heart begins beating very early, but it only becomes reliably detectable on ultrasound from around six to seven weeks of pregnancy — and that assumes your dates are accurate.

The most common reason a heartbeat is not seen at a six-week scan is simply that the pregnancy is slightly less advanced than expected. This is particularly likely if:

- Your periods are irregular, making it harder to pinpoint when you ovulated
- You conceived later in your cycle than the average day 14
- Your last period date was estimated rather than precisely known

Even a difference of four or five days in gestational age can mean the difference between a heartbeat being visible and not. A pregnancy genuinely at five weeks and five days will often show no heartbeat; one at six weeks and three days usually will.

Other factors that can affect whether the heartbeat is detected include:

- The type of scan — a transvaginal scan gives a much clearer image than an abdominal one at this stage
- The position of the uterus (a retroverted, or tilted, uterus can make early scanning more difficult)
- The quality and resolution of the ultrasound equipment used
- Body weight and abdominal wall thickness if using an abdominal probe

## What Usually Happens Next {#what-happens-next}

When no heartbeat is seen at a six-week scan, the standard clinical approach in the UK is to arrange a repeat scan in one to two weeks' time. This allows the pregnancy enough time to develop so that a heartbeat — if present — will be clearly visible.

Clinicians are generally cautious about reaching conclusions from a single early scan when dates are uncertain. Guidelines from the Royal College of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists (RCOG) and NHS trusts stress that a diagnosis of miscarriage should not be made on a single scan alone if there is any uncertainty about dates.

During the waiting period between scans, you may be asked to:

- Note any changes in symptoms (though the presence or absence of symptoms is not a reliable guide to pregnancy health)
- Rest and avoid strenuous exercise if there has been any bleeding
- Contact the EPU if you experience heavy bleeding or significant pain

The wait between an uncertain scan and a follow-up is extremely difficult emotionally. If you are struggling, it is completely reasonable to reach out to your midwife, GP, or a pregnancy loss support line for emotional support — even before a definitive outcome is known.

## When the News Is Difficult

If a repeat scan confirms that no heartbeat has developed, or that the pregnancy has not progressed, your care team will explain your options carefully. There is no pressure to make decisions immediately, and you will be offered support throughout.

If you are ever unsure about the information you have been given, you are entitled to ask for a second scan or a second opinion. In the UK, EPUs and early pregnancy services are there specifically to support you through situations like this.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Does no heartbeat at 6 weeks mean miscarriage?

Not necessarily, and often not. At exactly six weeks, a heartbeat may simply not yet be detectable, particularly if your dates are slightly off. A repeat scan one to two weeks later is the standard next step, and in many cases that scan shows a healthy heartbeat.

### When should a heartbeat definitely be visible?

By seven weeks of pregnancy, a heartbeat should be clearly visible on a transvaginal ultrasound in a continuing pregnancy. If dates are certain and no heartbeat is seen by seven weeks, your care team will discuss what this means and what to do next.

### What should I do while I wait for the repeat scan?

Follow the guidance your EPU or sonographer has given you. Try to rest, stay hydrated, and contact the unit if you develop heavy bleeding or significant pain. Reaching out to someone you trust for emotional support during the wait is also very important — you do not have to cope with the uncertainty alone.

### Can I have the repeat scan privately if the wait feels too long?

Yes. If the NHS waiting time for a repeat scan feels too long to bear, private early pregnancy clinics can often offer appointments within a few days. Make sure to share the results with your NHS care team so they have a full picture.
