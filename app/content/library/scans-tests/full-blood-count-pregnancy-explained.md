# Full Blood Count in Pregnancy Explained

What a full blood count (FBC) measures during pregnancy, why it is routine, and how to read the key values on your results.

## What a Full Blood Count Measures

A full blood count, sometimes called a complete blood count, is one of the first tests run at your booking appointment. It measures three main cell types: red blood cells (including haemoglobin, haematocrit and MCV), white blood cells, and platelets. Haemoglobin carries oxygen, white blood cells fight infection, and platelets help your blood clot. In pregnancy your blood volume expands by up to 50 percent, which naturally dilutes these cells, so normal ranges are adjusted for each trimester.

## Why the FBC Matters Across Pregnancy

Your midwife will usually repeat the full blood count at 28 weeks and again near your due date. A falling haemoglobin level is the most common finding and points to iron-deficiency anaemia, which affects around one in three pregnant women in the UK. A low platelet count, known as gestational thrombocytopenia, is usually harmless but can affect epidural options. Raised white cells are normal in late pregnancy and do not always mean infection. Understanding your numbers helps you ask the right questions at appointments.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is a normal haemoglobin level in pregnancy?**

In the UK, haemoglobin below 110 g/L in the first trimester, below 105 g/L in the second, and below 105 g/L in the third is classed as anaemia. Your midwife will compare your result to trimester-specific ranges rather than a single number.

**Will a low platelet count harm my baby?**

Mild gestational thrombocytopenia is common and rarely affects the baby. If platelets drop very low your team will investigate further and may discuss pain-relief options that avoid epidural needles.

**Can I improve my full blood count results with diet?**

Iron-rich foods such as lean red meat, lentils, fortified cereals and dark leafy greens can help haemoglobin. However, diet alone may not correct a deficiency quickly enough in pregnancy, so prescribed iron tablets are often needed.
