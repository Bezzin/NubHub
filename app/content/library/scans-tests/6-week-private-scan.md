# 6-Week Private Scan: What You'll See

A 6-week private scan is the earliest ultrasound you can book. At this stage, your baby is tiny — about the size of a sweetcorn kernel — and what you can see is limited. Here's exactly what to expect so you can decide if it's the right time for you.

## What You'll See at 6 Weeks {#see}

At 6 weeks, your baby is only about 4-5mm long — roughly the size of a grain of rice. On the ultrasound screen, you'll see:

- **The gestational sac**: A dark, fluid-filled circle in the uterus. This is the structure that contains your baby.
- **The yolk sac**: A small white circle inside the gestational sac. This provides nutrients to your baby before the placenta takes over.
- **The fetal pole**: A tiny smudge or line inside the sac — this is your baby. It may look like nothing more than a small blob.
- **Possibly a heartbeat**: If you're exactly 6 weeks or slightly beyond, and the scan is transvaginal, you may see a tiny flicker. This is the heartbeat.

At this stage, the baby doesn't look like a baby. It's too small. The scan is about confirming the pregnancy is in the right place and progressing, not about seeing a recognisable baby.

## Will You See a Heartbeat? {#heartbeat}

A heartbeat *may* be visible at 6 weeks, but it's not guaranteed. The heartbeat typically becomes visible around **6 to 7 weeks**, and even then it depends on:

- **Exact gestational age**: If you're 6+0 weeks, a heartbeat might not be detectable. If you're 6+4 or later, it's more likely.
- **Type of scan**: A transvaginal scan can detect a heartbeat earlier than an abdominal scan, because the probe is closer to the uterus.
- **Equipment quality**: High-end ultrasound machines in private clinics may detect a heartbeat slightly earlier than basic models.
- **Your dates**: If you're actually 5+5 rather than 6+2 (easy to misjudge with irregular cycles), the heartbeat won't be visible yet.

If you don't see a heartbeat at 6 weeks, it doesn't mean the pregnancy has failed. It usually means you're a few days earlier than expected. The sonographer will ask you to come back in 7-14 days for a repeat scan, by which time the heartbeat should be visible.

## How the Scan Is Done {#how}

At 6 weeks, the scan is almost always **transvaginal**. An abdominal scan at this stage would struggle to see anything, because the baby is deep in the pelvis and very small.

For a transvaginal scan:

- You'll remove your lower clothing and lie on a couch with your legs apart (similar to a smear test).
- The sonographer will insert a slim, covered probe into your vagina. This shouldn't be painful, but you may feel a bit of pressure.
- The probe is gently angled to get views of the uterus. The whole scan takes 5-10 minutes.

Some clinics start with an abdominal scan and switch to transvaginal if the view isn't clear. You'll need an empty bladder for a transvaginal scan.

## What the Sonographer Checks {#checks}

At 6 weeks, the sonographer will:

- **Confirm the pregnancy is in the uterus**: This rules out an ectopic pregnancy, which is the most important check at this stage.
- **Measure the gestational sac**: This gives an indication of how far along you are.
- **Look for the yolk sac and fetal pole**: These confirm the pregnancy is developing.
- **Check for a heartbeat**: If visible, this confirms the pregnancy is viable.
- **Count the number of sacs**: Twins can be detected from 6 weeks, though it's easier later.

## Why It Might Be Inconclusive {#inconclusive}

A 6-week scan can be inconclusive for several reasons:

- **No heartbeat yet**: As discussed, the heartbeat may not be visible until 6+3 or later.
- **Small gestational sac**: If the sac is smaller than expected, you may be earlier than your dates suggest.
- **Unclear dates**: If your periods are irregular, you might only be 5 weeks, meaning there's very little to see.
- **Empty sac**: A gestational sac with no visible fetal pole or yolk sac can mean it's too early, or (less commonly) a failed pregnancy. A repeat scan is needed to tell the difference.

An inconclusive scan at 6 weeks is very common. It doesn't mean something is wrong — it usually means the scan is simply too early to give definitive answers.

## Should You Wait Until 7 Weeks? {#wait}

Many clinics and sonographers recommend waiting until at least **7 weeks** for a private early scan. By 7 weeks:

- The heartbeat is almost always visible.
- The fetal pole is larger and easier to measure.
- The yolk sac is clearly seen.
- You're much less likely to have an inconclusive result.

If you can wait one more week, the experience will be much more reassuring. You're more likely to see what you came to see — a beating heart.

That said, if you've had an ectopic pregnancy before, are experiencing pain or bleeding, or simply can't bear the wait, a 6-week scan is still worth doing to confirm the pregnancy is in the right place.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Can you see twins at 6 weeks?

Yes, twins can sometimes be detected at 6 weeks, as two gestational sacs may be visible. However, it's easier to confirm at 7-8 weeks when the embryos are larger.

### Is a 6-week scan transvaginal?

Almost always. At 6 weeks, the baby is too small and deep in the pelvis for an abdominal scan to see clearly. A transvaginal scan gives a much better view.

### What if there's no heartbeat at 6 weeks?

It doesn't necessarily mean the pregnancy has failed. You may simply be earlier than expected. The sonographer will ask you to return in 7-14 days for a repeat scan.
