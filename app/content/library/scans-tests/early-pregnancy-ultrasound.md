# Early Pregnancy Ultrasound: What to Expect

An early pregnancy ultrasound is one of the first reassuring milestones you can experience after finding out you're pregnant. Whether you're having an NHS scan or a private one, knowing what to expect can ease your nerves and help you make the most of the appointment.

## What Is an Early Pregnancy Ultrasound? {#what-is-it}

An early pregnancy ultrasound is a scan carried out in the first trimester, typically between 6 and 10 weeks of pregnancy. It uses high-frequency sound waves to create an image of your baby inside the womb. Unlike X-rays, ultrasound does not use ionising radiation, making it safe for both you and your baby.

The main purposes of an early scan are to confirm the pregnancy is in the right place (inside the uterus), check for a heartbeat, estimate how far along you are, and rule out any early complications such as an ectopic pregnancy or a miscarriage. Many women book a private early scan for reassurance before their NHS dating scan at around 12 weeks, while others are referred for one on the NHS if they've experienced bleeding, pain, or a previous loss.

## When Do You Have One? {#when}

On the NHS, early scans are usually only offered if there's a clinical reason — for example, vaginal bleeding, abdominal pain, a history of ectopic pregnancy, or previous recurrent miscarriage. If you have none of these risk factors, your first NHS scan will be the dating scan between 11 and 14 weeks.

Privately, you can book an early scan from around 6 weeks, though many clinics recommend waiting until at least 7 weeks to maximise the chance of seeing a heartbeat. Scans done before 6 weeks are often too early to provide meaningful reassurance, as the baby is simply too small to measure accurately.

## What Happens During the Scan? {#what-happens}

When you arrive, the sonographer will ask you to lie on an examination couch. For an abdominal scan, you'll need to lift your top and lower your trousers or skirt slightly to expose your lower abdomen. The sonographer will apply a warm gel to your tummy and move a handheld probe (transducer) across it to capture images.

For a transvaginal scan, you'll be asked to remove your lower clothing and lie with your legs apart (similar to a smear test). A slim probe, covered with a sterile condom and gel, is gently inserted into the vagina. This gives a much clearer view in early pregnancy because the probe sits closer to the uterus.

The scan itself usually takes 10 to 20 minutes. The sonographer may be quiet while concentrating — this is normal and doesn't mean something is wrong. They'll explain what they can see once they've completed their measurements.

## What Will You See? {#what-you-see}

What you see depends on how many weeks pregnant you are:

- **At 6 weeks**, you may see a small gestational sac and, if you're lucky, a tiny flickering heartbeat. Your baby is only about the size of a lentil.
- **At 7 weeks**, the heartbeat is usually clearly visible. You may be able to make out the head and body as distinct shapes.
- **At 8 weeks**, your baby looks more like a little bean, with visible head, body, and limb buds. The heartbeat is strong and easier to detect.
- **At 9-10 weeks**, your baby has grown considerably and looks more recognisable. You may see movement.

The sonographer will measure the baby from head to bottom (the crown-rump length, or CRL) to estimate your due date. They'll also check the heartbeat rate and confirm the pregnancy is progressing normally.

## Abdominal vs Transvaginal Scans {#types}

In very early pregnancy (before about 8 weeks), a transvaginal scan is often preferred because it produces a clearer image. The ultrasound probe is closer to the uterus, allowing the sonographer to see the tiny gestational sac and embryo more easily.

An abdominal scan is more common from around 8 to 10 weeks onwards. You may be asked to have a full bladder for an abdominal scan, as a full bladder pushes the uterus up and provides a better "window" for the sound waves. Some clinics start with an abdominal scan and switch to transvaginal if the view isn't clear enough.

## What If They Can't See Anything? {#cant-see}

If the sonographer can't see a baby or heartbeat, it doesn't necessarily mean the worst. It may simply be too early — you might be earlier in your pregnancy than you think, especially if your cycle is irregular. The sonographer may ask you to come back in one to two weeks for a repeat scan.

If a repeat scan still shows no heartbeat or growth, the sonographer will discuss the findings with you sensitively and arrange follow-up with your GP or early pregnancy unit. Try not to jump to conclusions after a single early scan.

## How to Prepare {#prepare}

- Wear comfortable, loose clothing — a two-piece outfit is easier than a dress for abdominal scans.
- Check whether you need a full bladder — some clinics ask you to drink 1 pint of water 45 minutes beforehand.
- Bring your partner or a friend if you'd like support.
- Write down any questions in advance so you don't forget them in the moment.
- Don't panic if the sonographer is quiet — they're concentrating.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Is an early pregnancy ultrasound safe?

Yes. Ultrasound has been used in pregnancy for decades and there is no evidence that it harms the baby when used appropriately. It uses sound waves, not radiation.

### Can I get a photo from an early scan?

Private clinics almost always provide photos or digital images. NHS early pregnancy scans may not always offer photos, as these are usually clinical scans rather than keepsake ones.

### How much does a private early scan cost?

Prices vary across the UK but typically range from £50 to £150 depending on the clinic and what's included. Some packages include a heartbeat recording or a short video.
