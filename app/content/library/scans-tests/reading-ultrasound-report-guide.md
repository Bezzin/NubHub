# Reading Your Ultrasound Report: A Guide for Mums

Receiving a copy of your ultrasound report can feel overwhelming when you're faced with abbreviations, measurements, and medical language you've never encountered before. This plain English guide explains the most common terms you're likely to see, so you can make sense of what's on the page and know what questions to ask your midwife or sonographer.

## Common Terms on Your Scan Report

- **CRL (Crown-Rump Length)**: The measurement of your baby from the top of the head to the bottom. This is the most reliable way to date your pregnancy in the first trimester and is used to calculate or confirm your due date.
- **NT (Nuchal Translucency)**: The measurement of fluid at the back of your baby's neck. This is measured at the dating/NT scan (usually between 11 and 14 weeks) and forms part of the combined screening test for chromosomal conditions such as Down's syndrome.
- **GS (Gestational Sac)**: The fluid-filled sac that surrounds and protects your baby and amniotic fluid. It is usually the first structure visible on an early scan, often from around 5–6 weeks.
- **YS (Yolk Sac)**: A small round structure inside the gestational sac that provides nutrition to the embryo before the placenta is established. Seeing a yolk sac on an early scan is a reassuring sign.
- **FH (Fetal Heart)**: A note of whether a heartbeat was detected during the scan. "FH seen" or "FH present" is a reassuring finding.
- **FHR (Fetal Heart Rate)**: Your baby's heart rate measured in beats per minute (bpm). The rate changes significantly week by week in early pregnancy, so what is "normal" varies with gestation.
- **EFW (Estimated Fetal Weight)**: An estimate of your baby's weight calculated from several measurements. This is more commonly used in the second and third trimesters rather than early pregnancy.
- **BPD (Biparietal Diameter)**: The width of your baby's head from one side to the other. Used alongside other measurements to estimate gestational age and growth.
- **FL (Femur Length)**: The length of your baby's thigh bone (femur). Another measurement used to estimate gestational age and check growth is on track.
- **AC (Abdominal Circumference)**: A measurement around your baby's abdomen. Particularly useful for assessing growth and, in later pregnancy, estimating fetal weight.

## What the Location Terms Mean

You may also notice descriptive terms about where the pregnancy is situated or what was found:

- **Intrauterine**: The pregnancy is inside the uterus — exactly where it should be. This phrase is important because it rules out an ectopic pregnancy (one developing outside the uterus).
- **Single live intrauterine pregnancy**: One of the most reassuring phrases on an early scan report. It means there is one baby, a heartbeat has been confirmed, and the pregnancy is in the correct location.
- **Anterior/posterior placenta**: Describes where your placenta is positioned — at the front (anterior) or back (posterior) of the uterus. Both are completely normal.
- **Low-lying placenta**: If noted at your 20-week scan, this means the placenta is near the cervix. Many low-lying placentas move upward as your uterus grows. You will be offered a follow-up scan later in pregnancy.

## Measurements and Dates

Sonographers use the measurements taken during your scan to calculate or confirm your gestational age. If the dates from your scan differ slightly from your last menstrual period (LMP), the scan measurement is generally considered more accurate for dating purposes. A difference of a few days is not unusual.

If the scan-calculated dates differ significantly from what you expected, try not to panic. It may simply mean ovulation happened earlier or later than you thought. Your midwife or sonographer can explain what the measurements mean for your due date.

## When to Ask Questions

You don't need to understand every number on your report. If anything looks unclear or causes you concern, bring the report to your next midwife or GP appointment and ask for it to be explained. You are always entitled to a clear explanation of your results. If a finding needs follow-up, you will be told this at the time of your scan or shortly afterwards.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What does 'intrauterine' mean on my report?

It means the pregnancy is in the correct location — inside the uterus. Seeing this term is reassuring because it indicates the pregnancy is not ectopic (outside the uterus), which would require urgent medical attention.

### What does 'single live intrauterine pregnancy' mean?

This is good news. It means one baby has been found with a visible heartbeat, and the pregnancy is situated correctly inside the uterus. It is one of the most reassuring phrases you can see on an early pregnancy ultrasound report.

### My scan dates are different from my LMP dates — should I worry?

A small difference is very common and usually not a concern. Scan measurements are generally more accurate than period dates for establishing gestational age, especially if your cycles are irregular. Your midwife will explain if any significant discrepancy needs further investigation.

*Speak to your midwife or GP if you have questions about your scan report or if any findings have been flagged for follow-up.*
