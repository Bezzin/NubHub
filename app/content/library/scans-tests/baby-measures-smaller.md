# What If Baby Measures Smaller Than Expected?

Being told your baby is measuring smaller than expected can be worrying, but there are many reasons for this — and most of them are completely benign. Here's what it means and what happens next.

## What "Measuring Smaller" Means {#means}

At your ultrasound scan, the sonographer measures your baby from the crown (top of the head) to the rump (bottom). This measurement, called the crown-rump length (CRL), is compared to the expected size for your gestational age. If your baby's CRL is smaller than the average for your dates, the sonographer may say your baby is "measuring small" or "measuring behind."

At the dating scan (12 weeks), a difference of up to **5-7 days** from your expected dates is considered normal. If the difference is more than 7 days, your due date may be adjusted. At earlier scans, a difference of up to 5 days is usually within normal limits.

It's important to know that "measuring small" doesn't mean your baby is unhealthy — it usually means your dates are slightly off or your baby is simply on the smaller side of normal.

## Common Reasons for a Small Measurement {#reasons}

There are many reasons your baby might measure smaller than expected:

- **Incorrect dates**: This is the most common reason. If your cycle was longer than 28 days, you ovulated later than day 14, or you misremembered your last period, you may be a few days less far along than you think.
- **Late ovulation**: Even with a regular cycle, ovulation can be delayed by stress, illness, travel, or just natural variation.
- **Small but healthy baby**: Some babies are just naturally smaller, just as some adults are shorter. If both parents are petite, the baby may be too.
- **IVF dating differences**: In IVF pregnancies, the dates are very precise, but even so, embryos grow at slightly different rates.
- **Earlier than expected at early scans**: At 6-8 weeks, dating is less precise and small differences are common.
- **Poor growth (IUGR)**: In some cases, the baby isn't growing as it should due to placental problems, maternal health conditions, or other factors. This is more of a concern in later pregnancy than at the 12-week scan.
- **Incorrect measurement**: While rare, the measurement can sometimes be slightly off if the baby is curled up or in an awkward position.

## What Happens After the Scan {#after}

If your baby measures smaller than expected, the sonographer will:

1. **Re-measure carefully**: They'll take several measurements to ensure accuracy. If the baby is in a difficult position, they may wait for the baby to move.
2. **Compare to your dates**: If the difference is less than 7 days (at 12 weeks) or 5 days (at 6-8 weeks), it's usually within normal limits. Your due date may be adjusted.
3. **Check the heartbeat and other structures**: If everything else looks healthy, a small measurement is less concerning.
4. **Arrange a repeat scan if needed**: If the difference is significant or there are other concerns, you may be asked to come back in 1-2 weeks to check growth.
5. **Adjust your due date**: If the CRL clearly indicates a different gestational age, your due date will be changed. This is common and nothing to worry about.

## When It's a Concern {#concern}

A small measurement becomes more concerning when:

- **The baby measures more than 7 days behind at 12 weeks**: This may indicate a growth issue, and a repeat scan will be arranged.
- **The baby doesn't grow between scans**: If a repeat scan shows no growth, this is a red flag. Your healthcare team will investigate further.
- **Other abnormalities are present**: If the baby has other structural concerns alongside the small size, a specialist referral may be needed.
- **You have risk factors**: If you have high blood pressure, diabetes, or a history of growth-restricted babies, your team will monitor growth more carefully.

In the first trimester, the most common reason for a small measurement is simply incorrect dating. True growth restriction is rarely diagnosed this early.

## What You Can Do {#do}

If your baby is measuring small, there's not much you can do to change the baby's size directly, but you can:

- **Attend all your scans**: Follow-up scans are crucial to check growth over time. Don't skip them.
- **Eat well**: A balanced diet with plenty of nutrients supports your baby's growth. See our nutrition guides.
- **Take your supplements**: Folic acid, vitamin D, and any prescribed supplements are important.
- **Don't smoke**: Smoking is a major cause of poor fetal growth. If you smoke, ask your GP for help quitting.
- **Rest**: While bed rest isn't usually recommended, reducing stress and getting enough sleep is sensible.
- **Ask questions**: If you're worried, ask your midwife. Don't suffer in silence.

## Will You Need More Scans? {#more-scans}

If your baby is only slightly small and the heartbeat and structures are normal, you may not need any extra scans. Your due date will be adjusted, and you'll continue with routine care.

If the measurement is significantly small or there are other concerns, you may be offered:

- A **repeat growth scan** in 1-2 weeks to check the baby is growing
- **Additional monitoring** throughout pregnancy
- A **referral to a fetal medicine specialist** if there are structural concerns

Most women whose baby measures slightly small go on to have healthy pregnancies and babies. The key is monitoring to ensure growth continues.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Can my due date change if the baby measures small?

Yes. If the CRL at your dating scan differs from your dates by more than 5-7 days, your due date will be adjusted based on the scan measurements. This is standard practice.

### Does a small baby mean I'll have a premature birth?

No. A baby that measures small in the first trimester is usually just small for its gestational age or the dates are off. Premature birth is more related to later pregnancy complications, not early scan measurements.

### Should I eat more if my baby is measuring small?

Not necessarily. Eating more won't directly make your baby bigger in the first trimester. A balanced, nutrient-rich diet is more important than extra calories. Talk to your midwife about your specific situation.
