# No Heartbeat at 7 Weeks: Should I Worry?

Finding out there's no visible heartbeat at your 7-week scan is frightening, and it is completely natural to feel anxious and upset. But it does not always mean the worst. At 7 weeks, the situation is often genuinely uncertain, and many people who leave a scan without a confirmed heartbeat go on to see one at a follow-up appointment a week or two later.

## Why There Might Be No Heartbeat at 7 Weeks

Seven weeks is still relatively early in pregnancy, and there are several reasons why a heartbeat may not be visible at this stage.

**Your dates may be slightly off.** Pregnancy is dated from the first day of your last menstrual period, but this assumes a 28-day cycle with ovulation around day 14. If your cycle is longer, irregular, or if you ovulated later than expected, you may actually be 5–6 weeks pregnant rather than 7. At 5–6 weeks, it is entirely normal for no heartbeat to be detectable yet.

**The embryo may be very small.** The foetal heartbeat typically becomes visible on an ultrasound from around 6 to 6.5 weeks, but this can vary. A very small embryo or one that is positioned awkwardly may not show a clear heartbeat even at 7 weeks on an abdominal scan.

**The type of scan matters.** An abdominal (tummy) ultrasound is less sensitive than a transvaginal (internal) scan in early pregnancy. If you were scanned abdominally, the sonographer may not have been able to pick up a heartbeat that a transvaginal scan would detect. Many EPUs will offer a transvaginal scan if nothing is seen abdominally at this stage.

**In some cases, it may indicate a missed miscarriage.** A missed miscarriage (also called a silent miscarriage) is when the embryo has stopped developing but the body has not yet recognised this. In this situation, there will be no visible heartbeat and the pregnancy sac or embryo may appear smaller than expected for the gestational age. This is a possibility that the sonographer will discuss with you — but it is not the only explanation, and it cannot be confirmed at a single scan if dates are uncertain.

## What Happens Next

If no heartbeat is seen at 7 weeks, the sonographer or EPU clinician will usually recommend one or more of the following:

- **A repeat scan in 7–14 days.** This is standard practice when the situation is uncertain. It gives the pregnancy time to develop to a point where the result is clearer. A heartbeat seen at this follow-up scan is a very reassuring sign.
- **Blood tests to measure hCG levels.** Human chorionic gonadotrophin (hCG) is the pregnancy hormone. Taking two measurements 48 hours apart and comparing them can give useful information — in a continuing healthy pregnancy, hCG roughly doubles every 48–72 hours in early pregnancy, though this pattern varies.
- **A transvaginal scan** if an abdominal scan was used first. The transvaginal scan gives a much clearer image in very early pregnancy and may show a heartbeat that was not visible externally.

It can feel agonising to be sent home and told to wait. If your symptoms change significantly — for example, if you develop heavy bleeding or severe one-sided pain — contact your EPU or call 111 rather than waiting for your follow-up appointment.

## What the Follow-Up Scan Will Tell You

At the follow-up scan, the sonographer will look for growth in the gestational sac and embryo, and for the presence of a heartbeat. The outcome falls broadly into three categories:

- **A heartbeat is now visible** — this is a positive and reassuring finding. A heartbeat seen at 8–9 weeks significantly reduces the risk of pregnancy loss.
- **The pregnancy has grown but there is still no heartbeat** — this makes a missed miscarriage more likely, and the team will discuss next steps with you.
- **There has been no growth since the first scan** — this is a strong indicator that the pregnancy has not continued, and the team will talk through your options for management.

Whatever the result, you will not be left to find out alone. EPU teams are experienced in supporting people through this uncertainty, and you should feel able to ask as many questions as you need.

## Looking After Yourself While You Wait

The wait between scans is one of the hardest parts of this experience. A few things that may help:

- Tell someone you trust what is happening — you don't have to go through the uncertainty alone.
- Be kind to yourself about what you can and cannot manage at work or socially during this time.
- Avoid searching for certainty online — individual stories vary enormously and rarely apply exactly to your situation.
- If you are struggling to cope emotionally, speak to your GP, who can offer support or referral to counselling.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How common is no heartbeat at 7 weeks?

It is fairly common, particularly when pregnancy dates are uncertain or cycles are irregular. Many people who attend an early scan at what they believe to be 7 weeks are actually a week or so earlier than that. A follow-up scan at 8–9 weeks often resolves the uncertainty, with a heartbeat visible in many cases.

### Should I prepare for the worst if there was no heartbeat at 7 weeks?

Not necessarily. The absence of a heartbeat at 7 weeks has several possible explanations, and incorrect dating is the most common. The recommendation to return for a repeat scan is standard practice precisely because a single scan at this gestation is often inconclusive. Wait for your follow-up result before drawing conclusions if you can.

### When does a heartbeat usually become visible on a scan?

The embryonic heartbeat typically becomes detectable on a transvaginal ultrasound from around 6 to 6.5 weeks of pregnancy. On an abdominal scan, it may not be visible until 7 weeks or slightly later. If your dates are uncertain, there may be a gap between when you expect to see it and when it actually becomes visible.
