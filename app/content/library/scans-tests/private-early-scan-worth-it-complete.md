# Is a Private Early Scan Worth It - Complete Guide

Wondering if an early private scan is worth it? Break down the benefits, limitations, and best timing for reassurance.

## Why this matters

Information online is often inconsistent or written for a different healthcare system. This guide gives a clear, UK-specific overview of what to expect and what questions to ask your midwife.

## The key points

- **Timing matters.** The gestational age at which a scan or symptom occurs changes what you can expect to see and what decisions are available.
- **Measurements are estimates.** Ultrasound and blood tests carry a margin of error; a single result is rarely the whole picture.
- **Private and NHS pathways overlap.** Many private services use the same equipment and sonographers as the NHS, but differ in appointment length, timing, and follow-up.
- **Symptoms should always be checked.** Pain, bleeding, reduced movements, or anything unusual warrants a call to your maternity unit.

## What to expect at an appointment

The clinician will start with a brief history: how many weeks pregnant you are, any symptoms, your medical background, and previous pregnancies. The examination depends on the issue. For scans, this involves an ultrasound, most often transabdominal, sometimes transvaginal for a clearer early view. For complications, it may include blood pressure, urine samples, blood tests, or a physical examination.

Results are often available immediately for ultrasound, while blood tests may take a day or two. The clinician should explain what they can see, what they cannot yet rule out, and whether a follow-up is needed.

## Questions worth asking

- What exactly are you checking for today?
- What can you see clearly, and what is still too early to assess?
- Do I need a follow-up appointment, and if so, when?
- What symptoms should prompt me to call the maternity unit?

## Red flags: when to seek help immediately

Certain symptoms always warrant urgent assessment:

- Heavy vaginal bleeding
- Severe or worsening abdominal pain
- A fever above 38C or chills with feeling unwell
- Sudden swelling, visual disturbance, or severe headache (signs of pre-eclampsia)
- Reduced or absent fetal movements (from around 20 weeks)

Call your maternity unit, GP, or 111 straight away. In an emergency, dial 999.

## The bottom line

Most pregnancies progress without major problems, and the checks described here exist to catch the minority that do not. Understanding what is routine, what is urgent, and what is simply a variation of normal helps you feel more in control between appointments. When in doubt, always ask your midwife.
