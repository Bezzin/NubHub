# Empty Sac at 7 Weeks: What Happens Next?

Seeing an empty gestational sac on a scan at 7 weeks is frightening, and it's natural to fear the worst. But at this stage, a follow-up scan is almost always needed before any firm conclusion can be reached. Here is what an empty sac at 7 weeks can mean, what happens next, and how to navigate the uncertainty while you wait.

## What an Empty Sac at 7 Weeks Means

By 7 weeks of pregnancy, a scan would normally be expected to show a fetal pole — the earliest visible sign of an embryo — and often a visible heartbeat, particularly on a transvaginal (internal) scan. When neither is seen and only an empty sac is visible, there are two main possibilities:

**Your dates may be earlier than you think.** Even a few days' difference in ovulation timing can mean you are not as far along as your last menstrual period suggests. If you ovulated later than average in your cycle, or if your cycle is irregular, you could be measuring closer to 5 or 6 weeks gestationally rather than 7 — and at that stage, an empty sac is entirely expected. This is more likely if the sac itself is measuring smaller than expected for 7 weeks.

**The pregnancy may have stopped developing.** An empty sac that measures the size expected for 7 weeks, with no fetal pole visible, raises the possibility of a blighted ovum (also called an anembryonic pregnancy). In this situation, a gestational sac forms and grows but the embryo does not develop. This is a form of early pregnancy loss and is thought to be caused, in most cases, by a chromosomal abnormality in the fertilised egg — something that happens by chance and is not caused by anything you did or didn't do.

At 7 weeks, these two explanations can be genuinely difficult to distinguish on a scan, which is why a follow-up scan is always required before a diagnosis is made.

## What Happens Next

If your scan shows an empty sac at 7 weeks, your healthcare team will not make a diagnosis at that appointment. Instead, you will typically be asked to return for a follow-up scan, usually around 1 to 2 weeks later. This waiting period, though very hard, is necessary to allow time for the pregnancy to show clear development if dating was simply off.

At the follow-up scan, the sonographer will look for:
- A fetal pole that was not visible before
- A heartbeat
- Growth in the size of the gestational sac

If a fetal pole and heartbeat are now visible, the pregnancy is progressing and the earlier empty sac finding was due to early dating. If the sac is still empty and has not grown as expected, a diagnosis of missed miscarriage or blighted ovum is likely to be confirmed.

In the UK, most early pregnancy assessments are managed through an Early Pregnancy Unit (EPU). Your GP, midwife, or A&E department can refer you to one if you have not already been seen there. EPUs are experienced in supporting people through exactly this kind of uncertain waiting period and can answer questions specific to your scan findings.

## Coping With the Wait

Waiting 1 to 2 weeks for a follow-up scan when you don't know if your pregnancy is continuing is one of the hardest things early pregnancy can ask of you. A few things that may help:

- **Ask your EPU what your scan measurements showed.** Understanding whether the sac size is consistent with earlier-than-expected dating can help you assess how likely a dating discrepancy is.
- **Lean on trusted support.** You don't have to wait alone. A partner, close friend, or family member can be with you at your follow-up scan.
- **Be kind to yourself.** There is no way to influence the outcome of a follow-up scan by worrying or by staying calm — but looking after your own wellbeing during the wait matters.
- **Contact your EPU if you develop new symptoms.** If you experience heavy bleeding or severe pain before your follow-up scan, contact your EPU, call 111, or go to A&E.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### How common is an empty sac at 7 weeks?

It is not unusual. Many cases of an empty sac at 7 weeks turn out to be due to incorrect dating — particularly for people with irregular cycles or later ovulation. A follow-up scan is essential before any diagnosis is confirmed, as a single scan finding at this stage is rarely conclusive.

### Can an empty sac at 7 weeks still result in a healthy pregnancy?

Yes, it can — if the underlying cause is that you are earlier in your pregnancy than thought. If the follow-up scan shows a fetal pole and heartbeat, the pregnancy is progressing normally and the earlier finding was simply a matter of timing. There is genuine reason for cautious hope, particularly if your sac was measuring smaller than expected for 7 weeks at the first scan.

### What is a blighted ovum?

A blighted ovum (anembryonic pregnancy) is a type of early pregnancy loss in which the gestational sac develops but the embryo does not. It is thought to be caused in most cases by a chromosomal problem with the fertilised egg that occurs by chance. It is not caused by anything the pregnant person did, and it does not indicate a problem with future fertility. If this diagnosis is confirmed, your EPU will discuss management options with you, which typically include expectant management (waiting for the loss to pass naturally), medical management, or surgical management.

Speak to your midwife, GP, or Early Pregnancy Unit for personalised guidance based on your specific scan findings.
