# Slow-Rising hCG and Miscarriage Risk

What slow-rising hCG means for pregnancy viability, how it is investigated, and what to expect from follow-up testing and scans.

## What Counts as Slow-Rising hCG

hCG is described as slow-rising when it increases by less than around 35 to 49 percent over 48 hours. While the classic teaching is that hCG doubles every two days, real-world data shows that many healthy pregnancies rise more slowly, especially from 6 weeks onwards. A single slow rise is not enough to diagnose a miscarriage. Your team will usually arrange two or three blood tests over a week and an early ultrasound to build a full picture. Decisions are never made on one number, because the natural range is so wide.

## How Slow-Rising hCG Is Investigated

If your hCG is rising slower than expected, your care team will arrange repeat bloods and, once levels are high enough (usually above 1,500 to 2,000 IU/L for a transvaginal scan), an early ultrasound. The scan looks for a gestational sac, yolk sac, fetal pole and heartbeat. If the scan shows a pregnancy in the uterus with a heartbeat, slow hCG becomes less worrying. If the sac is empty or smaller than expected, your team may diagnose a missed miscarriage and discuss your options: expectant management, medical management or surgical management. If no pregnancy is seen in the uterus, ectopic pregnancy must be ruled out urgently.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Does slow-rising hCG always mean miscarriage?**

No. Some healthy pregnancies have slower hCG rises, especially after 6 weeks. Your team will use repeat bloods and an ultrasound before making any diagnosis.

**How many hCG blood tests will I need?**

Usually two tests 48 hours apart, sometimes a third a week later. Once hCG is high enough for a scan, ultrasound becomes the main tool for checking viability.

**What are my options if a miscarriage is confirmed?**

In the UK you can choose expectant management (letting it happen naturally), medical management (tablets to start the process), or surgical management (a small procedure). Your team will explain the pros and cons of each.
