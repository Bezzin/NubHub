# What Happens After a Molar Pregnancy

After treatment for a molar pregnancy, follow-up care is essential to ensure no abnormal cells remain. This monitoring is coordinated through a specialist centre and, for most women, is reassuring and straightforward. Understanding the process helps you know what to expect over the coming months.

## hCG Monitoring and the Registration Process

You will be registered with one of the UK's specialist trophoblastic disease centres (usually Charing Cross Hospital in London or Weston Park Hospital in Sheffield). They will arrange regular hCG blood or urine tests, initially every one to two weeks, to track your levels back to normal. For a complete mole, monitoring continues for six months after hCG returns to normal; for a partial mole, it is shorter. This is because in a small percentage of cases, persistent disease can develop, and early detection makes treatment highly successful.

## Trying Again and Long-Term Outlook

You will be advised to avoid pregnancy for six months after your hCG normalises, to ensure monitoring is not confused by pregnancy hormones. Reliable contraception is essential during this time. Once cleared, you can try to conceive, and the vast majority of women go on to have normal, healthy pregnancies: the recurrence rate is low, around one to two percent. If persistent disease does develop, usually treated with low-dose chemotherapy, the cure rate is nearly 100 percent and fertility is preserved.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### 1. How long will I need hCG monitoring for?

Usually six months after your levels return to normal for a complete mole, and less for a partial mole.

### 2. Can I get pregnant while being monitored?

No, you must use reliable contraception until you are cleared, as pregnancy hormones interfere with monitoring.

### 3. Will a molar pregnancy affect my future fertility?

Almost never. Even if low-dose chemotherapy is needed, fertility is typically preserved.
