# Miscarriage: A Complete Guide

Miscarriage is the loss of a pregnancy before 24 weeks. It's heartbreaking but unfortunately common. This guide is written with compassion to help you understand what's happening and where to get support.

## How Common Is Miscarriage? {#how-common}

Miscarriage affects approximately 1 in 4 pregnancies. The risk decreases as pregnancy progresses: at 6 weeks, the risk is about 10%; by 9 weeks, it drops to about 3%; by 12 weeks, it's less than 2%. Once a heartbeat is seen on ultrasound, miscarriage risk drops significantly.

## Signs of Miscarriage {#signs}

The main signs are: vaginal bleeding (especially bright red or heavy); abdominal cramping; passing clots or tissue; loss of pregnancy symptoms. However, many women experience bleeding and go on to have healthy pregnancies.

## What Causes Miscarriage? {#causes}

Most miscarriages are caused by chromosomal abnormalities in the baby - not by anything the mother did or didn't do. Other causes can include: blood clotting disorders; hormonal problems; immune conditions; structural problems with the uterus.

## Types of Miscarriage {#types}

Types include: threatened miscarriage (bleeding but pregnancy continues); missed miscarriage (baby has died but no symptoms); incomplete miscarriage (some tissue remains); complete miscarriage (all tissue has passed); blighted ovum (empty sac).

## What to Do If You Think You're Miscarrying {#what-to-do}

Call your GP or midwife, or go to your local Early Pregnancy Unit (EPU). They'll arrange an ultrasound to check the baby. Don't go to A&E unless bleeding is very heavy or you're in severe pain.

## After a Miscarriage {#after}

Physical recovery usually takes 1-2 weeks. Emotional recovery takes longer. You can try again when you feel ready - there's no need to wait. Most women who have one miscarriage go on to have successful pregnancies.

## Getting Support {#support}

Support is available from: The Miscarriage Association (miscarriageassociation.org.uk); Tommy's (tommys.org); Sands (sands.org.uk); your GP and midwife. Don't go through this alone.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Will I definitely miscarry if I'm bleeding?

No. Up to 25% of women experience bleeding in early pregnancy, and many go on to have healthy babies. Contact your GP or EPU for an ultrasound to check.

### When can I try again after a miscarriage?

Physically, you can try again after your next period. Emotionally, wait until you feel ready. There's no medical reason to wait, and most women who conceive after miscarriage have successful pregnancies.
