# Ectopic Pregnancy: Symptoms, Treatment & Recovery

An ectopic pregnancy is a serious medical condition in which a fertilised egg implants somewhere outside the uterus — most commonly in a fallopian tube. The pregnancy cannot develop normally and cannot be moved to the uterus. Without treatment, an ectopic pregnancy can become life-threatening. Knowing the symptoms and when to seek urgent help is important for anyone who is, or could be, pregnant.

## What Is an Ectopic Pregnancy? {#what-is-it}

An ectopic pregnancy occurs when a fertilised egg implants outside the uterus. In the vast majority of cases this is in one of the fallopian tubes, but it can also occur in the cervix, ovary, or abdomen — though these are rarer locations.

The fallopian tube is not designed to contain a growing pregnancy. As the embryo grows, it can cause the tube to stretch and, if untreated, to rupture. A ruptured ectopic pregnancy is a medical emergency that causes serious internal bleeding and requires immediate surgery.

Ectopic pregnancies cannot be transplanted into the uterus. Treatment always ends the pregnancy, which is a source of grief for many people who very much wanted to be pregnant.

Certain factors are associated with a higher likelihood of ectopic pregnancy, including a previous ectopic pregnancy, a history of pelvic inflammatory disease, previous fallopian tube surgery, or becoming pregnant while using an intrauterine device (IUD). However, ectopic pregnancies also occur in people with none of these risk factors.

## Symptoms {#symptoms}

Symptoms typically develop between weeks 4 and 10 of pregnancy, often before a person knows they are pregnant or before their first scan. Common symptoms include:

- **One-sided abdominal or pelvic pain** — often on the side where the ectopic pregnancy is located; the pain may be sharp or cramp-like and can come and go or be constant
- **Vaginal bleeding** — this is often lighter or darker than a typical period and may be mistaken for spotting
- **Shoulder tip pain** — pain at the tip of one or both shoulders, caused by blood irritating the diaphragm; this is an important warning sign that internal bleeding may be occurring
- **Dizziness, faintness, or collapse** — these can indicate significant internal bleeding and require a 999 call
- **Pain when going to the toilet** — discomfort when opening the bowels or passing urine

Not everyone with an ectopic pregnancy will have all of these symptoms, and symptoms can be mild at first. If you have any combination of early pregnancy signs with one-sided pain and bleeding, seek medical assessment promptly rather than waiting to see if it resolves.

**If you experience severe pain, shoulder tip pain, dizziness, or collapse — call 999 or go to A&E immediately.** A ruptured ectopic pregnancy is a life-threatening emergency.

## Diagnosis {#diagnosis}

Ectopic pregnancy is diagnosed through a combination of tests:

**Blood tests** — your hCG (human chorionic gonadotrophin) level is measured. In an ectopic pregnancy, hCG levels often rise more slowly than expected or plateau. Serial blood tests taken 48 hours apart help identify this pattern.

**Ultrasound** — a transvaginal ultrasound (internal scan) is usually needed to get a clear view at this early stage. The sonographer looks for a pregnancy sac inside the uterus; if none is visible but hCG levels indicate a pregnancy, an ectopic is strongly suspected.

**Laparoscopy** — in some cases, keyhole surgery is needed to confirm the diagnosis and treat the ectopic at the same time.

If ectopic pregnancy is suspected, you will usually be referred to an Early Pregnancy Unit (EPU) for assessment. You do not need a GP referral to attend most EPUs — you can self-refer or be referred by 111.

## Treatment {#treatment}

There are three main approaches to treating an ectopic pregnancy, and the right one depends on how early the ectopic is detected, your symptoms, and your hCG levels:

**Expectant management (watch and wait)** — if the ectopic pregnancy is very early, hCG levels are low and falling, and you have no serious symptoms, your doctor may recommend monitoring with repeat blood tests. The pregnancy may resolve on its own without intervention. You will be closely monitored throughout.

**Medical management** — an injection of methotrexate, a drug that stops the cells of the ectopic pregnancy from growing, is offered when the ectopic is detected early and has not ruptured. You will need follow-up blood tests to confirm the treatment has worked. Methotrexate is not suitable for everyone and has certain side effects and restrictions, which your doctor will discuss with you.

**Surgical management** — if the tube has ruptured, if there is significant pain, or if medical management is not suitable, surgery is performed. This is usually laparoscopic (keyhole) surgery. The surgeon removes the ectopic pregnancy, and in some cases the affected fallopian tube is also removed. Open surgery is needed if the situation is an emergency.

## Recovery and Emotional Impact {#recovery}

Physical recovery after an ectopic pregnancy varies depending on the treatment. Recovery from surgery typically takes a few weeks, and your medical team will give you specific guidance about activity and returning to work.

The emotional impact of an ectopic pregnancy is significant. It is a pregnancy loss as well as a frightening medical experience, and many people find the grief complex — particularly because the physical urgency of treatment can leave little space to process what has happened. Feelings of shock, sadness, guilt, and anxiety about future pregnancies are all common and valid.

In the UK, the Ectopic Pregnancy Trust offers specialist support, information, and a helpline for people who have experienced an ectopic pregnancy. Your GP or midwife can also refer you for counselling or talking therapy if you are struggling.

## Future Fertility {#fertility}

Most people who have had an ectopic pregnancy are able to conceive again. If one fallopian tube was removed, it is still possible to conceive naturally through the remaining tube. If both tubes are affected or removed, IVF becomes the primary route to pregnancy.

In any future pregnancy, you will be offered an early scan — usually at around 6 to 7 weeks — to confirm that the pregnancy is located inside the uterus. This is standard practice after an ectopic and is not a reflection of anything being wrong; it is simply careful monitoring.

Your doctor will usually advise waiting for at least two to three menstrual cycles after treatment before trying to conceive again, and longer if you received methotrexate. This allows your body to recover and ensures the drug has fully cleared your system.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### How do I know if my pain could be an ectopic pregnancy?

One-sided abdominal or pelvic pain in early pregnancy — particularly if combined with vaginal bleeding, shoulder tip pain, or dizziness — warrants urgent medical assessment. You do not need to be certain it is an ectopic before seeking help. Call 111 for guidance, or go directly to A&E or your nearest Early Pregnancy Unit if symptoms are severe or worsening. A ruptured ectopic is a medical emergency — call 999 if you collapse or have sudden severe pain.

### Can I get pregnant again after an ectopic pregnancy?

Yes, many people go on to have successful pregnancies after an ectopic. If one tube was removed, natural conception is still possible through the remaining tube. Your care team will offer early scanning in any future pregnancy to confirm the location of the embryo. It is worth discussing your individual situation with your GP or a fertility specialist if you have concerns.

### Will I always need surgery for an ectopic pregnancy?

Not necessarily. If the ectopic is detected early and your condition is stable, medical management with methotrexate or expectant monitoring may be appropriate. However, a ruptured ectopic always requires emergency surgery. The right treatment for you will be decided by your medical team based on your individual circumstances.
