# Waters Breaking in Early Pregnancy

If your waters break before 24 weeks of pregnancy, it is known as preterm prelabour rupture of membranes (PPROM), or sometimes previable PPROM. This is a serious and distressing situation, but it does not always mean the pregnancy cannot continue. Understanding what happens helps you prepare for the conversations and decisions ahead.

## What It Means and Immediate Steps

When the amniotic sac ruptures early, the fluid that cushions and protects the baby is lost or reduced. You should go to your maternity unit immediately so they can confirm whether your waters have gone, check for infection, monitor the baby's heartbeat, and assess the amount of remaining fluid. You may be admitted for observation, given antibiotics to prevent infection, and offered steroids if you are beyond 23 weeks to help the baby's lungs develop. The team will discuss the realistic outlook, which depends on gestation and fluid levels.

## Outcomes and Ongoing Care

The outlook depends heavily on how many weeks pregnant you are. Before 20 weeks, the chances of the baby surviving are low because the lungs may not develop adequately without fluid. After 23 to 24 weeks, survival chances improve significantly with specialist neonatal care. Some women do re-seal and continue the pregnancy with monitoring. You will be supported by a specialist team including obstetricians and neonatologists, and offered counselling. Whatever the outcome, psychological support is available and important.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### 1. Can my waters re-seal after breaking early?

In some cases, yes, particularly if the tear is small and fluid loss is minimal, but it is not common.

### 2. What is the outlook if my waters break before 24 weeks?

It depends on gestation. Before 20 weeks it is guarded; after 23 weeks, chances improve with specialist care.

### 3. Will I need to stay in hospital?

You may be admitted for initial monitoring and antibiotics, then managed as an outpatient depending on your situation.
