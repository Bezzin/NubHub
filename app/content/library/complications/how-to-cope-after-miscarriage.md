# How to Cope After a Miscarriage

Miscarriage is emotionally devastating. Here's how to cope with the physical and emotional recovery — and how to find support when you need it.

## Physical Recovery

What happens physically after a miscarriage depends on how far along you were and how the miscarriage was managed. In general:

- **Bleeding** typically lasts one to three weeks. It may be heavier than a period at first, then ease off gradually. If bleeding becomes very heavy — soaking more than one pad an hour for two or more hours — contact your Early Pregnancy Unit (EPU) or call 111.
- **Cramping** is common in the first few days and is usually manageable with paracetamol. Avoid ibuprofen if you are uncertain about your situation — your EPU or GP can advise.
- **Hormones** take time to settle. Your pregnancy hormone (hCG) levels drop gradually after a miscarriage, and it can take four to six weeks before they return to pre-pregnancy levels. During this time you may experience breast tenderness, bloating, mood changes, and fatigue as your body adjusts.
- **Your period** usually returns four to six weeks after a miscarriage, though for some women it takes a little longer. The first period may be heavier or different from usual.
- **Signs of infection** to watch for include a fever, foul-smelling vaginal discharge, or increasing rather than decreasing pain. If you notice any of these, contact your GP or EPU promptly, or call 111 out of hours.

Be gentle with yourself physically. Your body has been through something significant, even if the pregnancy was very early.

## Emotional Recovery

There is no right or wrong way to grieve a miscarriage. Some women feel profound sadness and loss from the moment they knew they were pregnant. Others feel more numb or disconnected. Some feel relief mixed with sadness. Grief after pregnancy loss can be complicated and does not always follow a predictable path.

**Give yourself permission to grieve.** Society can be awkward about early pregnancy loss, sometimes implying it is less significant than other bereavements. But if this pregnancy mattered to you, your grief is valid — regardless of how many weeks you were.

**Talk about it.** Sharing what you are going through with your partner, a trusted friend, or a family member can help enormously. If the people around you are struggling to know what to say, it can help to tell them directly what you need — whether that is someone to sit with you, practical help, or simply acknowledgement that this is hard.

**Consider a support group.** The Miscarriage Association (miscarriageassociation.org.uk) offers a helpline, online support groups, and information specifically for people who have experienced pregnancy loss. Many women find it helps to speak with others who understand from their own experience.

**Do not rush trying again.** There is no obligation to try to conceive again quickly. Wait until you feel physically and emotionally ready — for both of you, if you have a partner.

**Watch for signs that you need extra support.** It is normal to feel sad, anxious, or tearful for some weeks after a miscarriage. But if low mood or anxiety is persistent, worsening, or preventing you from functioning, speak to your GP. You may benefit from counselling, which your GP can refer you to, or which you can access through organisations such as the Miscarriage Association or Sands (if your loss was later in pregnancy).

## Looking After Your Relationship

Miscarriage can affect couples differently. You and your partner may grieve in different ways or at different paces. Try to talk openly about how you are both feeling, and give each other space to process the loss in your own way. If the miscarriage is creating significant strain in your relationship, couples counselling can be a helpful resource.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How long should I wait before trying again?

Physically, your body may be ready to conceive again once you have had a period, which typically returns four to six weeks after a miscarriage. There is no medical reason to wait longer after a single miscarriage, though your EPU or GP may give specific guidance depending on your circumstances. Emotionally, wait until you both feel ready — there is no rush, and it is entirely reasonable to take more time.

### When will I feel normal again?

Physical recovery usually takes one to two weeks, though fatigue and hormonal symptoms may linger a little longer. Emotional recovery varies enormously from person to person — some women feel more like themselves within a few weeks, while others find it takes several months. Both are completely normal. If you find that grief or anxiety is significantly affecting your daily life, please do reach out to your GP, who can arrange appropriate support.

### Is it common to feel anxious about trying again?

Yes, very. Fear of another miscarriage is one of the most common feelings people describe after pregnancy loss. This anxiety is understandable and does not mean something will go wrong again. If anxiety about a future pregnancy feels overwhelming, talking to a counsellor or your GP before you start trying can be helpful.

Speak to your GP or midwife for personalised advice about your physical and emotional recovery after miscarriage — you do not have to manage this alone.
