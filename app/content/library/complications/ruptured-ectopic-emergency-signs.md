# Ruptured Ectopic Pregnancy: Emergency Signs

A ruptured ectopic pregnancy is a life-threatening medical emergency. If you or someone you are with shows the signs described on this page, call 999 immediately — do not drive to hospital and do not wait to see if symptoms improve.

## What Is a Ruptured Ectopic Pregnancy?

An ectopic pregnancy occurs when a fertilised egg implants outside the uterus — most commonly in a fallopian tube. The fallopian tube cannot expand the way the uterus does, so as the pregnancy grows, it puts increasing pressure on the tube wall. If not treated in time, the tube can rupture (burst), causing internal bleeding that is rapidly life-threatening.

Ectopic pregnancies cannot survive and cannot be moved into the uterus. Treatment is always necessary. When caught early — before rupture — an ectopic pregnancy can often be treated with medication or a planned surgical procedure. A rupture, however, is an emergency requiring immediate surgery.

## Warning Signs Before Rupture

Many women with an ectopic pregnancy experience warning signs in the weeks before any rupture occurs. These do not always feel alarming, which is why ectopic pregnancy can be missed. Early warning signs include:

- One-sided lower abdominal pain — often on the same side as the affected tube
- Vaginal bleeding or spotting, which may be light and dark in colour
- Shoulder tip pain — an early sign of internal bleeding irritating the diaphragm
- Pain with urination or bowel movements
- Feeling generally unwell or faint

If you are pregnant and experience one-sided abdominal pain with or without bleeding, contact your GP, midwife, or early pregnancy unit (EPU) promptly. You do not need to wait until symptoms are severe.

## Emergency Signs of a Ruptured Ectopic

These signs indicate that a rupture may have occurred. This is a medical emergency — call 999 immediately.

**Sudden, severe abdominal pain.** This is typically described as a sharp, intense pain that comes on suddenly. It may begin on one side but can quickly spread across the abdomen. The pain is often much more severe than ordinary cramps or one-sided ache.

**Shoulder tip pain.** Blood from internal bleeding can accumulate beneath the diaphragm, causing referred pain at the tip of the shoulder. This is a significant warning sign and is often described as an aching or stabbing sensation at the very top of the shoulder. It tends to worsen when lying down.

**Dizziness, feeling faint, or collapsing.** Internal bleeding causes a rapid drop in blood pressure, which produces dizziness, light-headedness, or loss of consciousness. If someone collapses, call 999 immediately.

**Rapid heartbeat or palpitations.** The heart speeds up in response to blood loss, trying to maintain circulation. A racing or pounding heartbeat alongside abdominal pain is a serious sign.

**Paleness and clamminess.** Pale or grey skin that feels clammy or cold is a sign of shock caused by blood loss.

**Extreme weakness or inability to stand.** Some women describe suddenly being unable to support their own weight.

Any combination of these signs in a pregnant woman — or a woman who could potentially be pregnant — requires an emergency 999 call.

## What Happens at Hospital

If a ruptured ectopic is suspected, emergency surgery (usually laparoscopic — keyhole surgery) is performed to stop the internal bleeding and remove the affected fallopian tube or repair it if possible. A blood transfusion may be needed in cases of significant blood loss.

Recovery after emergency surgery takes longer than after planned ectopic treatment, but most women do recover fully. Fertility after ectopic pregnancy depends on several factors, including whether both tubes remain intact — your surgeon or gynaecologist will talk you through this after you have recovered.

## After a Ruptured Ectopic

Experiencing a ruptured ectopic pregnancy is a traumatic event, physically and emotionally. It is both a pregnancy loss and a serious medical emergency. Many women describe feeling frightened, shocked, and grieving after the experience.

NHS support is available, and your GP can refer you for bereavement counselling or psychological support. The Ectopic Pregnancy Trust (ectopic.org.uk) is a UK charity that offers information and peer support for those affected.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What are the signs of a ruptured ectopic pregnancy?

The main emergency signs are: sudden, severe abdominal pain (often starting on one side), shoulder tip pain caused by internal bleeding irritating the diaphragm, dizziness or fainting, rapid heartbeat, and pallor with clammy skin. These signs can appear together or rapidly one after another. A ruptured ectopic causes life-threatening internal bleeding — call 999 immediately if you experience these symptoms. Do not wait to see if they pass.

### Can a ruptured ectopic pregnancy be fatal?

Yes, without prompt emergency treatment a ruptured ectopic pregnancy can be fatal due to internal bleeding. It remains one of the most serious obstetric emergencies in the UK. This is why knowing the signs and calling 999 without delay is so important. Outcomes are very good when surgery happens quickly — the danger comes from delayed treatment.

### How quickly does a rupture happen?

There is no single answer — some ectopic pregnancies are caught before rupture; others rupture with very little warning. The risk of rupture generally increases as the pregnancy grows, typically from around 6–8 weeks, though it can happen earlier or later. This is why one-sided pain or shoulder tip pain in early pregnancy should always be assessed promptly rather than waited on.
