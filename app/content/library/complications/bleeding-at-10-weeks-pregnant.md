# Bleeding at 10 Weeks Pregnant

Seeing blood at 10 weeks can be terrifying. Your mind immediately goes to the worst-case scenario, and the wait to find out what's happening can feel unbearable. The reality is that bleeding at 10 weeks has a range of causes — some completely harmless, some that need monitoring, and some that require prompt care. This page explains what to look for, what different types of bleeding can mean, and when to get help.

## Common Causes of Bleeding at 10 Weeks {#section-1}

At 10 weeks you are nearing the end of the first trimester. The most common causes of bleeding at this stage include:

**Cervical changes (cervical ectropion)** — During pregnancy, hormonal changes cause the cells on the surface of the cervix to change and become more sensitive. This is called a cervical ectropion (or erosion). It is entirely benign and can cause light spotting, particularly after sex or after an internal examination. Many women only discover they have one when they're investigated for spotting and are told everything looks fine.

**Subchorionic haematoma** — This is a collection of blood between the placenta and the uterine wall. It is one of the more common causes of bleeding in the first trimester and is usually picked up on an ultrasound scan. Many subchorionic haematomas resolve without treatment and do not affect the pregnancy outcome, though they are monitored carefully.

**Implantation-related or early placental bleeding** — Occasionally, bleeding occurs as the placenta continues to develop and embed more deeply. This type of bleeding tends to be light and short-lived.

**Infection** — A vaginal or urinary tract infection can sometimes cause light spotting. Your GP or midwife can check for this and treat it safely in pregnancy.

**Miscarriage** — Bleeding at 10 weeks can be a sign that a miscarriage is occurring or has occurred. This is often accompanied by cramping and a heavier flow, though not always. It is important not to assume the worst from spotting alone — many women who bleed in early pregnancy go on to have healthy babies — but it does need to be investigated.

## What to Do If You're Bleeding at 10 Weeks {#what-to-do}

Whatever the amount of bleeding, contact a healthcare professional the same day. Do not wait to see if it settles on its own before getting checked.

**Who to contact:**
- Your **GP** — they can refer you to the Early Pregnancy Unit (EPU)
- Your **midwife** (if you have been seen for a booking appointment)
- The **Early Pregnancy Unit (EPU)** directly, if your hospital allows self-referral — many EPUs in the UK accept women who call directly without a GP referral; it is worth checking your local EPU's policy
- **NHS 111** — if you cannot reach your GP or midwife, 111 can advise and refer you

The EPU will usually arrange an ultrasound scan, which is the most effective way to determine what is happening. A scan at 10 weeks can confirm a heartbeat, assess the location of the pregnancy, and look for any causes such as a subchorionic haematoma.

## What Different Types of Bleeding Can Mean {#types-of-bleeding}

**Light pink or brown spotting** — Often the most benign. Brown blood in particular is usually old blood that has taken time to leave the body. Spotting after sex or an internal examination, with no other symptoms, is commonly caused by cervical sensitivity and is often nothing to worry about. Still get it checked.

**Bright red blood** — More active bleeding that warrants same-day assessment. Bright red bleeding does not automatically mean miscarriage, but it should be evaluated promptly.

**Heavy bleeding with clots or strong cramping** — This needs urgent attention. Go to A&E or call 999 if bleeding is very heavy, or call 111 if bleeding is significant but not immediately alarming.

## When to Go to A&E or Call 999 {#when-to-go-ae}

Seek emergency help immediately if you experience:

- Very heavy bleeding (soaking through a pad in an hour or less)
- Severe abdominal pain, particularly one-sided pain
- Feeling faint, dizzy, or like you might pass out
- Shoulder tip pain (which can indicate internal bleeding from an ectopic pregnancy)

At 10 weeks, ectopic pregnancy is less likely as most ectopic pregnancies present earlier, but it remains a possibility if the pregnancy location has not been confirmed on a previous scan.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Is bleeding at 10 weeks always a sign of miscarriage?

No. Bleeding at 10 weeks is frightening, but it does not always mean miscarriage. Many women who experience spotting or light bleeding in the first trimester go on to have healthy pregnancies. The only reliable way to know what is causing the bleeding is an ultrasound scan through your EPU or GP. Try to get an assessment rather than assuming the worst.

### When should I worry about bleeding at 10 weeks?

Any bleeding at 10 weeks should be assessed by a healthcare professional. Seek same-day care for any bleeding, and treat it as urgent if the bleeding is heavy, accompanied by strong cramping, or you feel unwell, faint, or have one-sided pain.

### Should I go to A&E or the EPU?

For mild to moderate bleeding without severe pain, contact your GP, midwife, or EPU for an urgent appointment. For heavy bleeding, severe pain, shoulder tip pain, or if you feel faint or very unwell, go to A&E or call 999. If you are unsure, call 111 for guidance.
