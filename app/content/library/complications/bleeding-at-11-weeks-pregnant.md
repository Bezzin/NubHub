# Bleeding at 11 Weeks Pregnant

Discovering blood at 11 weeks is frightening, and the anxiety of not knowing what it means can feel overwhelming. You are close to the end of the first trimester — a milestone many parents are counting down to — and any threat to that feels especially difficult. The important thing to know is that bleeding at 11 weeks has several possible causes, and many women who bleed at this stage go on to have healthy pregnancies. What matters most is getting assessed promptly so you know what's happening.

## Common Causes of Bleeding at 11 Weeks {#section-1}

At 11 weeks, the pregnancy is well established but still within the first trimester. The possible causes of bleeding include:

**Cervical sensitivity (cervical ectropion)** — Hormonal changes during pregnancy make the surface of the cervix more fragile. This is called a cervical ectropion and it is benign — it poses no risk to you or your baby. It can cause light spotting, particularly after sex or internal examination. It is one of the most common causes of innocent spotting throughout the first trimester.

**Subchorionic haematoma** — This is a pocket of blood between the placenta and the wall of the uterus. It is a relatively common finding in early pregnancy and is often picked up incidentally on a scan arranged to investigate bleeding. Many subchorionic haematomas resolve without any treatment and do not harm the pregnancy, though they are monitored. The bleed may be absorbed slowly, which can cause brown spotting over a period of days or weeks.

**Infection** — A vaginal infection, urinary tract infection, or sexually transmitted infection can occasionally cause spotting or light bleeding. Your GP or the Early Pregnancy Unit can test for this and treat any infection safely in pregnancy.

**Miscarriage** — Bleeding at 11 weeks can indicate a miscarriage is starting, has occurred, or is threatened. It is often — though not always — accompanied by cramping and a progressively heavier flow. Spotting alone does not confirm miscarriage, and only an ultrasound can determine whether the pregnancy is continuing. Many women who bleed in the first trimester do not miscarry.

**Implantation or placental changes** — Occasionally, low-level bleeding occurs as the placenta continues to develop and anchor more firmly. This is usually light and short-lived.

## What to Do If You're Bleeding at 11 Weeks {#what-to-do}

Contact a healthcare professional the same day — do not wait to see if the bleeding stops on its own before seeking assessment.

**Who to contact:**
- Your **GP** can refer you urgently to the Early Pregnancy Unit (EPU)
- Your **midwife**, if you have been booked in for antenatal care
- The **Early Pregnancy Unit (EPU)** — many hospitals in the UK allow women to self-refer to the EPU without a GP referral; call your local hospital to check
- **NHS 111** — available 24 hours a day if you cannot reach your GP or midwife

The EPU will typically arrange an ultrasound scan, which is the most reliable way to assess what is happening. At 11 weeks, a scan can clearly show the baby, a heartbeat, and any structural issues such as a subchorionic haematoma. It can also confirm the pregnancy is in the correct location (within the uterus rather than the fallopian tube), which is important if this has not previously been confirmed.

## Understanding What the Bleeding Looks Like {#types-of-bleeding}

The appearance of the bleeding can offer some guidance, though it is not a substitute for a scan.

**Light brown or pink spotting** — Brown blood is usually older blood that has taken time to leave the body. Spotting of this kind, particularly if it occurs after sex or after passing wind or straining, is more likely to have a benign cause such as cervical sensitivity or slow-resolving subchorionic blood. It still warrants assessment.

**Bright red bleeding** — Fresh, active bleeding should be assessed the same day. It does not automatically mean miscarriage, but it needs to be evaluated.

**Heavy bleeding with cramping or clots** — This needs urgent attention. Contact your GP or EPU immediately, or go to A&E if bleeding is heavy and cannot wait.

## When to Go to A&E or Call 999 {#when-to-go-ae}

Seek emergency help straight away if you experience:

- Very heavy bleeding — soaking through a pad in an hour or less
- Severe lower abdominal pain or one-sided pain
- Feeling faint, dizzy, or like you might collapse
- Shoulder tip pain (which can be a sign of internal bleeding)
- Feeling very unwell alongside the bleeding

These symptoms together could indicate a complication needing immediate medical attention. Call 999 or go to A&E rather than waiting for a GP appointment.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Is bleeding at 11 weeks always a sign of miscarriage?

No. While it is natural to fear the worst, bleeding at 11 weeks does not always mean miscarriage. Cervical sensitivity, subchorionic haematoma, and infection are all common causes of first-trimester bleeding that do not affect the pregnancy. An ultrasound scan through your EPU is the only reliable way to find out what is causing the bleeding and whether the pregnancy is continuing.

### When should I worry about bleeding at 11 weeks?

Any bleeding at 11 weeks should be assessed promptly. Contact your GP, midwife, or EPU on the same day. Treat the situation as urgent — go to A&E or call 999 — if the bleeding is heavy, you have severe pain, you feel faint, or you have one-sided abdominal or shoulder pain.

### Should I go to A&E or the EPU?

For light to moderate bleeding without severe pain, contact your GP, midwife, or EPU for urgent assessment — this is the appropriate pathway for most cases. For heavy bleeding, severe pain, or if you feel very unwell or faint, go to A&E or call 999 immediately. If you are unsure, call 111 at any time of day and they will advise you on the right place to go.
