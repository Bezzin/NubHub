# Pregnant and Self-Employed: Your Complete Rights Guide

Being self-employed and pregnant in the UK puts you in a different position to employed women — you don't have a line manager to notify, you don't receive Statutory Maternity Pay (SMP), and there is no HR department to walk you through what you're entitled to. That can feel isolating, but there is meaningful financial and practical support available. This guide sets out what you can claim, how to claim it, and what you need to think about as you plan your maternity leave.

## Maternity Allowance: The Main Payment {#maternity-allowance}

If you are self-employed, you cannot receive Statutory Maternity Pay (SMP) — that is only available to employees. Instead, the primary financial support available to you is **Maternity Allowance (MA)**.

Maternity Allowance is paid by the government (via Jobcentre Plus, not your employer) and is worth up to £184.03 per week (as of April 2025 — this rate is reviewed annually) for up to 39 weeks. To qualify at the standard rate, you need to have been self-employed and paying Class 2 National Insurance contributions for at least 26 weeks in the 66 weeks before your baby's due date. These 26 weeks do not need to be consecutive.

If you do not meet that threshold — for example, if your earnings have been lower or you have not been registered as self-employed for long — you may still qualify for a lower flat rate. It is worth checking your eligibility regardless of how long you have been self-employed.

**When can you start claiming?** You can begin your Maternity Allowance up to 11 weeks before your due date. Payments stop either when you return to work, or after 39 weeks — whichever comes first.

**How to claim:** You apply using form MA1, which you can download from GOV.UK or get from your midwife. You will need to include your SRoE (Self-employed Record of Earnings) or evidence of your self-employed status. Applications can be submitted from 26 weeks pregnant.

**Keeping and updating records:** Make sure your self-assessment records and National Insurance payment history are up to date before you apply. If you have gaps in your Class 2 NI record, it may be possible to make voluntary contributions to fill them — speak to HMRC or a qualified accountant for advice on this.

## Other Financial Support {#other-support}

Maternity Allowance is the main payment for self-employed women, but it is not the only support available:

**Sure Start Maternity Grant:** A one-off payment of £500 to help with the costs of a new baby. You can claim this if you or your partner receive certain means-tested benefits (such as Universal Credit) and it is your first child, or if you already have children but your new baby is part of a multiple pregnancy. You can apply from 11 weeks before your due date until your baby is three months old.

**Universal Credit:** If your self-employed income is low, you may be entitled to Universal Credit during pregnancy and after birth. Self-employed people on Universal Credit are subject to a "minimum income floor" (which assumes a minimum level of earnings), but this floor is not applied during the first year of self-employment or during maternity leave, which may affect your entitlement calculation. It is worth contacting the Universal Credit helpline or a Citizens Advice adviser to understand your specific situation.

**Healthy Start vouchers:** If you are more than 10 weeks pregnant and receive certain benefits, you may qualify for Healthy Start vouchers to help buy food and milk. Ask your midwife for more information.

**Free NHS prescriptions and dental care:** All pregnant women in England are entitled to free NHS prescriptions and NHS dental treatment during pregnancy and for 12 months after the birth. You need a Maternity Exemption Certificate (MATEX), which your midwife or GP can arrange.

**Tax-Free Childcare and free childcare hours:** These are not specific to pregnancy, but once your baby arrives and you return to work, you may be eligible depending on your working hours and income. It is worth familiarising yourself with these before your baby is born.

**Child Benefit:** Once your baby is born, you can claim Child Benefit regardless of your employment status. You do not need to be working or earning to receive it, though there is a high income tax charge for households with one or both earners above a certain threshold. The current figures and thresholds are published on GOV.UK.

## Planning Your Time Off {#planning}

Unlike an employee, you have no statutory obligation to take a minimum or maximum amount of maternity leave. You can technically return to work the day after giving birth if you choose, though this would not be advisable for most people's health and recovery. You decide how long you step back from your business.

In practice, planning your self-employed maternity leave involves a few things that employees don't have to worry about:

**Client and contract management.** Consider how far in advance you need to inform clients, whether you have ongoing contracts that need to be wound down or handed over, and whether you want to accept new work in the months leading up to your due date.

**Income planning.** Maternity Allowance is substantially less than most self-employed people earn, so planning your finances well in advance is important. Consider what savings you can build up, whether there are passive income streams you can set up, and whether your partner's income will cover household costs during your time off.

**Business administration.** Your self-assessment tax return obligations do not pause during maternity leave. If you have an accountant, let them know your plans. If you file your own returns, make sure you are organised before your baby arrives.

**Returning to work.** There is no formal "return to work" notification required when you are self-employed. You simply resume working when you are ready. Be aware that resuming self-employed work may affect your Maternity Allowance payments — any week in which you do any self-employed work counts as a week you are not on Maternity Allowance.

## Antenatal Care Rights {#antenatal}

One area where self-employed women are in the same position as employees is antenatal care. You have the right to attend all NHS antenatal appointments recommended by your midwife or GP, and this does not require approval from anyone — because you are your own boss. There is no obligation for a client or contractor to compensate you for time spent at antenatal appointments, but equally no one can require you to skip them.

Make sure you attend all recommended appointments, including your booking appointment, dating scan, 20-week anomaly scan, and any additional appointments your midwife recommends based on your individual circumstances. These appointments exist to monitor your health and your baby's development, and self-employment is not a reason to postpone or skip them.

## Health and Safety Considerations {#health-safety}

If your self-employed work involves any physical demands, exposure to hazardous substances, significant stress, long hours, or anything else that might pose a risk in pregnancy, it is important to discuss this with your midwife or GP. You do not have the same formal risk assessment process that employed women are entitled to, but your health and your baby's wellbeing should take priority over work commitments.

Consider whether any aspects of your work need to be adjusted during pregnancy, particularly in the later stages. If you work from home, ergonomics (desk setup, chair support) become relevant. If you work on site, assess any physical risks carefully. If your work involves client-facing commitments with irregular hours, think about how you will manage rest and recovery in the final weeks.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### How do I claim Maternity Allowance as a self-employed woman?

You apply using form MA1, available to download from GOV.UK or obtainable from your midwife. You will need to provide evidence of your self-employment and National Insurance contributions — your Class 2 NI payment record is usually sufficient. You can submit your claim from 26 weeks pregnant, and payments can begin up to 11 weeks before your due date. Applications are processed by Jobcentre Plus.

### Can I keep working while pregnant and self-employed?

Yes — you can continue to work throughout your pregnancy as long as you feel well enough and your work does not pose a specific risk. There is no legal requirement to take time off before your baby is born. Many self-employed women work right up to their due date and step back only when labour begins, though this is a personal choice. If your work involves physical demands, hazardous substances, or significant stress, discuss this with your midwife so you can plan appropriately. Resuming self-employed work while receiving Maternity Allowance will end your MA payments for the weeks you work, so factor this into your planning.

### What if I don't qualify for Maternity Allowance?

If you do not meet the eligibility criteria for the standard Maternity Allowance rate, you may still qualify for a lower rate (currently £27 per week for up to 14 weeks). If you receive Universal Credit or other means-tested benefits, these may increase during your pregnancy and the postnatal period. Contact Citizens Advice or your local Jobcentre for a benefits check — it is free and ensures you are not missing support you are entitled to.
