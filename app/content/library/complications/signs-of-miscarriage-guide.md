# Signs of Miscarriage: What to Look For

This article explores a clear guide to the signs and symptoms of miscarriage, when to seek urgent help, and what to expect if you contact your early pregnancy unit. We cover what you need to know in plain language.

## Common Signs to Watch For {#section-1}

The most common sign of miscarriage is vaginal bleeding, but not all bleeding means you are losing the pregnancy. Light spotting affects up to one in four pregnancies and often resolves without harm. Bleeding that becomes heavy, bright red, or contains clots or tissue is more concerning. Cramping or abdominal pain that feels stronger than normal period pain, especially alongside bleeding, can be a sign. You may also notice that pregnancy symptoms such as nausea or breast tenderness suddenly disappear, though symptoms naturally fluctuate and fading symptoms alone are not diagnostic. If you pass tissue or a sac, keep anything you pass in a clean container so a clinician can examine it. Trust your instincts: if something feels wrong, contact your early pregnancy unit, GP, or NHS 111.

## When to Seek Urgent Help {#section-2}

Some symptoms warrant immediate medical attention. Go to A&E or call 999 if you are bleeding very heavily, feel dizzy or faint, have severe abdominal or shoulder tip pain, or develop a high temperature or chills. Heavy bleeding with clots and feeling unwell could indicate a haemorrhage or infection. For less severe but worrying symptoms, call NHS 111 or your early pregnancy unit, which can arrange a scan, usually within 24 to 48 hours. Try to note when symptoms started, how much bleeding there has been, and whether you have passed any tissue. Most early pregnancy units accept self-referrals, so you do not always need a GP appointment. Remember that having symptoms does not always mean miscarriage: many women with bleeding go on to have healthy pregnancies.

## FAQ {#faq}

### Is light spotting always a sign of miscarriage?

No. Light spotting affects around one in four pregnancies and often resolves without harm. Heavy bleeding with pain or clots is more concerning and should be assessed promptly.

### What should I do if I pass tissue?

Place anything you pass in a clean container and bring it to your early pregnancy unit or GP. They can examine it and may be able to confirm whether it was pregnancy tissue.

### Do symptoms disappearing mean miscarriage?

Not necessarily. Pregnancy symptoms fluctuate naturally, and fading nausea or breast tenderness alone are not diagnostic. If you are worried, ask for a scan rather than relying on symptoms alone.
