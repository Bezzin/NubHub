# Red Flag Symptoms Early Pregnancy Complete

Knowing which symptoms in early pregnancy need urgent attention — and which are normal — can feel daunting. Most discomforts in the first trimester are not dangerous, but a small number of symptoms genuinely require urgent care. This guide explains clearly what to watch for, what action to take, and when to call 999, go to A&E, or contact 111 or your midwife.

## Call 999 or Go to A&E Immediately {#s1}

Some symptoms in early pregnancy are medical emergencies. Do not wait for a GP appointment or call 111 first if you experience any of the following:

**Severe sudden abdominal pain — especially one-sided.** Sharp, severe pain on one side of your lower abdomen in early pregnancy can be a sign of an ectopic pregnancy (where the fertilised egg has implanted outside the uterus, usually in a fallopian tube). An ectopic pregnancy can rupture and cause life-threatening internal bleeding. This is a genuine emergency.

**Heavy bleeding that soaks through a pad within an hour or comes in large clots.** Heavy bleeding in early pregnancy always warrants urgent assessment. While it can sometimes be the result of a threatened miscarriage that settles, it can also indicate a miscarriage in progress or a serious complication. Do not try to manage this at home.

**Shoulder tip pain combined with bleeding or abdominal pain.** Pain felt at the very tip of the shoulder (not the shoulder joint or neck) alongside other symptoms can indicate blood pooling under the diaphragm — a sign of internal bleeding, often from a ruptured ectopic pregnancy. This is a red flag that requires immediate emergency care.

**Collapse or loss of consciousness.** If you faint, collapse, or feel as though you are about to, and you are pregnant, seek emergency help immediately.

**Fever above 39°C with severe pain or confusion.** A very high temperature alongside severe symptoms may indicate a serious infection that requires emergency treatment.

## Go to Your Early Pregnancy Unit (EPU) or A&E {#s2}

The following symptoms warrant same-day assessment, either at your local EPU (if you have access to one — most NHS trusts operate walk-in EPU services for women under 16 weeks), or at A&E if you cannot access an EPU quickly:

- **Any bright red bleeding**, even if not heavy, especially if accompanied by pain
- **Moderate abdominal cramping** that does not ease and is getting worse
- **Fever above 38°C** — this can signal infection, including miscarriage-related infection or a urinary tract infection that has spread to the kidneys
- **Inability to keep fluids down for more than 24 hours** — this may indicate hyperemesis gravidarum, which can lead to dehydration and requires treatment
- **Signs of a urinary tract infection that is not improving** — burning when you urinate, back or side pain, fever, and feeling unwell can indicate a kidney infection (pyelonephritis), which is more serious in pregnancy and needs prompt antibiotic treatment

If you are unsure whether your EPU accepts self-referrals, call NHS 111 for guidance — they can direct you to the most appropriate service.

## Call 111 or Your Midwife {#s3}

The following symptoms are worth getting assessed but are not typically emergencies. Call NHS 111 or contact your midwife team:

- **Light spotting or pink discharge** without pain — often harmless (implantation bleeding or cervical sensitivity) but worth reporting, especially for reassurance
- **Mild to moderate cramping** without bleeding — often normal in early pregnancy due to the uterus stretching, but persistent or worsening cramping should be checked
- **Persistent nausea and vomiting** that you are managing but finding difficult — your GP can prescribe safe anti-sickness medication if needed
- **Concern about a change in symptoms** — if something feels different and you're worried, you are always entitled to seek reassurance
- **Possible signs of a water infection** — frequent urination with burning, cloudy urine, or mild back pain should be assessed promptly as UTIs in pregnancy need treatment
- **Sudden complete loss of pregnancy symptoms**, particularly if you also have pain or bleeding

## Symptoms That Are Usually Normal

Not every discomfort in early pregnancy is a warning sign. The following are generally considered normal, though always worth mentioning at your next appointment if they concern you:

- Mild cramps or a pulling sensation, particularly around weeks 5–10 as your uterus stretches
- Light pink or brown spotting in the first few weeks, especially around the time your period would have been due
- Nausea and vomiting, particularly in the morning or when your stomach is empty
- Breast tenderness and swelling
- Fatigue and exhaustion
- Bloating and gas
- Increased urination
- Headaches (ensure you're staying well hydrated)
- Slight dizziness when standing up quickly

The key distinction is severity and whether the symptom is isolated or accompanied by other warning signs. A small amount of pink spotting on its own is very different from heavy red bleeding with severe pain.

## Knowing Your Local Services

It helps to find out the phone number and opening hours of your local Early Pregnancy Unit before you need it, rather than searching in a moment of panic. Your booking appointment paperwork or your GP surgery can provide this. Many EPUs operate during weekday daytime hours only, so knowing this in advance means you can plan accordingly — outside those hours, NHS 111 is your first call, and they will advise whether A&E is needed.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### What are the red flag symptoms in early pregnancy?

The most serious red flags are: heavy bleeding (soaking a pad in under an hour), severe or one-sided abdominal pain, shoulder tip pain alongside bleeding or abdominal pain, collapse or fainting, fever above 38–39°C, and inability to keep any fluids down. These symptoms need urgent assessment — call 999 or go to A&E for the most severe, or contact your EPU or 111 for same-day assessment for others. Do not wait to see if things improve when these symptoms are present.

### Should I call 999 or 111 for bleeding in pregnancy?

It depends on the severity. Call 999 or go to A&E immediately if bleeding is heavy (soaking through a pad quickly), if you are in severe pain, have shoulder tip pain, or feel faint or have collapsed. For lighter bleeding without severe pain, call 111 or your midwife, or attend your local Early Pregnancy Unit for assessment. Light spotting with no pain can often wait until the next day, but you should still report it.

### What should I do if I think I have an ectopic pregnancy?

If you suspect an ectopic pregnancy — particularly if you have severe one-sided lower abdominal pain, shoulder tip pain, or you feel faint alongside any bleeding — call 999 or go to A&E immediately. Do not wait. An ectopic pregnancy that ruptures is life-threatening. Risk factors include a previous ectopic pregnancy, previous pelvic inflammatory disease, IUD in place at time of conception, or previous fallopian tube surgery, but ectopic pregnancy can happen without any known risk factors.
