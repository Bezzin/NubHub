# Irish Baby Names: Beautiful Ideas for Boys and Girls

Irish baby names have a warmth and distinctiveness that appeals to many families across the UK — whether or not you have Irish heritage. From melodic Gaelic names with centuries of history to shorter modern Irish names that feel fresh on a birth announcement, there is a wide range to explore.

This guide covers popular choices for girls and boys, explains the meanings and (importantly) the pronunciation of some of the more unusual spellings, and offers a few tips for choosing a name that will work well in everyday UK life.

## Popular Irish Girl Names {#s1}

**Aoife** (pronounced EE-fah) — means "beautiful" or "radiant." One of the most popular girls' names in Ireland and increasingly chosen in Britain. Strong, simple, and elegant once you know how to say it.

**Niamh** (pronounced NEEV) — means "bright" or "radiance." From Irish mythology, Niamh of the Golden Hair was a figure of the otherworld. Consistently popular in the UK.

**Saoirse** (pronounced SEER-sha) — means "freedom." A name with a strong, modern feel despite its ancient roots. Gained wider recognition in the UK in recent years.

**Ciara** (pronounced KEER-ah) — means "dark-haired" or "dark." Also spelled Keira or Kira in anglicised forms. A straightforward name to use in the UK.

**Maeve** — means "intoxicating" or "she who intoxicates." From the legendary Irish queen Medb. Pronounced exactly as written, making it very accessible outside Ireland.

**Erin** — the poetic name for Ireland itself. Widely used across the UK and easy to spell and pronounce. A gentle, feminine choice.

**Orla** — means "golden princess." Simple, easy to pronounce, and works well internationally. A popular choice for families wanting something Irish without a difficult spelling.

**Róisín** (pronounced roh-SHEEN) — means "little rose." The Irish equivalent of Rose or Rosa, with a lyrical quality. Sometimes anglicised as Roisin.

**Caoimhe** (pronounced KEE-vah) — means "gentle" or "beautiful." One of the more challenging spellings for those unfamiliar with Irish, but the sound is soft and lovely.

**Sinéad** (pronounced shin-ADE) — the Irish equivalent of Janet or Jane. Well known in the UK and relatively easy to pronounce once you know the fada (accent) affects the sound.

## Popular Irish Boy Names {#s2}

**Cillian** (pronounced KIL-ee-an) — means "associated with the church." Widely recognised in the UK, partly through cultural references. Has a strong, distinctive sound.

**Conor** — also spelled Connor. Traditionally means "lover of hounds" or "wolf-lover." One of the most accessible Irish names for UK families, with a familiar sound.

**Declan** — thought to mean "man of prayer." The name of an Irish saint, and popular across Britain and Ireland. Easy to spell and pronounce.

**Finn** — means "fair" or "white." From the legendary hero Fionn mac Cumhaill. Short, strong, and works beautifully in the UK without any pronunciation issues.

**Liam** — the Irish short form of William, meaning "strong-willed protector." One of the most popular boys' names in the UK. Unmistakably Irish in origin but universally familiar.

**Ronan** — means "little seal." A gentle name with a soft sound. Saint Ronan is associated with Ireland and Scotland. Increasingly chosen in the UK.

**Senan** (pronounced SHEN-an) — means "old" or "wise." A less common choice, which may appeal to parents looking for something distinctive.

**Tadhg** (pronounced TEYG, rhyming with "vague") — means "poet" or "philosopher." One of the most Gaelic-sounding names on this list and the trickiest for non-Irish speakers, but the sound itself is warm and unusual.

**Oisín** (pronounced uh-SHEEN) — a figure from Irish mythology, the son of Finn. A beautiful, lyrical name that is becoming more familiar in the UK.

**Cormac** — means "charioteer" or "son of the chariot." A strong, traditional Irish name that translates well internationally.

## Tips for Choosing an Irish Name in the UK {#s3}

**Consider the pronunciation barrier.** Names like Aoife, Caoimhe, and Tadhg are genuinely difficult for people unfamiliar with Irish. This is not a reason to avoid them — many families love that their child's name prompts a story — but it is worth thinking about whether repeated mispronunciation will bother you or your child. Names like Finn, Maeve, Declan, and Orla carry Irish identity without the spelling challenge.

**Check the meaning.** Irish names often have vivid, poetic meanings rooted in mythology, nature, or early Christian history. Knowing what a name means can make it feel even more personal.

**Think about nicknames.** Some Irish names are already short and do not shorten further. Others lend themselves to nicknames naturally (Cillian → Cill, Róisín → Roisín). Consider whether you want a built-in nickname option.

**Spellings matter for registration.** When registering a birth in England, Wales, or Scotland, you can use any spelling you choose — including fadas (accents), though these are not always reproduced on official documents. Some families choose the anglicised spelling to reduce administrative friction; others prefer the traditional Irish spelling as a point of cultural pride.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Are Irish names popular in the UK?

Yes. Names like Liam, Conor, Niamh, and Maeve are firmly established in UK baby name charts. Others like Saoirse, Aoife, and Finn have grown steadily in popularity. There is a strong tradition of Irish names being used across Britain — particularly in areas with Irish heritage communities — and broader interest in Celtic names has grown among families without direct Irish connections.

### How do I know how to pronounce Irish baby names?

Irish pronunciation follows rules that are consistent once you learn them, but they differ significantly from English. The key is the fada (the accent mark over a vowel, such as in Aoife or Síle), which changes the vowel sound. Looking up audio pronunciations online is the most reliable approach before settling on a name. It is also worth considering how easily the name can be corrected — a name that people mispronounce but quickly correct when told is very different from one that causes ongoing confusion.

### Can I use an Irish name even if I have no Irish heritage?

Absolutely. Names cross borders all the time, and choosing a name from any culture because you love its sound or meaning is entirely normal. That said, if a name has deep cultural or religious significance within Irish tradition, it is worth understanding that context — not because it should put you off, but because it enriches the choice.
