# Buying a Pram: When and What to Look For

Choosing a pram is one of the biggest baby purchases you'll make. Here's our guide.

## When to Buy {#s1}

Start researching in the second trimester. Buy by the third trimester. Don't buy too early — you want to be sure the pregnancy is progressing well first.

Most parents find that the sweet spot for purchasing is between 20 and 30 weeks pregnant. By this point you will likely have had your anomaly scan, you will know whether you are having twins, and you will have a better sense of your lifestyle and budget. Buying too close to your due date can be stressful if something needs to be ordered in or exchanged.

If you are buying online or from a specialist retailer, check the lead times — some popular travel systems can take several weeks to arrive. Aim to have your pram set up and ready at least a few weeks before your due date.

## What to Look For {#s2}

The right pram for you depends on your lifestyle, your home, and your budget. Consider these key factors before you buy:

**Weight and foldability**
Think about how often you will be lifting the pram into a car boot, up steps, or onto public transport. A lightweight pram is much easier to manage alone. Test the fold mechanism in the shop — it should be easy to do one-handed.

**Suspension**
Good suspension makes a real difference on uneven pavements, cobblestones, or gravel paths. If you live rurally or walk on rough ground regularly, prioritise a pram with a robust suspension system.

**Storage basket**
A large, accessible basket underneath the seat is genuinely useful for shopping, a changing bag, or a spare layer. Check that you can reach the basket easily when the seat is in use.

**Adjustable handle height**
If you and your partner are significantly different heights, an adjustable handle will save a lot of discomfort. Many mid-range prams now include this as standard.

**Car seat compatibility**
If you want a travel system — where the car seat clicks directly onto the pram chassis — check compatibility before you buy. Not all car seat brands are compatible with all pram chassis. Some retailers can show you these combinations in store.

**Seat direction**
Newborns benefit from facing you (parent-facing) so you can monitor them and maintain eye contact. Some prams allow you to switch between parent-facing and forward-facing as your baby grows.

**Longevity**
Some prams are suitable from birth to toddlerhood; others are designed only for newborns and will need replacing with a pushchair later. A pram that carries a child up to 15–25 kg will cost more upfront but may represent better long-term value.

## Types of Pram {#s3}

**Traditional pram (carriage-style):** A large, flat lie-flat carriage ideal for newborns but bulkier and heavier. Often not suitable past six months.

**Travel system:** A modular system where a carrycot, pushchair seat, and car seat all clip onto the same chassis. Versatile and popular for a reason — you get multiple configurations from one purchase.

**Stroller or buggy:** Lightweight and compact, usually suitable from around six months when a baby can sit unaided. Good for travel and city use, but not suitable from birth unless the seat lies fully flat.

**All-terrain pram:** Built for rough surfaces with large air-filled tyres and robust frames. Heavier and bulkier but excellent for active parents who spend a lot of time outdoors.

**Tandem or twin pram:** If you are expecting twins or have a toddler, a tandem pram carries two children either one in front of the other or side by side.

## FAQ {#faq}

### How much should I spend on a pram?

Prams range from £100 to £1,500+. A mid-range pram (£300–600) is fine for most families. Consider second-hand — many are barely used. The most expensive isn't necessarily the best. Whatever your budget, try before you buy if you can; fold it, lift it, and push it around the shop to check it suits you.

### Is it safe to buy a second-hand pram?

Many parents buy second-hand prams successfully. Check that the frame is not bent or cracked, all harness straps are intact and working, the brakes engage properly, and any fabrics can be removed and washed. Avoid buying a second-hand car seat, as you cannot verify its history — but the pram chassis itself is generally safe to buy used if it is in good condition.

### When is the best time to buy a pram in pregnancy?

Between 20 and 30 weeks is the most common window. This gives you time to research properly, take advantage of sales (retailers often run baby events), and have everything ready well before your due date. Check delivery times when ordering online, especially for popular models.

Always speak to your midwife or GP if you have any questions about preparing for your baby's arrival.
