# Budgeting for Baby: First Trimester Tips

The first trimester is a great time to start financial planning for your baby. You may still be in the early weeks, keeping the news quiet, and feeling quite rough — but underneath all of that, getting your finances in order now will save you stress later. Babies don't need to be expensive, but a little organisation in these early months goes a long way.

## Start a Baby Fund {#s1}

One of the simplest things you can do right now is open a separate savings account specifically for baby expenses. Even setting aside a small amount each month will add up over nine months. Consider setting up an automatic standing order so the money moves without you having to think about it each payday.

If money is tight, don't worry — even a modest monthly amount is worth starting. The goal is to build a habit and create a cushion, not to save a specific target sum.

## Find Out What You're Entitled To {#s2}

Before you can plan properly, you need to know what income you'll have during and after maternity leave. This varies depending on whether you're employed, self-employed, or on a zero-hours contract.

**Key things to look into:**
- **Statutory Maternity Pay (SMP):** Most employed women who meet the qualifying criteria receive SMP, which is paid by your employer. The first six weeks are paid at a percentage of your average earnings; the remaining weeks are paid at a flat statutory rate. Check your employment contract and speak to your HR department.
- **Maternity Allowance:** If you don't qualify for SMP (for example, if you're self-employed or recently changed jobs), you may qualify for Maternity Allowance from the government instead.
- **Sure Start Maternity Grant:** A one-off payment for parents who qualify for certain benefits. Worth checking if you're on a lower income.
- **Child Benefit:** You can claim this once your baby is born. It's paid per child and is not means-tested, though high earners may need to repay some or all of it through their tax return.
- **Free childcare hours:** Not immediately relevant in early pregnancy, but knowing when your child will become eligible for funded hours can influence your longer-term financial planning.

The government's GOV.UK website has benefit calculators and up-to-date entitlement information.

## Research the Big Costs Early {#s3}

Some baby items carry significant costs, and leaving them until the last minute means you'll have less time to shop around, wait for sales, or source good second-hand options.

**Bigger items to research early:**
- Pram or pushchair (prices vary enormously — second-hand is often a good option)
- Car seat (buy new if possible, so you know its history)
- Moses basket or bedside crib
- Nursing chair or feeding support
- Nursery furniture, if you plan to have a separate nursery

You don't need to buy everything at once. Create a list, research prices, join local baby-selling Facebook groups, and watch for retailer sales events (many happen around Black Friday and January).

## What You Don't Need to Buy New {#s4}

New parents are often surprised by how much baby kit they can source second-hand. Clothing in particular depreciates extremely fast — babies outgrow sizes in weeks — so buying second-hand or accepting hand-me-downs from friends and family is very sensible. Other items that are generally fine second-hand include:

- Prams and pushchairs (check it hasn't been in an accident)
- Bouncers, swings, and playmats
- Nursing pillows and baby carriers (check condition carefully)
- Cots and bedding (though check the mattress condition — a new mattress for an old cot frame is a reasonable compromise)

Car seats are the notable exception: it's generally recommended to buy these new, or only accept one second-hand from someone you know and trust completely, since you cannot verify whether it has been in an impact.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### When should I start buying baby things?

Use the first trimester for research and list-building rather than purchasing. Many parents prefer to wait until after the 12-week scan before committing to larger buys. The second trimester — once you're past the early anxiety and have more energy — tends to be the most practical time to start purchasing. Leaving everything until the third trimester can feel rushed.

### How much does a baby cost in the first year?

Costs vary hugely depending on your choices and circumstances. The biggest variables tend to be childcare costs (which can be very significant in the UK), whether you breastfeed or formula-feed, and how much second-hand versus new equipment you buy. Focus on the non-negotiables first and resist the pressure to buy every gadget on the market.

### Are there any government grants I can apply for in pregnancy?

The Sure Start Maternity Grant is available to people receiving certain qualifying benefits. You can also apply for Healthy Start vouchers if you're pregnant and on a qualifying benefit — these can be used towards food and vitamins. Check your eligibility on GOV.UK, as these schemes have specific criteria.

Speak to your midwife or GP if you have concerns about managing financially during pregnancy, as they can signpost you to appropriate support services.
