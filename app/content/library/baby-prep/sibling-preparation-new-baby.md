# Sibling Preparation New Baby

Welcoming a new baby is a huge change for an older child. With a little preparation and a lot of reassurance, you can help your child feel involved, valued, and ready for life as a big sibling — rather than blindsided by a tiny newcomer who suddenly takes centre stage.

## When to Tell Your Child

Timing matters, especially for young children. Toddlers have very little concept of time — telling a two-year-old at six weeks pregnant means they will ask "Is the baby here yet?" every single day for the next seven months.

As a general guide:
- **Toddlers (under 3):** Wait until your bump is visible, usually around 16–20 weeks. Their world is immediate, so a shorter wait between "there's a baby coming" and the baby actually arriving is kinder on them.
- **Pre-school age (3–5):** You can tell them a little earlier, around 12–16 weeks, as they can manage slightly longer timeframes and will enjoy being involved in preparations.
- **Primary school age (5+):** They understand more and may feel left out if they find out late. Telling them after your dating scan, once the risk of miscarriage has reduced, is often a good time.

Wherever possible, tell your child before they hear it from someone else — nothing makes a child feel less important than being the last to know.

## How to Tell Your Child

Keep it simple, warm, and age-appropriate. For a toddler, you might say: "There's a tiny baby growing in Mummy's tummy — you're going to be a big brother or sister." Showing them your scan photo can help make it feel real.

Reassure them immediately that you love them and that nothing will change between you. Children often worry, even if they don't say it, that a new baby means they will be loved less. Repeating "you are so loved" in the weeks ahead costs nothing and means everything.

## Involving Your Child in Preparations

Feeling included makes a huge difference. Practical ways to involve older children:

- Let them come to a private scan or hear the heartbeat
- Ask for their opinion when choosing baby items (though keep the final decision yours)
- Read books together about becoming a big sibling — there are lovely options for all ages
- Show them photos of when they were a baby and talk about how you cared for them
- Set up a small "big sibling bag" with a new activity or book ready for when the baby is born
- Let them help wash or fold baby clothes

Giving them a role — however small — helps them feel like a participant rather than a bystander.

## Managing the Early Weeks After Birth

The arrival of the baby is when jealousy and regression are most likely. This is completely normal. An older child may suddenly want a bottle, start having accidents again, or become clingy. They are not being naughty — they are communicating that they need extra love.

Helpful strategies in the early weeks:
- Have someone bring a gift "from the baby" when they first meet
- Keep as much of the older child's routine in place as possible — same bedtime, same activities
- Carve out one-to-one time with your older child every day, even if it is just ten minutes at bedtime
- Let them hold, stroke, or talk to the baby if they want to — and never force it if they don't
- Praise kind behaviour specifically: "You were so gentle with the baby just then — that was lovely"

## Handling Sibling Jealousy

Some jealousy is almost inevitable, and it does not mean you have done anything wrong. What helps:

- Name the feeling without shaming it: "I can see you're feeling a bit left out — that makes sense"
- Avoid comparing the children or making the older child feel their feelings are wrong
- Make sure grandparents and visitors greet the older child first when they arrive
- Let the older child set the pace for their relationship with the baby — some children take weeks to warm up

## Frequently Asked Questions

### When should I tell my toddler about the new baby?

Wait until you are showing or have passed the first trimester — around 16–20 weeks is usually right for toddlers. They have little sense of how long time lasts, so a shorter gap between the news and the birth is easier for them to manage. Pre-school and school-age children can be told a little earlier, usually after your dating scan at around 12 weeks.

### How do I handle sibling jealousy after the baby arrives?

Sibling jealousy is normal and usually peaks in the first few weeks. Keep your older child's routine as stable as possible, set aside one-to-one time each day, and name their feelings without dismissing them. Involving them in caring for the baby — fetching a nappy, singing to the baby — can help them feel important rather than replaced.

### What if my older child doesn't want anything to do with the new baby?

That is absolutely fine. Never force affection or interaction. Some children need weeks or even months to warm up to a new sibling. Keep your relationship with your older child warm and consistent, and let the bond develop naturally at their pace.

Speak to your midwife or health visitor for personalised guidance on supporting your family through this transition.
