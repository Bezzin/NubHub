# Birth Plans: When to Start Thinking About Them

A birth plan is a written record of your preferences for labour and birth. It is not a strict contract but a way to communicate your wishes to your midwifery team. Knowing when to start thinking about your birth plan helps you feel prepared without rushing decisions.

## When to start your birth plan {when-to-start}

Most NHS midwives suggest starting your birth plan around 28-32 weeks, after your antenatal classes and when you have had time to consider your birth options. You do not need a finished document early on, but you can begin jotting down thoughts from around 20 weeks once you know more about your options. Your midwife will usually ask about it at your 36-week appointment.

## What to include {what-to-include}

A good birth plan covers where you want to give birth, who you want as a birth partner, your preferred pain relief options, positions for labour, whether you want active management or physiological third stage of labour, your views on induction, and your preferences for immediately after birth (skin-to-skin, delayed cord clamping, feeding). Keep it concise: one side of A4 is plenty.

## Keep it flexible {keep-flexible}

Birth is unpredictable. A useful birth plan acknowledges that things may change and includes a section on what you would prefer if complications arise, such as an emergency caesarean. Discuss backup preferences with your partner so they can advocate for you. Flexibility helps you feel calmer if labour takes an unexpected turn.

## Share it with your team {share-with-team}

Give a copy to your midwife at your 36-week appointment and pack another in your hospital bag. Your birth partner should also have a copy. If you are having a home birth, your community midwives will keep it on file. Clear communication means your team knows your wishes even if you are too focused to explain them during labour.

## FAQ {#faq}

### Do I have to have a birth plan?

No, it is optional. Some parents prefer to go with the flow, but a written plan helps your team understand your preferences.

### Can I change my birth plan late in pregnancy?

Yes, you can update it at any time. Many parents revise their plan after antenatal classes or if their circumstances change.

### What if my birth doesn't go to plan?

Most birth plans are guides, not guarantees. Maternity teams are used to adapting. Discussing 'what if' scenarios in advance helps reduce disappointment.
