# Pregnant When a Friend Is Trying to Conceive

You're pregnant. A close friend is trying — month after month, with no success. The dynamic between you has shifted, and you may feel like you're walking through a field of landmines. How do you share your life without causing pain, and how do you handle the guilt that comes with wanting your own happiness?

## The Emotional Landscape

Your friend may feel genuine joy for you and acute grief for themselves — often simultaneously. They may withdraw, overcompensate, or seem fine and then crumble later. There's no predictable script, because infertility grief is unpredictable.

What they are almost certainly not feeling is anger at you personally. Their pain is about their own situation, not a judgement on your right to be pregnant. That distinction is important to hold onto when the guilt threatens to swallow you.

It's also worth understanding that your friend may already be aware of what they're experiencing — they may have researched it, named it, and be managing it privately. Treating them as fragile can feel more isolating than the pregnancy itself. Most people going through fertility difficulties say they'd rather be included and given the choice to step back than be quietly excluded "for their own good."

## How to Share the News

Telling a friend who is trying to conceive is one of the more delicate conversations you'll navigate in early pregnancy. A few things tend to help:

- **Tell them early and privately.** Don't let them find out via a group announcement, a social media post, or someone else's slip. Being the last to know — especially in a close friendship — adds an extra layer of hurt.
- **Be direct but gentle.** Something like: "I'm pregnant, and I wanted you to hear it from me because you matter to me. I know this is complicated, and I don't expect anything from you."
- **Don't expect or require a specific reaction.** They may cry, go quiet, say congratulations flatly, or be warm in the moment and then need space later. All of those are valid responses.
- **Give them an out.** "You don't need to reply straight away. Take whatever space you need." This removes the pressure of having to perform an emotion they may not feel able to give in that moment.
- **Text or letter over an in-person call** if you think it would help. It gives them time to react privately before responding to you.

## Navigating the Friendship Day to Day

Once the news is out, the friendship needs ongoing care. There's no single correct approach — it depends on how close you are, how your friend is coping, and how open you both tend to be with each other.

- **Let them set the pace.** If they want to hear about scans and symptoms, share. If they go quiet, don't force connection — but do check in occasionally so they know the door is open.
- **Don't hide your pregnancy, but don't centre it.** You don't have to pretend you're not expecting. But you also don't have to make every conversation about it. A friendship can exist alongside a pregnancy without the pregnancy dominating every interaction.
- **Make milestone events genuinely optional.** Baby showers, gender reveals, and birth announcements can be particularly hard. Invite them, but make it clear that not coming won't damage the friendship. "I'd love you there, but I completely understand if it's not the right time for you."
- **Be thoughtful about complaint-sharing.** Exhaustion, sickness, and pregnancy discomforts are real and legitimate. But sharing every pregnancy struggle with someone who is desperate to be pregnant themselves can sting even when that's not your intention. You may want to reserve those conversations for other friends, your partner, or a community like Nub Hub where the audience is broader.

## Your Own Feelings

It's OK to feel sad, guilty, or frustrated. You are allowed to want your pregnancy celebrated, and you're allowed to grieve a shift in a friendship that matters to you. Holding space for your friend's pain doesn't mean shrinking your own life — it means sharing it with thought and care.

If the guilt is persistent and heavy, it may help to talk to someone outside the friendship — your partner, another friend, or a counsellor. The charity Tommy's offers support for pregnancy-related emotional wellbeing, and Fertility Network UK has resources for those supporting friends through fertility difficulties.

## What to Avoid

Some well-meaning phrases tend to land badly:

- "You'll be next!" — you genuinely don't know that, and it can feel dismissive of the difficulty they're experiencing.
- "At least you can focus on yourself / travel / sleep." — not comforting, and it implies a trade-off they didn't choose.
- "I almost didn't tell you because I was worried about you" — while honest, this can make them feel like a problem to be managed rather than a friend to be trusted.
- Hiding major news "to protect them" beyond the initial private conversation. Being excluded from your life can feel worse than hearing hard news directly.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Should I tell my friend who's TTC before anyone else?

Yes, if this is a close friendship, telling them privately and ahead of any wider announcement is one of the most important things you can do. It gives them the chance to react privately, on their own terms, before they're expected to perform happiness in a group setting.

### What if my friend becomes distant after I tell them?

Give them space without disappearing entirely. A short message every few weeks — not about the pregnancy — lets them know you're still thinking of them and the friendship still matters. If the distance continues for a long time, a gentle, honest conversation when the time feels right may help more than staying silent.

### How do I handle pregnancy announcements and milestones around a TTC friend?

Make attendance at any pregnancy milestone events genuinely optional and say so explicitly. Share major news (like a birth announcement) privately before posting publicly. You don't have to stop celebrating your pregnancy — just be thoughtful about how and when you include someone who may be finding those moments particularly hard.

The fact that you're thinking carefully about this already means you care about getting it right. That counts for a great deal.

Speak to your midwife or GP if you're finding the emotional complexity of your pregnancy weighing heavily on you — they can signpost you to appropriate support.
