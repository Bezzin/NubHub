# Pregnant and Alone: Where to Get Support

Feeling alone in pregnancy is more common than you might think, and it does not mean you are doing anything wrong. Whether you are a single parent by choice or circumstance, estranged from family, or simply surrounded by people who do not seem to understand what you are going through, that sense of isolation can be one of the harder parts of early pregnancy. The good news is that there is real, practical support available to you in the UK — much of it free.

## NHS Support: Your Starting Point

Your NHS care team is your first and most important source of support, regardless of your personal situation.

- **Your midwife** is the central person in your care. You will be assigned a community midwife at your booking appointment (usually around 8–10 weeks). They can discuss your circumstances, flag any extra support you need, and connect you with other services.
- **Your GP** can help with both physical and mental health concerns. If you are struggling with anxiety, low mood, or feeling overwhelmed, telling your GP is the right first step — they can refer you to perinatal mental health services.
- **Health visitors** become involved from around 28 weeks and continue to support you after birth. They are a good point of contact for practical questions about baby care, benefits, and local services.

You are entitled to exactly the same antenatal care as anyone else, regardless of whether you have a partner.

## Charities and Community Support

A number of UK charities specifically support pregnant women and new parents who feel alone or are parenting without a partner.

- **Gingerbread** (gingerbread.org.uk) is the leading charity for single parents in England and Wales. They offer a helpline, advice on benefits and rights, and local peer support groups.
- **Home-Start** (home-start.org.uk) recruits trained volunteers who visit families at home for a few hours each week. They offer practical help, a listening ear, and signposting to local services. Referrals are usually made through a health visitor or GP.
- **Tommy's** (tommys.org) offers information, a midwife helpline, and online forums specifically for pregnancy.
- **PANDAS Foundation** (pandasfoundation.org.uk) supports people experiencing perinatal mental health difficulties, including anxiety and depression during pregnancy.
- **NCT** (nct.org.uk) runs antenatal classes where you can meet other people at the same stage of pregnancy. They also have local groups and a helpline. Bursaries are sometimes available if cost is a barrier.

## Practical and Financial Support

Feeling alone in pregnancy is sometimes made harder by financial worries. There are several entitlements worth knowing about:

- **Free NHS prescriptions and dental care** are available throughout pregnancy and for 12 months after birth — apply for a maternity exemption certificate (MATEX) through your GP or midwife.
- **Healthy Start vouchers** provide free vouchers for milk, fruit, vegetables, and vitamins if you are on certain benefits and more than 10 weeks pregnant.
- **Sure Start Maternity Grant** is a one-off payment available to people on certain benefits having their first child.
- **Citizens Advice** (citizensadvice.org.uk) can help you understand your full entitlements including housing, benefits, and employment rights.

## Building a Network Around You

Even a small network can make a meaningful difference. Some practical ways to start:

- **Online communities**: Mumsnet and Netmums have active forums for single parents and those feeling isolated in pregnancy. Being able to talk to people who genuinely understand your situation, at any hour, can help.
- **Local Facebook groups**: Many areas have local pregnancy, parenting, or mums' groups where you can meet people nearby.
- **Antenatal classes**: NHS antenatal classes are free and run locally. They are a good way to meet other parents-to-be in your area — even if you attend alone.
- **Children's Centres**: Many local authorities still run children's centre services, including antenatal drop-ins and baby groups, which are free to attend.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What support is available for pregnant women without a partner?

You are entitled to full NHS maternity care regardless of your circumstances. Charities including Gingerbread, Home-Start, and Tommy's offer additional support. You may also be eligible for financial help such as the Healthy Start voucher scheme or Sure Start Maternity Grant. Your GP and midwife can help you identify what you are entitled to.

### Can I get a support person for my birth?

Yes. You can bring any person you choose to your birth — a friend, family member, or doula. Some hospitals also offer the option of having a midwifery student present. In some areas, volunteer doula services support people without access to personal birth companions. Ask your midwife what is available locally.

### What if I am struggling emotionally as well as practically?

Tell your midwife or GP honestly how you are feeling. Perinatal mental health support — including talking therapies and specialist teams — is available free on the NHS. You do not need to be in crisis to ask for help. Feeling isolated, anxious, or low during pregnancy is recognised and taken seriously by the NHS.

Always speak to your midwife or GP for personalised advice about your situation and the support available to you.
