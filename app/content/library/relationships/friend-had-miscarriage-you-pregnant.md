# When a Friend Has Had a Miscarriage and You're Pregnant

Your friend has lost a pregnancy, and you are carrying one. The contrast is agonising — for both of you. You may feel guilty for being pregnant, terrified of saying the wrong thing, and unsure how to be a friend when your body is a daily reminder of their loss. This is one of the more emotionally complex situations pregnancy can place you in, and it is worth thinking through carefully.

## Understanding Miscarriage Grief

Miscarriage is a real loss, often unacknowledged publicly. Your friend is grieving a baby, a future, and often a sense of safety in pregnancy. The grief is not straightforward — it can be invisible to others, dismissed with well-meaning platitudes, and painfully isolating.

Your friend's grief may resurface with your milestones — your scan, your bump, your baby's movements, and eventually the arrival of your baby. This is not envy; it is loss being mirrored back. Being around your pregnancy may bring up everything they have lost, even on days when they appear fine.

There is no predictable timeline for this kind of grief. Some people feel better within weeks; others carry it for months or years, particularly if they go on to experience more losses or struggle to conceive again.

## How to Tell Them You're Pregnant

Breaking the news thoughtfully can make a real difference to your friendship.

- **Tell them privately, ideally in writing.** A text or message gives them space to react without an audience, without having to manage their expression in front of you.
- **Acknowledge the difficulty.** Something like: "I'm pregnant, and I know this may be really hard to hear. You don't need to reply right away."
- **Don't cushion it with platitudes.** Avoid "everything happens for a reason," "at least you know you can get pregnant," or "it'll happen for you soon." These are painful to hear, even when kindly meant.
- **Respect their response.** Silence, tears, a brief "congratulations," or a longer reply — all are valid. Don't push for a reaction.
- **Consider your timing.** If they are in the very early weeks after their loss, you may want to hold off if your pregnancy is not yet visible or widely known, unless you feel they would want to hear it from you rather than someone else.

## Being a Friend Through Your Pregnancy

- **Let them choose their level of involvement.** Some friends want to celebrate with you; others need to step back from pregnancy-related conversations for a while. Follow their lead rather than deciding for them.
- **Don't hide your pregnancy, but don't centre it with them.** Talk about the rest of your friendship — things you have in common, things they care about. You can be pregnant and still be interested in their life.
- **Mark their loss.** Don't let your pregnancy erase what happened to them. On the due date they lost, or on the anniversary of their miscarriage, a simple "I'm thinking of you today" can mean a great deal.
- **Avoid complaining about pregnancy symptoms in front of them.** They would endure every wave of nausea and every sleepless night for the baby they lost.
- **Be patient if they pull back.** Distance is sometimes necessary for self-protection, not rejection.

## Managing Your Guilt

Guilt is nearly universal in this situation. You may feel that your happiness is happening at their expense, or that your bump is somehow cruel to them. It is worth naming this: you did not cause their loss, and your joy does not steal from their grief. Both things can exist at the same time.

If guilt is becoming overwhelming or affecting your ability to enjoy your pregnancy, talking it through with a counsellor, your partner, or a trusted friend outside the situation can help. You are allowed to be happy. You are also allowed to feel complicated about it.

## If the Friendship Changes

Some friendships strain under the weight of this asymmetry. That does not make either of you a bad person — it is simply a reflection of how painful and difficult the circumstances are. If your friend needs distance, try not to take it personally.

Keep the door open gently: "I miss you, and I completely understand if you need space. I'm here whenever you're ready." Avoid withdrawing yourself out of guilt or awkwardness — friendships can and do recover once some time has passed, particularly if both people felt genuinely cared for.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Should I tell my friend I'm pregnant before I tell others?

Generally, yes — if the friendship is close. Hearing about your pregnancy from a mutual friend or social media will feel worse than hearing it from you directly. A private, thoughtful message gives them control over how and when they process the news.

### What if my friend seems fine but I still feel guilty?

Guilt doesn't require the other person to be visibly suffering. It is worth acknowledging your feelings, perhaps in a journal or with a counsellor, rather than suppressing them. At the same time, don't project your guilt onto your friend by constantly apologising or asking if they're okay — that can put them in the position of managing your emotions alongside their own grief.

### What do I do if my friend stops talking to me?

Give them time without withdrawing permanently. A gentle message every few weeks — not about your pregnancy — lets them know you're still there. Most friendships that go quiet during a painful period find their way back once circumstances shift, especially if both people feel the friendship was genuinely valued.
