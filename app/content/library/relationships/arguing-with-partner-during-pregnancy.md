# Arguing With Your Partner During Pregnancy

Pregnancy hormones, stress, and life changes can lead to more arguments. Here's how to handle it.

## Why Arguments Increase {#section-1}

It is very common for couples to argue more during pregnancy. There are several reasons why conflict can increase at this time:

- **Hormonal changes** affect your mood, patience, and emotional responses. Rising levels of oestrogen and progesterone can make you feel more irritable or emotionally sensitive, sometimes without a clear reason.
- **Financial stress** — the cost of having a baby, especially if one of you is planning to take maternity or paternity leave, can make money a source of tension.
- **Disagreements about parenting** — pregnancy brings up big questions about how you will raise your child, and you may discover that you and your partner have very different ideas about things like childcare, discipline, or feeding choices.
- **Physical discomfort** — nausea, fatigue, back pain, and poor sleep can make you less resilient. When you feel rough, small irritations can feel much bigger.
- **Fear and anxiety** — both of you may be feeling worried about the birth, your health, or becoming parents, even if you do not talk about it directly. Unexpressed anxiety can come out as irritability or snapping at each other.
- **Feeling unsupported** — you may need more from your partner at this time, and if they do not intuitively know what you need, you can feel let down.

It is important to remember that going through a period of more conflict does not mean your relationship is failing. Many couples find that acknowledging these pressures openly makes a big difference.

## How to Reduce Conflict {#section-2}

There are practical steps that can help you and your partner navigate this challenging time without damaging your relationship.

**Acknowledge the role of hormones and stress.** Simply knowing that some of your emotional reactions are amplified by pregnancy can help both of you be more patient. It is not an excuse, but it is useful context. Ask your partner to try to meet you with more understanding, and try to do the same for them.

**Take a timeout when things get heated.** If a conversation starts escalating, it is always better to pause than to say something you regret. Agree in advance that either of you can call a timeout and come back to the discussion when you're both calmer.

**Pick your battles.** Not every disagreement needs to be resolved right now. Some things that feel urgent in pregnancy (like the exact nursery colour) are less important than the relationship itself. Save your energy for conversations that really matter.

**Communicate your needs clearly and early.** Rather than waiting until you feel resentful, tell your partner what you need — whether that is practical help, emotional support, or just someone to listen without offering solutions. Partners often want to help but do not always know how.

**Make time to connect outside of the practical.** Pregnancy can quickly become all logistics — scans, symptoms, finances. Try to keep spending time together that is not focused on the baby, even if it is just watching something you both enjoy or going for a walk.

**Consider relationship counselling.** If arguments are frequent, escalating, or leaving you feeling unhappy in your relationship, talking to a couples counsellor can make a real difference. You do not have to wait until things are very bad to seek support. Your GP can advise you, or you can self-refer to a counsellor through organisations such as Relate.

## When Conflict Becomes a Concern

There is a difference between ordinary relationship tension and something more serious. If arguments involve threats, controlling behaviour, or any form of physical or emotional harm, that is not normal relationship conflict — that is domestic abuse, and pregnancy can unfortunately be a time when abuse begins or escalates.

If you ever feel unsafe, contact the National Domestic Abuse Helpline on 0808 2000 247 (free, 24 hours). You can also speak confidentially to your midwife.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is it normal to argue more during pregnancy?

Yes. Hormonal changes, stress, and the life transition of having a baby can increase conflict. This is common and does not mean your relationship is in trouble. Many couples find that openly acknowledging the pressures of pregnancy — rather than letting them build up unspoken — helps them manage conflict better.

### Can arguing harm the baby?

Everyday arguing and ordinary relationship tension does not harm your baby. However, chronic, severe, or ongoing stress — particularly if it involves fear, anxiety, or feeling unsafe — is not good for you, and wellbeing during pregnancy matters. If conflict feels relentless or you feel very distressed, it is worth seeking support both for yourself and your relationship.

### When should I seek professional help for relationship conflict in pregnancy?

Consider speaking to your GP or midwife if arguments are happening very frequently, leaving you feeling depressed or anxious, or if you feel the relationship is in genuine difficulty. Organisations like Relate offer counselling for couples. If there is any element of control, threats, or physical harm, please reach out to the National Domestic Abuse Helpline immediately.

Speak to your midwife or GP if you have concerns about your mental health or the dynamics of your relationship during pregnancy — they are there to support you, not to judge.
