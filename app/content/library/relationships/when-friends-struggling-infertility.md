# When Friends Are Struggling with Infertility

Being pregnant while a close friend is struggling to conceive is one of the most emotionally complex situations pregnancy can bring. Joy and guilt arrive together. You want to share your news, celebrate openly, and lean on your friend — but you are acutely aware that your happiness may cause them pain. There is no perfect way through this, only honest, compassionate choices made one at a time.

## Understanding their experience

Infertility grief is often described as cyclical — hope and loss repeating monthly, sometimes over years. It can touch every part of a person's life: their sense of identity, their relationship with their partner, their financial security, and their self-worth. What can look like jealousy from the outside is usually grief, and it often coexists with genuine love for you.

Your friend may feel happy for you and devastated for themselves at the same time. Both are real and both are allowed. People going through fertility treatment are frequently exhausted — physically from the process and emotionally from the uncertainty. Understanding this helps you show up for them without expecting them to perform feelings they do not have.

## Telling them your news — with care

How you tell a friend who is struggling to conceive can make a meaningful difference to how they receive the news.

- **Tell them privately, before any public announcement.** Finding out through a group message, social media post, or from someone else can feel like a blow that is hard to process in company. A private message or conversation gives them space to react honestly.
- **Keep it simple and direct.** Something like: "I wanted you to hear this from me first. I know this might be hard, and I completely understand whatever you feel." You do not need to over-explain or over-apologise — acknowledge the complexity and leave room for their response.
- **Do not manage their reaction.** If they need space, if they cry, or if they go quiet, let them. Do not ask them to reassure you, tell you they are happy for you, or hurry through their feelings.
- **Give them time before the next contact.** They may need a day or two to process before they can be in touch. Sending one warm follow-up message after a few days is fine — repeating it anxiously is not.
- **Avoid complaining about pregnancy symptoms to them.** Morning sickness, tiredness, and bump discomforts are legitimate struggles, but they land very differently on someone who would endure anything to be in your position. Find other people to vent to for these conversations.

## What ongoing support looks like

Once you have shared your news, the way you maintain the friendship matters as much as the initial conversation.

- **Follow their lead.** Some friends will want to stay closely involved in your pregnancy; others will need to step back for a while. Ask gently what they need, and ask again as circumstances change — it is not a one-time question.
- **Keep the friendship broader than babies.** Make space to talk about the things you have always shared — work, life, humour, the world — not only your pregnancy. They are still a whole person with a whole life.
- **Acknowledge what matters to them.** If you know they have had a difficult treatment cycle, a failed round, or an important appointment, checking in with a simple message shows you have not forgotten what they are going through.
- **Avoid unhelpful reassurances.** "It'll happen for you," "just relax and it will work," or "have you thought about adoption?" are painful to receive, even when kindly meant. What most people want is simply to be heard and not forgotten.

## Managing your own feelings

Being pregnant alongside a friend's infertility can stir up feelings you did not expect. Guilt about your own good fortune. Sadness that you cannot share things freely. Loneliness if they need to step back. Resentment, occasionally, that your joy is complicated by someone else's grief.

All of these are understandable and human. You are allowed to feel joy about your pregnancy. You are allowed to find this situation genuinely hard. What matters is where you put those feelings — lean into other friends, a partner, or a therapist for the things you cannot bring to this particular friendship right now. That is not a betrayal; it is good emotional management.

## If the friendship changes shape

Some friendships inevitably shift during this period. A friend who is deep in fertility treatment may not be able to be present for your pregnancy milestones in the way you hoped. That is a real loss, and it is okay to feel it. It does not mean the friendship is over.

Many friendships that pause during this season find their way back — often once the immediate intensity eases, whether that means a pregnancy for them, or simply time passing. Staying kind, staying in light contact, and not taking their distance as rejection goes a long way.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Should I tell my friend about my pregnancy in person, by message, or by phone?

This depends on your friendship and how raw things feel for them. A private message or phone call — rather than in person — often works best, because it gives them time and space to react honestly without having to manage their face or emotions in front of you. If you do meet in person, choose somewhere private rather than a public or social setting.

### What if my friend completely withdraws from our friendship?

Give them space without disappearing entirely. An occasional low-key message — not about the pregnancy — keeps the door open without pressuring them. If you genuinely miss them, it is okay to say so simply: "I miss you. No pressure, but I'm here when you're ready." If they need a longer break, try not to interpret it as permanent. Grief has its own timeline.

### Is it normal to feel guilty for being pregnant when a friend is struggling?

Yes, very common. Pregnancy guilt in this situation does not mean you have done anything wrong. It usually reflects how much you care about your friend. Acknowledge the feeling, talk it through with a partner or another close friend, and try not to let the guilt push you into either over-managing your friend's emotions or hiding your own happiness entirely. Both extremes tend to make things harder in the long run.
