# When Friends Don't Understand Your Pregnancy Anxiety

Pregnancy anxiety can be relentless — scan worries, birth fears, obsessive symptom-checking, catastrophic thoughts at 3am. And then a friend says "try not to worry" or "stress isn't good for the baby," and you feel both unheard and scolded. When friends don't get it, the loneliness can be as hard as the anxiety itself.

## Why friends may not understand
Most people's image of pregnancy is the glowing bump, not the 4am panic spiral. If your friends haven't been pregnant, or had straightforward experiences, they may genuinely not know what perinatal anxiety feels like. Their dismissiveness is often ignorance, not cruelty — though it still hurts.

## How to bridge the gap
- **Be specific about what helps.** "I don't need reassurance that everything's fine — I need someone to sit with me while I feel scared."
- **Name the condition.** "I've got perinatal anxiety. It's a recognised thing. Logic doesn't switch it off."
- **Ask for what you don't want.** "Please don't tell me to relax — it makes it worse. Can we just talk about something normal?"
- **Share an article.** Sometimes a third-party explanation lands better than your own words.

## The friends who do get it
Not every friend will be your pregnancy support. That's OK. You may find your best sources of understanding in antenatal groups, perinatal mental health communities, or online forums where people are living it too. Expanding your circle isn't betrayal — it's practical.

## Protecting the friendships you value
If a friend is trying but missing the mark, give them another chance with clearer guidance: "When I say I'm anxious, I'm not asking you to fix it. I just want to be heard." Most caring people adjust when they know the recipe.

## When to step back
If a friend repeatedly dismisses, mocks, or stresses you, you can limit contact without ending the friendship. "I'm needing quieter weekends at the moment" is a kind way to create space.

## Looking after yourself
Untreated perinatal anxiety can persist into postnatal life. If it's affecting sleep, appetite, or daily function, talk to your midwife or GP. CBT, counselling, and sometimes medication are effective and pregnancy-safe options.

## A reminder
Your anxiety is real, even when others can't see it. The right support exists — you may just need to look in different places to find it.
