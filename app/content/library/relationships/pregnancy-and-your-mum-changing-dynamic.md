# Pregnancy and Your Mum: The Changing Dynamic

Becoming a mum yourself changes your relationship with your own mum in ways that are hard to predict. For some women it brings a new closeness — a sense of finally understanding what she went through. For others it surfaces tension, old patterns, or a feeling that the boundary lines need redrawing entirely. Most experiences fall somewhere in between. Whatever yours looks like, knowing what to expect makes it easier to navigate.

## How the Relationship Shifts {#section-1}

Pregnancy triggers a shift in your mum's role at the same time as it triggers a shift in yours. She is becoming a grandmother — a new identity she may be processing alongside her existing one as your mother. That combination of change on both sides is why the dynamic can feel amplified during pregnancy, even in families that are generally close and low-conflict.

Common changes you might notice:

- **She becomes more involved, or wants to be.** Grandmotherhood is a significant life event for her too, and her enthusiasm — or her need to feel included — can come through in ways that feel like pressure to you.
- **She offers unsolicited advice.** This is probably the most universal experience. The advice may be outdated, contradict what your midwife has told you, or simply not reflect how you want to approach your pregnancy. This does not make it easy to receive.
- **She compares your pregnancy to hers.** Every generation of pregnant women has been told "we didn't worry about that in my day." Guidelines change. What was standard advice thirty years ago may not align with current NHS guidance.
- **You feel closer than before.** Many women describe a new understanding of their own mother's experience once they are pregnant themselves — a sense of stepping into her shoes for the first time and finding unexpected empathy.
- **You feel more distant, or old tensions resurface.** Pregnancy can bring pre-existing family dynamics into sharper focus. If your relationship with your mum has always been complicated, the additional emotional weight of pregnancy does not make that simpler.

## When You Feel Closer {#section-2}

If pregnancy has brought you and your mum closer, that shift is worth acknowledging and enjoying. Women often describe a particular kind of connection that comes from joining what might be called the "motherhood club" — a shared experience that transcends the parent-child dynamic and introduces something more like peer understanding.

You might find yourself asking her questions you never thought to ask before: what her pregnancy was like, what birth felt like for her, what she was most frightened of. These conversations can be genuinely meaningful and open up parts of her experience that she may never have felt invited to share.

If this closeness comes with a tendency for her to be very involved in your pregnancy decisions, it is still worth thinking about where your boundaries are — not to push her away, but to make sure the closeness is sustainable rather than something that eventually generates resentment.

## When It Feels Complicated {#section-3}

It is also completely normal if pregnancy brings up difficult feelings about your relationship with your mum. Perhaps your relationship has always been strained and the expectation that pregnancy will fix it feels like pressure. Perhaps she is not well or not alive, and her absence during this time is its own kind of grief. Perhaps she is very present in ways that feel overwhelming rather than supportive.

Some specific situations that women commonly find hard:

- **Your mum had a difficult birth or pregnancy and shares those stories frequently.** This can increase anxiety rather than offering comfort. It is reasonable to tell her that you find those stories hard to hear right now.
- **She dismisses your concerns as overthinking.** Guidelines around things like food safety, drinking, and medication in pregnancy have changed significantly. Your caution is based on current NHS advice; her dismissal is based on different-era experience. Both of those things can be true simultaneously.
- **She wants to be more involved in birth plans, scan appointments, or name choices than you are comfortable with.** These are your decisions. Wanting space to make them without input is not unkind.
- **She and your partner do not get on.** Pregnancy can escalate existing tensions and create a sense that you are being pulled in different directions.

## Managing the Relationship Day to Day {#section-4}

There is no formula that works for every mother-daughter pair, but these approaches tend to help:

- **Set boundaries early and clearly, but warmly.** It is much easier to establish what you do and do not want to discuss at the start of a pregnancy than to try to renegotiate later. You might say something like: "I know you're excited and I love that — I'd really rather hear your stories after the baby arrives rather than during, just because I get anxious."
- **Acknowledge her excitement.** She is becoming a grandmother. That is meaningful to her. Even when her enthusiasm is hard to manage, recognising it tends to go over better than responses that feel like rejection.
- **Redirect advice rather than shutting it down entirely.** "My midwife has given me guidance on that" is a phrase that works well because it is both true and non-confrontational.
- **Share what you are comfortable sharing, and no more.** You are not obligated to tell your mum everything about your pregnancy — your symptoms, your anxieties, your birth plans, or your scan results — in real time. Choose what to share based on what feels good for you, not out of guilt.
- **Remember that her advice comes from love, even when it is not helpful.** That does not mean you have to follow it, but it can make it easier to receive.

Speak to your midwife or GP if you are finding family relationships during pregnancy significantly affecting your wellbeing — it is more common than you might think, and support is available.

## FAQ {#faq}

### My mum keeps giving me unwanted pregnancy advice. What do I do?

Set boundaries gently but clearly. A phrase like "I know you're trying to help, and I've got advice from my midwife that I'm following" acknowledges her intention without inviting further input. If the advice keeps coming, it is reasonable to be a little more direct: "I'd rather not have advice on this one — I just want to enjoy the pregnancy." Most people respond better to a clear boundary than to hints.

### Is it normal to feel closer to my mum during pregnancy?

Yes, and it is one of the most commonly described shifts in the mother-daughter relationship during this time. Pregnancy gives you a new perspective on what your mum went through — you may understand her choices, fears, and experiences in a way you simply could not before. That can feel like a genuinely new kind of connection. Not every woman experiences this, and that is equally normal. Relationships are shaped by long history, not just the current moment.

### What if my mum is not around or we are not close?

Pregnancy can bring grief over an absent or distant mother to the surface, sometimes unexpectedly. If your mum has died, is estranged, or is not someone you can lean on, that loss can feel particularly present at a time when many women around you seem to have close maternal support. Talking to a counsellor, joining a peer support group, or being honest with your midwife about your circumstances can all help. You do not need a particular family structure to have a supported pregnancy.

Speak to your midwife or GP for personalised support if you are finding the emotional side of pregnancy relationships difficult.
