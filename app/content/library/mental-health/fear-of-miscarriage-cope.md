# Fear of Miscarriage: How to Cope

Fear of miscarriage is one of the most common anxieties in early pregnancy, and it can cast a shadow over what should be an exciting time. Understanding the facts, learning to manage anxious thoughts, and knowing where to turn can help you cope during weeks 0–12.

## Understanding the Real Statistics {#section-1}

The overall miscarriage rate is around one in five pregnancies, but this includes many very early losses, some of which happen before a woman even knows she is pregnant. By the time a heartbeat is seen on scan, the risk drops significantly. After a confirmed heartbeat at 8 weeks, the risk of loss is generally low. Most miscarriages are caused by chromosomal issues that arise at the point of conception and are not preventable. Knowing these facts can help counter catastrophic thoughts that miscarriage is inevitable or likely at every stage.

It is also worth understanding that the risk reduces further as pregnancy progresses. The majority of miscarriages occur in the first eight weeks. By 10 weeks, particularly once a heartbeat has been confirmed, many care providers describe the ongoing risk as substantially reduced. This does not mean there is no risk after this point, but the trajectory of risk tends to reassure many women as they move through the first trimester.

If you have previously experienced a miscarriage, your anxiety in a subsequent pregnancy is entirely understandable. Previous loss does not increase your statistical risk in most cases, but it can make it much harder to feel confident that this pregnancy will progress. That heightened vigilance is a normal psychological response to loss, not a sign that something is wrong.

## Managing Anxious Thoughts About Miscarriage {#section-2}

Anxiety about miscarriage often works by treating uncertainty as danger. Your mind may catastrophise symptoms, interpret the absence of symptoms as a warning sign, or constantly seek reassurance through scanning apps, symptom lists, or online forums. These behaviours can maintain or worsen anxiety over time rather than relieving it.

Some approaches that can help:

**Recognise thoughts as thoughts, not facts.** An anxious thought that something is wrong is not the same as evidence that something is wrong. Naming the thought — "I'm having the worry again" — can create a small but useful distance from it.

**Limit symptom checking.** Pregnancy symptoms naturally fluctuate day to day and week to week. Repeatedly checking for symptoms, googling changes, or comparing yourself to others on forums tends to fuel anxiety rather than settle it.

**Set boundaries on online searching.** It can help to decide on a specific time window for reading about pregnancy — perhaps 10 minutes in the evening — rather than searching throughout the day. Loss stories are shared widely online, but they represent a small proportion of pregnancy outcomes.

**Talk to your midwife.** Many women hold fears privately rather than mentioning them to their care team. Midwives are accustomed to hearing these concerns and can offer reassurance, context, and referrals if needed.

**Ask about additional support if you have had a previous loss.** Some areas offer access to early reassurance scans or additional midwife contact for women with a history of pregnancy loss. It is worth asking what is available locally.

**Consider professional support.** If fear of miscarriage is dominating your days, stopping you from enjoying the pregnancy, or causing panic attacks, NHS Talking Therapies (previously IAPT) offer free counselling and CBT that is safe in pregnancy. Your GP can refer you or you can self-refer in most areas.

## When to Contact Your Midwife or Maternity Unit {#when-to-call}

Anxiety is not a medical emergency, but some physical symptoms do need prompt assessment. Contact your midwife or EPU (Early Pregnancy Unit) if you experience heavy bleeding (soaking a pad), severe one-sided pain, pain in your shoulder tip, or feeling faint alongside bleeding. These can be signs of complications that need to be checked urgently. If you are ever unsure, NHS 111 can advise you.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### What is the actual miscarriage rate in early pregnancy?

Around 10–20 percent of confirmed pregnancies end in miscarriage, with most occurring before 12 weeks. The risk is not uniform throughout those 12 weeks — it is highest in very early pregnancy and falls as gestation progresses and a heartbeat is confirmed.

### Does stress or exercise cause miscarriage?

No. Everyday stress, moderate exercise, lifting, and working do not cause miscarriage. Most losses are due to chromosomal factors present from the point of conception, not anything a parent did or did not do.

### How do I stop constantly worrying about miscarriage?

Professional support through NHS Talking Therapies, grounding techniques, limiting online searches, talking openly to your midwife, and — where available — early reassurance scans can all help reduce persistent fear. If anxiety is severe or debilitating, speak to your GP.
