# Pregnancy And Addiction Getting Help

Struggling with addiction during pregnancy? Where to get help without judgment.

Finding out you are pregnant while struggling with alcohol, drugs, or other substance use can feel overwhelming. You may be afraid of being judged, of being in trouble, or of what your use might mean for your baby. These feelings are completely understandable — but the most important thing to know is this: help is available, and reaching out is the best thing you can do for both yourself and your baby.

## Why Honesty With Your Healthcare Team Matters

Your midwife and GP are not there to judge you or report you. They are there to help you have the safest possible pregnancy. When they know about substance use, they can:

- Monitor you and your baby more closely
- Help you reduce or stop safely (sudden withdrawal from some substances can be dangerous in pregnancy and must be medically supervised)
- Connect you with specialist services
- Plan for your baby's care around the time of birth
- Support you with any mental health needs alongside your addiction

Hiding substance use from your care team puts you and your baby at greater risk, not less. The earlier you are honest, the more your team can do to help.

## Substances and Risks During Pregnancy

Different substances carry different risks, and your healthcare team will be able to give you personalised guidance. In general terms:

**Alcohol:** There is no established safe level of alcohol in pregnancy. The NHS recommends not drinking any alcohol when pregnant or trying to conceive. If you are dependent on alcohol, stopping suddenly without medical support can be dangerous — speak to your GP before cutting down.

**Smoking and tobacco:** Smoking in pregnancy increases the risk of complications. NHS Stop Smoking services offer free, non-judgmental support and can provide nicotine replacement therapy (NRT) that is considered safer than continuing to smoke.

**Cannabis:** Increasingly normalised but not safe in pregnancy. It is associated with low birth weight and other complications. Your midwife can help you access support to stop.

**Opioids (including heroin and prescribed opioids):** Stopping opioids abruptly in pregnancy can cause withdrawal that harms the baby. A specialist team can prescribe supervised substitution treatment (such as methadone or buprenorphine) that is far safer than uncontrolled use. This is a well-established, NHS-supported approach.

**Other drugs:** Whether it is cocaine, MDMA, prescription medication misuse, or anything else, your care team needs to know so they can plan appropriate monitoring and support.

## Where to Get Help in the UK

You do not have to wait for your next midwife appointment if you need help now. These services are free and confidential:

- **Your GP or midwife** — always the first port of call. They can refer you to specialist perinatal substance misuse services.
- **FRANK** — free drug information and referral helpline: **0300 123 6600**, available 24 hours. Frank is non-judgmental and can help you find local services.
- **NHS Smokefree** — free stop-smoking support online and by phone: **0300 123 1044**.
- **Alcoholics Anonymous UK** — **0800 9177 650**.
- **Turning Point, Change Grow Live, and other local drug and alcohol services** — available in most areas of the UK. Your GP can refer you, or you can self-refer in many areas.

## What to Expect from Specialist Services

Specialist perinatal substance misuse teams exist in many areas of the UK and are designed specifically to support pregnant women and new mothers. They are staffed by professionals who understand addiction and pregnancy and will not treat you differently because of your history. Services typically include:

- Medical assessment and supervised withdrawal or substitution if appropriate
- Counselling and psychological support
- Practical help such as housing or benefits advice
- Close liaison with your maternity team

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Will I get in trouble for substance use in pregnancy?

Healthcare professionals are there to help, not to punish. Disclosing substance use to your midwife or GP will not automatically result in your baby being taken away. Social services involvement, when it happens, is focused on supporting families — not removing babies as a first response. Honesty with your care team actually puts you in a much stronger position: it shows you are engaged and working to give your baby the best start.

### Can I get help for addiction during pregnancy?

Absolutely. Specialist perinatal addiction services exist across the UK. Your GP or midwife can refer you, or you can contact FRANK on 0300 123 6600 to find local services. Treatment is adapted for pregnancy — for example, medically supervised opioid substitution is considered much safer than continuing to use uncontrolled opioids, and stop-smoking services can provide nicotine replacement therapy safe for use in pregnancy.

### Is it safe to stop using substances abruptly when I find out I'm pregnant?

It depends on the substance. For some substances, such as smoking cannabis or stopping recreational drug use, stopping immediately is straightforward and beneficial. For alcohol dependence or opioid dependence, stopping abruptly can cause withdrawal that may be dangerous in pregnancy. Always speak to your GP or midwife before making sudden changes — they will help you stop safely with proper medical support.

Always speak to your midwife or GP if you have any concerns about substance use during your pregnancy.
