# Pregnancy After Stillbirth: Support and Coping

Pregnancy after stillbirth is one of the most emotionally complex experiences a mother can go through. You are grieving one baby while carrying another, and both feelings can exist at the same time. This page covers what is normal to feel, the additional care you can expect from the NHS, and where to find support.

## The Emotional Complexity {#section-1}

There is no single right way to feel in a pregnancy that follows stillbirth. Many women describe a constant undercurrent of anxiety, an inability to look too far forward, or a feeling that hope itself feels dangerous. All of these responses are understandable, and none of them mean you are failing.

You may feel:

- **Guilt** for being pregnant again — as though moving forward is somehow a betrayal of the baby you lost
- **Fear that history will repeat itself**, even if your previous stillbirth had a cause that has been addressed or is unlikely to recur
- **Difficulty bonding** with the new baby — deliberately holding back emotionally as a form of self-protection
- **Grief that intensifies** as due dates, milestones, or scans bring your loss back to the surface
- **Pressure to be happy** when you are actually terrified, particularly if those around you expect this pregnancy to simply be joyful
- **Numbness or detachment**, especially in the early weeks

These are not signs that something is wrong with you. They are a normal response to profound loss. Acknowledging the complexity — rather than pushing the grief aside — is part of what helps over time.

## Your Antenatal Care After Stillbirth {#section-2}

The NHS recognises that pregnancies following stillbirth need additional support. You should not have to fight for enhanced care, though being clear about your history at your booking appointment helps your team put a plan in place early.

What you can expect, or request:

- **Additional scans** — more frequent monitoring, particularly from the point at which your previous loss occurred and in the third trimester
- **A named midwife or consultant** who oversees your care throughout, rather than seeing different faces at every appointment
- **A written care plan** created with you that acknowledges your previous loss and reflects your preferences
- **Earlier admission discussions** — some women prefer a planned early induction, and this should be a conversation with your consultant
- **Access to perinatal mental health services** — your team can refer you if anxiety or low mood is significantly affecting your wellbeing

If your previous stillbirth was investigated and a cause found, make sure your new care team has access to those notes. This helps them understand what monitoring is most relevant for you.

## Getting Support {#section-3}

Practical steps that can genuinely help:

- **Sands** (sands.org.uk) is the UK's leading stillbirth and neonatal death charity. They run support groups, a helpline, and an online community — including specific support for people in a subsequent pregnancy.
- **Tommy's** (tommys.org) funds research into stillbirth and offers resources for people pregnant after loss, including a midwife helpline.
- **PETALS** (petalscharity.org) offers free specialist counselling for people who have experienced pregnancy loss, including stillbirth.
- **Rainbow Trust** supports families during and after a difficult pregnancy.
- **Ask for a specialist perinatal mental health referral** — your GP or midwife can refer you. Talking therapy during pregnancy is effective and available on the NHS.
- **Consider counselling specific to pregnancy after loss** — a therapist experienced in grief and reproductive trauma can offer a space that general support groups do not always provide.
- **Create a memory box or ritual for your lost baby** — many parents find that acknowledging their baby's place in the family helps, rather than feeling they must put the grief away for the sake of the new pregnancy.

You do not have to choose between grieving your baby and welcoming this one. Both can be true at the same time.

## FAQ {#faq}

### Is pregnancy after stillbirth considered high risk?

Yes, and the NHS will treat it as such. You will be offered enhanced monitoring throughout your pregnancy, and your care team should create a personalised plan that reflects your individual history. What that monitoring looks like depends on the cause and circumstances of your previous loss, which is why a thorough review at the start of your antenatal care is important. Many women go on to have healthy subsequent pregnancies after stillbirth.

### How do I cope with the fear of losing this baby too?

There is no way to make the fear disappear entirely, and trying to suppress it often makes it worse. Many women find it helps to take the pregnancy one scan, one week, or one milestone at a time rather than looking too far ahead. Letting your care team know how you are feeling — especially if anxiety is affecting sleep, eating, or daily functioning — opens the door to additional support. Perinatal mental health services, counselling, and peer support groups from charities such as Sands can all make a real difference.

### Should I tell my new care team about my previous stillbirth?

Yes, and as early as possible — ideally at your booking appointment. Your history will shape your care plan, the monitoring you are offered, and the support you receive. You should not have to repeatedly explain your loss to different clinicians; a good care team will ensure your history is documented and understood by everyone involved in your care.

Speak to your midwife or GP for personalised guidance on your care and the support available to you.
