# Pregnancy After Loss: Week by Week Coping

Pregnancy after loss can feel like an emotional obstacle course, with each week bringing new hopes and new fears. This week-by-week guide offers coping strategies specific to weeks 0–12, the period most commonly associated with anxiety after miscarriage or loss.

## Weeks 0-4: The unknown and the wait {#section-1}

Before a positive test, or in the earliest days after one, you may feel caught between hope and protective detachment. Both responses are completely understandable. Some women who have experienced previous loss describe feeling numb when they see a positive test — not because they are not happy, but because happiness feels risky. That is a normal psychological response, not a sign that something is wrong with you or your pregnancy.

In these early weeks:

- **Avoid obsessive testing.** Taking multiple tests each day to monitor line darkness is a common anxiety behaviour, but it rarely provides the reassurance it promises and can fuel worry if results fluctuate.
- **Contact your midwife early.** In the UK you can self-refer to midwifery care from the moment you have a positive test. Mention your previous loss clearly so your notes are flagged and appropriate care can be arranged.
- **Limit symptom-spotting.** Pregnancy symptoms vary enormously between women and between pregnancies. The absence or presence of a particular symptom does not reliably indicate the health of the pregnancy at this stage.

## Weeks 5-8: The earliest symptoms and first appointments {#section-2}

By five to eight weeks, symptoms such as nausea, breast tenderness, and fatigue may be present — or may not be. This period is often particularly hard for those who experienced a missed miscarriage previously, because the absence of symptoms in a prior pregnancy may have been the first sign something was wrong.

It is worth telling your midwife if anxiety is significantly affecting your daily life at this stage. Many NHS trusts, and all Early Pregnancy Units (EPUs), are experienced in supporting women with a history of loss. You may be offered an early scan from around seven weeks if you have had a previous miscarriage or ectopic pregnancy — ask your GP or midwife about local policy.

Strategies that help in these weeks include:

- **Keeping a routine.** Ordinary structure — meals, sleep, gentle movement, work — gives the mind something other than the pregnancy to anchor to.
- **Choosing your confidants carefully.** You may not want to announce the pregnancy widely yet, but having one or two trusted people who know can reduce the isolation of early anxiety.
- **Acknowledging both feelings at once.** It is possible to be simultaneously hopeful and terrified. You do not have to resolve this paradox — just name it.

## Weeks 9-12: Scans, milestones, and managing fear {#section-3}

The dating scan, usually offered between 10 and 14 weeks, is a significant milestone. For women with a history of loss, the days leading up to a scan can be some of the most anxiety-laden of the pregnancy.

**Plan the day around the scan.** Book something comforting afterwards — a walk, a meal with your partner, time at home with a film. Give yourself permission not to work that day if possible.

**Tell the sonographer your history.** NHS sonographers are used to supporting anxious patients, and knowing about a previous loss allows them to offer appropriate reassurance as they scan. They cannot always tell you everything in real time, but most will talk you through what they are seeing.

**Ask for continuity of carer where possible.** Seeing the same midwife consistently is associated with better emotional outcomes in pregnancy. Ask your midwife team whether this is available to you.

**Consider a private reassurance scan.** Between NHS appointments, some women find a private early scan helpful. This is a personal choice — for some it reduces anxiety; for others it temporarily reassures but does not address the underlying fear. Your midwife can advise.

If anxiety is overwhelming your day-to-day life — you are unable to sleep, eat, work, or enjoy anything — please ask your midwife for a referral to perinatal mental health services. These services exist specifically for pregnant women and are available on the NHS. You do not have to be coping at a minimum level before you qualify for support.

## Caring for yourself throughout {#section-4}

Beyond specific weeks, a few principles apply throughout the first trimester after loss:

- **Grief and hope can coexist.** You are allowed to mourn the baby you lost at the same time as hoping for this one. These feelings are not contradictory.
- **Protect your energy.** You are not obliged to respond to every piece of pregnancy-related advice or attend every social event. Rest is medically legitimate.
- **Connect with others who understand.** Organisations such as the Miscarriage Association and Tommy's offer specific peer support for women pregnant after loss, including online communities and helplines.

## FAQ {#faq}

### When can I have an early scan after loss?

Many NHS trusts offer early pregnancy scans from around seven weeks for women who have experienced a previous miscarriage or ectopic pregnancy. Access varies by trust. Ask your GP or self-referral midwife team about what is available locally, or contact your nearest Early Pregnancy Unit directly.

### How do I cope with the wait between scans?

Distraction, maintaining routine, talking to trusted people, and limiting pregnancy forum use can all help. Some women find it useful to book private reassurance scans between NHS appointments. Managing anxiety between scans is genuinely hard — if it becomes overwhelming, a referral to perinatal mental health services can make a meaningful difference.

### Is it normal not to feel excited after loss?

Yes. Protective detachment — holding back excitement in order to protect yourself from potential pain — is a common and understandable response after pregnancy loss. It does not mean you will not bond with your baby. Many women find that excitement and attachment grow gradually as the pregnancy progresses and they reach milestones that felt uncertain. Be patient with yourself.
