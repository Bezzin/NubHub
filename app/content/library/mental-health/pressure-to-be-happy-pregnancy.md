# The Pressure to Be Happy During Pregnancy

You're pregnant, so you should be glowing, right? That's what the world tells you — through social media, well-meaning relatives, adverts, and even your antenatal app. But for many women, pregnancy is not nine months of unbroken joy, and feeling obliged to perform happiness can become a weight in itself. This page is for anyone who has found the gap between how they "should" feel and how they actually feel a difficult place to sit.

## Where the pressure comes from

Pregnancy is treated as a public celebration. People ask if you're excited, comment on your glow, and assume you're thrilled. When your internal reality doesn't match that story, the gap can feel isolating. You may worry that admitting sadness or ambivalence makes you ungrateful, especially if the pregnancy was planned or followed fertility struggles.

Social media plays a particular role. Carefully curated maternity shoots, bump photos, and announcements present an almost universally joyful view of pregnancy. Seeing this repeatedly can make ordinary difficult days feel like personal failures rather than what they are — a normal part of a physically and emotionally demanding experience.

Family and cultural expectations add another layer. In some families, pregnancy is immediately a communal event. Relatives ask questions, share unsolicited opinions, and interpret anything other than radiant happiness as a problem to be solved. If your own family struggled to conceive or you had previous losses, this can feel especially complicated — as if joy is owed to them, too.

Well-meaning comments such as "make the most of it before the baby arrives" or "you'll miss being pregnant" can leave you feeling that your current discomfort is trivial and that you are failing to appreciate what you have. These remarks are usually kindly meant, but they carry an implicit message: your difficult feelings are not welcome here.

## Why it's normal to feel mixed emotions

Pregnancy brings enormous physical, hormonal, and life change. Anxiety about birth, financial stress, relationship shifts, morning sickness, and sleep disruption all take a toll. Mixed feelings are not a sign you won't love your baby. They are a sign you are a human undergoing a major transition.

Hormonal fluctuations in the first trimester can cause low mood, irritability, and tearfulness — the very time when many people feel most pressure to keep up a happy front while waiting to share their news. Exhaustion alone can make the world feel darker. Add to that the uncertainty of early pregnancy, worries about miscarriage risk, and the sheer number of decisions suddenly in front of you, and it is no surprise that the emotional experience is often far messier than the world suggests.

Antenatal anxiety and low mood are among the most common experiences in pregnancy, yet many women do not mention them to their midwife because they fear being seen as ungrateful or as struggling. This silence makes an already isolating experience lonelier still. The cultural script around pregnancy as a time of pure joy actively discourages honesty, and that matters — because unexpressed distress that goes unsupported tends to worsen over time.

It is also worth noting that ambivalence about pregnancy — even a very much wanted pregnancy — is entirely normal. Feeling excited one moment and overwhelmed the next is not a contradiction; it is the reality of navigating a life-changing event. Ambivalence does not predict how good a parent you will be.

## How to handle the pressure

- **Name it honestly.** Acknowledging "I'm struggling today" to yourself or a trusted person reduces the load. You do not have to perform happiness for people who aren't experiencing what you are.
- **Curate your feed.** Unfollow accounts that only show glowing bumps. Seek realistic content from people who talk openly about the full range of pregnancy experiences.
- **Set a gentle script.** When someone asks "Aren't you just so excited?" you don't owe them your interior life. Try: "I'm doing OK, thanks. It's been a tougher week." Most people will accept this without pressing.
- **Identify who is safe.** There are usually one or two people in your life with whom you can be fully honest. Leaning on those people, rather than trying to manage everyone else's feelings, protects your energy.
- **Limit conversations that drain you.** If certain family members or friends consistently make you feel pressured to perform positivity, it is reasonable to reduce those interactions or keep them brief.
- **Validate yourself.** You do not need external permission to feel what you feel. Recognising that your emotions are legitimate — even when the world is telling you otherwise — is an act of genuine self-care.

## When to seek help

If you feel persistently low, tearful, numb, or detached from the pregnancy for more than two weeks, or if you are having thoughts of harming yourself, tell a healthcare professional. Antenatal mental health support is common and effective — you are not wasting anyone's time, and these feelings are taken seriously.

Your midwife, GP, or health visitor can refer you to perinatal mental health services, which exist specifically to support people during pregnancy. Talking therapy and, where needed, medication options that are considered safe in pregnancy are both available on the NHS. You do not need to be in crisis to access these services — seeking help early leads to better outcomes.

If you need to speak to someone urgently, you can contact the Samaritans on 116 123 (free, 24 hours), or call 111 for urgent non-emergency mental health support.

## What those around you can do

If someone you love is pregnant and seems less than glowing, the most useful thing is to ask open questions and then actually listen — not to reassure them that everything is fine, or to tell them they'll feel differently soon. "How are you actually feeling?" followed by genuine attention is worth more than a dozen reminders of how lucky they are.

Resist the urge to fix the mood or redirect toward the positives. Simply bearing witness to someone's difficult feelings, without rushing to resolve them, is a quietly powerful form of support. Saying "that sounds really hard" before anything else creates space for honesty. That space is often all a struggling pregnant woman needs to feel less alone.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Is it normal to feel unhappy or anxious during pregnancy?

Yes. Mixed emotions, anxiety, and low mood are all common in pregnancy, particularly in the first trimester. They do not mean something is wrong with you or that you will not bond with your baby. If these feelings are persistent or severe, speak to your midwife or GP.

### What if I feel guilty for not enjoying my pregnancy?

Guilt is a very common response, especially for people who tried hard to conceive or who have experienced loss. It is worth remembering that emotion is not a choice — you cannot will yourself into happiness any more than you can will away morning sickness. Guilt about your feelings is an extra burden you do not need to carry. Talking to a counsellor or perinatal mental health professional can help.

### How do I tell my midwife I'm struggling emotionally?

Your midwife will ask about your emotional wellbeing at several points during pregnancy using standard questions. You can also bring it up yourself at any appointment. Simply saying "I've been feeling quite low and I wanted to mention it" is enough to open the conversation. Midwives are trained to respond to this and will not judge you.
