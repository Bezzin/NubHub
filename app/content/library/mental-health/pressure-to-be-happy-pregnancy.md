# The Pressure to Be Happy During Pregnancy

You're pregnant, so you should be glowing, right? That's what the world tells you — through social media, well-meaning relatives, adverts, and even your antenatal app. But for many women, pregnancy is not nine months of unbroken joy, and feeling obliged to perform happiness can become a weight in itself.

## Where the pressure comes from
Pregnancy is treated as a public celebration. People ask if you're excited, comment on your glow, and assume you're thrilled. When your internal reality doesn't match that story, the gap can feel isolating. You may worry that admitting sadness or ambivalence makes you ungrateful, especially if the pregnancy was planned or followed fertility struggles.

## Why it's normal to feel mixed emotions
Pregnancy brings enormous physical, hormonal, and life change. Anxiety about birth, financial stress, relationship shifts, morning sickness, and sleep disruption all take a toll. Mixed feelings are not a sign you won't love your baby. They are a sign you are a human undergoing a major transition.

## How to handle the pressure
- **Name it honestly.** Acknowledging "I'm struggling today" to yourself or a trusted person reduces the load.
- **Curate your feed.** Unfollow accounts that only show glowing bumps. Seek realistic content.
- **Set a gentle script.** Try: "I'm doing OK, thanks. It's been a tougher week." You don't owe anyone a performance.
- **Talk to your midwife.** Low mood that lasts over two weeks, or thoughts of self-harm, needs professional support.

## When to seek help
If you feel persistently low, tearful, numb, or detached from the pregnancy, tell a healthcare professional. Antenatal mental health support is common and effective — you are not wasting anyone's time.

## A reminder
Happiness is not a requirement of pregnancy. You are allowed to feel exactly as you feel, and those feelings can coexist with love for your baby. Real wellbeing comes from being supported honestly, not from performing joy.
