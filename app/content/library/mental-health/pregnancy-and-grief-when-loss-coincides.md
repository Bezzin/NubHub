# Pregnancy and Grief: When Loss Coincides

Being pregnant while grieving a loss is one of the most emotionally complicated experiences a person can go through. You are holding two enormous things at once — the joy and hope of a new life, and the pain and sadness of losing someone or something that mattered deeply. Both are real, and both deserve space.

## When Grief and Pregnancy Overlap

Grief during pregnancy can arise from many different circumstances:

- **Losing a loved one** — a parent, partner, close friend, or other significant person dying while you are pregnant
- **A previous pregnancy loss** — grief from a miscarriage, stillbirth, or termination for medical reasons that has not fully resolved before a new pregnancy begins
- **Pregnancy after loss** — the fear and sadness that can accompany a new pregnancy when a previous one ended in loss
- **Other significant losses** — relationship breakdown, loss of a job or identity, a diagnosis that changes how you see your future

Whatever the source of your grief, it does not pause because you are pregnant. You may find yourself crying unexpectedly, feeling detached from the pregnancy, struggling to feel excited, or oscillating between happiness and profound sadness in ways that feel confusing or even shameful. None of this means you are failing as a pregnant person or as a parent-to-be.

## The Emotional Complexity of Holding Both

One of the hardest things about grieving during pregnancy is the guilt that can accompany mixed emotions. You might feel guilty for feeling happy about the baby when someone you love has died. Or guilty for feeling sad about a loss when you are "supposed to" feel grateful for the pregnancy. You might worry that your grief is somehow harming your baby, or that feeling joy is a betrayal of the person you have lost.

These conflicting feelings are a normal part of holding grief and hope simultaneously. Feeling joy does not mean you have stopped grieving. Feeling grief does not mean you are not bonding with your baby. Both can be true at the same time, and neither cancels the other out.

It is also common to feel a sense of isolation during this time — as though no one around you fully understands what you are going through. Friends and family may focus on the pregnancy as good news, not realising how complicated things feel for you. Or they may be caught up in their own grief and not have the capacity to support you as a pregnant person.

## What Helps

There is no single right way to grieve during pregnancy. What helps will vary from person to person, but some things that many people find useful include:

**Talking to someone who understands.** This might be your midwife — who is trained to support your emotional wellbeing, not just your physical health — a counsellor or therapist, or a support group for people who have experienced pregnancy loss or bereavement. Your GP can refer you to talking therapies, or you can self-refer to many NHS IAPT (Improving Access to Psychological Therapies) services.

**Giving yourself permission to feel what you feel.** Trying to suppress grief or perform happiness you do not feel is exhausting and does not make the grief go away. Allowing yourself to feel sad, to cry, to talk about the person or thing you have lost, is not harmful — it is healthy.

**Marking the loss.** This might mean lighting a candle, creating a small ritual, writing in a journal, or setting aside time to grieve alongside time to focus on the pregnancy. Some people find it helpful to create a symbolic connection between the person they have lost and the baby they are carrying.

**Leaning on practical support.** Grief is physically tiring, and so is pregnancy. If people offer practical help — meals, company, transport — accepting it is not weakness.

**Seeking specialist support.** Cruse Bereavement Support (0808 808 1677) offers free confidential bereavement support in the UK. The Samaritans (116 123) are available around the clock if you need to talk and feel overwhelmed. If your grief relates to a previous pregnancy loss, organisations such as SANDS (stillbirth and neonatal death) and the Miscarriage Association offer specialist peer support.

## When to Talk to Your Midwife or GP

You should let your midwife or GP know if you are experiencing grief during pregnancy. They can monitor your emotional wellbeing alongside your physical health, refer you to perinatal mental health support if needed, and ensure your care plan takes your circumstances into account. You do not need to be in crisis to ask for support — your mental health matters throughout pregnancy, not only if things become severe.

If you are experiencing thoughts of harming yourself or feel unable to cope, contact your GP, call 111, or attend your nearest A&E. You can also call the Samaritans at any time on 116 123.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is it normal to grieve during pregnancy?

Yes, completely. Grief does not pause because you are pregnant, and pregnancy does not erase grief. If you have experienced a bereavement, a previous pregnancy loss, or any significant loss, it is entirely normal to carry those feelings during pregnancy. Many people also experience anticipatory grief — fearing something will go wrong with the current pregnancy — particularly after a previous loss. All of these experiences are valid, and support is available.

### Can grief affect my pregnancy?

Significant, sustained emotional distress can affect your general health and wellbeing, which is why it is important to seek support rather than try to cope alone. However, feeling sad, grieving, or having complicated emotions during pregnancy does not mean you are harming your baby. The most important thing is to look after yourself — eat as well as you can, rest, and ask for help. Your midwife or GP can advise on the support available to you.

### Where can I get support for grief during pregnancy in the UK?

Your midwife or GP is a good first point of contact — they can refer you to perinatal mental health services or talking therapies. Cruse Bereavement Support (0808 808 1677) provides free specialist bereavement support. The Miscarriage Association and SANDS offer peer support for those grieving a previous pregnancy loss. The Samaritans (116 123) are available 24 hours a day for anyone who needs to talk.

Speak to your midwife or GP for personalised support and to discuss your individual circumstances.
