# When People Touch Your Bump Without Asking

It happens in shops, at family gatherings, at work — someone reaches out and places a hand on your stomach without a word. For many pregnant women, this is one of the most jarring, intrusive experiences of pregnancy. You're not overreacting if you hate it. Your body is still your body.

## Why it feels so violating
Touch without consent bypasses your autonomy. Even when the person means it warmly, the physical intrusion can trigger discomfort, anxiety, or past trauma. Pregnancy can also make your skin more sensitive and your personal space feel smaller. A hand on your bump is not a greeting — it's a claim.

## The cultural excuse
There's a strange social norm that pregnancy bumps are communal property. People may say "it's a baby, not you" — but the baby lives inside *your* body, and you are the one being touched. There is no point in pregnancy where consent to your body is suspended.

## What you can say in the moment
- **The step back.** Physically move away and smile: "I'm a bit ticklish, thanks!"
- **The direct request.** "Please don't touch my bump — I'd rather people ask first."
- **The firm line.** "I'm not comfortable being touched without asking."
- **The humorous shut-down.** "That's a VIP area — tickets only!"

## If you freeze
Many women report that they freeze when it happens, then feel angry afterwards. Freezing is a normal stress response, not consent. If it happens, don't blame yourself — you can still address it next time or message the person later: "I realised I wasn't comfortable when you touched my bump earlier. I'd prefer people ask from now on."

## Enlisting allies
Tell your partner, friends, or family ahead of time: "If someone goes for my bump, can you redirect them?" Having someone step in is especially useful at gatherings where you don't want to make a scene.

## Teaching by example
If you have other children, this is a chance to model body autonomy: "We always ask before touching someone." The lesson lands more deeply when they see you protect your own boundaries too.

## A reminder
You don't owe anyone access to your body — bump or otherwise. "No" is not rude. Unwanted touch is.
