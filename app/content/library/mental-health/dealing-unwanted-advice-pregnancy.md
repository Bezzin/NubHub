# Dealing With Unwanted Advice in Pregnancy

Everyone has opinions about pregnancy. Here's how to handle unwanted advice gracefully.

The moment your bump becomes visible — sometimes even before — the unsolicited opinions tend to start. Relatives share horror stories you never asked for. Strangers in supermarkets comment on what you are eating. Well-meaning friends tell you what worked for them as though it is universal law. If you find this exhausting, overwhelming, or even upsetting, you are in very good company.

## Why People Give Advice {#s1}

Understanding where unsolicited advice comes from can make it slightly easier to tolerate, even if it does not make it less annoying.

Most people who offer unprompted pregnancy opinions are not trying to undermine you. They are usually:

- **Genuinely excited** and want to connect with you over something meaningful.
- **Sharing their own experience** as a way of processing it — your pregnancy has reminded them of theirs.
- **Trying to be helpful**, even if the delivery is clumsy or the advice is outdated.
- **Anxious themselves** and projecting that anxiety onto you in the form of warnings and cautions.

Recognising this does not mean you have to tolerate every intrusion with a smile. But it can help you choose your response without building unnecessary resentment.

## How to Respond {#s2}

You do not owe anyone a detailed explanation of your choices during pregnancy. A calm, brief response is almost always sufficient. Here are some approaches depending on the situation:

**The polite deflection** — for general advice you simply do not want to engage with:
- "Thanks, I'll bear that in mind."
- "I appreciate you sharing that."
- "We're looking into everything, thanks."

These phrases are pleasant, non-committal, and close the conversation.

**The midwife shield** — particularly useful when someone is pushing something medical or safety-related:
- "My midwife has given me guidance on that, so I'm all sorted."
- "I've already spoken to my GP about it — thanks for thinking of us."

This signals that you are taking your health seriously and deflects further input without opening a debate.

**The clear boundary** — for repeat offenders or particularly intrusive advice:
- "I appreciate the thought, but we've decided what we're doing."
- "We're keeping this one between us and our healthcare team."
- "I'd rather not discuss that, but thank you."

You do not need to justify these responses or apologise for them.

**The subject change** — simply moving on:
- Pivot to a question about them: "Anyway — how are things with you?"
- Comment on something nearby: useful in social situations where a subtle redirect is all you need.

## When It Crosses a Line {#s3}

Most unsolicited advice is irritating but harmless. There are situations, though, where it is reasonable to be more direct:

- If someone repeatedly ignores your stated preferences despite gentle deflections, a clearer boundary is appropriate: "I've mentioned before that I'm not looking for advice on this. Can we talk about something else?"
- If advice comes with judgment, criticism, or scaremongering — particularly about choices you have made after discussion with your healthcare team — it is fine to disengage entirely from that conversation.
- If someone touches your bump without asking, you are well within your rights to say, "I'd rather you didn't do that, thanks" and step back.
- If a colleague or acquaintance is asking intrusive questions about your pregnancy, health, or plans, "I'm keeping the details private for now" is a complete answer.

## Protecting Your Mental Health {#s4}

Constant commentary on your body, your choices, and your pregnancy can genuinely wear you down — especially if you are already managing anxiety or exhaustion. A few things that help:

- **Limit conversations about the pregnancy** with people who make you feel worse, not better.
- **Talk it through with your partner or a close friend** who gets it — venting to someone who validates your feelings can diffuse the frustration.
- **Remember you do not have to justify your choices** to anyone who is not your healthcare provider.
- **Some advice will actually be useful.** Rather than blanket rejection, it can help to listen briefly and then decide privately whether anything is worth taking forward.

If unsolicited comments are making you feel genuinely anxious, criticised, or distressed, it is worth mentioning to your midwife. Antenatal anxiety is common, and support is available.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How do I stop unwanted pregnancy advice?

You cannot prevent people from forming opinions, but you can stop engaging with them. Polite deflections ("thanks, I'll keep that in mind" or "my midwife has advised me on that") close most conversations quickly. For persistent advice-givers, a clear but calm statement — "I appreciate your concern, but I'm following my healthcare team's guidance" — draws a firmer line. You do not need to debate, explain, or justify.

### What do I say when strangers comment on my bump or pregnancy?

You are not obliged to respond at all, but if you want to say something, brief and pleasant is usually enough: "Ha, thanks" and move on. If the comment is rude or intrusive, "I'd rather not discuss that" is a complete and acceptable response. Most strangers who comment mean no harm and are simply making conversation — the quickest way to end it is not to engage beyond a polite acknowledgement.

### Is it normal to find pregnancy advice overwhelming?

Completely normal. Pregnancy often brings out other people's strong opinions and unprocessed anxieties, and when you are already managing physical changes, fatigue, and your own worries, constant input from others can feel like too much. Many pregnant women describe feeling scrutinised in a way they never expected. Acknowledging that it is genuinely hard — rather than telling yourself you should not mind — is a good place to start.

Always speak to your midwife if unsolicited advice or other social pressures are affecting your mental wellbeing during pregnancy. You deserve support, not just tolerance.
