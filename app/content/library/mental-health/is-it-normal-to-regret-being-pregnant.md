# Is It Normal to Regret Being Pregnant?

It's one of the most taboo thoughts in pregnancy, and one of the most common: "What have I done?" If a wave of regret has hit you — about being pregnant, about the changes ahead, or even about having a child at all — you are not broken, and you are not alone.

## How Common Is It?

Far more common than anyone admits. Ambivalence in pregnancy is well-documented in perinatal mental health research, yet rarely discussed openly because it carries such heavy stigma. Many women feel deep regret at some point and never say so, carrying secret shame alongside the pregnancy. The silence around these feelings makes them feel more alarming than they need to — it can seem as though everyone else is thrilled, and you are uniquely flawed. You are not.

Pregnancy involves enormous change: physical, emotional, financial, relational, and identity-level all at once. It would be unusual to feel nothing but joy when so much is shifting simultaneously. Ambivalence and regret are a rational response to an overwhelming life event, not evidence of bad character.

## Why Regret Can Appear

- **Physical toll.** Severe sickness, pain, or exhaustion can make you wish it would stop. When your body is struggling, it is hard to feel grateful.
- **Life upheaval.** Career, freedom, finances, and identity all shift — sometimes in ways you had not fully anticipated until you were actually in it.
- **Relationship strain.** A wobbling partnership, disagreements about parenting, or a lack of support at home can make the future feel frightening rather than exciting.
- **Hormonal impact.** Brain chemistry is literally changing throughout pregnancy. Mood, perspective, and emotional regulation all shift with fluctuating hormones.
- **Unplanned pregnancy.** When the timing was not yours to choose, grief for your previous life is entirely natural and valid.
- **Long-wanted pregnancy.** Even pregnancies that took years to achieve can bring shock once they are real. Wanting something and adjusting to it are two separate things.
- **Previous loss.** If you have experienced miscarriage, stillbirth, or loss, conflicted feelings this time round are very common.
- **External pressure.** When others expect you to be visibly overjoyed, feeling anything else can be isolating and magnify the shame.

## The Crucial Distinction

Regret about being pregnant is not the same as not wanting your baby or being an unfit parent. The first is a feeling about a massive life change; the second is a judgment on your character and worth. Conflating the two causes needless shame. Feelings — even difficult ones — are not predictions of the future. Many people who felt profound regret in pregnancy go on to feel deeply bonded with their children. Feelings in the moment do not determine who you will become as a parent.

## What to Do With the Feeling

- **Name it safely.** Saying "I regret this today" to a trusted person or writing it in a journal can release pressure that builds when you suppress it entirely.
- **Separate feeling from fact.** A feeling is information about your current state, not a verdict on your future or your worth.
- **Notice patterns.** Is the regret constant, or does it come in waves tied to particularly hard days — days of illness, exhaustion, or conflict? Context matters.
- **Avoid isolation.** Shame grows in silence. Speaking to someone — a partner, close friend, or a helpline — reduces the weight of carrying it alone.
- **Talk to your midwife or GP.** They hear this regularly and will not judge you. It is part of their role to support your emotional wellbeing, not just your physical health.

## When It Is More Than Ambivalence

If regret comes alongside persistent low mood, hopelessness, numbness, detachment from the baby, inability to experience pleasure, significant sleep disruption beyond normal pregnancy, or thoughts of harming yourself, this may be antenatal depression or anxiety. These are recognised medical conditions, not character flaws, and they are treatable. Early support significantly changes outcomes — please do not wait until after birth to seek help.

You can speak to your midwife at any appointment, contact your GP directly, or call the Samaritans on 116 123 (free, 24 hours) if you need to talk. If you are having thoughts of harming yourself or ending your life, please call 999 or go to your nearest A&E.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Does regretting pregnancy mean I will be a bad parent?

No. Feeling regret or ambivalence during pregnancy does not predict how you will parent. Many people feel conflicted during pregnancy and go on to be loving, attentive parents. Emotions during a huge life change are not a reliable indicator of your parenting capacity.

### Will these feelings pass on their own?

For many people, yes — particularly when regret is tied to specific hard phases like severe nausea or exhaustion. As circumstances shift, feelings often do too. However, if low mood is persistent, it is worth speaking to a professional rather than waiting and hoping it lifts.

### Who can I talk to if I can't face telling my midwife?

The Samaritans (116 123) are free and available around the clock. Tommy's charity runs a free midwife helpline. PANDAS Foundation supports those experiencing perinatal mental health difficulties. You do not need to be in crisis to reach out.

Mixed or negative feelings in pregnancy do not make you a bad parent. They make you a person undergoing a life-changing event. Honest support is always better than silent shame. Speak to your midwife or GP for personal guidance suited to your situation.
