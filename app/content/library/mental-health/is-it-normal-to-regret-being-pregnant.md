# Is It Normal to Regret Being Pregnant?

It's one of the most taboo thoughts in pregnancy, and one of the most common: "What have I done?" If a wave of regret has hit you — about being pregnant, about the changes ahead, or even about having a child at all — you are not broken, and you are not alone.

## How common is it?
Far more common than anyone admits. Ambivalence in pregnancy is well-documented in perinatal mental health research, yet rarely discussed openly because it carries such heavy stigma. Many women feel deep regret at some point and never say so, carrying secret shame alongside the pregnancy.

## Why regret can appear
- **Physical toll.** Severe sickness, pain, or exhaustion can make you wish it away.
- **Life upheaval.** Career, freedom, finances, and identity all shift.
- **Relationship strain.** A wobbly partnership can make the future feel frightening.
- **Hormonal impact.** Brain chemistry is literally changing — mood and perspective shift with it.
- **Unplanned pregnancy.** When the timing wasn't yours, grief for your previous life is natural.
- **Fertility history.** Even long-wanted pregnancies can bring shock once real.

## The crucial distinction
Regret about being pregnant is not the same as not wanting your baby or being an unfit parent. The first is a feeling about a massive life change; the second is a judgment on your worth. Conflating them causes needless shame.

## What to do with the feeling
- **Name it safely.** Saying "I regret this today" to a trusted person or journal can release pressure.
- **Separate feeling from fact.** A feeling is information, not a verdict.
- **Notice patterns.** Is it constant, or does it come in waves tied to hard days?
- **Talk to your midwife or GP.** They hear this regularly and won't judge.

## When it's more than ambivalence
If regret comes with persistent low mood, hopelessness, numbness, detachment from the baby, or thoughts of harming yourself, this may be antenatal depression. It is treatable. Please tell a professional — early support changes outcomes.

## A reminder
Mixed or negative feelings in pregnancy do not make you a bad parent. They make you a person undergoing a life-changing event. Honest support is always better than silent shame.
