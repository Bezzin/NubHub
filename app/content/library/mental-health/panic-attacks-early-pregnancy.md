# Panic Attacks in Early Pregnancy

A panic attack is a sudden, intense surge of fear that comes with physical symptoms like a racing heart, shortness of breath, and dizziness. Panic attacks can be frightening, especially in pregnancy when you may worry they signal something is wrong with your baby. They are common, they are treatable, and they do not harm your baby.

## Recognising a panic attack in pregnancy {#section-1}

Typical symptoms include a pounding or racing heartbeat, chest tightness, feeling faint or dizzy, shortness of breath, tingling in the hands or face, a sense of unreality, and an overwhelming urge to flee. In pregnancy these symptoms can be confused with physical changes, but panic attacks come on suddenly and peak within minutes.

## Safe ways to cope with a panic attack {#section-2}

Try slow belly breathing: in through your nose for four counts, hold for four, out through your mouth for six. Ground yourself with the 5-4-3-2-1 technique: name five things you see, four you feel, three you hear, two you smell, one you taste. Remind yourself the attack will pass. If attacks are frequent, ask your GP or midwife about a referral to a perinatal mental health team.

## FAQ {#faq}

### Can a panic attack harm my baby?

No. A short burst of stress hormones from a panic attack does not harm your baby. The main impact is on your own wellbeing, which is why treatment matters.

### Are panic attacks common in early pregnancy?

Yes, especially if you have a history of anxiety or have experienced pregnancy loss. Hormonal changes and uncertainty can be triggers.

### What if my panic attack symptoms feel like a physical emergency?

If you have chest pain, fainting, or bleeding alongside panic symptoms, seek urgent medical care via 111 or A&E to rule out other causes.
