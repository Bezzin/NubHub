# Depression in Early Pregnancy: Signs to Watch

Low mood in early pregnancy is often dismissed as hormones or tiredness, but persistent sadness, numbness, or hopelessness can be signs of antenatal depression. Recognising it early means you can get support sooner, which is better for you and your baby.

## Signs of Depression in Early Pregnancy {#section-1}

Watch for persistent low mood lasting more than two weeks, loss of interest in things you usually enjoy, feeling numb or disconnected from the pregnancy, changes in appetite, difficulty concentrating, excessive guilt, and thoughts that you are a bad mother or would be better off not here. If you notice these, speak to your GP or midwife.

Depression during pregnancy does not always present as obvious crying or visible distress. Many women describe it as a heavy flatness — going through the motions of daily life without feeling connected to it. You might notice that you cannot feel excited about milestones that should feel meaningful, or that you are withdrawing from people without being able to explain why. Some women become intensely irritable and feel guilty about it, not realising that irritability is a recognised feature of depression.

It is important to know that these feelings do not mean you are a bad parent or that you do not love your baby. Depression is a health condition, not a reflection of your character or your capacity to care for your child.

## How Depression Differs from Normal Pregnancy Mood Changes {#section-2}

Mood swings in pregnancy come and go and often relate to specific triggers — a difficult conversation, physical discomfort, or a worrying thought that passes. They tend to lift within hours and do not consistently impair daily functioning. Depression is more constant, lasts longer, and affects your ability to function across different areas of life — work, relationships, self-care, and daily tasks.

A key distinction is duration and pervasiveness. Feeling tearful for an hour after a difficult midwife appointment is different from feeling persistently low for two weeks with no obvious trigger, or feeling unable to get out of bed most mornings. Depression can also make you feel detached from your baby or pregnancy in a way that ordinary hormonal shifts do not. You may feel as though you are observing your pregnancy from a distance rather than experiencing it.

If you are unsure whether what you are experiencing is normal mood variation or something more, it is always right to ask. Your midwife or GP will not judge you for raising it, and no concern is too small to mention.

## How to Get Support in the UK {#getting-support}

Your first point of contact should be your midwife or GP. At your booking appointment and again later in pregnancy, you will be asked routine questions about your mood — this screening is specifically designed to catch antenatal depression early. You can also raise it at any appointment, not just at the times it is formally asked.

Talking therapies such as cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) are the first-line treatment recommended for depression in pregnancy and are available on the NHS through Talking Therapies services (previously called IAPT). You can self-refer in most areas of England without needing a GP referral first. Waiting times vary, so the earlier you ask, the sooner you will be seen.

In some cases, your GP may discuss antidepressant medication. Some antidepressants are considered suitable during pregnancy, and your GP or a perinatal psychiatrist can advise you on the balance of risks and benefits for your individual situation. Never stop prescribed medication suddenly without medical advice.

If you are having thoughts of self-harm or suicide, contact your GP or midwife urgently, call NHS 111, or go to your nearest A&E. You can also contact the Samaritans at any time on 116 123.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Is depression in pregnancy the same as postnatal depression?

No. Antenatal depression occurs during pregnancy and is just as common as postnatal depression, though it is less often discussed publicly. Both are recognised conditions that respond well to treatment when identified early.

### Can depression in early pregnancy affect my baby?

Untreated depression can affect your ability to care for yourself, attend appointments, eat well, and bond with your baby — all of which matter for pregnancy outcomes. Seeking and accepting support is a positive step for both you and your baby, not a sign of failure.

### What treatment is safe for depression in pregnancy?

Talking therapies such as CBT are first-line and have no physical risks in pregnancy. Some antidepressants are considered safe and are prescribed when the benefits outweigh the risks. Your GP can advise on options specific to your circumstances and how far along you are.
