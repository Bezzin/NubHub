# Pregnancy and OCD: How to Manage It

OCD can worsen during pregnancy or appear for the first time. Perinatal OCD is more common than people realise, and with the right support it is very treatable — so knowing the signs and reaching out early makes a real difference.

## Perinatal OCD Symptoms {#section-1}

OCD during pregnancy tends to centre on fears related to the baby's safety and wellbeing, though it can take many forms. Common symptoms include:

- **Intrusive thoughts** about harm coming to the baby — these are unwanted, distressing, and repetitive
- **Compulsive checking** behaviours, such as repeatedly checking the baby's movements, monitoring apps, or thermometers
- **Excessive cleaning or hand washing**, driven by contamination fears
- **Reassurance-seeking** from midwives, partners, or online sources — repeatedly asking for reassurance that everything is fine, but never feeling truly settled
- **Avoidance** of certain situations, people, or objects that feel threatening
- **Rituals** that feel necessary to prevent something bad happening, even when you know logically they make no difference

The most important thing to understand is this: intrusive thoughts — even very dark ones — are not a reflection of your true wishes or character. They are a symptom of OCD. Having a thought does not mean you will act on it. People with OCD are typically horrified by their own intrusive thoughts, which is precisely what distinguishes OCD from intent.

## Why Pregnancy Can Trigger or Worsen OCD {#section-why}

The hormonal shifts of pregnancy — particularly sharp rises in oestrogen and progesterone — affect the brain's serotonin system, which plays a key role in anxiety and OCD. This is one reason why perinatal OCD (OCD that occurs during pregnancy or the first year after birth) is a recognised and well-documented condition.

Pregnancy also brings a genuine increase in responsibility and uncertainty, which can feed the OCD cycle. If you have had OCD before, pregnancy may cause a flare. If you have never had OCD before, pregnancy can sometimes be the first trigger. Either way, you are not to blame, and it is not a sign you are unfit to be a parent.

Postpartum OCD (after birth) is also very common and is sometimes confused with postpartum depression or psychosis. It is important that healthcare providers identify which condition is present, as the treatments differ.

## Getting Help {#section-2}

Perinatal OCD responds well to the right treatment. You do not have to wait until after the baby is born.

**Talk to your GP or midwife** — tell them what you are experiencing as honestly as you can. Perinatal mental health is taken seriously by the NHS. Your midwife can refer you to a specialist perinatal mental health service if one is available in your area.

**Ask about Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT) with Exposure and Response Prevention (ERP)** — ERP is the recommended psychological therapy for OCD. It involves gradually facing feared thoughts or situations without performing compulsions. It is highly effective and is recommended by NICE guidelines. Talking therapies in pregnancy are safe and have no risks to the baby.

**Medication** — some medications used to treat OCD (certain SSRIs) are considered safe during pregnancy and are used when symptoms are severe enough to affect daily functioning. Your GP or psychiatrist will weigh up the benefits and risks with you individually.

**PANDAS Foundation** and **OCD-UK** both offer support and information specifically for perinatal OCD and are good resources if you are waiting for a referral.

Do not suffer in silence. Many women fear that disclosing OCD or intrusive thoughts will lead to their baby being taken away — this is a very common worry, and it is not how the system works. Seeking help shows you are taking care of yourself and your baby.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### Can pregnancy trigger OCD?

Yes. Hormonal changes during pregnancy can trigger OCD in women who have never had it before, or cause existing OCD to worsen significantly. Perinatal OCD is well-recognised by the NHS and mental health professionals, and it is treatable. If you recognise the symptoms, speak to your GP or midwife — the sooner you get support, the better.

### Are intrusive thoughts in pregnancy a sign of danger?

No. Intrusive thoughts — including disturbing thoughts about the baby — are a hallmark symptom of OCD, not a sign that you are dangerous. People with OCD are typically deeply distressed by these thoughts, which is what sets them apart from genuine intent. If you are struggling with intrusive thoughts, talk to your GP or midwife so you can access the right support.

### What treatment is available for OCD in pregnancy on the NHS?

CBT with Exposure and Response Prevention (ERP) is the first-line recommended treatment and is available through NHS Talking Therapies (formerly IAPT) or specialist perinatal mental health services. Some SSRIs are also used in pregnancy when the clinical need is significant. Your GP can discuss what is appropriate for your situation.

Always speak to your midwife or GP for personal advice about managing OCD during pregnancy.
